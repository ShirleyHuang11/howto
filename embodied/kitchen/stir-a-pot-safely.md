## Goal

The pot's contents are stirred evenly (nothing sticking or scorching on the bottom) without steam burns, splatter, or a utensil left in the pot.

## Preconditions

- A pot on an active burner, contents at or below 2/3 full.
- A long-handled stirring utensil: wooden spoon or silicone spatula. Metal is acceptable only in bare stainless or enamel, never in nonstick.
- A spoon rest, small plate, or folded paper towel within reach of the stove.

## Steps

1. **Rotate the pot handle inward.** Turn it so it points over the counter, not out over the floor or a walkway. → *Expect:* handle cannot be snagged by a passing hip or reaching child.
2. **Choose the utensil for the pot and contents.** [BRANCH: nonstick pot → wood or silicone only | thick contents (risotto, oatmeal, sauce) → flat-edged spatula that can scrape the bottom | thin liquid (soup, stock) → any long spoon] → *Expect:* utensil handle long enough that your knuckles stay above the rim while the head reaches the bottom.
3. **Take the grip.** Hold the utensil like a hammer, overhand near the handle's end, and steady the pot by its handle with your other hand (through a pot holder if the handle is metal). → *Expect:* pot cannot rotate or walk on the burner when you stir.
4. **If lidded, vent before opening.** Lift the lid tilted away from your face and let the steam plume rise on the far side, then set the lid down wet-side up. → *Expect:* steam cloud vents away from you; no condensation dripped on the burner or floor.
5. **Stand to the side of the steam column.** Position yourself so rising steam passes in front of you, not into your face and forearm. → *Expect:* you can see the surface of the contents without leaning over the pot.
6. **Stir bottom-first, then in a slow figure-eight.** Drag the utensil edge flat across the bottom to lift anything settling, then sweep the walls and middle. Keep the speed low: splatter comes from fast stirring, not thorough stirring. → *Expect:* even color and texture; no dark flecks lifting off the bottom (dark flecks → F2); no drops leaving the pot.
7. **Park the utensil on the spoon rest between stirs.** Never leave it standing in the pot (it heats up, tips the pot, or melts) and never flat on the counter (it glues sauce to the surface). → *Expect:* utensil on the rest, drips contained, handle cool for the next stir.
8. **Re-lid if the recipe wants it, tilted vent-open for foamy contents.** Starches (pasta, oats, beans) boil over under a sealed lid. → *Expect:* lid seated (or cracked open for starches); simmer steady, not climbing.

## Decision points

- Contents thick enough that the utensil stands up on its own → stir every 1 to 2 minutes and drop the burner one notch; thick mixtures scorch fastest.
- Pot is overfull and stirring slops the rim → ladle some out before stirring again, do not fight it.
- Someone (especially a child or pet) enters the work zone mid-stir → slow down, park the utensil, and keep your body between them and the stove until they pass.

## Failure modes & recovery

- **F1 Steam burn on the stirring hand:** knuckles dipped below rim height → run cool (not icy) water over the skin for 10+ minutes; resume with a longer utensil.
- **F2 Scorch layer forming on the bottom:** utensil drags rough, dark flecks surface, smell turns toasty → stop scraping immediately, pour the contents into a clean pot without disturbing the burnt layer, and continue at lower heat; scraping mixes burnt into everything.
- **F3 Boil-over while lidded:** foam at the lid edge, hissing on the burner → lift the lid off (away from face) or kill the heat; the foam collapses in seconds; wipe the stovetop after it cools.
- **F4 Utensil slid into the pot:** retrieve with tongs or a second utensil, never fingers; a fully submerged wooden spoon is a tongs job even if it "looks reachable".

## Verification

Contents are uniform in color and texture with nothing stuck to the bottom, the utensil rests on the spoon rest (not in the pot, not on the bare counter), the pot handle points inward, and no burns or splatter occurred.

## Variations

- Induction or electric coil: the pot walks more easily than on gas grates; the stabilizing hand on the handle matters more.
- Deep stockpot: use a long-handled spoon held near the end and stir from a step back; leaning over a tall pot puts your face in the steam.

## Safety & privacy

Medium risk, all thermal: steam scalds (vent lids away, stand off the steam column), scorch-and-splatter (slow stirring, bottom-first), and the snagged handle (always rotated inward). The spoon rest is not fussiness; a sauce-covered counter is how the next person's sleeve ends up in a burner.
