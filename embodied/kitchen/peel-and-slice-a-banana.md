## Goal

A banana is peeled cleanly and cut into even slices on a board, delivered to a plate or bowl before browning sets in.

## Preconditions

- A ripe banana: yellow, yielding slightly to a gentle squeeze. Green bananas peel poorly and taste starchy; heavily brown ones smash rather than slice.
- A cutting board that does not slide (damp paper towel underneath if it does).
- A small sharp knife (paring or petty). A dull knife crushes soft fruit instead of cutting it.

## Steps

1. **Orient the banana.** Identify the stem end (the thick, curved attachment point) and the blossom end (the small dark nub at the opposite tip). → *Expect:* both ends identified; fruit held in the non-dominant hand.
2. **Open from the blossom end.** Pinch the dark nub between thumb and forefinger and squeeze; the peel splits cleanly. This is the reliable end: the stem end often bends without breaking and bruises the top of the fruit into mush. [BRANCH: blossom end opens → continue | banana is under-ripe and won't pinch open → nick the peel at the blossom end with the knife tip, on the board, blade away from the holding hand] → *Expect:* peel split at the tip with the fruit's top intact, not mashed.
3. **Peel in thirds.** Pull each strip of peel down slowly toward the stem, one at a time, keeping the strips attached at the stem end. → *Expect:* naked fruit, peel strips hanging like a skirt from the stem end.
4. **Strip the strings.** Run a thumb down the fruit to remove any flat white strings (phloem strands) still clinging. → *Expect:* smooth fruit surface; strings gone with the peel.
5. **Detach and discard the peel.** Snap the peel off at the stem, and put it directly in the bin or compost, never left on the counter or floor. → *Expect:* peel in the bin; board clear.
6. **Slice on the board with a claw grip.** Lay the banana on the board. Curl the fingertips of the holding hand so the knuckles (not fingertips) face the blade, and cut straight down in slow strokes, moving the claw back between cuts. Aim for uniform rounds, about 1 cm for cereal and snacking, thinner for topping toast. → *Expect:* even coins of consistent thickness; fingertips never in the blade's path.
7. **Transfer at once.** Slide the slices off the board into the bowl or onto the plate with the back of the knife or a scooped hand. → *Expect:* slices in the serving vessel within a minute or two of cutting; knife set at the back of the board, edge away.

## Decision points

- Slices will sit longer than ~15 minutes before eating → toss them with a little lemon, lime, or orange juice; the acid slows the enzymatic browning that starts as soon as the flesh meets air.
- Banana is very soft (spotty-brown) → skip slicing; it will smear. Mash it for oatmeal or baking instead.
- No knife available or the eater is a small child → break the peeled banana into chunks by hand; ragged but safe.

## Failure modes & recovery

- **F1 Stem end bent but didn't open:** top of the fruit is bruising under repeated attempts → stop, flip, and pinch the blossom end; the bruised tip can be trimmed off on the board.
- **F2 Peel tears off in stubby pieces (under-ripe fruit):** score the peel lengthwise with the knife tip on the board, shallow enough not to cut the flesh, then lift each panel off.
- **F3 Slices browning before serving:** gray-brown surface on the coins → an acid toss (see decision points) rescues appearance for a few hours; browned slices remain safe to eat regardless.
- **F4 Board sliding while cutting:** any lateral movement under the knife → stop, wipe the counter, and set the board on a damp paper towel or cloth before the next cut.

## Verification

Even slices sit in the serving vessel with no browning, the peel is in the bin (not on any surface someone could slip on), the knife is at the back of the board with the edge facing away, and all fingertips are intact.

## Variations

- Serving style: rounds for cereal and pancakes; lengthwise halves for banana splits (steady the fruit flat-side down after the first cut).
- Meal-prep: bananas brown fast even with acid; slice the morning of, not the night before.

## Safety & privacy

Low risk overall. The two real hazards are the knife (claw grip, cut on a stable board, never toward the holding hand) and the dropped peel underfoot (the cliché is a cliché because wet peel on smooth flooring genuinely is slick). Browning is cosmetic, not a safety issue.
