## Goal

A loaded breakfast tray (plate, hot drink, juice, cutlery, extras) is carried from kitchen to the recipient and set down with nothing spilled, tipped, or dropped en route.

## Preconditions

- A rigid tray with raised edges, wide enough for a plate plus two drinks with a finger of clearance between items. A flat board with no lip is not a tray.
- The route walked once empty: doors noted, floor obstacles (toys, cables, pet bowls) cleared, destination surface cleared and reachable.
- Food and drinks ready before loading begins; a tray is a bad staging area for cooking.

## Steps

1. **Line the tray.** Lay a cloth napkin or non-slip mat over the tray base. This one change stops most in-transit sliding. → *Expect:* liner flat, corners not folded up under where items will sit.
2. **Load heavy and low in the center.** The plate goes in the middle; it is the mass anchor. → *Expect:* tray balances level when lifted a centimeter by both hands.
3. **Place drinks inboard, filled to 80 percent.** Mug and juice glass go near the center-line of the tray, not at the edges, handles turned inward. The last 20 percent of a full cup is what ends up on the floor. [BRANCH: teapot or carafe → spout pointing inward over the tray, lid checked seated | no pot → skip] → *Expect:* no vessel within 3 cm of the tray rim; liquid levels below the sloshing line.
4. **Add smalls in the gaps.** Cutlery parallel to the tray's long edge (rolls otherwise), napkin under the cutlery to pin it, jam and butter in remaining gaps. → *Expect:* nothing overlaps a plate rim or leans on another item; every item touches the liner.
5. **Do the shake test.** Grip both ends and tilt the tray one or two degrees each way. Anything that slides now slides worse mid-hallway; re-seat it. → *Expect:* zero movement at small tilt.
6. **Lift with two hands at the balance points, thumbs over the rim.** Elbows in, tray held slightly above waist height, back straight. Do not hug the tray against your chest; you need to see its surface. → *Expect:* level tray, both drink surfaces still, view of the route over the tray.
7. **Walk the route slowly, eyes ahead not on the drinks.** Staring at the liquid causes over-correction; the liquid follows your gait. Announce yourself at blind corners ("coming through, hot drinks"). Anyone crossing your path → stop fully, let them pass. → *Expect:* smooth, even steps; no slosh rings climbing the mug walls.
8. **Handle doors before or without hands.** [BRANCH: door already open or can be shouldered gently → pass through | latched door → set the tray down on a nearby stable surface, open and block the door, re-lift] Never balance the tray on one hand or a raised knee to work a handle. → *Expect:* both hands on the tray whenever it is airborne.
9. **Set down back-edge first.** Land the tray's far edge on the destination surface, then lower the near edge, then release. Confirm the surface holds it (no overhang past half the tray depth) before letting go. → *Expect:* tray fully supported, drinks unspilled, recipient warned which item is hot.

## Decision points

- Route includes stairs → drop drink fill to two-thirds, or make two trips (drinks separately); stairs plus full cups is the classic tray failure.
- Tray recipient is in bed → have them sit up and stabilize their lap or a bed table before you release; a tray on loose bedding tips.
- No tray with a lip available → two trips instead; carrying drink-on-plate stacks by hand fails more often than it saves time.

## Failure modes & recovery

- **F1 Slosh starts mid-carry:** rings climbing the cup → stop walking entirely, let the liquid settle to still, resume at half speed; do not try to counter-tilt in motion.
- **F2 An item starts sliding:** pause, level the tray, and if it keeps creeping, set the whole tray down on the nearest stable surface and re-seat; mid-air one-handed rescues drop the whole load.
- **F3 Hot liquid spilled on carrier:** set the tray down at once (anywhere stable beats nowhere), then cool the skin under running water for 10+ minutes; the tray can wait.
- **F4 No clear surface at the destination:** never hold the loaded tray while rearranging one-handed → back up to the last good surface, set down, clear the destination, second trip.

## Verification

The tray sits fully supported at the destination, all liquid remains in its vessels at their original levels, nothing shifted from its loaded position, the recipient knows which items are hot, and the route behind you has no drips.

## Variations

- Bed tray with legs: same loading rules; deploy the legs before loading, not after.
- Serving multiple people: one tray per person's hot drink maximum; overload is the failure, not trip count.

## Safety & privacy

Medium risk from hot liquid over hands and lap, concentrated in three moments: lifting, doors, and set-down. The protocol (80 percent fills, shake test, full stop for crossers, back-edge-first landing) exists because every one of those rules is a spill that happened.
