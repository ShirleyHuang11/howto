## Goal

The essentials a person needs from bed (water, medication, light, a way to call for help, their glasses and phone) are arranged on a bedside surface so every one of them is reachable without standing up, with medications visible and clearly organized.

## Preconditions

- A stable nightstand or table beside the bed at roughly mattress height.
- The person's own medication schedule and any care instructions, so the right things are staged (never guess doses; stage only what a caregiver or the person confirms).
- The person present or their reach range known: which side they lie on, how far their arm extends.

## Steps

1. **Clear and stabilize the surface.** Remove clutter, wipe it, confirm the nightstand does not rock. → *Expect:* a clean, steady surface at about the height of the mattress top.
2. **Place water within easy reach, spill-safe.** A lidded cup or a bottle with a straw beats an open glass that gets knocked over in the dark. Set it on the side the person's dominant hand reaches. → *Expect:* the person can grasp and drink without leaning off the bed; a spill would not soak bedding or electronics.
3. **Stage medication visibly and unambiguously.** Use a labeled day/time pill organizer, not loose bottles, so "did I take it" is answerable by looking. Keep it in a consistent spot and orientation. ⚠️ *Irreversible:* an accidental double dose is real harm. Stage only the doses due, per the person's schedule, and keep the rest out of the station if confusion or a double-dose risk exists. → *Expect:* the correct compartment is visible and distinguishable; nothing about which pills, when, is left to memory.
4. **Position light for one-touch on/off.** A lamp or a touch light the person can turn on before sitting up, reachable without fumbling. Route the cord so it cannot pull the lamp down. → *Expect:* light comes on with a single reachable touch; the cord is tucked, not draped across the reach path.
5. **Set the call device where it is always found.** Phone on its charger, or a dedicated call button or bell, in a fixed spot the person can locate in the dark by feel. → *Expect:* the person taps or presses it from lying down; it is charged and (if a phone) volume-up and unlocked-to-dial or on emergency-accessible.
6. **Add the small dignity items.** Glasses (folded, lenses up), tissues, the TV remote, anything the person reaches for reflexively. → *Expect:* each item in a consistent, felt-for spot within the reach zone.
7. **Run the reach test from the bed.** Have the person lie as they normally do and touch each item in turn: water, meds, light, call device, glasses. Anything they strain for gets moved closer. → *Expect:* every essential is reached without the person lifting their torso off the bed or overbalancing.

## Decision points

- Person has limited or one-sided mobility (post-stroke, injury) → build the whole station on their strong side; a well-stocked far side is useless.
- Confusion or dementia risk → medications do not stay on an open bedside station; a caregiver dispenses them, and only non-drug items live within reach.
- Fall risk on standing → make the station complete enough that there is no reason to get up in the night for water, light, or the phone.

## Failure modes & recovery

- **F1 Item keeps getting knocked over:** move it behind a lip or into a caddy, and switch open cups for lidded ones. The dark plus a sweeping arm is the cause.
- **F2 Person can't find the call device at night:** anchor it to a fixed tactile landmark (clip to the bed rail, a specific corner) and test finding it with the light off.
- **F3 Cord or cable across the reach path:** re-route so nothing crosses where a hand sweeps or a foot lands; a pulled cord drags a lamp or phone to the floor.
- **F4 Uncertain whether a dose was taken:** the organizer answers it by sight; if still unclear, do not add a dose. Check with the caregiver or pharmacy record.

## Verification

From their normal lying position the person can reach water, the correct staged medication, a one-touch light, and a charged call device, plus glasses and personal items, without standing or overbalancing; medications are visible and organized rather than loose; no cord crosses the reach path.

## Variations

- Hospital or care-facility bed: use the rail-mounted table and the nurse call button as the call device; same reach-test principle.
- Couple sharing a bed: two stations, each on the sleeper's own side, so neither reaches across the other.

## Safety & privacy

Medium risk, concentrated in medication (the double-dose ⚠️) and fall prevention. The two rules: stage only confirmed doses in a way that makes "taken or not" answerable by sight, and make the station complete enough that the person never needs to stand in the dark. Medication layout also reveals health information, so keep the station out of view of casual visitors if the person prefers.
