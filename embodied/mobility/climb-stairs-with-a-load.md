## Goal

A carried load is moved up (or down) a staircase without a fall, a dropped item, or a strain, keeping one hand free for the rail and clear sight of each step.

## Preconditions

- A load light enough to carry with one arm, or split into two trips, so the other hand can reach the rail. If it needs both arms, it needs a second person or a different route.
- A staircase with at least one usable handrail, adequately lit.
- Footwear with grip and laces tied; the clear path checked for objects, pets, and slick spots.

## Steps

1. **Assess the load against the free-hand rule.** If you cannot hold the load and still free one hand for the rail, stop and rebalance: split the load, use a backpack, or get help. → *Expect:* one hand committed to the load, one hand available for the rail.
2. **Shape the load for sightlines.** Carry it on the hip or to one side, not stacked in front of your face. You must see the tread edge of every step. → *Expect:* an unobstructed downward line of sight to the next two steps.
3. **Set the load onto the rail side you will not use, or the opposite.** Hold the load on the side away from the rail so the free hand meets the rail cleanly. → *Expect:* rail-side hand empty and tracking the rail; load-side hand bearing the weight.
4. **Establish the rail before the first step.** Grip the rail at the bottom newel before lifting a foot. → *Expect:* firm rail contact established while both feet are still on level ground.
5. **Climb one step at a time, leading consistently.** Plant the full foot on each tread, not just the ball on the edge; slide the rail hand up in pace with each step rather than reaching ahead. ⚠️ *Irreversible:* a missed edge with a load is how stair falls happen; never take two treads at once while loaded, and never let the load block your view of the next edge. → *Expect:* each foot lands flat and centered on its tread; rail contact never breaks.
6. **Rest at landings, not mid-flight.** [BRANCH: single flight → climb through | multiple flights or heavy load → set the load down at each landing, breathe, regrip] On a long or heavy carry, use landings to reset grip and posture. → *Expect:* on multi-flight climbs, a controlled pause with the load set down on the flat landing, never balanced on a step.
7. **Clear the top or bottom fully before releasing the rail.** Step onto the level surface with both feet before letting go and repositioning the load. → *Expect:* both feet on level ground, then rail released, then load set down or carried onward.

## Decision points

- Load blocks your view of the treads → it is too big for stairs as held; reshape, split, or route it differently before starting.
- Only a rail on the wrong side for your load hand → switch the load to the other arm so the free hand meets the rail; do not climb rail-less by choice.
- Stairs are wet, waxed, or carpet-loose → set the load down and deal with the hazard, or take a single item at a time with extra rail commitment.
- Descending rather than ascending → the same rules, but sightlines and edge-misses matter more; descend slower, heels controlled, and consider facing slightly toward the rail.

## Failure modes & recovery

- **F1 Load shifts or slips mid-flight:** felt as a lurch → stop on the current tread with both feet planted, secure the rail, then rebalance the load against your body; do not chase a sliding item down the stairs.
- **F2 Foot finds only the edge, not the tread:** detected by an unstable half-footing → do not commit weight; pull on the rail, reset the foot fully onto the tread before loading it.
- **F3 Out of breath mid-flight with no landing near:** → set the load on a step only if it will not roll or slide, keep a hand on it and the rail, and rest; better, back down to the last landing.
- **F4 Someone approaches on the same flight:** the load makes you wide and slow → stop at a landing or the top, let them pass, then continue; do not pass mid-flight while loaded.

## Verification

The load reached the destination floor intact; at no point were both hands off the rail while loaded on the stairs; every step was taken one tread at a time with a flat footing; rests happened on flat landings, not on treads; no fall, drop, or strain occurred.

## Variations

- Two-person carry of a long item (sofa, mattress): the person on the downhill end sets the pace and calls each step; the uphill person takes more weight; both keep a rail hand when possible and communicate every pause.
- Loaded backpack instead of a hand-carried load: frees both hands for rails but raises your center of gravity, so lean slightly forward going up and keep the pack snug.
- Carrying a person or a pet who may move: treat as a live, shifting load; extra rail commitment, slower pace, and never rush the last step onto the landing.

## Safety & privacy

Medium risk: stair falls while carrying are a leading home-injury cause, and a loaded fall is worse than an empty one because the hands are committed. The two non-negotiables are the free-hand rail rule and clear sight of every tread edge. When in doubt about the weight, take two trips; there is no prize for one.
