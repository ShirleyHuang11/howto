## Goal

You board a moving escalator, ride it by the local standing convention, and step off cleanly at the top or bottom, with the loose-item and footwear hazards handled.

## Preconditions

- An escalator moving in your direction (the entry has a green indicator or visibly approaching steps; a stopped escalator is stairs with awkward step heights, which is fine to walk).
- Hands free enough to grasp the handrail; loose items secured: long coats, scarf ends, and bag straps gathered, shoelaces tied (`daily/self-care/tie-shoelaces` earns its keep here; a dragging lace at the comb plate is the classic entrapment).
- **Not for:** strollers with children in them, wheeled luggage you cannot lift, wheelchairs, or carts. The elevator (`embodied/mobility/use-an-elevator`) exists for exactly these; escalator stroller accidents are severe and common enough that the signage pleads.

## Steps

1. **Approach and time your board.** Watch the steps emerge flat from the comb plate; step on decisively as a fresh step appears, one foot then the other, onto the middle of a step. Hesitating half-on is worse than waiting for the next step. → *Expect:* both feet planted on one step, body caught up to the step's motion.
2. **Grasp the handrail immediately.** It moves at roughly step speed (slight drift is normal; regrip occasionally). → *Expect:* one hand on the rail for the whole ride.
3. **Stand by the local convention.** Stand-right-walk-left in most of the world (reversed in some cities; mirror the locals); with no walkers around, the safest ride is feet on one step, facing forward, clear of the step edges and side skirts. → *Expect:* standing on the conventional side, bags in front or beside you, not on the neighboring walk lane.
4. **Keep feet inside the yellow lines and clear of the skirt.** Soft shoes (crocs, rubber boots) especially: side-skirt entrapment is that footwear's known failure. Children ride holding a hand, feet centered, never sitting on steps. → *Expect:* footwear inside the marked zone the whole ride.
5. **Prepare the dismount three steps early.** Face forward, weight slightly forward, items gathered. → *Expect:* readiness as the steps flatten.
6. **Step off across the comb plate without stopping.** One decisive step onto solid floor, then *keep walking* two or three paces: the exit zone must stay clear for the people the escalator is delivering behind you at constant rate. → *Expect:* clean dismount, exit zone vacated immediately.

## Decision points

- Heavy luggage: lift it fully onto your step (wheels stall at the comb plate), or take the elevator; a suitcase escaping downhill on an escalator endangers everyone below.
- Dogs: small dogs carried; large dogs walk stairs or ride elevators — paw injuries at the comb plate are a veterinary genre.
- Stopped escalator: walk it as stairs with the handrail, expecting the odd step heights at top and bottom; do not press the (usually red, covered) emergency stop for anything except an actual entrapment emergency.

## Failure modes & recovery

- **F1 Stumble on boarding or mid-ride:** grip the handrail hard (it is why your hand was there), regain footing against a step; fallen and unable to rise: shout for someone to hit the emergency stop at either end.
- **F2 Item dropped on the steps:** let it ride to the end and collect it there; never chase or reach down mid-ride. Dropped *into* the machinery side: report to staff, mourn appropriately.
- **F3 Entrapment (shoe, lace, hem caught):** this is the emergency-stop button's purpose — it is at both ends, usually red under a small cover; hit it or have anyone hit it, then work the item free with the machine dead.
- **F4 Blocked exit (someone stopped at the top):** announce loudly and early ("stepping off!"); physics does not pause, and a firm voice beats a pileup.

## Verification

You boarded on a fresh step with a hand on the rail, rode inside the yellow zone by the local convention, dismounted without stopping, and cleared the exit zone, with nothing loose trailing at any point.

## Variations

- Moving walkways (flat escalators) follow the identical recipe minus the height; luggage wheels behave better but the comb plates are the same teeth.
- `jp` metro escalator etiquette is strict and posted (increasingly stand-both-sides for safety); `uk` tube culture enforces stand-right with religious fervor; read the signs, mirror the natives.

## Safety & privacy

Medium risk concentrated at three points: the comb plates (loose items, soft shoes, laces), the skirt gap, and the stroller/luggage misuse the preconditions ban. The handrail grip and the keep-walking dismount are the two habits that neutralize almost everything else.
