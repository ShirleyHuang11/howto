## Goal

Heat exhaustion is treated early by cooling and hydration, and heat stroke warning signs trigger emergency services.

## Preconditions

- This is first aid guidance, not a substitute for medical care, heat illness training, or workplace emergency procedures.
- Call emergency services immediately for confusion, altered mental state, fainting that does not quickly resolve, seizure, collapse, very high body temperature, or hot skin with severe illness.
- Move the person only if it is safe and avoid leaving them alone.
- Give fluids only if the person is awake, able to swallow, and not vomiting repeatedly.
- Heat stroke is a medical emergency; do not wait for a thermometer reading if mental status is abnormal.

## Steps

1. **Stop heat exposure.** Move the person to shade, air conditioning, or the coolest available place. → *Expect:* heat gain from the environment decreases.
2. **Have them rest.** Sit or lie them down and stop physical activity. → *Expect:* exertion no longer adds heat load.
3. **Check mental status.** Ask simple questions and look for confusion, slurred speech, agitation, seizure, or loss of consciousness. → *Expect:* heat stroke signs are identified quickly.
4. **Call for heat stroke signs.** [BRANCH: altered mental state | alert and sweating] call emergency services immediately if mental status is abnormal; continue cooling while waiting. → *Expect:* life-threatening heat illness is escalated.
5. **Remove excess clothing.** Loosen or remove unnecessary layers, shoes, pads, or gear. → *Expect:* skin can release heat more effectively.
6. **Cool the body.** Apply cool wet cloths, mist with water, fan the person, or use cool packs at neck, armpits, and groin. → *Expect:* skin temperature begins to drop.
7. **Give small sips.** Offer cool water or an electrolyte drink if they are alert and can swallow. → *Expect:* fluid is taken without choking or vomiting.
8. **Keep cooling for 15 minutes.** Continue active cooling and reassess symptoms. → *Expect:* dizziness, weakness, nausea, or cramps improve.
9. **Escalate if not improving.** Seek urgent medical care if symptoms persist, worsen, or return after cooling. → *Expect:* heat exhaustion does not progress unnoticed.
10. **Avoid return to heat.** Keep the person out of strenuous activity and heat for the rest of the day. → *Expect:* relapse risk is reduced.

## Decision points

- Any confusion, abnormal behavior, seizure, or unconsciousness → call emergency services and cool rapidly.
- Person cannot drink safely or keeps vomiting → seek urgent medical care.
- Symptoms improve promptly with cooling and fluids → continue rest and monitor.
- Worker, athlete, older adult, child, or medically fragile person is affected → seek medical evaluation sooner.
- Symptoms began after exertion in high heat → do not return to activity that day.

## Failure modes & recovery

- **F1 Heat stroke missed:** detect confusion or collapse treated as simple exhaustion, recover by calling emergency services.
- **F2 Fluids forced:** detect drowsiness, vomiting, or choking risk, recover by stopping fluids and seeking help.
- **F3 Cooling too weak:** detect persistent symptoms after 15 minutes, recover by increasing cooling and escalating care.
- **F4 Left alone:** detect no observer nearby, recover by assigning someone to stay and monitor breathing and alertness.

## Verification

The person is in a cool place, activity has stopped, cooling is underway, fluids are given only if safe, and emergency services are called for any altered mental state.

## Variations

- Workplace setting: follow the site heat plan and notify a supervisor without delaying emergency care.
- No air conditioning: use shade, fans, wet cloths, cool water, and removal of unnecessary clothing.
- Suspected heat stroke: prioritize rapid cooling and emergency services over transport by private car.

## Safety & privacy

High risk because heat exhaustion can progress to fatal heat stroke. Call emergency services for altered mental state or severe symptoms, get heat illness first aid training, and treat this recipe as temporary care until professionals advise.
