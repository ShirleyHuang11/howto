## Goal

A jellyfish sting is rinsed safely, tentacle material is removed, pain is reduced, and serious reactions are escalated.

## Preconditions

- The person is out of the water and safe from more stings, surf, or drowning risk.
- Seawater, vinegar where locally recommended, tweezers or gloved hands, and hot water are available if possible.
- Seek professional or emergency care when there is trouble breathing, chest pain, fainting, severe weakness, widespread swelling, sting to the face or genitals, or a large area affected.
- Local species matter, so follow posted beach or lifeguard guidance when available.

## Steps

1. **Move to safety.** Help the person leave the water and sit or lie down away from surf. → *Expect:* there is no continued exposure to jellyfish or drowning risk.
2. **Assess urgent symptoms.** Check breathing, alertness, chest pain, severe weakness, and widespread swelling. → *Expect:* emergency symptoms are identified immediately.
3. **Rinse with seawater.** Flush the sting with seawater to remove loose tentacle material. → *Expect:* sand and loose fragments wash away.
4. **Use vinegar when appropriate.** [BRANCH: vinegar advised locally | vinegar not advised locally] apply vinegar if lifeguard or local guidance recommends it, otherwise continue seawater rinsing. → *Expect:* stinging cells are not triggered by fresh water.
5. **Avoid fresh water.** Do not rinse with tap water, ice directly, urine, alcohol, or vigorous rubbing. → *Expect:* the sting is not aggravated by common irritants.
6. **Remove tentacles.** Use tweezers, a gloved hand, or a plastic edge to lift visible tentacles from the skin. → *Expect:* no visible tentacle strands remain.
7. **Soak in hot water.** Immerse or shower the area in hot but not scalding water for 20 to 45 minutes. → *Expect:* pain begins to ease without burning the skin.
8. **Protect the skin.** Pat dry and cover lightly if clothing will rub the area. → *Expect:* the area is protected from friction.
9. **Monitor the person.** Watch for worsening pain, spreading swelling, vomiting, dizziness, trouble breathing, or confusion. → *Expect:* serious progression triggers medical care.
10. **Document the location.** Note beach, time, and any lifeguard advice. → *Expect:* details are available if symptoms require care.

## Decision points

- Sting may be from a dangerous local species → follow lifeguard directions and seek urgent care.
- Pain does not improve after hot water or remains severe → seek medical advice.
- [BRANCH: mild localized sting | systemic symptoms] mild localized stings can be monitored, while systemic symptoms require emergency care.
- ⚠️ *Irreversible:* Do not apply scalding water because burns can permanently injure skin.

## Failure modes & recovery

- **F1 Fresh-water rinse:** detect increased stinging after tap water, recover by stopping and rinsing with seawater.
- **F2 Tentacles rubbed in:** detect more burning after rubbing, recover by lifting fragments gently and avoiding friction.
- **F3 Water too hot:** detect redness or burn pain from soaking, recover by cooling to tolerable hot water and checking skin.
- **F4 Serious reaction:** detect breathing trouble, chest pain, fainting, or confusion, recover by calling emergency services.

## Verification

The person is out of danger, visible tentacles are removed, pain is improving, and no emergency symptom is present.

## Variations

- Lifeguarded beach: ask the lifeguard about local vinegar or antidote protocols.
- No hot-water access: use the warmest safe water available and seek care for severe pain.
- Child stung: use extra care to avoid overheating skin during soaking.

## Safety & privacy

Medium risk because some jellyfish stings can cause systemic illness. Seek professional or emergency care when symptoms spread beyond the skin, pain is severe, the sting covers a large area, or a high-risk body area is involved.
