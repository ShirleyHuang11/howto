## Goal

A responsive choking adult or child is helped while emergency services are called when the airway is blocked.

## Preconditions

- This is emergency bystander guidance, not a substitute for professional care or a hands-on first aid course.
- Call emergency services immediately if the person cannot breathe, speak, cry, or cough effectively, or if they become unresponsive.
- Use this adult and child sequence only for someone older than an infant; choking first aid for infants is different.
- Approach only if the scene is safe, and get consent if the person can respond.
- If another bystander is present, assign them to call emergency services and get an AED.

## Steps

1. **Check for severe choking.** Ask if they are choking and look for silent coughing, inability to speak, blue or pale color, panic, or hands at the throat. → *Expect:* you can distinguish mild choking from a blocked airway.
2. **Encourage coughing if air is moving.** [BRANCH: effective cough | weak or silent cough] tell them to keep coughing if they can breathe or speak; act immediately if they cannot. → *Expect:* mild choking is not interrupted with forceful maneuvers.
3. **Call emergency services for severe choking.** Point to a specific person to call, or call on speaker if alone. → *Expect:* dispatch is reached or being contacted while care begins.
4. **Position for back blows.** Stand to the side and slightly behind, support the chest with one arm, and lean the person forward. → *Expect:* the airway points downward so the object can come out.
5. **Give five back blows.** Strike firmly between the shoulder blades with the heel of your hand, checking after each blow. → *Expect:* the object may dislodge or the person may begin coughing strongly.
6. **Position for abdominal thrusts.** Stand behind, place a fist just above the navel and below the breastbone, and grasp it with the other hand. → *Expect:* your hands are on the abdomen, not the ribs or breastbone.
7. **Give five abdominal thrusts.** Pull sharply inward and upward, checking after each thrust. → *Expect:* the pressure may force air out and expel the object.
8. **Repeat cycles.** Alternate five back blows and five abdominal thrusts until the object clears, help takes over, or the person becomes unresponsive. → *Expect:* care continues without long pauses.
9. **If they become unresponsive, lower them safely.** Place them on a firm, flat surface and start CPR if trained or instructed by dispatch. → *Expect:* compressions begin and emergency services are already involved.
10. **After breathing returns, arrange medical evaluation.** Keep watching breathing and encourage evaluation, especially after thrusts, persistent coughing, or injury. → *Expect:* delayed airway or internal injury is not ignored.

## Decision points

- Person can cough forcefully or speak → encourage coughing and monitor closely.
- Person cannot cough, speak, cry, or breathe → call emergency services and start back blows plus abdominal thrusts.
- Person is visibly pregnant or too large for abdominal thrusts → use chest thrusts if trained or follow dispatcher instructions.
- Person is an infant under 1 year → do not use abdominal thrusts; use infant choking guidance and call emergency services.
- Object is visible in the mouth → remove it only if you can see and grasp it easily.

## Failure modes & recovery

- **F1 Mild choking overtreated:** detect forceful coughing or speech, recover by stopping maneuvers and encouraging coughing.
- **F2 Hands too high:** detect pressure on ribs or breastbone, recover by repositioning just above the navel.
- **F3 Blind finger sweep:** detect reaching into the mouth without seeing an object, recover by stopping because it can push the object deeper.
- **F4 No caller assigned:** detect bystanders watching silently, recover by naming one person to call emergency services.

## Verification

The person can breathe, speak, or cough effectively, emergency services have been called if severe choking occurred, and they are monitored until safe care takes over.

## Variations

- Infant choking: use alternating back blows and chest thrusts, not abdominal thrusts.
- Wheelchair user: lock the chair and use abdominal or chest thrusts from behind if safe.
- Alone and choking: call emergency services first if possible, then use self-abdominal thrusts or a firm chair back.

## Safety & privacy

High risk because a blocked airway can be fatal within minutes. Call emergency services for severe choking, follow dispatcher instructions, get certified first aid training, and treat this recipe as support only until professional care arrives.
