## Goal

Your feet are prepared for hiking so friction, moisture, and pressure points are controlled before blisters form.

## Preconditions

- Hiking footwear, moisture-wicking socks, and blister supplies such as tape, moleskin, hydrocolloid bandages, or lubricant.
- Toenails trimmed straight across and not painfully short.
- No open infected foot wound, severe swelling, numbness, or diabetes-related foot concerns without medical guidance.

## Steps

1. **Fit boots with hiking socks.** Try footwear with the socks you will wear and enough room for toes on downhill steps. → *Expect:* toes do not jam and heel lift is minimal.
2. **Break in footwear early.** Wear boots or shoes on short walks before the hike. → *Expect:* pressure points appear close to home, not on the trail.
3. **Choose moisture-wicking socks.** Use wool or synthetic hiking socks and avoid cotton for long hikes. → *Expect:* feet stay drier under effort.
4. **Reduce friction before starting.** Apply foot lubricant or pre-tape known hot spots such as heels, toes, or the ball of the foot. → *Expect:* high-rub areas are protected.
5. **Pack quick-access supplies.** Keep tape, blister pads, spare socks, and a small wipe where you can reach them. → *Expect:* you can treat a hot spot without unpacking everything.
6. **Stop at the first hot spot.** Pause as soon as you feel rubbing, heat, or a sharp sock wrinkle. → *Expect:* skin is still intact.
7. **Dry and cover the hot spot.** Smooth the sock, dry the skin, then apply tape or moleskin without wrinkles. → *Expect:* rubbing stops when you resume walking.
8. **Change wet socks.** Swap socks when feet are soaked from sweat, rain, or stream crossings. → *Expect:* moisture load drops.
9. **Adjust lacing.** Tighten for downhill toe control or loosen pressure over the top of the foot. → *Expect:* foot movement is controlled without numbness.

## Decision points

- A blister forms but skin is intact → cover with a hydrocolloid or donut pad and avoid popping it.
- A blister is large, very painful, infected-looking, or you have diabetes, poor circulation, or immune suppression → seek medical care.
- Numbness or blue toes after lacing → loosen immediately and reassess footwear fit.

## Failure modes & recovery

- **F1 Tape wrinkles:** remove it before it causes a new blister and reapply smoothly to dry skin.
- **F2 Heel keeps lifting:** try a heel-lock lacing pattern or thicker sock if circulation remains normal.
- **F3 Toes hit the front downhill:** tighten midfoot lacing, trim nails if needed later, and consider different footwear.
- **F4 Skin opens on trail:** clean with safe water if available, cover with a sterile dressing, and monitor for infection.

## Verification

Footwear is broken in, socks manage moisture, hot spots are taped as soon as they appear, and no blister is ignored until skin breaks.

## Variations

- Wet climates: carry more sock changes and dry feet during longer breaks.
- Trail running shoes: lighter footwear still needs toe room and hot-spot taping.
- Multi-day trips: air feet at camp and inspect soles and between toes daily.

## Safety & privacy

Low risk for healthy feet, but foot wounds can become serious in diabetes or poor circulation. See a doctor for spreading redness, pus, fever, red streaks, severe pain, numbness, or nonhealing open skin.
