## Goal

A person having a seizure is protected from injury, timed, and escalated to emergency services when warning signs are present.

## Preconditions

- This is bystander first aid guidance, not a substitute for medical care, seizure action plans, or first aid training.
- Call emergency services if the seizure lasts more than 5 minutes, repeats, occurs in water, causes injury, or the person is pregnant, diabetic, having their first seizure, or not breathing well afterward.
- Do not restrain the person and do not put anything in their mouth.
- Move hazards away if safe, but do not move the person unless they are in immediate danger.
- Stay with the person until they are awake, breathing normally, and safe.

## Steps

1. **Note the start time.** Check a clock or phone as soon as you recognize seizure activity. → *Expect:* the seizure duration can be reported accurately.
2. **Clear nearby hazards.** Move chairs, sharp objects, hot items, or hard objects away from the person. → *Expect:* the area around the person is safer.
3. **Protect the head.** Place a folded jacket or soft flat item under the head if available. → *Expect:* the head is cushioned without forcing movement.
4. **Loosen tight neck items.** Loosen scarves, ties, or tight collars if you can do so safely. → *Expect:* breathing is not restricted by clothing.
5. **Do not restrain.** Let the seizure run its course and keep others from holding the person down. → *Expect:* injury from forced restraint is avoided.
6. **Keep the mouth clear from outside.** Do not insert fingers, spoons, wallets, or medicine into the mouth. → *Expect:* teeth, jaw, and airway are not endangered by objects.
7. **Call when criteria are met.** [BRANCH: emergency criteria present | known brief seizure] call emergency services if any red flag is present; otherwise keep timing and monitoring. → *Expect:* urgent situations are escalated.
8. **Turn after jerking stops.** If they are breathing but not fully awake, roll them onto their side in the recovery position. → *Expect:* saliva or vomit can drain and the airway stays open.
9. **Reassure during recovery.** Speak calmly, explain what happened, and keep bystanders back. → *Expect:* confusion and embarrassment are reduced.
10. **Monitor until fully alert.** Check breathing, injuries, and orientation until the person returns to their usual state or help arrives. → *Expect:* delayed problems are noticed.

## Decision points

- Seizure lasts more than 5 minutes → call emergency services.
- Another seizure starts before full recovery → call emergency services.
- Breathing is difficult after the seizure → call emergency services and follow dispatcher instructions.
- Person was injured, pregnant, diabetic, in water, or has no known seizure history → call emergency services.
- Person has a written seizure action plan → follow it if it does not delay emergency care.

## Failure modes & recovery

- **F1 Object in mouth:** detect anyone trying to insert an item, recover by stopping them and keeping the area clear.
- **F2 Restraint:** detect held limbs or pinned body, recover by releasing and moving hazards instead.
- **F3 No timing:** detect unknown duration, recover by starting timing now and estimating from witnesses.
- **F4 Food offered early:** detect food, drink, or pills before full alertness, recover by waiting until fully awake.

## Verification

The seizure was timed, hazards were cleared, nothing was placed in the mouth, emergency services were called if criteria were met, and the person is monitored in recovery.

## Variations

- Wheelchair: support the person in place, secure wheels, protect the head, and do not remove them unless necessary for safety.
- Water seizure: support the head above water, remove from water when safe, and call emergency services.
- Known epilepsy: still call if the seizure differs from their normal pattern or their action plan says to call.

## Safety & privacy

High risk because seizures can involve injury, airway problems, or serious underlying illness. Call emergency services when indicated, get first aid training, respect the person's privacy, and use this recipe only as bystander support.
