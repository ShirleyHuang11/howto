## Goal

Possible heart attack symptoms are recognized early, emergency services are called, and the person rests while waiting for medical care.

## Preconditions

- This is emergency bystander guidance, not a substitute for medical evaluation, dispatcher instructions, or first aid training.
- Call emergency services immediately for possible heart attack symptoms; do not wait to see if they pass.
- Do not drive yourself or let the person drive because EMS can begin treatment and manage sudden cardiac arrest.
- Aspirin may help some adults, but only use it if dispatch or a clinician advises and there is no allergy, bleeding risk, or instruction to avoid it.
- Be ready to start CPR and use an AED if the person becomes unresponsive and is not breathing normally.

## Steps

1. **Look for chest discomfort.** Ask about pressure, squeezing, fullness, burning, or pain in the center or left chest. → *Expect:* chest symptoms are described in the person's own words.
2. **Check related symptoms.** Ask about shortness of breath, sweating, nausea, lightheadedness, unusual fatigue, or a fast or irregular heartbeat. → *Expect:* non-chest warning signs are not missed.
3. **Check spreading discomfort.** Ask about pain or pressure in one or both arms, back, neck, jaw, shoulder, or upper abdomen. → *Expect:* referred symptoms are recognized.
4. **Consider atypical patterns.** [BRANCH: chest pain present | no chest pain but concerning symptoms] treat either pattern as possible heart attack, especially in women, older adults, and people with diabetes. → *Expect:* absence of classic chest pain does not delay action.
5. **Call emergency services.** Call on speaker or assign one named bystander to call. → *Expect:* dispatch is connected and can guide care.
6. **Keep the person resting.** Have them sit or lie in a comfortable position and loosen tight clothing. → *Expect:* exertion stops and breathing is easier.
7. **Avoid self-transport.** Tell the person not to drive and arrange EMS transport unless dispatch says otherwise. → *Expect:* rapid medical transport is handled by professionals.
8. **Ask about aspirin only if appropriate.** Follow dispatcher or clinician advice about chewing aspirin, and skip it for allergy, active bleeding, blood thinner concern, or prior instruction to avoid aspirin. → *Expect:* aspirin is not given blindly.
9. **Watch for cardiac arrest.** Monitor responsiveness and normal breathing until help arrives. → *Expect:* collapse or abnormal breathing is detected immediately.
10. **Start CPR if needed.** If unresponsive and not breathing normally, begin hands-only CPR and use an AED if available. → *Expect:* circulation support starts while EMS is on the way.

## Decision points

- Symptoms are severe, new, worsening, or last more than a few minutes → call emergency services.
- Symptoms come and go → call emergency services because heart attacks can start slowly.
- Person has prescribed nitroglycerin → help them follow their own prescription and dispatcher guidance.
- Person becomes unresponsive and not breathing normally → start CPR and use an AED if available.
- Aspirin is uncertain → ask dispatch rather than guessing.

## Failure modes & recovery

- **F1 Dismissed symptoms:** detect explanations like indigestion or stress despite warning signs, recover by calling emergency services.
- **F2 Self-driving:** detect the person preparing to drive, recover by stopping exertion and using EMS.
- **F3 Unsafe aspirin:** detect allergy, bleeding, anticoagulants, or uncertainty, recover by waiting for dispatcher or clinician instruction.
- **F4 No AED search:** detect multiple bystanders present, recover by assigning one to find an AED.

## Verification

Emergency services have been called, the person is resting, self-transport is avoided, and CPR or AED readiness is in place if they collapse.

## Variations

- Women: symptoms may include shortness of breath, nausea, vomiting, back or jaw pain, anxiety, or unusual fatigue.
- Diabetes or older age: pain may be mild or absent, so sudden weakness, breathlessness, or sweating deserves urgent attention.
- Remote area: call emergency services anyway and follow dispatcher instructions while help travels.

## Safety & privacy

High risk because delayed heart attack care can be fatal. Call emergency services, follow professional instructions, seek proper first aid and CPR training, and do not treat this recipe as a replacement for medical care.
