## Goal

A person with flu-like illness is supported with rest, fluids, fever care, isolation, and timely medical escalation.

## Preconditions

- Flu symptoms often include fever, chills, cough, sore throat, body aches, headache, fatigue, and sometimes vomiting or diarrhea.
- Call emergency services for severe trouble breathing, chest pain, blue lips, confusion, seizure, inability to wake, or signs of severe dehydration.
- Call a clinician early for infants, older adults, pregnancy, chronic disease, immune suppression, or worsening symptoms.
- Ask a clinician promptly about antiviral medicine if symptoms began within the last 48 hours or the person is high risk.
- Use separate towels, cups, utensils, and bedding when possible.

## Steps

1. **Set up a rest space.** Give the sick person a place to rest away from others when possible. → *Expect:* exposure to others is reduced.
2. **Encourage fluids.** Offer water, oral rehydration solution, broth, or other tolerated fluids often. → *Expect:* urine is pale enough and the person can keep fluids down.
3. **Manage fever and aches.** [BRANCH: adult | child] use age-appropriate acetaminophen or ibuprofen as labeled unless a clinician says not to. → *Expect:* fever discomfort and body aches are reduced.
   - ⚠️ *Irreversible:* Do not give aspirin to children or teenagers with viral illness unless a clinician specifically directs it.
4. **Support breathing comfort.** Use humidified air, honey for cough only if over 1 year old, and upright positioning. → *Expect:* cough or throat irritation is easier to tolerate.
5. **Isolate while contagious.** Keep the person home and away from others until fever is gone for at least 24 hours without fever-reducing medicine and symptoms are improving. → *Expect:* spread risk is reduced.
6. **Use masks and ventilation.** Have the sick person mask around others if tolerated, improve airflow, and avoid visitors. → *Expect:* household exposure is lower.
7. **Watch red flags twice daily.** Check breathing, hydration, alertness, fever pattern, chest pain, and symptom direction. → *Expect:* worsening is caught early.
8. **Call for high-risk care.** Contact a clinician for high-risk people, symptoms lasting or worsening after several days, or fever that returns after improving. → *Expect:* complications are considered.
9. **Clean hands and surfaces.** Wash hands often and clean high-touch surfaces. → *Expect:* shared spaces are less contaminated.

## Decision points

- Severe breathing trouble, chest pain, blue lips, confusion, seizure, or inability to wake → call emergency services.
- Dehydration signs such as very little urine, dizziness, dry mouth, or inability to keep fluids down → urgent medical advice.
- Fever in an infant under 3 months → call a clinician urgently.
- Symptoms improve then fever or cough worsens → call a clinician for possible complication.
- Household includes high-risk people → increase masking, separation, ventilation, and clinician contact.

## Failure modes & recovery

- **F1 Dehydration:** detect minimal urination, dizziness, dry mouth, or lethargy, recover by offering oral rehydration and calling a clinician.
- **F2 Medication dosing error:** detect overlapping acetaminophen products or wrong child dose, recover by stopping and calling poison control or a clinician.
- **F3 Spread in household:** detect shared cups or close contact, recover by separating items, masking, and handwashing.
- **F4 Worsening after improvement:** detect returning fever or worse cough, recover by seeking medical advice.

## Verification

The person is resting, drinking fluids, isolated from others, and has no emergency red flags or has been escalated for medical care.

## Variations

- High-risk patient: contact a clinician early about antivirals and monitoring.
- Child: use weight-based medicine dosing and avoid honey under 1 year old.
- Shared home: prioritize masks, ventilation, hand hygiene, and separate sleeping space if available.

## Safety & privacy

Medium risk because flu can become pneumonia, dehydration, or respiratory distress. Protect health information while arranging care, but share symptoms, age, pregnancy status, and risk factors with clinicians.
