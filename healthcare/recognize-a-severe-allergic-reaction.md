## Goal

A severe allergic reaction is recognized early so epinephrine is used and emergency services are called without delay.

## Preconditions

- This is emergency recognition guidance, not a substitute for professional care, an allergy action plan, or anaphylaxis training.
- Call emergency services immediately for suspected anaphylaxis, even if symptoms improve after epinephrine.
- Use the person's prescribed epinephrine auto-injector promptly when severe allergy is suspected.
- Do not rely on antihistamines alone for airway, breathing, circulation, or multi-system symptoms.
- Keep the person from standing or walking if they feel faint or have signs of shock.

## Steps

1. **Identify possible exposure.** Ask about foods, stings, medicines, latex, exercise after food, or other known allergens. → *Expect:* a trigger may be identified without delaying care.
2. **Check skin signs.** Look for hives, itching, flushing, swelling of lips, eyelids, face, or widespread rash. → *Expect:* allergic skin involvement is recognized.
3. **Check airway signs.** Listen for hoarse voice, throat tightness, tongue swelling, trouble swallowing, or noisy breathing. → *Expect:* airway danger is identified.
4. **Check breathing signs.** Look for wheeze, shortness of breath, persistent cough, or chest tightness. → *Expect:* lower airway involvement is recognized.
5. **Check circulation and gut signs.** Ask about dizziness, faintness, collapse, severe vomiting, diarrhea, or cramping. → *Expect:* shock or multi-system symptoms are considered.
6. **Decide if severe.** [BRANCH: airway or breathing or faintness | only mild local symptoms] treat airway, breathing, faintness, collapse, or multiple body systems as anaphylaxis. → *Expect:* severe allergy is not minimized.
7. **Use epinephrine if available.** Give the prescribed auto-injector into the outer thigh or help the person use it. → *Expect:* first-line treatment is given promptly.
8. **Call emergency services.** Call immediately after recognizing anaphylaxis or after injecting if injection had to come first. → *Expect:* EMS is responding.
9. **Position and monitor.** Lay them flat with legs raised if tolerated, or sit upright for breathing difficulty, and monitor breathing. → *Expect:* collapse risk is reduced.
10. **Watch for biphasic recurrence.** Keep monitoring because symptoms can return hours later. → *Expect:* improvement is not treated as the end of the emergency.

## Decision points

- Hives plus vomiting, wheeze, throat symptoms, or dizziness → use epinephrine and call emergency services.
- Throat, tongue, or breathing symptoms after allergen exposure → use epinephrine and call emergency services.
- Symptoms improve after epinephrine → still go for emergency evaluation because recurrence can occur.
- No epinephrine is available → call emergency services and follow dispatcher instructions.
- Person becomes unresponsive and not breathing normally → start CPR if trained and use an AED if available.

## Failure modes & recovery

- **F1 Antihistamine delay:** detect waiting for pills to work, recover by using epinephrine for anaphylaxis and calling emergency services.
- **F2 Mild label applied:** detect airway, breathing, faintness, or multi-system symptoms called mild, recover by treating as severe.
- **F3 Standing collapse:** detect walking or standing while dizzy, recover by lying the person down safely.
- **F4 Recurrence ignored:** detect symptoms returning after improvement, recover by giving second epinephrine if available and instructed, and updating EMS.

## Verification

Anaphylaxis warning signs have been checked, epinephrine is used when indicated, emergency services are called, and the person is monitored for recurrent symptoms.

## Variations

- Food allergy: symptoms can begin quickly or evolve over time, so keep observing after exposure.
- Insect sting: remove from the area if safe and treat systemic symptoms as anaphylaxis.
- Asthma history: wheezing after allergen exposure may be anaphylaxis and should not be treated as asthma alone.

## Safety & privacy

High risk because anaphylaxis can obstruct the airway or cause shock. Call emergency services, get anaphylaxis and first aid training, protect medical privacy, and use this recipe only until professional care takes over.
