## Goal

An epinephrine auto-injector is used promptly for suspected anaphylaxis while emergency services are called.

## Preconditions

- This is emergency bystander guidance, not a substitute for medical care, device instructions, or anaphylaxis training.
- Call emergency services immediately for suspected anaphylaxis, even if epinephrine improves symptoms.
- Use the person's prescribed auto-injector when available, and follow the label for that exact device.
- Recognize anaphylaxis as allergic symptoms plus breathing trouble, throat or tongue swelling, faintness, collapse, severe vomiting, or symptoms in multiple body systems.
- Do not delay epinephrine for antihistamines, inhalers, or uncertainty when anaphylaxis is likely.

## Steps

1. **Recognize anaphylaxis.** Look for hives, swelling, breathing trouble, wheeze, throat tightness, hoarse voice, vomiting, dizziness, or collapse after a possible allergen. → *Expect:* a severe allergic reaction is treated as time-critical.
2. **Call emergency services.** Assign a caller or call on speaker before or immediately after giving epinephrine. → *Expect:* EMS is on the way while care continues.
3. **Position the person.** Have them lie flat with legs raised if tolerated; sit upright if breathing is difficult; do not let them stand or walk. → *Expect:* circulation and breathing are supported.
4. **Prepare the EpiPen.** Hold it in your fist with blue safety cap up and orange tip down: blue to sky, orange to thigh. → *Expect:* fingers are away from both ends.
5. **Remove the blue cap.** Pull the safety cap straight off only when ready to inject. → *Expect:* the device is armed and pointed at the outer thigh.
6. **Inject into the thigh.** Press the orange tip firmly into the outer mid-thigh through clothing if needed until it clicks. → *Expect:* the dose starts delivering into the thigh muscle.
7. **Hold in place.** Keep firm pressure for the time listed on the device label, commonly 3 seconds for current EpiPen devices. → *Expect:* the full dose has time to deliver.
8. **Remove and protect.** Lift the device away and keep it for EMS, needle end covered by the device guard. → *Expect:* responders can identify what was given and when.
9. **Monitor for return of symptoms.** If symptoms persist or return and a second prescribed auto-injector is available, give it after about 5 to 15 minutes or as instructed by dispatch. → *Expect:* worsening anaphylaxis is treated promptly.
10. **Prepare for CPR.** If the person becomes unresponsive and is not breathing normally, begin CPR if trained and follow dispatch instructions. → *Expect:* life support starts if anaphylaxis causes collapse.

## Decision points

- Breathing trouble, throat swelling, faintness, or symptoms in multiple systems → use epinephrine and call emergency services.
- Only mild localized itching with no progression → follow the person's allergy action plan and monitor closely.
- Person is vomiting or faint → keep them lying on their side if needed to protect the airway.
- Device is not an EpiPen brand → follow that device label, but still inject the outer thigh and call emergency services.
- Symptoms improve after epinephrine → still go to emergency care because symptoms can return.

## Failure modes & recovery

- **F1 Cap removed too early:** detect exposed armed device before positioning, recover by keeping fingers off ends and injecting promptly.
- **F2 Wrong end against thigh:** detect blue end aimed at leg, recover by rotating to orange tip on outer thigh.
- **F3 Delayed EMS call:** detect improvement after injection, recover by calling emergency services anyway.
- **F4 Standing after injection:** detect walking or upright faintness, recover by helping the person lie down safely.

## Verification

Epinephrine has been given into the outer thigh, emergency services have been called, the injection time is noted, and the person is monitored for recurrent symptoms.

## Variations

- Child dose: use the prescribed pediatric device if that is what the person carries.
- Asthma overlap: severe allergy with wheeze still needs epinephrine first when anaphylaxis is suspected.
- Public location: ask someone to find additional auto-injectors, an AED, and the person's emergency contacts.

## Safety & privacy

High risk because anaphylaxis can be fatal and can recur after initial improvement. Call emergency services, seek proper anaphylaxis and CPR training, and treat this recipe as temporary support until professional care arrives.
