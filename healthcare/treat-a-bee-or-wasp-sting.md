## Goal

A bee or wasp sting is treated for local pain and swelling while severe allergy signs are recognized quickly.

## Preconditions

- The person is away from the insect nest or swarm.
- Soap, water, a clean card edge, cold pack, cloth barrier, and an oral antihistamine are available if appropriate.
- Seek professional or emergency care when there is trouble breathing, throat tightness, wheezing, dizziness, fainting, swelling of lips or tongue, widespread hives, or repeated vomiting.
- If the person has prescribed epinephrine for allergy, use it exactly as directed and call emergency services.

## Steps

1. **Move away.** Leave the area calmly to avoid more stings. → *Expect:* no additional insects are nearby.
2. **Check for anaphylaxis.** Look for breathing trouble, throat tightness, widespread hives, faintness, or facial swelling. → *Expect:* emergency symptoms are recognized before local care.
3. **Scrape out a bee stinger.** Use a fingernail or card edge to scrape sideways across the skin. → *Expect:* a visible stinger is removed without squeezing the venom sac.
4. **Clean the sting.** Wash the area with soap and water. → *Expect:* the skin is clean.
5. **Apply cold.** Wrap ice or a cold pack in cloth and hold it on the area for 10 minutes at a time. → *Expect:* pain and swelling begin to ease.
6. **Raise the area.** Elevate the limb if the sting is on an arm or leg. → *Expect:* swelling feels less tight.
7. **Consider antihistamine.** Use an age-appropriate antihistamine only if the label allows it and the person can take it safely. → *Expect:* itching is addressed without unsafe dosing.
8. **Use pain relief carefully.** Take an appropriate over-the-counter pain reliever if tolerated and allowed by the label. → *Expect:* pain is manageable.
9. **Do not scratch.** Keep nails away and cover lightly if needed. → *Expect:* skin is not opened by scratching.
10. **Monitor for delayed symptoms.** Watch for spreading swelling, infection signs, or allergic symptoms over the next several hours. → *Expect:* worsening symptoms lead to care.

## Decision points

- Any anaphylaxis sign appears → use prescribed epinephrine if available and call emergency services.
- Sting is in the mouth, throat, or near the eye → seek urgent medical care.
- [BRANCH: single local sting | multiple stings] a single local sting can be observed, while many stings need medical advice.
- ⚠️ *Irreversible:* Do not delay epinephrine for known severe allergy because waiting can allow the reaction to progress.

## Failure modes & recovery

- **F1 Stinger squeezed:** detect pinching or tweezing of the sac, recover by scraping it out and washing the site.
- **F2 Cold injury:** detect numb, pale, or painful skin from ice, recover by removing the cold pack and using a cloth barrier.
- **F3 Allergy missed:** detect hives away from the sting, breathing trouble, or faintness, recover by treating as an emergency.
- **F4 Infection signs:** detect increasing redness, warmth, pus, or fever, recover by contacting a clinician.

## Verification

The stinger is removed if present, the site is clean and cooling, symptoms are localized, and no anaphylaxis sign is present.

## Variations

- Wasp sting: there may be no stinger left behind, so focus on washing, cold, and monitoring.
- Child sting: use only child-labeled medicine and correct dosing.
- Outdoor group: check others for stings before leaving the area.

## Safety & privacy

Medium risk because anaphylaxis can become life-threatening quickly. Seek professional or emergency care when allergic symptoms, many stings, mouth or throat stings, or infection signs occur.
