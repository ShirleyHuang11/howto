## Goal

Possible dehydration is recognized early, mild fluid loss is corrected, and severe signs are escalated.

## Preconditions

- Drinking water or oral rehydration solution is available.
- You can observe urine color, thirst, alertness, sweating, and recent fluid losses such as fever, vomiting, diarrhea, heat, or heavy exercise.
- Seek professional or emergency care when there is confusion, fainting, very little urination, severe weakness, rapid breathing, inability to keep fluids down, or signs in an infant or frail older adult.
- This is screening guidance, not a diagnosis of the cause.

## Steps

1. **Check recent losses.** Ask about vomiting, diarrhea, fever, sweating, heat exposure, alcohol, or hard exercise. → *Expect:* a likely fluid-loss trigger is known.
2. **Look for early signs.** Notice thirst, dry mouth, headache, dizziness, fatigue, or reduced sweating. → *Expect:* mild dehydration signs are identified.
3. **Check urine.** Look for dark yellow urine, strong smell, or fewer trips to urinate. → *Expect:* urine gives a hydration clue.
4. **Assess high-risk people.** Treat infants, older adults, pregnant people, and people with chronic illness as higher risk. → *Expect:* vulnerable people are not managed casually.
5. **Start rehydration.** Sip water or oral rehydration solution steadily rather than chugging. → *Expect:* fluids are tolerated.
6. **Replace salts when needed.** Use oral rehydration solution, broth, or electrolyte drink after heavy sweating, vomiting, or diarrhea. → *Expect:* salt loss is addressed.
7. **Rest and cool down.** Move to shade or a cool room and reduce activity. → *Expect:* sweating and heat stress decrease.
8. **Avoid dehydrating choices.** Pause alcohol and limit very sugary drinks if they worsen stomach symptoms. → *Expect:* fluid loss is not aggravated.
9. **Recheck after 30 to 60 minutes.** Look again at dizziness, thirst, urine, alertness, and ability to drink. → *Expect:* symptoms are improving or a care decision is clear.
10. **Escalate severe signs.** Seek care for confusion, fainting, no urination, severe weakness, or inability to keep fluids down. → *Expect:* dangerous dehydration is not treated only at home.

## Decision points

- Infant has no wet diaper for several hours, sunken soft spot, unusual sleepiness, or no tears → seek urgent care.
- Older adult becomes confused, very weak, or dizzy on standing → seek medical care promptly.
- [BRANCH: mild and improving | severe or not improving] mild cases can continue oral fluids, while severe cases need care.
- ⚠️ *Irreversible:* Do not delay care for confusion or fainting because dehydration and heat illness can progress quickly.

## Failure modes & recovery

- **F1 Chugging fluids:** detect nausea or vomiting after rapid drinking, recover with small frequent sips.
- **F2 Salt not replaced:** detect cramps or ongoing diarrhea with only plain water, recover with oral rehydration solution if tolerated.
- **F3 Heat illness missed:** detect hot skin, confusion, or fainting, recover by cooling and calling emergency services.
- **F4 Infant risk missed:** detect fewer wet diapers or unusual sleepiness, recover by seeking pediatric care.

## Verification

The person is drinking and keeping fluids down, urine and alertness are acceptable, and no severe dehydration sign is present.

## Variations

- Exercise dehydration: include electrolytes when sweating heavily for a long period.
- Stomach illness: use small sips every few minutes.
- Medication or chronic disease: ask a clinician because fluid and salt advice may differ.

## Safety & privacy

Medium risk because dehydration can worsen quickly in infants, older adults, heat exposure, or illness. Seek professional or emergency care when severe signs, high-risk age, or inability to drink safely is present.
