## Goal

A bystander starts hands-only CPR for an unresponsive person who is not breathing normally while emergency services are being called.

## Preconditions

- Scene is safe enough to approach.
- Person appears collapsed, unresponsive, and not breathing normally.
- Phone or another bystander is available to call emergency services.
- Automated external defibrillator (AED) may be nearby.
- This is basic bystander guidance, not a substitute for a real CPR course.

## Steps

1. **Check scene safety.** Look for traffic, electricity, fire, violence, or other immediate danger before approaching. → *Expect:* you can reach the person without becoming another casualty.
2. **Check responsiveness.** Tap shoulders and shout to ask if they are okay. → *Expect:* no purposeful response means emergency action is needed.
3. **Call emergency services immediately.** Tell a specific person to call, or call yourself on speaker. → *Expect:* dispatch is contacted and can guide you.
4. **Send for an AED.** Point to someone and ask them to bring an AED if available. → *Expect:* a search starts without delaying compressions.
5. **Check breathing quickly.** Look for normal breathing for no more than 10 seconds; gasping is not normal breathing. → *Expect:* absent or abnormal breathing triggers CPR.
6. **Position the person.** Lay them flat on their back on a firm surface if possible. → *Expect:* chest is accessible and not on a soft mattress.
7. **Place hands correctly.** Put the heel of one hand in the center of the chest on the lower half of the breastbone, other hand on top. → *Expect:* shoulders stack over locked elbows.
8. **Push hard and fast.** Compress at least 2 inches deep in adults at 100-120 compressions per minute, letting the chest recoil fully. → *Expect:* chest visibly moves down and springs back each compression.
9. **Do not stop unnecessarily.** Continue until an AED is ready, trained help takes over, the person breathes normally, or you are physically unable. → *Expect:* interruptions stay very short.
10. **Use the AED when it arrives.** Turn it on and follow voice prompts. ⚠️ *Irreversible:* shock delivery can harm someone touching the person; loudly clear everyone before shock. → *Expect:* AED prompts guide pads, analysis, and any shock.

## Decision points

- Person is breathing normally → monitor, keep airway open if trained, and wait for emergency responders.
- You are alone with no phone in hand → call emergency services first, then start compressions.
- Person is a child or drowning victim → rescue breaths may matter; follow dispatcher instructions and get trained.
- You are unsure if CPR is needed → if unresponsive and not breathing normally, start compressions.
- AED says no shock advised → resume compressions immediately unless the person wakes or breathes normally.

## Failure modes & recovery

- **F1 Fear of hurting ribs:** rib injury can happen, but untreated cardiac arrest is fatal; continue compressions.
- **F2 Compressions are shallow:** fatigue or soft surface reduces depth; switch rescuers or move to firm ground if possible.
- **F3 Long pauses:** checking too often stops blood flow; resume immediately after AED prompts or necessary moves.
- **F4 Hands drift off center:** reposition on the center of the chest and continue.
- **F5 Rescuer exhaustion:** ask another bystander to switch every about 2 minutes with minimal pause.

## Verification

Emergency services have been called, chest compressions are ongoing at 100-120 per minute with full recoil, and interruptions occur only for AED prompts, responder takeover, normal breathing, or rescuer inability.

## Variations

- Opioid overdose suspicion: call emergency services, give naloxone if available, and start CPR if not breathing normally.
- Public AED: follow the device prompts even if they differ slightly from memory.
- Formal training: take a recognized CPR/AED course to practice depth, recoil, and AED use.

## Safety & privacy

High risk because cardiac arrest is life-threatening. Call emergency services first, follow dispatcher instructions, use an AED if available, and seek real CPR training before an emergency occurs.
