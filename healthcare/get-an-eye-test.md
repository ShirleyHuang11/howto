## Goal

You have a complete eye examination, understand your results, obtain a copy of your prescription, and can order glasses or contacts from the provider of your choice.

## Preconditions

- A reason to test (routine check, changed vision, headaches, new glasses) and access to an optometrist or optical shop with an exam room.
- Your ID, any insurance/vision-plan details, and your current glasses or a note of your current prescription.
- A list of relevant symptoms and any family history of eye disease (glaucoma, macular degeneration).

## Steps

1. **Book on the right cadence.** Book an eye exam (a "sight test") every 1 to 2 years for most adults, sooner if vision changed or you have diabetes or a family history of eye disease. Say if you also want a contact-lens fitting, which is a separate assessment. → *Expect:* an appointment booked with the exam types you need noted.
2. **Confirm coverage and cost.** Ask whether your vision plan or public scheme covers the exam and how much glasses or contacts cost separately. Note that dilation or retinal imaging may be an add-on. → *Expect:* a clear picture of what the exam and any imaging cost.
3. **Prepare and arrive.** Bring current glasses or contacts, your ID, plan details, and your symptom and history list. If you wear contacts, ask whether to remove them beforehand. Consider that dilating drops blur near vision for hours, so avoid driving yourself if dilation is likely. → *Expect:* you arrive with your current correction and a way home if dilated.
4. **Complete the vision and health checks.** Expect a letter chart for acuity, a refraction ("which is clearer, one or two?"), a puff or contact test for eye pressure, and often a retinal look or imaging. → *Expect:* the optometrist measures each eye and describes what they see.
5. **Get the results explained.** Ask what your acuity is, whether your prescription changed, and whether the health checks (pressure, retina, optic nerve) were normal. → *Expect:* plain-language answers, and a flag on anything needing monitoring or referral.
6. **Obtain your prescription in writing.** Ask for a copy of the finalized prescription; you are generally entitled to it and are not required to buy glasses where you tested. It lists sphere, cylinder, axis, and any add or prism, plus a pupillary distance (PD) for glasses. → *Expect:* a written or emailed prescription with a validity date, PD included or measured on request. ⚠️ *Irreversible:* confirm the numbers match what the optometrist told you before you leave; correcting a wrong prescription later means another visit.
7. **Order glasses or contacts.** [BRANCH: buy in the same shop | take the prescription to another optician | order online using the prescription and PD] Choose lens options (single vision, progressive, coatings) to fit your needs and budget. → *Expect:* an order placed with the correct prescription and PD; a fitting date or delivery estimate given.
8. **Check the finished eyewear.** On collection, confirm the lenses match the prescription, the frames sit level and comfortable, and vision is clear at the intended distance. → *Expect:* clear, comfortable vision with no distortion or persistent headache after a short adaptation.

## Decision points

- Optometrist flags high eye pressure, an abnormal retina, or sudden vision change → this needs referral to an ophthalmologist; a new-glasses errand becomes a medical priority.
- Buying online to save money → you must have your prescription and an accurate PD; complex prescriptions (strong, progressive, prism) are riskier to order unseen.
- New progressive lenses → expect a genuine adaptation period of days to weeks; do not conclude they are wrong on day one.

## Failure modes & recovery

- **F1 Shop is reluctant to hand over your prescription:** you are generally entitled to a copy after the exam; ask again citing that right, since you may buy glasses elsewhere.
- **F2 New glasses cause headaches or distortion beyond adaptation:** return within the shop's remake window; the axis, PD, or lens centering may be off and is correctable.
- **F3 PD missing from the prescription:** ask the shop to measure it (it is needed to order), or measure carefully at home only as a last resort; a wrong PD ruins otherwise correct lenses.
- **F4 Ordered online and lenses feel wrong:** compare the made-up prescription against your written one; reputable sellers remake on their own error, but self-measured PD errors may be on you.

## Verification

The exam is complete, you can state your acuity and whether your prescription changed and the health checks were normal, you hold a written prescription with PD, and any glasses or contacts you ordered match it and give clear, comfortable vision.

## Variations

- `us`: exams and eyewear are often billed separately; a vision plan may cover a yearly exam and a frame allowance. You have a federal right to your glasses prescription.
- `uk`: NHS-funded sight tests apply to eligible groups (children, over-60s, certain conditions); opticians must give you your prescription and you can spend it anywhere.
- Contact-lens wearers: the contact prescription differs from the glasses one and needs a separate fitting and follow-up; do not order lenses off a glasses prescription.

## Safety & privacy

Low risk. The most valuable part is medical: an eye exam catches glaucoma, diabetic changes, and other conditions early, so do not skip the health checks to save on an add-on. Your prescription is yours to take anywhere. Your eye-health findings are medical data; share them through the provider, and act promptly on any referral.
