## Goal

A fever in yourself or a household member is measured accurately, managed with rest and fluids, and escalated to a clinician the moment a red-flag threshold is crossed.

## Preconditions

- A working thermometer (digital oral/axillary, ear, or forehead). Know which type you have; each reads differently.
- Access to fluids, a cool room, and paracetamol/acetaminophen or ibuprofen with a known dose.
- For infants: the age in months. Under 3 months changes every rule below.

## Steps

1. **Pick the right thermometer for the person.** Oral for adults and cooperative children over 4; ear or axillary for younger; forehead scanners are fast but least accurate. → *Expect:* a device you can place correctly without a struggle.
2. **Measure at rest, not right after activity or a hot drink.** Wait 15 minutes after eating/drinking/bathing for an oral reading. → *Expect:* a stable numeric reading, not one still climbing on the display.
3. **Interpret the number by site.** Fever is roughly ≥38.0°C (100.4°F) core. Axillary reads ~0.5°C low; forehead/ear vary. → *Expect:* you know whether this crosses the fever line for the site you used.
4. **Note the trend, not just one value.** Re-measure every 4 to 6 hours and write down time, value, and site. → *Expect:* a short log showing rising, plateau, or falling.
5. **Treat the discomfort, not the number.** Give paracetamol or ibuprofen at the correct weight-based dose only if the person is uncomfortable; a well child at 38.5°C needs no drug. ⚠️ *Irreversible:* never exceed the max daily dose and never combine two products containing the same drug (overdose is liver/kidney damage). → *Expect:* dose recorded with the clock time of the next allowed dose.
6. **Push fluids and cool the environment.** Small frequent sips; light clothing; room comfortable, not cold. Do not sponge with cold water or alcohol (causes shivering, which raises temperature). → *Expect:* the person is drinking and passing urine; skin not clammy-cold.
7. **Watch for red flags continuously.** [BRANCH: any red flag present → contact urgent care / emergency now | none, improving → continue home care and rest] → *Expect:* a clear decision each time you re-check.

## Decision points

- Adult fever above 39.4°C (103°F), or any fever lasting more than 3 days → call a clinician.
- Infant under 3 months with any fever ≥38.0°C → emergency assessment, no home dosing first.
- Fever with stiff neck, rash that does not fade under pressure, breathing difficulty, confusion, or a seizure → emergency services immediately.

## Failure modes & recovery

- **F1 Falsely low reading:** cold hands, mouth-breathing, or a probe not seated → re-measure at a second site before deciding "no fever".
- **F2 Double-dosing:** two caregivers medicate unaware of each other → keep one written dose log everyone signs; when in doubt, wait rather than repeat.
- **F3 Dehydration creeping in:** dark urine, no tears, sunken eyes, lethargy → switch to oral rehydration solution in tiny sips; if refused or vomiting persists, escalate.
- **F4 Febrile seizure in a child:** brief full-body stiffening/jerking with fever → lay on their side, clear the area, time it, do NOT restrain or put anything in the mouth; call emergency if over 5 minutes or first-ever.

## Verification

The person's temperature is logged with time and site, any medication is within safe dose and recorded, fluids are going in and urine is coming out, and no red-flag symptom is present. If any threshold was crossed, a clinician has been contacted.

## Variations

- Children: dosing is by weight not age; use the syringe supplied, and never adult tablets.
- Elderly or immunosuppressed: a lower threshold to escalate; they can be seriously ill with only a mild fever.

## Safety & privacy

Medium risk: the real hazards are medication overdose and missing a red flag in the very young. Keep the dose log accurate, respect per-drug daily maximums, and treat under-3-month fever and non-blanching rash as emergencies without delay.
