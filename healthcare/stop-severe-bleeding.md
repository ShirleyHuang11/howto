## Goal

Life-threatening external bleeding is controlled with direct pressure while emergency services are called.

## Preconditions

- This is emergency first aid guidance, not a substitute for professional care, Stop the Bleed training, or first aid certification.
- Call emergency services immediately for spurting blood, continuous heavy flow, pooled blood, partial or complete amputation, or signs of shock.
- Approach only when the scene is safe, and use gloves or a barrier if available.
- Do not remove embedded objects and do not remove the original soaked dressing from a severe wound.
- Use a tourniquet only for life-threatening limb bleeding when direct pressure cannot control it or when trained and equipped.

## Steps

1. **Check scene safety.** Look for traffic, weapons, electricity, glass, or other hazards before approaching. → *Expect:* you can help without becoming injured.
2. **Call emergency services.** Assign a caller or call on speaker for severe bleeding. → *Expect:* EMS is activated early.
3. **Expose the wound.** Move or cut clothing enough to see the bleeding source, leaving embedded objects in place. → *Expect:* pressure can be applied to the correct spot.
4. **Apply direct pressure.** Press firmly on the wound with gloved hands, gauze, a clean cloth, or clothing. → *Expect:* bleeding slows under steady pressure.
5. **Maintain pressure.** Keep constant firm pressure without peeking repeatedly. → *Expect:* clotting is not disrupted.
6. **Add dressing if soaked.** Place more material on top if blood soaks through, but do not remove the original dressing. → *Expect:* pressure continues over the wound.
7. **Elevate if practical.** Raise the injured limb above heart level only if it does not worsen pain or disturb pressure. → *Expect:* bleeding may slow while direct pressure remains primary.
8. **Use tourniquet for uncontrolled limb bleeding.** Place a commercial tourniquet high and tight above the wound if direct pressure fails or bleeding is immediately life-threatening. ⚠️ *Irreversible:* prolonged tourniquet use can damage tissue; note the time and do not remove it once tightened. → *Expect:* limb bleeding stops or greatly decreases.
9. **Treat for shock.** Have the person lie still, keep them warm, and avoid food or drink. → *Expect:* faintness and chilling are reduced while waiting.
10. **Monitor breathing and responsiveness.** Continue pressure until EMS takes over or you are unable to continue. → *Expect:* changes are noticed and reported.

## Decision points

- Bleeding is spurting, flowing continuously, or pooling rapidly → call emergency services and apply direct pressure.
- Direct pressure controls bleeding → keep pressure and wait for professional care.
- Direct pressure fails on an arm or leg wound → apply a tourniquet if available and appropriate.
- Object is embedded in the wound → apply pressure around it and stabilize it.
- Person becomes unresponsive and not breathing normally → start CPR if trained and follow dispatcher instructions.

## Failure modes & recovery

- **F1 Pressure too light:** detect continued heavy flow, recover by pressing harder directly over the source.
- **F2 Dressing removed:** detect someone lifting soaked gauze, recover by replacing pressure and adding material on top.
- **F3 Tourniquet too loose:** detect ongoing bright bleeding below it, recover by tightening until bleeding stops.
- **F4 Shock ignored:** detect pale, cold, clammy, dizzy, or confused appearance, recover by lying them down, warming, and updating EMS.

## Verification

Emergency services have been called, firm direct pressure is maintained, soaked dressings are left in place, and any tourniquet time is recorded.

## Variations

- No first aid kit: use the cleanest available cloth or clothing and your hand pressure.
- Nosebleed: this recipe is for severe external wounds; use nosebleed-specific guidance unless bleeding is traumatic and severe.
- Multiple rescuers: one maintains pressure while another calls, finds supplies, and watches for shock.

## Safety & privacy

High risk because severe bleeding can kill quickly. Call emergency services, use barriers when possible, get Stop the Bleed or first aid training, and use this recipe only as emergency support until professional care arrives.
