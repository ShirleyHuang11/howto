## Goal

A stocked, organized, in-date home first-aid kit exists in a known location, and everyone in the household knows where it is and how to use the basics.

## Preconditions

- A sturdy container (a labeled box, zip bag, or dedicated pouch) and a cool, dry, accessible storage spot.
- A shopping list or a pre-made kit to build on, plus a way to note expiry dates.
- Knowledge of household-specific needs: children, allergies (an epinephrine auto-injector if prescribed), chronic conditions, pets.

## Steps

1. **Choose the container and location.** Pick a box that closes fully and fits the shelf; store it somewhere cool and dry that adults reach easily but young children cannot. Avoid the bathroom, since heat and humidity degrade supplies. → *Expect:* a closable, labeled container in a spot every adult can name.
2. **Stock wound care and dressings.** Assorted adhesive bandages, sterile gauze pads, a roll of gauze, medical tape, non-stick pads, adhesive wound-closure strips, and antiseptic wipes. → *Expect:* you can cover a range of cut and scrape sizes.
3. **Add tools and barriers.** Tweezers, blunt-tip scissors, a digital thermometer, disposable gloves, a CPR face shield, safety pins, and an instant cold pack. → *Expect:* the tools to remove a splinter, cut tape, check a fever, and protect yourself while helping.
4. **Add medicines and topicals, with a household check.** Antiseptic cream, hydrocortisone cream, antihistamine, pain and fever reducers (an adult one and a child dose if children live there), rehydration salts, and any prescribed emergency meds like an auto-injector or inhaler. → *Expect:* each medicine is appropriate for your household ages; child and adult doses are not confused. ⚠️ *Irreversible:* never stock aspirin for children (Reye's syndrome risk) or mix up adult and pediatric dosing; label clearly.
5. **Add reference and emergency information.** A first-aid instruction leaflet, a card with emergency numbers, poison-control, doctor and pharmacy numbers, and a list of each household member's allergies and key conditions. → *Expect:* a stranger or babysitter could act using only what is in the kit.
6. **Label and date everything.** Write the earliest expiry on the outside, or keep a dated inventory card inside listing each item and its expiry. → *Expect:* you can see the next expiry at a glance without unpacking.
7. **Run an expiry and restock audit on a schedule.** Twice a year (tie it to clock changes so you remember) open the kit, discard expired or used items, and replace them. → *Expect:* nothing expired remains; the inventory card is refreshed and dated.
8. **Brief the household.** Show every capable member where the kit lives, how it is organized, and how to use the auto-injector or other emergency item if present. → *Expect:* each adult can find the kit and demonstrate the emergency-item steps unprompted.

## Decision points

- Household has a life-threatening allergy → the auto-injector is the priority item; store a second one and make the briefing mandatory, since these are used by bystanders.
- Small children present → add child-dose medicines, keep the kit locked or high, and never store adult meds where a child could reach.
- You also want a car or travel kit → build a smaller duplicate rather than moving the home kit around, so the home one is never absent when needed.

## Failure modes & recovery

- **F1 Kit raided for everyday use and now empty:** keep a separate stash of everyday bandages and painkillers so the emergency kit stays intact; restock immediately after any real use.
- **F2 Found expired medicines during an emergency:** this is why the twice-yearly audit exists; dispose of expired meds properly (pharmacy take-back, not the toilet) and set a calendar reminder.
- **F3 No one but you knows where it is:** re-do the household briefing; a kit only you can find fails the moment you are the one hurt.
- **F4 Auto-injector or inhaler expired or missing:** these are prescription items with hard expiry; refill through the pharmacy ahead of the date and keep the old one until the new arrives.

## Verification

The kit contains in-date wound care, tools, barriers, household-appropriate medicines, and emergency info; the earliest expiry is visible on a dated inventory; and every capable household member can locate the kit and use its emergency items.

## Variations

- Families with infants: add pediatric thermometer, saline drops, and infant-dose medicines; confirm doses with a pharmacist.
- Outdoor or rural households: add a tourniquet, larger trauma dressings, a splint, and tick removal tools, and learn their use.
- `us` vs `uk` and elsewhere: brand names and available over-the-counter medicines differ; match the categories, not the labels, and follow local dosing.

## Safety & privacy

Low risk to build. The real hazards are dosing errors (especially child vs. adult medicines) and expired emergency items failing when needed, both handled by clear labeling and the twice-yearly audit. The kit holds household health details (allergies, conditions); keep that card inside the box, not posted publicly.
