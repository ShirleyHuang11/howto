## Goal

An irritated outer ear canal after water exposure is kept dry, protected from further trauma, and escalated when infection signs need a clinician.

## Preconditions

- Ear discomfort, itching, or fullness after swimming or moisture exposure.
- No severe pain, fever, swelling around the ear, dizziness, facial weakness, or drainage of blood or pus.
- No known hole in the eardrum, ear tubes, recent ear surgery, or object stuck in the ear.

## Steps

1. **Stop water exposure.** Stay out of swimming and keep shower water out of the ear until symptoms settle. → *Expect:* the ear is no longer being re-wetted.
2. **Dry the outer ear.** Tilt the head, let water drain, and dry only the outside with a towel. → *Expect:* visible outer ear moisture is gone.
3. **Do not insert cotton buds.** Keep cotton swabs, earbuds, hair pins, and fingers out of the canal. → *Expect:* no added scratches or packed wax.
4. **Use drops only when appropriate.** [BRANCH: clinician-prescribed drops | no prescription] use prescribed ear drops as directed, or ask a pharmacist or clinician before using drying drops if you might have eardrum damage. → *Expect:* drops are used only when safe for your ear history.
5. **Place drops correctly.** Lie with the affected ear up, add the directed number of drops, and stay there for a few minutes. → *Expect:* drops reach the canal instead of running out.
6. **Manage mild pain.** Use an over-the-counter pain reliever you can take safely. → *Expect:* discomfort is reduced enough to rest.
7. **Avoid ear devices.** Skip earbuds, earplugs, and hearing aids in that ear if they worsen pain, unless medically necessary. → *Expect:* less rubbing inside the canal.
8. **Watch the next 24 to 48 hours.** Track pain, hearing, discharge, and fever. → *Expect:* mild irritation improves rather than worsens.

## Decision points

- Moderate or severe pain, pus-like discharge, fever, worsening hearing, or symptoms lasting more than 48 hours → see a doctor.
- Diabetes, immune suppression, or severe pain spreading around the ear → seek prompt medical care.
- Ear tubes, eardrum hole, or recent ear surgery → do not use nonprescribed drops until a clinician advises.

## Failure modes & recovery

- **F1 Drops burn sharply:** stop using them and contact a clinician, especially if eardrum damage is possible.
- **F2 Water keeps entering during showers:** use a shower cap or cotton with petroleum jelly at the outer opening, not pushed into the canal.
- **F3 Pain increases when pulling the outer ear:** this supports swimmer's ear and should prompt medical care if moderate or worsening.
- **F4 Hearing feels blocked:** avoid digging; wax, swelling, or fluid needs proper evaluation if it persists.

## Verification

The ear canal is kept dry, nothing is inserted into it, drops are used only when safe, and pain or discharge thresholds trigger medical care.

## Variations

- Prevention after healing: dry ears after swimming and ask a clinician whether acidifying drops are safe for you.
- Hearing aids: clean and dry the device, and contact the hearing clinician if pain recurs.
- Children: ear pain with fever, drainage, or sleep disruption should be assessed.

## Safety & privacy

Medium risk because untreated infection can spread and unsafe drops can harm an injured eardrum. See a doctor for pain, discharge, fever, swelling, diabetes, immune suppression, dizziness, or facial weakness.
