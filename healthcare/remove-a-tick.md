## Goal

A tick is removed intact as soon as possible, the bite site is cleaned, and symptoms that need medical care are monitored.

## Preconditions

- Fine-tipped tweezers, soap and water, antiseptic, a small sealed bag or container, and good lighting are available.
- The tick is on the skin and can be reached without cutting or digging.
- Seek professional or emergency care when the person has trouble breathing, facial swelling, weakness, severe headache, confusion, fever, or a spreading rash.
- This guidance does not replace local medical advice about tick-borne disease prevention.

## Steps

1. **Wash your hands.** Clean hands with soap and water before touching the bite area. → *Expect:* hands are clean before removal.
2. **Expose the tick.** Move hair or clothing aside and steady the skin. → *Expect:* the tick and where it enters the skin are clearly visible.
3. **Grip close to the skin.** Place fine tweezers around the tick as close to the skin surface as possible. → *Expect:* the tweezers hold the tick near the mouthparts, not the swollen body.
4. **Pull steadily upward.** Use even pressure without twisting, jerking, squeezing, burning, or coating the tick. → *Expect:* the tick releases from the skin.
5. **Check the bite site.** Look for any remaining dark mouthpart fragments at the surface. → *Expect:* no large attached tick body remains.
6. **Clean the area.** Wash with soap and water, then apply antiseptic if available. → *Expect:* the bite site is clean.
7. **Save the tick.** Put it in a sealed bag or container with the date and bite location. → *Expect:* the tick can be identified later if symptoms develop.
8. **Record exposure.** Note where and when the bite likely happened and how long the tick may have been attached. → *Expect:* a clinician could assess risk if needed.
9. **Monitor symptoms.** Watch for expanding rash, fever, chills, headache, muscle aches, joint pain, or unusual fatigue for several weeks. → *Expect:* concerning symptoms are noticed early.
10. **Avoid unsafe methods.** Do not twist, crush, burn, use petroleum jelly, nail polish, or alcohol to make it detach. → *Expect:* removal is mechanical and prompt.

## Decision points

- Tick cannot be removed or parts remain deeply embedded → contact a clinician.
- [BRANCH: no symptoms | rash or fever] no symptoms means monitor, while rash or fever means seek medical advice promptly.
- Bite occurred in an area with Lyme disease or tick was attached a long time → ask a clinician whether preventive treatment is appropriate.
- ⚠️ *Irreversible:* Do not burn the tick on the skin because it can injure tissue and delay removal.

## Failure modes & recovery

- **F1 Tick crushed:** detect squeezed or broken body, recover by cleaning the site and saving any parts for identification.
- **F2 Mouthparts remain:** detect small dark specks at the surface, recover by leaving deeply embedded fragments alone and seeking advice if irritation persists.
- **F3 Rash missed:** detect expanding ring, oval rash, or widespread rash, recover by seeking medical care and sharing the exposure date.
- **F4 Infection at bite:** detect increasing redness, warmth, pus, or pain, recover by contacting a clinician.

## Verification

The tick is removed or contained, the bite site is cleaned, and symptom monitoring details are recorded.

## Variations

- Tick on a child: keep the child still with help from another adult.
- Tick on a pet: use the same steady tweezer pull and contact a veterinarian for pet-specific prevention.
- No tweezers: seek help rather than pinching with bare fingers if possible.

## Safety & privacy

Medium risk because tick bites can transmit infections and symptoms may appear later. Seek professional or emergency care when rash, fever, neurologic symptoms, allergic symptoms, or worsening bite-site infection occurs.
