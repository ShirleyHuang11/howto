## Goal

A likely mild ankle sprain is protected, swelling is limited, and fracture red flags are identified for medical evaluation.

## Preconditions

- This is first aid guidance, not a substitute for diagnosis, imaging, rehabilitation advice, or first aid training.
- Call emergency services for an open wound, severe deformity, loss of circulation or feeling, uncontrolled pain, or a person who cannot be moved safely.
- Seek medical care promptly if fracture red flags are present or walking is not possible.
- Use rest, ice, compression, and elevation early, but do not force movement through severe pain.
- Remove tight footwear or jewelry early if swelling is expected and removal does not worsen pain.

## Steps

1. **Stop activity.** Have the person sit or lie down and avoid putting weight on the injured ankle. → *Expect:* pain is not worsened by continued use.
2. **Check for emergency signs.** Look for obvious deformity, open wound, numbness, blue or cold foot, or severe uncontrolled pain. → *Expect:* urgent injuries are separated from routine sprains.
3. **Remove constricting items.** Loosen shoe laces and remove tight footwear or ankle jewelry if this can be done gently. → *Expect:* swelling will not trap tight material.
4. **Rest the ankle.** Keep weight off the ankle and use support when moving. → *Expect:* the joint is protected from further injury.
5. **Ice the area.** Apply a cold pack wrapped in cloth for 15 to 20 minutes at a time. → *Expect:* pain and swelling begin to settle without freezing the skin.
6. **Compress lightly.** Wrap with an elastic bandage from toes upward, snug but not tight. → *Expect:* swelling is supported while toes stay warm and pink.
7. **Elevate the ankle.** Raise the ankle above heart level when possible. → *Expect:* swelling drains away from the foot.
8. **Check weight bearing.** [BRANCH: can take four steps | cannot take four steps] try only gentle standing if safe; stop if pain is sharp or unstable. → *Expect:* walking ability is assessed without forcing it.
9. **Screen for x-ray red flags.** Check for bony tenderness at either ankle bone, the base of the fifth metatarsal, or the navicular area on top of the midfoot. → *Expect:* Ottawa-style warning signs are noted.
10. **Plan recovery.** Arrange medical care for red flags, or begin gentle range of motion after pain allows and follow up if not improving in 5 to 7 days. → *Expect:* recovery has a safe next step.

## Decision points

- Cannot take four steps immediately after injury or during assessment → seek medical evaluation for possible x-ray.
- Bone tenderness at the ankle bones, base of fifth metatarsal, or navicular → seek medical evaluation.
- Numbness, cold foot, deformity, or open wound → call emergency services or seek emergency care.
- Pain and swelling improve over 24 to 48 hours → continue protection and gradual return.
- Pain persists, instability continues, or swelling worsens → get clinical assessment.

## Failure modes & recovery

- **F1 Wrap too tight:** detect numb toes, tingling, blue color, or increased pain, recover by loosening the bandage.
- **F2 Ice burn:** detect direct ice on skin or numb white skin, recover by removing ice and using a cloth barrier.
- **F3 Fracture missed:** detect inability to walk or bony tenderness, recover by seeking x-ray evaluation.
- **F4 Return too soon:** detect limping or sharp pain during activity, recover by resting and progressing more slowly.

## Verification

The ankle is rested, iced with a skin barrier, lightly compressed, elevated, and checked for inability to take four steps or focal bone tenderness.

## Variations

- Athlete: return only when walking, hopping, strength, and balance are pain-free or cleared by a clinician.
- Older adult: seek care earlier because fracture risk and balance risk are higher.
- Recurrent sprain: ask a clinician or physical therapist about bracing and strengthening.

## Safety & privacy

Medium risk because fractures and circulation problems can look like sprains. Call emergency services for severe signs, get first aid training, and use this recipe as temporary support rather than a diagnosis.
