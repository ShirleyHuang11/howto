## Goal

A metered-dose inhaler is used with correct timing and breathing technique, and urgent help is sought when symptoms do not improve.

## Preconditions

- This is general inhaler technique guidance, not a substitute for a clinician's asthma or COPD action plan, emergency care, or training.
- Call emergency services for severe breathlessness, blue lips, inability to speak full sentences, exhaustion, confusion, or symptoms not improving after reliever medicine.
- Use the medication prescribed for the person; know which inhaler is the quick-relief reliever and which is a daily preventer.
- A spacer should be used when available, especially for children or anyone who struggles with timing.
- Check the dose counter and expiration date if time allows, but do not delay emergency care.

## Steps

1. **Identify the inhaler.** Confirm it is the prescribed medicine and whether it is a reliever or preventer. → *Expect:* the right inhaler is selected for the situation.
2. **Sit upright.** Have the person sit or stand tall with shoulders relaxed. → *Expect:* the chest can expand more easily.
3. **Shake the inhaler.** Remove the cap, check the mouthpiece is clear, and shake the inhaler well. → *Expect:* medicine is mixed and the mouthpiece is unobstructed.
4. **Attach spacer if available.** Insert the inhaler into the spacer and keep the spacer level. → *Expect:* medicine will be easier to inhale deeply.
5. **Breathe out first.** Have the person exhale fully away from the inhaler or spacer. → *Expect:* lungs are ready for a slow deep breath in.
6. **Seal lips.** Put lips tightly around the mouthpiece, or seal a mask spacer over the nose and mouth. → *Expect:* air does not leak around the device.
7. **Press and inhale.** Press one puff while starting a slow deep breath in, continuing until the lungs feel full. → *Expect:* medicine is carried into the airways.
8. **Hold breath.** Hold for up to 10 seconds if able, then breathe out slowly. → *Expect:* medicine has time to settle in the lungs.
9. **Repeat only as directed.** Wait about 30 to 60 seconds between puffs if another puff is prescribed. → *Expect:* dosing follows the person's action plan or label.
10. **Escalate if overused or ineffective.** [BRANCH: improves | does not improve] continue the action plan if improving; seek urgent help if repeated reliever doses are needed or symptoms worsen. → *Expect:* dangerous breathing trouble is not managed alone.

## Decision points

- Quick-relief inhaler is needed more often than the action plan allows → seek medical advice urgently.
- Severe symptoms or no improvement after reliever use → call emergency services.
- Preventer inhaler is the only inhaler available during an attack → call for urgent help because preventers do not work fast.
- Person cannot coordinate pressing and inhaling → use a spacer or follow clinician-taught technique.
- Steroid inhaler used routinely → rinse the mouth afterward if instructed.

## Failure modes & recovery

- **F1 Puff fired before inhale:** detect medicine spraying into air or mouth, recover by resetting and timing press with the start of inhalation.
- **F2 No seal:** detect whistling or air leaks, recover by resealing lips or mask.
- **F3 Empty inhaler:** detect zero dose counter or no spray, recover by using backup medicine and seeking help if symptomatic.
- **F4 Reliever overuse:** detect repeated doses without relief, recover by calling emergency services or following the emergency action plan.

## Verification

The correct inhaler was used with an exhale, sealed mouthpiece or spacer, one puff during slow inhalation, breath hold, and escalation if symptoms did not improve.

## Variations

- Spacer with mask: keep the mask sealed for several normal breaths after each puff.
- Dry powder inhaler: do not shake unless the label says to, and inhale fast and deep according to the device instructions.
- Child user: an adult should count breaths and follow the child's written asthma action plan.

## Safety & privacy

Medium risk because breathing symptoms can become life-threatening. Call emergency services for severe or worsening symptoms, get device-specific training from a clinician or pharmacist, and do not use this recipe instead of professional care.
