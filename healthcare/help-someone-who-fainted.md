## Goal

A person who fainted is protected from injury, checked for breathing, and escalated when fainting may signal a serious problem.

## Preconditions

- This is first aid guidance, not a substitute for medical evaluation, dispatcher instructions, or first aid training.
- Call emergency services if the person does not wake quickly, has chest pain, trouble breathing, severe headache, injury, seizure, pregnancy, diabetes, or repeated fainting.
- If they are unresponsive and not breathing normally, call emergency services and start CPR if trained.
- Do not give food or drink until the person is fully awake and able to swallow.
- Do not move them if you suspect neck, back, or major injury unless needed for safety or CPR.

## Steps

1. **Check scene safety.** Make sure the area is safe from traffic, heat, electrical hazards, or crowd pressure. → *Expect:* you can assist safely.
2. **Check responsiveness.** Tap shoulders and speak loudly to see if they wake. → *Expect:* you know whether this is fainting or ongoing unresponsiveness.
3. **Check breathing.** Look for normal breathing for no more than 10 seconds. → *Expect:* absent or abnormal breathing is recognized.
4. **Call if not breathing normally.** [BRANCH: not breathing normally | breathing normally] start CPR if trained when breathing is absent or abnormal; continue fainting care if breathing is normal. → *Expect:* cardiac arrest is not mistaken for fainting.
5. **Lay them flat.** If no injury prevents it, place them on their back. → *Expect:* blood flow to the brain is supported.
6. **Raise the legs.** Lift legs about 12 inches if comfortable and no injury is suspected. → *Expect:* circulation may improve.
7. **Loosen tight clothing.** Loosen collars, belts, or restrictive items. → *Expect:* breathing and comfort improve.
8. **Use recovery position if needed.** If they are breathing but not fully awake, roll them onto their side. → *Expect:* the airway stays open and fluid can drain.
9. **Keep them resting after waking.** Have them remain lying or seated for several minutes before standing. → *Expect:* repeat fainting risk is reduced.
10. **Escalate serious features.** Call emergency services for red flags, injury, or failure to recover promptly. → *Expect:* dangerous causes are not dismissed.

## Decision points

- Person is not breathing normally → call emergency services, start CPR if trained, and get an AED.
- Person wakes quickly and feels well with no injury or concerning symptoms → monitor and advise medical follow-up if cause is unclear.
- Fainting occurred during exercise or with chest pain, palpitations, or shortness of breath → call emergency services.
- Person hit their head, is pregnant, diabetic, or has repeated episodes → seek urgent medical care.
- Person vomits or remains drowsy → use recovery position and call emergency services.

## Failure modes & recovery

- **F1 Cardiac arrest missed:** detect gasping or no normal breathing, recover by calling emergency services and starting CPR.
- **F2 Standing too soon:** detect dizziness on sitting or standing, recover by laying them down again.
- **F3 Drink given early:** detect drowsiness or poor swallowing, recover by stopping oral intake until fully alert.
- **F4 Injury moved:** detect fall with neck, back, hip, or head pain, recover by keeping still and calling emergency services.

## Verification

The person is breathing normally, lying safely or in recovery position, monitored until fully alert, and emergency services are called for any red flag.

## Variations

- Heat-related fainting: move to a cool place and assess for heat exhaustion or heat stroke.
- Blood or needle trigger: keep the person lying down longer and protect privacy from onlookers.
- Public place: ask one bystander to create space and another to meet responders if called.

## Safety & privacy

Medium to high risk because fainting can hide heart, neurologic, injury, or heat emergencies. Call emergency services when indicated, get first aid training, and do not use this recipe as a replacement for professional care.
