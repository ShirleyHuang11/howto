## Goal

A minor open wound is cleaned, covered, monitored, and escalated if it may need medical care.

## Preconditions

- Clean running water, soap, sterile gauze or clean cloth, bandage, and disposable gloves if available.
- The bleeding is not severe, spurting, or uncontrolled after firm pressure.
- Seek professional or emergency care when the wound is deep, gaping, on the face, caused by a bite, dirty object, rusty puncture, or if tetanus vaccination may be out of date.
- This is first aid, not a substitute for diagnosis or wound repair.

## Steps

1. **Wash your hands.** Use soap and water for at least 20 seconds or hand sanitizer if water is unavailable. → *Expect:* hands are clean before touching the wound.
2. **Control bleeding.** Press sterile gauze or a clean cloth directly on the wound. → *Expect:* bleeding slows or stops within several minutes.
3. **Check whether stitches may be needed.** Look for separated edges, visible fat or muscle, deep puncture, loss of movement, or numbness. → *Expect:* serious features are identified before home care continues.
4. **Rinse the wound.** Hold the wound under clean running water or pour clean water over it to irrigate debris. → *Expect:* loose dirt and visible grit wash away.
5. **Clean around it.** Wash the surrounding skin with soap, keeping soap out of the open wound when possible. → *Expect:* nearby skin is clean without extra stinging inside the wound.
6. **Remove loose debris only.** Use clean tweezers rinsed with alcohol for obvious surface particles. → *Expect:* only easy-to-lift debris is removed.
7. **Apply a thin protective layer.** Use a small amount of petroleum jelly or antibiotic ointment if tolerated. → *Expect:* the wound surface is lightly coated, not packed.
8. **Cover the wound.** Place sterile gauze or an adhesive bandage over it. → *Expect:* the wound is covered without cutting off circulation.
9. **Change the dressing.** Replace it daily, when wet, or when dirty. → *Expect:* each new dressing is clean and dry.
10. **Watch for infection.** Check redness, warmth, swelling, pus, increasing pain, red streaks, fever, or delayed healing. → *Expect:* worsening signs trigger care instead of being ignored.

## Decision points

- Bleeding soaks through dressings or continues after 10 minutes of firm pressure → seek urgent care.
- Wound edges stay open or the cut is deep → seek care promptly because closure is time-sensitive.
- [BRANCH: clean minor scrape | dirty puncture] clean minor scrapes can be dressed at home, while dirty punctures need medical advice.
- ⚠️ *Irreversible:* Do not remove a deeply embedded object because it may be limiting bleeding; stabilize it and seek emergency care.

## Failure modes & recovery

- **F1 Reopened bleeding:** detect fresh bleeding through the dressing, recover with direct pressure and urgent care if it does not stop.
- **F2 Trapped dirt:** detect remaining grit after rinsing, recover by rinsing longer and seeking care if debris is embedded.
- **F3 Dressing stuck:** detect gauze pulling on a scab, recover by soaking with clean water before removal.
- **F4 Infection signs:** detect spreading redness, warmth, pus, fever, or worsening pain, recover by contacting a clinician promptly.

## Verification

The wound is visibly clean, bleeding is controlled, a clean dressing covers it, and no urgent-care warning sign is present.

## Variations

- Small scrape: a simple adhesive bandage may be enough after washing.
- Hand or joint wound: choose a flexible dressing and seek care if movement is limited.
- Allergy to ointment: use petroleum jelly or a nonstick dressing instead.

## Safety & privacy

Medium risk because wounds can hide infection, tendon injury, foreign material, or tetanus risk. Seek professional or emergency care when bleeding is uncontrolled, the wound is deep or gaping, an object is embedded, or infection signs appear.
