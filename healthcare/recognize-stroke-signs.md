## Goal

Possible stroke signs are recognized quickly, emergency services are called immediately, and the time symptoms began is recorded.

## Preconditions

- This is emergency bystander guidance, not a substitute for professional diagnosis, stroke care, or first aid training.
- Call emergency services immediately for any sudden face droop, arm weakness, speech trouble, confusion, vision loss, severe headache, dizziness, or loss of balance.
- Do not drive the person yourself unless dispatch explicitly tells you to because stroke care is time-sensitive.
- Do not give food, drink, or medicine unless emergency clinicians instruct you.
- Use FAST as a quick screen, but call even if symptoms do not fit perfectly.

## Steps

1. **Check scene safety.** Make sure you can approach and speak with the person without danger. → *Expect:* you can focus on symptoms without creating another emergency.
2. **Test face.** Ask the person to smile and look for one side drooping or not moving normally. → *Expect:* facial asymmetry is noticed quickly.
3. **Test arms.** Ask them to raise both arms and watch for one arm drifting down or failing to lift. → *Expect:* one-sided weakness is identified if present.
4. **Test speech.** Ask them to repeat a simple sentence and listen for slurring, wrong words, or inability to speak. → *Expect:* speech change is recognized without arguing about cause.
5. **Act on time.** [BRANCH: any FAST sign | no FAST sign but sudden neurologic symptom] call emergency services now if any sign is present or symptoms are sudden and concerning. → *Expect:* EMS activation is not delayed.
6. **Note symptom timing.** Ask when the person was last known well and when symptoms were first noticed. → *Expect:* a specific clock time or best estimate is available for responders.
7. **Keep them resting.** Have the person sit or lie safely with the head and shoulders slightly raised if comfortable. → *Expect:* they are protected from falls while waiting.
8. **Give nothing by mouth.** Do not offer food, drink, or pills because swallowing may be unsafe. → *Expect:* choking risk is reduced.
9. **Monitor breathing and alertness.** Recheck responsiveness and breathing until help arrives. → *Expect:* any deterioration is noticed and reported.
10. **Prepare information.** Gather medication list, allergies, known medical problems, and witness contact information if available. → *Expect:* responders receive useful context quickly.

## Decision points

- Any FAST sign appears, even if it resolves → call emergency services immediately.
- Sudden severe headache, vision loss, confusion, dizziness, or balance trouble appears → call emergency services even if FAST is negative.
- Person wants to sleep it off → keep them safe and explain that stroke treatment is time-sensitive.
- Person stops breathing normally → start CPR if trained and follow dispatcher instructions.
- Symptoms began during sleep → report the last time the person was known well before sleeping.

## Failure modes & recovery

- **F1 Waiting for improvement:** detect hesitation because symptoms are mild, recover by calling emergency services now.
- **F2 Unknown onset time:** detect unclear timing, recover by recording the last known well time and who saw them.
- **F3 Food or drink offered:** detect swallowing concern, recover by stopping oral intake until clinicians assess.
- **F4 Self-transport planned:** detect someone getting car keys, recover by calling EMS for stroke-capable transport.

## Verification

Emergency services have been called, the last-known-well time is recorded or estimated, and the person receives no food or drink while being monitored.

## Variations

- Transient symptoms: treat as urgent because a transient ischemic attack can warn of a larger stroke.
- Language barrier: demonstrate smile and arm raise, then use emergency dispatch or phone interpretation.
- Public place: ask one bystander to meet responders and another to find medical information.

## Safety & privacy

High risk because stroke can cause death or permanent disability. Call emergency services immediately, do not substitute this recipe for professional care, and get first aid training so you can recognize time-critical emergencies.
