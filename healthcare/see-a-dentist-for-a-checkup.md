## Goal

You attend a routine dental checkup and cleaning, understand what the dentist found, and leave with a clear, prioritized plan for any follow-up treatment.

## Preconditions

- A dental practice you can access (existing dentist, or a new one accepting patients) and your ID plus any dental insurance/plan details.
- Awareness of the recommended cadence: a routine checkup roughly every 6 months for most adults, adjusted by your dentist based on risk.
- Any current dental symptoms noted (sensitivity, pain, bleeding gums, a chipped tooth).

## Steps

1. **Book on the right cadence.** Call or use the practice's portal and ask for a checkup plus cleaning (a "recall" or "scale and polish"). If it has been well over a year, say so; they may allow extra time. → *Expect:* an appointment booked, with cleaning included in the same visit where the practice offers it.
2. **Confirm coverage before the visit.** Ask whether the practice is in-network / accepts your plan, what the checkup and cleaning cost, and what your share is. Note any annual limit. → *Expect:* a clear price and coverage picture, no billing surprise at the desk.
3. **Prepare and arrive.** Brush and floss beforehand (do not overbrush the gums raw), bring ID, plan details, and a list of symptoms and current medications. Arrive early for any new-patient paperwork. → *Expect:* checked in on time with your symptom list ready.
4. **Have the examination.** The dentist checks teeth, gums, bite, and does an oral cancer soft-tissue check; X-rays may be taken periodically, not every visit. → *Expect:* the dentist describes findings out loud (cavities, gum health, wear); ask them to point to anything on the X-ray or a mirror.
5. **Have the cleaning.** A hygienist or dentist scales tartar, polishes, and may floss and apply fluoride. Mild gum bleeding during scaling is normal, especially if flossing has been sparse. → *Expect:* teeth feel smooth; you are told where your brushing and flossing are missing.
6. **Get the findings and any treatment plan explained.** Ask which items are urgent, which are watch-and-wait, and which are optional or cosmetic. Ask for the plan in writing with costs. → *Expect:* a written treatment plan sorted by priority, with each item's cost and whether it can wait.
7. **Book follow-ups and set the next recall.** Schedule any needed treatment (filling, deeper cleaning) and book or note your next routine checkup before leaving. → *Expect:* follow-up treatment scheduled and the next recall date captured so it does not slip.

## Decision points

- Dentist proposes expensive or extensive work → for anything major (crowns, extractions, multiple fillings at once), it is reasonable to ask for the written plan and get a second opinion before committing.
- Bleeding gums flagged as gingivitis → this is usually reversible with better daily cleaning; ask for technique guidance rather than assuming you need major treatment.
- No dentist and pain is present → seek an urgent dental appointment or emergency dental service; a checkup is not the route for acute pain.

## Failure modes & recovery

- **F1 Quoted a large bill you did not expect:** ask which items are truly necessary now vs. deferrable, request the itemized plan, and check coverage before agreeing; you can decline optional work.
- **F2 Gums sore or sensitive after the cleaning:** normal for a day or two, especially after tartar removal; rinse with warm salt water and use a sensitive toothpaste, and call if pain worsens past a few days.
- **F3 Told you need deep cleaning (scaling and root planing):** this is a legitimate treatment for gum disease, not always an upsell; ask to see the gum-pocket measurements that justify it.
- **F4 Anxiety prevents you attending:** tell the practice; many offer shorter appointments, breaks, or sedation options, and going regularly keeps visits short and cheap.

## Verification

The checkup and cleaning are done, you can state what the dentist found and which follow-up items are urgent vs. optional, you have the plan and costs in writing, and your next recall date is set.

## Variations

- `us`: dental insurance is usually separate from medical, with annual maximums; two cleanings a year are commonly covered. Ask about in-network status.
- `uk`: NHS dental care is banded (fixed charges per band), if you can find an NHS place; private practices bill per item.
- Higher-risk patients (gum disease, diabetes, smokers) are often placed on a 3 to 4 month recall rather than 6; follow the interval your dentist sets.

## Safety & privacy

Low risk: routine checkups are safe. The real hazards are financial (over-treatment) and neglect (skipping recalls until a small cavity becomes a root canal). Ask for written, prioritized plans and second opinions on major work. Your dental and medical history is confidential; share it with the practice, not casually.
