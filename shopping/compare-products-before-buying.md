## Goal

For a purchase that matters, you converge on the right product at a fair price in bounded time — informed enough to be confident, without falling into review-reading forever.

## Preconditions

- A defined need with your top 2–3 *requirements* written down (what it must do), separate from nice-to-haves — this list is what stops the scroll.
- A budget band, honestly set before looking (prices anchor hard once you look).

## Steps

1. **Time-box by stakes.** Cheap/reversible (< an hour's pay, returnable): 10 minutes, pick the well-reviewed default, done — over-researching these is the real loss. Expensive/long-lived (appliance, mattress, laptop): the full recipe below. → *Expect:* a conscious lane choice before any tab is opened.
2. **Establish the category's landscape in one pass.** One trusted roundup/wirecutter-class source or two: learn the 3–4 usual candidates, the price tiers, and the *vocabulary* (what spec actually matters in this category vs. marketing numbers). → *Expect:* a shortlist of ~3 and knowledge of which one or two specs are load-bearing.
3. **Compare the shortlist on your requirements only.** A tiny table (phone note suffices): rows = your 2–3 requirements + price; columns = candidates. Specs not on your list don't get columns — that's the discipline. → *Expect:* a table where a winner is usually visible; ties broken by the nice-to-haves, not by reopening research.
4. **Read reviews for failure patterns, not scores.** Sort to critical reviews, recent first, on the finalists: repeated *specific* complaints ("hinge breaks at month 3", "runs two sizes small") are signal; lone rants and vague praise are noise. Check the review-count-to-age smell (thousands of 5-stars in a week = bought reviews). → *Expect:* each finalist's characteristic weakness known — and judged tolerable or disqualifying against *your* use.
5. **Verify the price is normal, not theatrical.** A price-history check (tracker sites/extensions) or a 30-second cross-store search: is today's "40% off" the everyday price in costume? → *Expect:* the real price band known; urgency-marketing defused.
6. **Decide and stop.** Buy the winner (`shopping/buy-a-product-online`), or calendar the known sale season if the history says wait. Close the tabs — post-purchase re-research has no upside. → *Expect:* a purchase (or a dated waiting plan) and the research loop actually closed.

## Decision points

- Two finalists genuinely tied on requirements → they're equivalent for you; take the better return policy or the cheaper one and spend the reclaimed hour on anything else.
- The category's reviews all mention the same rival ("just get the X instead") → promote X into the table; crowds are good at this specific correction.
- Requirements grew mid-research (spec creep: "maybe I *do* need the pro tier") → re-test against the original need in one sentence; upgrades must be justified by the need, not by adjacency to shinier tabs.

## Failure modes & recovery

- **F1 Analysis paralysis (day three, twelve tabs):** the requirements list has failed or never existed — write the 2–3 requirements *now*, apply step 3's table to the current finalists, decide today; a good-enough choice this week beats a perfect one never.
- **F2 Bought, then saw it cheaper:** many retailers price-match/refund within a window — one polite ask; otherwise file under the price of done and mute the deal alerts for that item.
- **F3 The winner is out of stock:** the table's runner-up exists for exactly this; resist re-opening the whole search.
- **F4 Regret from a known weakness you'd accepted:** the process worked — the weakness was known and priced in; distinguish that from process failure (a weakness reviews *did* flag and you skipped step 4).

## Verification

The purchase satisfies the written requirements, its characteristic weakness was known in advance, the price was sane against history, total research time matched the stakes lane — and the decision closed without a post-purchase research relapse.

## Variations

- In-store version: the same table built from shelf tags + `daily/food/read-food-labels`-style per-unit comparison; the phone in the aisle does the price-history check.
- Second-hand candidates: condition and seller trust join the requirements rows — overlapping with `shopping/buy-second-hand` territory.

## Safety & privacy

Low risk; the hazards are attentional (paralysis, anchor pricing, fake reviews) and the countermeasures are structural: the requirements list, the time-box, the price history. Deal-alert emails you signed up for during research: unsubscribe after (`digital/unsubscribe-from-a-mailing-list`).
