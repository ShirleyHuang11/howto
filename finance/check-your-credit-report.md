## Goal

Review your credit report for accuracy, understand which items help or hurt credit, and start disputes for errors or signs of identity theft.

## Preconditions

- You know the official free annual credit report source for your country or region.
- You have identifying information needed to verify your identity.
- You can save or print the report securely.
- You have records for loans, cards, addresses, and disputes if something is wrong.

## Steps

1. **Use the official free source.** Go to the government-approved or bureau-approved free annual credit report site, not an ad or paid monitoring page. → *Expect:* you can request reports without buying a score or subscription.
2. **Request the right bureau reports.** Choose one or all available credit bureaus depending on your review plan. → *Expect:* you know which bureau's file you are reading.
3. **Verify identity carefully.** Answer identity questions and avoid guessing if a question appears unfamiliar. → *Expect:* the report opens or gives a clear next step for manual verification.
4. **Save the report securely.** Download or print the report to a private location before leaving the session. → *Expect:* you can review it later without logging in again.
5. **Read personal information.** Check name variations, addresses, employers, birth date, and identity numbers where shown. → *Expect:* old but accurate history is separated from wrong or suspicious identity data.
6. **Review account sections.** Check each loan, card, balance, limit, payment status, open date, closed date, and ownership type. → *Expect:* every account is yours and reported accurately.
7. **Review inquiries and public records.** Look for recent hard inquiries, collections, bankruptcies, judgments where applicable, or other negative items. → *Expect:* unfamiliar or outdated items are marked for action.
8. **Classify what helps and hurts.** Identify on-time payments, low balances, long account history, and healthy account mix as helpful; late payments, high utilization, collections, defaults, and unnecessary hard inquiries as harmful. → *Expect:* you know which items affect creditworthiness.
9. **Dispute errors.** [BRANCH: bureau error | creditor error] submit a bureau dispute for wrong report data; contact the creditor or collector when the source is reporting bad information. → *Expect:* you receive a confirmation number or written dispute record.
10. **Set a review frequency.** Schedule periodic checks, commonly at least annually and more often after identity theft, denied credit, major loans, or disputes. → *Expect:* the next review date is on your calendar.

## Decision points

- You cannot pass online identity verification → request the report by mail or phone using official instructions.
- You find an account you did not open → treat it as possible identity theft and consider a fraud alert or credit freeze.
- A negative item is accurate but old → check whether it should still legally appear before disputing.
- You are applying for a mortgage or large loan soon → avoid new credit applications unless necessary.

## Failure modes & recovery

- **F1 Paid lookalike site:** detect requests for a subscription or trial, recover by leaving and using the official free report source.
- **F2 Locked report access:** detect failed identity questions or technical denial, recover by following the bureau's mail or phone verification path.
- **F3 Wrong account data:** detect incorrect balance, limit, status, owner, or late payment, recover by disputing with evidence.
- **F4 Identity theft signal:** detect unknown accounts or inquiries, recover by reporting identity theft and placing protective alerts or freezes.
- **F5 Dispute lost:** detect no confirmation or no response by the expected date, recover by resubmitting with copies and tracking numbers.

## Verification

Each available report has been saved, personal data and account sections have been reviewed, errors are either absent or disputed, and the next review date is scheduled.

## Variations

- Jurisdiction: free report frequency, bureau names, dispute deadlines, and consumer rights vary by country and region.
- Thin credit file: fewer accounts may appear, so verify identity data and inquiries especially carefully.
- Joint or authorized-user accounts: confirm whether the ownership type is correct.
- Post-fraud review: check all bureaus, not only the one that first showed the problem.

## Safety & privacy

Medium risk because the report contains identity, address, account, and debt history. Use a private network, avoid saving reports on shared devices, and do not enter personal data into unofficial credit sites.
