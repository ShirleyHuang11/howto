## Goal

Understand annual percentage rate well enough to compare borrowing costs, avoid cash advance surprises, and see why minimum payments can keep debt expensive.

## Preconditions

- You have the credit agreement, card terms, loan estimate, statement, or offer.
- You know the amount borrowed, fees, payment schedule, and whether the rate can change.
- You can use a calculator or spreadsheet for rough payment comparisons.
- You will seek qualified advice before taking on debt you cannot clearly afford.

## Steps

1. **Identify the product.** Determine whether the APR is for a credit card, personal loan, auto loan, mortgage, overdraft, or promotional offer. → *Expect:* the APR is interpreted in the right product context.
2. **Find what APR includes.** Read whether origination fees, broker fees, annual fees, points, or compulsory charges are included. → *Expect:* you know whether APR is broader than the simple interest rate.
3. **Separate APR types.** Check purchase APR, cash advance APR, balance transfer APR, penalty APR, variable APR, and promotional APR. → *Expect:* each transaction type has its own rate if applicable.
4. **Check the grace period.** For cards, find when purchases avoid interest and what cancels the grace period. → *Expect:* you know whether paying the statement balance by the due date avoids purchase interest.
5. **Read promotional terms.** Note start date, end date, transfer fee, deferred-interest rule, and what happens after the promo. → *Expect:* the date and balance that must be paid before the promo ends are clear.
6. **Estimate periodic interest.** Divide APR by the number of billing periods as an approximation, then compare with the issuer's stated daily or monthly method. → *Expect:* the interest calculation is understandable enough to check a statement.
7. **Test minimum-payment math.** Use the statement warning or calculator to compare paying minimum only, a fixed higher amount, and full balance. → *Expect:* payoff time and interest cost differences are visible.
8. **Compare offers using total cost.** Compare APR, fees, term length, monthly payment, and flexibility together. → *Expect:* the cheaper-looking payment is not confused with the cheaper loan.
9. **Pause before accepting unaffordable terms.** ⚠️ *Irreversible:* before signing or drawing funds, confirm the payment fits your budget and the key rate terms are written. → *Expect:* acceptance happens only after cost and obligation are understood.

## Decision points

- APR is variable → check the benchmark, margin, adjustment frequency, and maximum rate where disclosed.
- A cash advance is tempting → treat it as high cost because interest often starts immediately and fees apply.
- A promo is deferred interest → pay the full promo balance before expiration or avoid the offer.
- The debt is large or secured → get qualified financial, legal, or housing advice before committing.

## Failure modes & recovery

- **F1 Confusing APR with payment:** detect by choosing the lowest monthly payment despite a longer term, recover by comparing total repayment.
- **F2 Grace period lost:** detect by interest on new purchases, recover by paying the full balance and confirming when grace returns.
- **F3 Cash advance shock:** detect by immediate interest and fee, recover by paying that balance quickly and avoiding repeats.
- **F4 Promo cliff:** detect by promo expiration notice, recover by paying off or refinancing before the higher APR starts.

## Verification

The APR types, included fees, grace period rule, promotional expiration, and estimated cost under minimum versus higher payments are documented.

## Variations

- `us`: APR disclosure rules differ for credit cards, mortgages, auto loans, and payday or small-dollar loans by federal and state law.
- `uk`: representative APR and total amount payable rules apply, and overdraft pricing uses standardized annual rates.
- `eu`: annual percentage rate of charge and consumer credit disclosures are implemented through national rules.

## Safety & privacy

Medium risk because misunderstanding APR can make debt unaffordable. This recipe explains terms and comparisons, not whether a specific loan is suitable for you.
