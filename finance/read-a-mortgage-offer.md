## Goal

Read a mortgage offer well enough to identify the loan type, rate, fees, repayment obligations, and major risks before committing.

## Preconditions

- You have the full written mortgage offer, loan estimate, key facts document, or binding offer.
- You know the property price, deposit or down payment, expected closing date, and planned holding period.
- You can ask the lender, broker, solicitor, conveyancer, attorney, or housing counselor questions.
- You understand this recipe does not replace regulated mortgage or legal advice.

## Steps

1. **Confirm borrower and property details.** Check names, property address, loan amount, currency, occupancy type, and offer expiry. → *Expect:* the offer matches the intended transaction.
2. **Identify rate and type.** Note fixed, variable, tracker, adjustable, interest-only, repayment, offset, or balloon features. → *Expect:* the interest structure and repayment model are clear.
3. **Read the term and payment.** Check loan term, first payment date, monthly payment, escrow or tax amounts, and payment changes. → *Expect:* the regular payment obligation is stated.
4. **List upfront fees.** Record arrangement, origination, valuation, appraisal, legal, broker, title, recording, and closing fees. → *Expect:* cash needed before or at completion is visible.
5. **Check early-repayment charges.** Find prepayment penalties, early-repayment charges, lock-in periods, overpayment limits, and exit fees. → *Expect:* the cost of refinancing, selling, or overpaying is known.
6. **Compare total cost.** Review APR, APRC, total amount payable, total interest, and assumptions used. → *Expect:* the long-term cost is visible beyond the monthly payment.
7. **Review conditions.** Note required insurance, appraisal, survey, income verification, repairs, documents, or final credit checks. → *Expect:* unmet conditions are listed with owners and deadlines.
8. **Ask advice before signing.** Consult a qualified mortgage adviser, housing counselor, solicitor, conveyancer, or attorney for anything unclear. → *Expect:* material questions are answered in writing.
9. **Accept only when ready.** ⚠️ *Irreversible:* before signing or completing, confirm affordability, conditions, fees, repayment risk, and legal obligations. → *Expect:* acceptance reflects an informed commitment.

## Decision points

- Rate can change → model higher payments and ask how often the rate adjusts.
- Interest-only is offered → confirm the credible repayment plan for the principal.
- You may sell or refinance soon → early-repayment charges can dominate the decision.
- The offer conflicts with advice or disclosure → pause and get the discrepancy corrected in writing.

## Failure modes & recovery

- **F1 Payment shock:** detect by variable or reset terms, recover by modelling stressed payments before accepting.
- **F2 Fee surprise:** detect by cash-to-close or completion amount higher than expected, recover by requesting an updated itemized disclosure.
- **F3 Condition missed:** detect by lender not clearing conditions, recover by supplying documents or renegotiating timelines.
- **F4 Penalty overlooked:** detect by early-repayment charge after a sale or refinance plan, recover by comparing another product before commitment.

## Verification

The offer's rate type, term, payment, fees, early-repayment terms, total cost, conditions, expiry, and advice questions are documented before acceptance.

## Variations

- `us`: compare Loan Estimate and Closing Disclosure, APR, points, escrow, adjustable-rate caps, and state closing rules.
- `uk`: compare mortgage illustration, APRC, ERCs, valuation, solicitor role, and Financial Conduct Authority regulated advice.
- `eu`: ESIS disclosures, cooling-off periods, notary roles, and early-repayment rules differ by country.

## Safety & privacy

High risk because a mortgage is a large secured debt and default can lead to foreclosure or repossession. Get qualified professional advice before committing or changing course.
