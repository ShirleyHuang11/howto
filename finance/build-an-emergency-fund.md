## Goal

You have a separate account holding a target number of months of essential expenses, fed by an automatic transfer, kept apart from daily spending, and used only for genuine emergencies.

## Preconditions

- A budget or at least a known monthly essentials figure (rent, food, utilities, minimum debt payments, transport). See `finance/create-a-simple-budget`.
- The ability to open a second savings account, ideally at a different bank or in a distinct product so it is not one tap away.
- Any high-interest debt already being addressed; a small starter fund first, then debt, then the full fund, is the usual order.

## Steps

1. **Compute one month of essentials.** Sum only what you must pay to keep living: housing, food, utilities, insurance, minimum debt, transport. Exclude discretionary spending. → *Expect:* a monthly essentials number smaller than your total spending.
2. **Set the target in months.** Stable single income, few dependents: 3 months. Variable income, dependents, or sole earner: 6 months or more. Multiply essentials by the chosen count. → *Expect:* a target fund size with an explicit rationale.
3. **Set a starter milestone first.** A full fund is daunting; aim for one small round figure (one month, or a fixed starter amount) as the first goal so momentum is visible. → *Expect:* a near-term milestone you can reach in weeks, not years.
4. **Open a separate account for it.** A distinct high-yield savings account, not a sub-label inside your checking account. Friction is the feature: it should take a day, not a tap, to reach. → *Expect:* a new account with a zero or starter balance, kept off your everyday card.
5. **Automate the transfer on payday.** Schedule a standing transfer for the day after income lands, so the money leaves before you can spend it. Start with an amount you will not miss and raise it later. → *Expect:* a recurring transfer confirmed; the first one executes next payday.
6. **Let it grow untouched and review quarterly.** Do not check it daily or move it into anything illiquid. Every quarter, recompute essentials (rent rises) and nudge the transfer up. → *Expect:* balance climbing each month; target adjusted for cost-of-living changes.
7. **Define what counts as an emergency, in writing.** Job loss, urgent medical bills, essential home or car repair. A sale is not an emergency. Writing the rule now prevents rationalizing later. → *Expect:* a short written list of qualifying uses.
8. **Refill after any use.** ⚠️ *Irreversible:* spending the fund is the point, but treat replenishment as the next priority: restart or raise the transfer until it is back to target. → *Expect:* after a withdrawal, the automatic transfer resumes toward the target.

## Decision points

- High-interest debt (credit cards) outstanding → build only a small starter fund, then throw surplus at the debt, then return to the full fund; the interest saved beats the interest earned.
- Tempted to invest the fund for higher returns → do not; an emergency fund's job is instant, no-loss access, not growth. Keep it in cash-equivalent savings.
- Income is irregular → size the fund larger and base essentials on a lean month, since the fund is exactly what smooths the lean ones.

## Failure modes & recovery

- **F1 Fund keeps getting raided for non-emergencies:** the account is too accessible. Move it to a different bank, remove it from your everyday app, and re-read your written emergency rule.
- **F2 Transfer bounces or overdraws checking:** the amount is too high for your cash flow; lower it to a level that survives your worst week and raise it gradually.
- **F3 Fund never grows because it is discretionary:** you are transferring what is left over, which is nothing. Automate it on payday so it is first, not last.
- **F4 Inflation quietly shrinks the real target:** essentials rose but the target did not; the quarterly recompute in step 6 exists to catch this.

## Verification

A separate account holds at least your starter milestone (and is climbing toward the month-based target), an automatic payday transfer is active, and you have a written definition of what the fund may be used for.

## Variations

- `us`: a high-yield savings account or money market account keeps the cash accessible while earning interest.
- Households: hold the fund jointly or split across partners' accounts, but agree the emergency rule together so one person cannot drain it unilaterally.

## Safety & privacy

Low risk. The main hazard is behavioral, not security: keep the fund liquid and separate so it survives both market dips and impulse. Use a distinct strong password if it is at a new institution, and confirm the account is insured under your jurisdiction's deposit scheme.
