## Goal

You board the correct ferry with the right ticket class (foot passenger or vehicle), ride a comfortable stretch of the crossing, and disembark at your destination port with your belongings and, if applicable, your car.

## Preconditions

- A ticket or the means to buy one, matched to whether you are a foot passenger or bringing a vehicle (these are different fares and different boarding lanes).
- Arrival at the terminal with margin: foot passengers 30 to 45 minutes early, vehicles 45 to 90 minutes early, because vehicle loading closes well before departure.
- Knowledge of your destination port, since some routes serve multiple stops and boarding the wrong sailing is easy.

## Steps

1. **Confirm the sailing and buy or collect the ticket.** Match the departure time, destination, and passenger-versus-vehicle class on the board or app. → *Expect:* a ticket (paper or mobile) showing today's date, this port, and the correct class.
2. **Go to the correct queue.** Vehicles line up in numbered marshalling lanes by destination; foot passengers use a separate terminal entrance. Do not join the car lanes on foot. → *Expect:* you are in a lane whose sign matches your ticket class and destination.
3. **Clear the ticket and any security check.** Scan or show the ticket; some international or high-security routes check ID and scan bags. → *Expect:* a green light, gate open, or a wave-through from staff.
4. **Board when your class is called.** Vehicles drive on in lane order at crew direction; foot passengers walk the gangway or ramp. Note your deck and, for cars, your exact parking spot and lane. ⚠️ *Irreversible for this sailing:* once vehicle loading closes it does not reopen, and a missed vehicle boarding usually forfeits the fare. → *Expect:* you are aboard, engine off and in gear or parked handbrake-on for a vehicle, on the gangway for foot passengers.
5. **Note where you left the car and lock it.** On vehicle decks you cannot usually return mid-crossing, so take everything you need up to the passenger decks. → *Expect:* car locked, valuables and jacket with you, deck and lane number remembered or photographed.
6. **Choose a seat for the conditions.** For seasickness, sit low and central near the waterline where motion is least, facing forward, near a window with a view of the horizon. Avoid the top decks and the bow in rough weather. → *Expect:* a stable-feeling seat with horizon visibility; less pitching than the extremities.
7. **Watch for the arrival announcement and return to your vehicle only when told.** Crew announce when drivers may go down to the car decks. → *Expect:* the announcement, then an orderly move to your deck; foot passengers gather near the exit.
8. **Disembark at your port.** Vehicles drive off in crew-directed order; foot passengers follow the gangway. Confirm the port name before stepping off a multi-stop route. → *Expect:* you are ashore at your intended destination with all belongings and, if applicable, your car.

## Decision points

- Prone to seasickness and forecast is rough: take a non-drowsy remedy 30 to 60 minutes before boarding, secure a low central seat, and keep your eyes on the horizon rather than a screen.
- Multi-stop route: verify the destination at every port call, because passengers do occasionally disembark a stop too early.
- Tight vehicle-loading cutoff and heavy queue: if you may miss the marshalling deadline, ask staff at once; they sometimes reassign you to the next sailing without penalty.

## Failure modes & recovery

- **F1 Missed the vehicle loading cutoff:** report to the ticket desk immediately. Many operators rebook vehicles onto the next sailing, sometimes free, if you were in the terminal on time.
- **F2 Seasickness sets in:** move to a low central seat, get cool air on the open lower deck if safe, fix your gaze on the horizon, and sip water. Cabin air and screens worsen it.
- **F3 Cannot find your car on a huge vehicle deck:** the photo of your deck and lane number solves it; otherwise ask crew, who know the deck layout.
- **F4 Boarded the wrong sailing:** tell crew before departure if you can. Once underway you ride to the wrong port and must return; a quick word at boarding prevents it.

## Verification

You disembarked at the intended port with all your belongings, your vehicle (if any) drove off with you, and no fare was forfeited to a missed loading window.

## Variations

- `eu` and island routes often sell reclining seats, cabins, and lounge classes for longer overnight crossings; short urban commuter ferries are single-class, tap-in like a bus, and board in minutes.
- Reservation-required car ferries in peak season sell out; walk-on foot fares are usually available same-day even when vehicle space is gone.

## Safety & privacy

Low risk overall. The real hazards are slips on wet decks and stairs (hold the rail) and, rarely, rough-weather motion. Follow crew instructions and the safety briefing, note the muster station, and keep children within reach near open decks and railings.
