## Goal

Ride in a bike lane predictably while managing doors, junctions, turning vehicles, pedestrians, and other riders.

## Preconditions

- Bike brakes, tires, lights, and bell or voice are working.
- Helmet and visible clothing or lights are ready for conditions.
- You understand local traffic direction and basic right-of-way rules.

## Steps

1. **Enter in the legal direction.** Ride with traffic unless the lane is explicitly marked two-way. → *Expect:* signs, arrows, or lane markings match your direction.
2. **Scan before merging in.** Check behind, signal, and merge into the bike lane when there is a safe gap. → *Expect:* nearby riders and drivers can predict your move.
3. **Stay out of the door zone.** Keep distance from parked cars, especially where doors can open into the lane. → *Expect:* you have room to react if a door opens.
4. **Signal turns and lane exits.** Use hand signals early, then return hands to brakes before turning or slowing. → *Expect:* other road users see your intention before you move.
5. **Slow at junctions.** Watch for right-turning or left-turning vehicles crossing the bike lane, and make eye contact only as a bonus, not a guarantee. → *Expect:* you can stop if a driver turns across you.
6. **Yield where required.** [BRANCH: pedestrians | buses | turning vehicles] obey signs, signals, and crosswalk rules even when the lane continues visually. → *Expect:* conflicts are resolved by law and caution, not speed.
7. **Pass other riders carefully.** Call "passing left" or ring once, pass with space, and return smoothly. → *Expect:* the other rider does not swerve from surprise.
8. **Be visible in low light.** Use front white and rear red lights, avoid blind spots, and do not ride beside large vehicles at intersections. → *Expect:* drivers and pedestrians have a better chance to detect you.
9. **Leave the lane when it is unsafe.** Merge into the traffic lane if legally allowed and necessary to avoid debris, doors, construction, or blocked bike lanes. → *Expect:* you avoid hazards without sudden swerves.

## Decision points

- Protected lane ends at an intersection → slow, scan, and merge or wait according to markings.
- Parked cars line the lane → ride far enough left to avoid doors, even if that narrows the lane.
- Vehicle blocks the lane → merge after mirror or shoulder check, or stop and wait if the gap is unsafe.
- Pedestrians step into the lane → slow and yield; a bell is a warning, not permission to force through.

## Failure modes & recovery

- **F1 Door opens ahead:** a parked car door enters your path → brake firmly, steer away only if clear, and stop if needed.
- **F2 Right hook risk:** a driver turns across the lane → slow before the junction and yield if the driver is not clearly stopping.
- **F3 Wrong-way rider:** someone approaches head-on → slow, hold your line where safe, and avoid sudden swerves into traffic.
- **F4 Debris or pothole:** the lane surface is unsafe → signal, merge out when clear, then re-enter after the hazard.
- **F5 Low visibility:** drivers are not noticing you → add lights, choose calmer streets, or stop riding until conditions improve.

## Verification

You complete the bike-lane segment in the correct direction with predictable signals, safe spacing from doors, and controlled behavior at every junction.

## Variations

- Two-way protected lanes: check both directions at driveways and crossings because drivers may look only one way.
- Bus-bike lanes: expect buses to merge and stop frequently, and never pass a bus on the side where passengers board.
- Shared paths: reduce speed and yield to pedestrians.
- Winter or rain: painted lanes become slippery, so brake earlier and avoid metal plates.

## Safety & privacy

High risk because crashes can cause serious injury. Obey signals, stay sober, use lights, avoid headphones that block traffic sound, and do not let route apps distract you at intersections.
