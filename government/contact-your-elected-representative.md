## Goal

Contact the correct elected representative with a concise, specific request and create a record for follow-up.

## Preconditions

- You know the address where you are represented or the district affected by the issue.
- You can describe the issue, bill, agency problem, or local concern in plain language.
- You have contact information you are comfortable sharing with a public office.
- You can use at least one channel such as phone, email, letter, web form, or town hall.

## Steps

1. **Find who represents you.** Use an official election, legislature, parliament, council, or district lookup tool and enter your address if required. → *Expect:* you have the representative's name, office, district, and official contact page.
2. **Choose the right level of government.** Match the issue to local, regional, national, school, or special-district authority. → *Expect:* you are contacting an office that can act on the matter.
3. **Choose a channel.** [BRANCH: call | email or web form | letter | town hall] use a call for timely issues, written contact for detail, a letter for formal records, or a town hall for public questions. → *Expect:* the channel fits the urgency and complexity.
4. **State your relationship to the district.** Give your name, city or neighborhood, and whether you are a constituent, business owner, student, worker, or affected person. → *Expect:* the office can route and count your message.
5. **Be concise and specific.** Name the bill, policy, service request, agency case, budget item, or local problem, then state the action you want. → *Expect:* the staff can summarize your request in one sentence.
6. **Add relevant facts.** Include dates, locations, case numbers, personal impact, and evidence links only when they support the request. → *Expect:* the message is persuasive without becoming hard to process.
7. **Ask for a response or action.** Request a vote position, casework help, meeting, status update, or explanation of next steps. → *Expect:* the office knows what follow-up you expect.
8. **Send or deliver the message.** Confirm spelling, contact details, attachment privacy, and office destination before submitting. → *Expect:* you receive a sent email, form confirmation, call note, postal receipt, or meeting record.
9. **Record the follow-up date.** Save the message, staff name if known, confirmation number, and a date to follow up. → *Expect:* you have a usable contact log.
10. **Follow up if needed.** If the office does not respond within a reasonable time, contact again with the prior date and confirmation details. → *Expect:* the second contact builds on the first instead of starting over.

## Decision points

- The issue is urgent public safety → contact emergency or service agencies first, then the representative if policy or oversight is needed.
- You are not a constituent → contact your own representative, or explain why this office still has authority over the issue.
- The issue involves personal casework → use the office's privacy release process before sharing sensitive documents.
- You want to speak at a town hall → check rules for registration, time limits, and public comment order.

## Failure modes & recovery

- **F1 Wrong representative:** detect a response saying you are outside the district or issue area, recover by using an official district lookup and redirecting the message.
- **F2 Message too broad:** detect no clear action request, recover by rewriting it around one bill, vote, service issue, or question.
- **F3 No confirmation:** detect a web form that closes without receipt or email copy, recover by saving screenshots or resending through another official channel.
- **F4 Sensitive data overshared:** detect identity numbers, medical records, or private addresses in a general message, recover by asking for the office's secure casework process.
- **F5 No response:** detect no reply by the follow-up date, recover by calling the office and referencing the earlier message.

## Verification

The correct representative and government level are identified, a concise request has been sent through an appropriate channel, and a follow-up record exists.

## Variations

- Jurisdiction: district lookup tools, office titles, legislative procedures, public comment rules, and response expectations vary by country and local government.
- Casework request: representatives may help with agency problems but usually cannot override courts or private companies.
- Group campaign: personalize the message so it is not only a copied script.
- Town hall: spoken comments often need a shorter version than an email or letter.

## Safety & privacy

Low risk for ordinary policy messages, but casework can involve identity, immigration, tax, health, or benefits information. Share sensitive documents only through the office's official secure process.
