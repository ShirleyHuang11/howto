## Goal

File a noise complaint through the correct channel with enough detail for the city, landlord, or non-emergency responder to act.

## Preconditions

- Current address or location of the noise.
- Approximate source, such as party, construction, vehicle, business, dog, or equipment.
- Dates and times the noise occurred.
- Local quiet hours or noise ordinance if available.
- A safe place to document the issue without confrontation.

## Steps

1. **Document the noise pattern.** Write down start time, stop time, address or landmark, type of sound, and how it affects you. → *Expect:* you have a dated log with at least one specific incident.
2. **Check immediate danger.** If there is violence, threats, injury, fire, or suspected emergency, call emergency services instead. → *Expect:* the situation is classified as emergency or non-emergency.
3. **Identify the right channel.** [BRANCH: non-emergency line | city service portal | landlord or building manager | council or code enforcement] Match the source to the listed enforcement channel. → *Expect:* you have the correct phone number, portal, or email.
4. **Gather evidence carefully.** Record short audio or video only where lawful, capture decibel app readings as rough context, and keep witness notes. → *Expect:* evidence shows date, time, and location without trespassing.
5. **Make the complaint.** Provide location, current status, duration, repeat history, and whether you want contact. → *Expect:* you receive a service request number, call log number, or written acknowledgment.
6. **Avoid direct escalation during the incident.** Do not bang on doors or confront people if intoxication, anger, or crowds are involved. → *Expect:* you remain physically separated from the source.
7. **Track the response.** Save the case number and note whether an officer, inspector, or manager responded. → *Expect:* your log includes response time and outcome.
8. **Escalate repeated violations.** If the same source continues, attach the prior case numbers and request code enforcement or council review. → *Expect:* the agency sees the pattern rather than a single isolated complaint.
9. **Close the loop.** If the noise stops, mark the incident resolved or tell the landlord/agency. → *Expect:* the record reflects the current condition.

## Decision points

- Noise is happening right now and violates quiet hours → use the non-emergency line if local rules allow dispatch.
- Noise is recurring but not active → use code enforcement, city service portal, landlord, HOA, or council contact.
- Construction has a posted permit → check allowed hours before filing.
- The source is a business → licensing or environmental health may handle repeated complaints.
- You fear retaliation → ask whether anonymous complaints are accepted and avoid personal confrontation.

## Failure modes & recovery

- **F1 Wrong office:** detect a referral or closed request; recover by asking which department enforces that noise source.
- **F2 No case number:** detect a verbal complaint with no record; recover by submitting through a written portal or asking dispatch for the call number.
- **F3 Evidence unusable:** detect recordings without date, location, or context; recover by keeping a written log and lawful short clips.
- **F4 Retaliation risk:** detect threats or harassment after reporting; recover by documenting it separately and contacting law enforcement or property management.
- **F5 No enforcement:** detect repeated closures with continued noise; recover by sending a timeline and case numbers to a supervisor, elected representative, or tenant authority.

## Verification

You have a complaint record number or written acknowledgment that names the noise location, date, time, and responsible handling channel.

## Variations

- `apartment`: lease quiet-hours rules may be easier to enforce than city ordinances.
- `construction`: permitted daytime work may be legal even if annoying.
- `short-term-rental`: platforms or local rental enforcement teams may require listing details.
- `vehicle-noise`: police may need the vehicle plate, location, and direction of travel.
- `rural-area`: county sheriff or zoning office may replace city code enforcement.

## Safety & privacy

Do not trespass, film inside private homes, or put yourself into a confrontation. Complaint records may become public, so ask about anonymity before including sensitive personal details.
