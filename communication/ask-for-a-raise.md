## Goal

Ask for a raise with a documented business case, market context, clear number, and professional response to the answer.

## Preconditions

- You know your current compensation, role scope, and recent performance evidence.
- You have access to credible market salary data for similar roles and location.
- You can book a meeting with your manager or compensation decision maker.

## Steps

1. **Collect accomplishments.** List outcomes from the last 6 to 12 months, including revenue, savings, quality, speed, risk reduction, or leadership. → *Expect:* evidence focuses on results, not effort alone.
2. **Map work to role scope.** Show how your responsibilities match or exceed the current role expectations. → *Expect:* the case explains why pay should change now.
3. **Gather market data.** Use several sources for comparable title, level, location, industry, and company size. → *Expect:* you have a realistic pay range, not one outlier.
4. **Choose the ask.** Pick a specific salary, hourly rate, or percentage increase and know the minimum acceptable outcome. → *Expect:* the request is clear enough to approve or counter.
5. **Book the meeting.** Ask for time to discuss compensation and growth, rather than surprising your manager in a busy hallway. → *Expect:* a focused conversation is scheduled.
6. **Present the case briefly.** Explain role growth, outcomes, market range, and the specific ask in a calm sequence. → *Expect:* the manager understands the business reason.
7. **Listen to constraints.** [BRANCH: yes | no | later] ask about process, budget cycle, criteria, and timeline. → *Expect:* the next step is explicit.
8. **Confirm in writing.** Send a short recap with the ask, evidence summary, and agreed timeline or decision. → *Expect:* there is a written record.
9. **Handle the answer.** If approved, confirm effective date; if delayed or denied, ask what measurable changes would reopen the discussion. → *Expect:* you leave with decision, date, or criteria.

## Decision points

- Company has fixed review cycles → time the ask before budgets lock.
- Manager cannot approve pay → ask who decides and what information they need.
- Market data is weak → emphasize documented scope growth and internal impact.
- You would leave without a raise → decide privately before the meeting, not during it.

## Failure modes & recovery

- **F1 Vague ask:** detect "I was hoping for more," recover by naming a number or range.
- **F2 Evidence too personal:** detect arguments about bills or fairness only, recover by returning to business impact.
- **F3 Surprise timing:** detect manager is unprepared or rushed, recover by scheduling a dedicated follow-up.
- **F4 No timeline:** detect "we will see," recover by asking for a date and decision path.
- **F5 Defensive reaction:** detect tension rising, recover by pausing and asking what information would be useful.

## Verification

You made a specific compensation request supported by documented impact and market data, then saved the decision, timeline, or criteria in writing.

## Variations

- Hourly role: focus on rate, hours, duties, reliability, and comparable local listings.
- Promotion case: separate title change from pay change, but connect both to expanded scope.
- Remote role: compare against the company's stated location policy.
- Union or civil service role: follow the formal pay step or classification process.

## Safety & privacy

Medium career risk. Do not falsify offers or market data, and avoid sharing coworkers' pay unless they consent and local rules protect that discussion.
