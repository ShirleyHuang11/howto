## Goal

Write a LinkedIn connection note that gives the recipient a clear reason to accept, feels human, and does not immediately demand time, referrals, or favors.

## Preconditions

- A LinkedIn account and the recipient's profile open.
- A real reason for connecting: shared event, mutual contact, admired work, same field, alumni tie, or recent conversation.
- Enough context to write one specific hook honestly.

## Steps

1. **Identify why this person.** Read their headline, recent work, shared groups, mutual contacts, or where you met. → *Expect:* one specific reason is available without pretending familiarity.
2. **Choose the hook.** Use the most natural connection: "I enjoyed your panel," "We both worked with Sam," "Your post on clinical operations was useful." → *Expect:* the note could not be sent unchanged to 50 people.
3. **Keep it short.** Aim for 2 or 3 sentences and about 250 characters if the interface limits notes. → *Expect:* the recipient can understand it in one glance.
4. **Name why you, lightly.** Include your relevant role or context only if it helps: "I work on hospital analytics" or "I'm a recent alum exploring policy roles." → *Expect:* they know where you fit without reading your whole profile.
5. **Avoid the immediate ask.** Do not request a referral, job, meeting, pitch review, or customer intro in the connection note. [BRANCH: met before | cold note] mention the meeting if you met; use admiration plus relevance if cold. → *Expect:* accepting does not feel like agreeing to work.
6. **Write the final note.** Example: "Hi Maya, I appreciated your comments on patient intake at the HIMSS panel. I work on clinic workflow tools and would enjoy following your work here." → *Expect:* the note is specific, brief, and low-pressure.
7. **Review tone before sending.** Remove flattery that sounds inflated, generic "synergy" language, and any false closeness. ⚠️ *Irreversible:* once sent, a connection note cannot usually be edited or unsent. → *Expect:* the final text is professional and true.
8. **Send and wait.** If they accept, do not immediately pile on a large request; let the connection breathe or send a separate thoughtful follow-up later. → *Expect:* the relationship starts with context, not extraction.

## Decision points

- If you have no specific hook → do not send yet; find a better reason or wait.
- If you need something soon → send a separate message after connection, still short and respectful.
- If the person is very senior → make the note even clearer and lower demand.
- If you met at an event → send within a few days while memory is fresh.

## Failure modes & recovery

- **F1 Note sounds generic:** detect phrases like "I'd like to add you to my network" alone → add the specific source, topic, or mutual context.
- **F2 Note asks too much:** detect a meeting, referral, or favor request → move that to a later message after acceptance.
- **F3 Hook is inaccurate:** detect uncertainty about their work or title → verify from the profile or avoid the claim.
- **F4 No response:** detect no acceptance after a few weeks → do nothing unless you have a new, legitimate reason to follow up elsewhere.

## Verification

The note is under the platform limit, includes one specific hook, identifies your relevant context, and contains no immediate favor request.

## Variations

- Recruiter connection: mention the role or field they recruit for, not a vague job hunt.
- Alumni connection: name the school, program, or professor only if true.
- Post author: refer to the actual topic of the post and what you found useful.

## Safety & privacy

Low risk, but professional identity is public. Do not imply relationships, credentials, employment, or event attendance that are not true, and avoid sharing confidential job-search details in a connection note.
