## Goal

A complaint letter that states the facts, asks for a specific remedy, and creates a clear record without sounding careless or hostile.

## Preconditions

- You know who should receive the complaint and whether a portal, email, or postal address is required.
- You have dates, order numbers, names, photos, receipts, policies, or prior messages.
- You can state what outcome you want, such as refund, repair, apology, replacement, correction, or investigation.

## Steps

1. **Gather the record first.** Put dates, amounts, reference numbers, names, and evidence in one place. → *Expect:* the complaint rests on checkable facts.
2. **Address the right office.** Use customer relations, landlord, school office, regulator, or manager as appropriate. → *Expect:* the letter reaches someone with authority to act.
3. **Open with the issue and desired outcome.** State what happened and what you want in the first paragraph. → *Expect:* the reader knows the purpose immediately.
4. **Lay out facts in order.** Use short paragraphs or bullets with dates, what was promised, what happened, and what you already tried. → *Expect:* the timeline is easy to follow.
5. **Stay calm and firm.** Remove insults, sarcasm, threats you cannot follow through on, and broad claims you cannot prove. → *Expect:* the letter sounds credible under review.
6. **Name the remedy and deadline.** [BRANCH: simple fix | formal escalation] Ask for the specific fix and a reasonable response date; if rights or money are involved, cite the policy or law you are relying on. → *Expect:* the recipient knows what action would resolve it.
7. **Include the escalation path.** Say what you will do next if there is no response, such as contacting a regulator, card issuer, tenant board, or supervisor. → *Expect:* escalation is clear without sounding reckless.
8. **Check before sending.** Attach evidence, save a copy, and verify the address. ⚠️ *Irreversible:* sending false claims or private information to the wrong party can cause real harm, so confirm every factual allegation. → *Expect:* the sent letter is accurate and preserved.

## Decision points

- If the complaint involves legal rights, use precise facts and consider professional advice.
- If you mainly want an apology, still say what response would satisfy you.
- If the matter is urgent, call or use the emergency channel, then document in writing.
- If you are angry, draft hot, send cool.
- If multiple people are affected, decide whether one shared letter or separate letters are stronger.

## Failure modes & recovery

- **F1 Too emotional:** detect it when adjectives outweigh facts → revise to timeline, evidence, and remedy.
- **F2 Vague remedy:** detect it when "make this right" is the only ask → specify the action, amount, or deadline.
- **F3 Wrong recipient:** detect it when replies say they cannot help → ask for the correct escalation contact and forward the record.
- **F4 No response:** detect it after the deadline passes → send one follow-up, then use the escalation path you named.

## Verification

The letter includes facts and dates, a specific remedy, a response deadline, evidence, escalation path, and a saved copy.

## Variations

- Consumer purchase: include order number, receipt, warranty, and photos.
- Housing complaint: include lease terms, repair history, and access times.
- School or workplace complaint: use policy language and keep tone especially factual.

## Safety & privacy

Medium risk because complaints can affect money, housing, work, or legal records. Do not exaggerate, publish private details unnecessarily, or threaten action you will not take.
