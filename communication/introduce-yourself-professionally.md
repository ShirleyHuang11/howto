## Goal

Prepare and deliver a professional self-introduction that states your name, role, value, context, and handoff in about 20 seconds.

## Preconditions

- You know the setting, such as meeting, interview, conference, class, or client call.
- You know what the other person likely needs from the introduction.
- You can say your current role or relevant background plainly.

## Steps

1. **Identify the context.** Decide whether the introduction is for networking, a meeting, an interview, or a new team. → *Expect:* the tone and detail level are clear.
2. **Write the name-role-value line.** Combine your name, role or background, and the useful thing you bring. → *Expect:* one sentence explains who you are.
3. **Add one context detail.** Mention the project, team, problem, or reason you are in the conversation. → *Expect:* the listener understands why you are relevant now.
4. **Keep it to 20 seconds.** Aim for 3 or 4 sentences, roughly 40 to 60 spoken words. → *Expect:* it feels complete but not like a speech.
5. **Tailor the wording.** [BRANCH: formal | casual | interview] adjust title, detail, and warmth to the setting. → *Expect:* the introduction fits the room.
6. **Prepare the handoff.** End with a question, next person cue, or reason you are looking forward to the discussion. → *Expect:* conversation can continue naturally.
7. **Practice aloud twice.** Listen for jargon, rambling, or details that require too much explanation. → *Expect:* the wording sounds spoken, not memorized text.
8. **Deliver with basics.** Say your name clearly, make brief eye contact or camera contact, and stop after the handoff. → *Expect:* the listener can repeat your name and role.
9. **Adapt after response.** If they ask a follow-up, expand with one relevant example rather than restarting the introduction. → *Expect:* the conversation moves forward.

## Decision points

- Audience knows your field → use role-specific terms lightly.
- Audience is outside your field → translate your work into outcomes and plain language.
- You are unemployed or changing careers → use background and target direction without apologizing.
- Group introductions are timed → cut to name, role, and one relevant sentence.

## Failure modes & recovery

- **F1 Too long:** detect listeners shifting or timer pressure, recover by ending with the handoff.
- **F2 Too vague:** detect that your role could describe anyone, recover by adding one concrete value or focus area.
- **F3 Jargon overload:** detect acronyms needing explanation, recover by using plain outcome language.
- **F4 Weak handoff:** detect awkward silence, recover by asking a simple context question.
- **F5 Name missed:** detect mispronunciation or confusion, recover by repeating it calmly once.

## Verification

Your introduction can be spoken in about 20 seconds and includes name, role or background, value, tailored context, and a clear handoff.

## Variations

- Interview: connect role fit and one strength, then stop.
- Conference: include what you are working on and what conversation you welcome.
- New team: mention responsibility and how people should work with you.
- Client call: emphasize the part of the project you own.

## Safety & privacy

Low risk. Avoid oversharing personal history, confidential employer details, salary information, or private client names unless the setting requires it.
