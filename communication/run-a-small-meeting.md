## Goal

A meeting of roughly 3 to 8 people runs against a written agenda, every item reaches a recorded decision or a named owner, and it ends on time or early with a shared record of who does what next.

## Preconditions

- A scheduled meeting with the right people and a stated purpose (not "sync" but a real question to resolve).
- An agenda drafted in advance: 2 to 5 items, each phrased as a decision to make or a thing to produce, with a rough minute budget.
- A place to capture notes live (shared doc, whiteboard, or a notepad) visible to at least you.

## Steps

1. **Send the agenda before the meeting, not during.** Paste it into the invite or a message the day before so people arrive prepared. → *Expect:* attendees can see the item list and the decision each item asks for.
2. **Start on time with the goal, not with small talk.** State the purpose in one sentence and the hard stop. → *Expect:* the room quiets and orients; late arrivals catch up without a restart.
3. **Assign a timekeeper and a note-taker.** For a small meeting you can hold both, but naming them out loud makes it real. → *Expect:* someone is watching the clock and someone is writing decisions down.
4. **Walk items in order, one at a time.** Introduce the item, let the relevant people speak, then drive to a decision. → *Expect:* discussion stays on the current item; tangents get named and deferred.
5. **When an item runs over its budget, force a fork.** [BRANCH: decide now with the information present | assign an owner to resolve it offline by a date | park it for a later meeting] → *Expect:* the clock is respected; nothing stalls the whole agenda.
6. **Capture every decision as it happens, out loud.** Say "so the decision is X, owner is Y, by Z" and write it verbatim. → *Expect:* the owner hears their name and does not object; the note matches what was said.
7. **Catch parking-lot items explicitly.** When something off-agenda but real comes up, write it in a visible "parking lot" and move on. → *Expect:* the raiser feels heard without the meeting derailing.
8. **Close by reading back the decisions and owners.** Two minutes, out loud, before anyone leaves. → *Expect:* no one is surprised later; corrections happen now while everyone is present.
9. **End early if the work is done.** Do not fill the booked time. → *Expect:* the meeting releases people ahead of the hard stop when possible.
10. **Send the notes within a few hours.** Decisions, owners, dates, and the parking lot, in that order. → *Expect:* a written record reaches every attendee plus anyone who missed it.

## Decision points

- More than 8 people or no clear decision to make → this is a briefing or a workshop, not a small meeting; restructure or cancel.
- Key decision-maker absent → do not decide without them; gather input, assign the decision to them with a deadline, and stop.
- Discussion is heated and circular → call it, name the specific disagreement, and route it to two people to resolve offline.

## Failure modes & recovery

- **F1 No agenda:** if you arrive and there is none, spend the first two minutes building one on the whiteboard from what people came to discuss, then run it normally.
- **F2 One person dominates:** name the round-robin ("let me hear from the people who haven't spoken"); redirect to specific quiet attendees by name.
- **F3 Decisions made but not captured:** stop the meeting the moment you notice, reconstruct the last decision aloud, and confirm it before continuing.
- **F4 Runs over time:** at the hard stop, stop; list remaining items as parking lot and schedule a follow-up rather than holding people hostage.

## Verification

The meeting ended at or before its scheduled end, every agenda item has a recorded outcome (decision, owner with date, or explicit park), and a written summary listing decisions and owners was sent to all attendees.

## Variations

- `remote`: share the agenda doc on screen and type decisions into it live so remote attendees see the record forming; call on people by name since visual turn-taking cues are weak.
- Recurring meeting: keep a rolling doc where last meeting's action items are the first agenda item ("did we do them?"), which kills the drift that makes recurring meetings pointless.

## Safety & privacy

Low risk. The main exposure is the notes: if the meeting touched personnel, pay, or confidential plans, restrict the summary's distribution to attendees and avoid quoting individuals verbatim in a record that may circulate wider than the room.
