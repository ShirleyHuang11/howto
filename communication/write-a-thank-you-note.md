## Goal

You write and send a thank-you note that names a specific gift or act, expresses genuine appreciation, and reaches the recipient promptly.

## Preconditions

- The recipient's name and mailing address (or email, if that is the norm for the relationship).
- A card or clean notepaper and a pen; a stamp if mailing.
- A moment of memory: what exactly they gave or did, and its effect on you.

## Steps

1. **Recall one specific detail.** Not "the gift" but "the blue scarf" or "driving me to the airport at 5 a.m." Specificity is what separates a real note from a form letter. → *Expect:* one concrete detail written on scratch paper.
2. **Send it promptly.** Aim within a few days of a gift or favor, within two weeks of a wedding or interview. Late is still worth doing; a late note beats none. → *Expect:* a target send date set, sooner rather than later.
3. **Open by naming the person and the thing.** "Dear [name], thank you so much for the [specific thing]." → *Expect:* the first sentence names both the person and the specific gift/act.
4. **Add the second sentence: its impact.** Say how you used it, felt about it, or will use it. This is the sentence that proves you mean it. → *Expect:* a concrete statement of effect ("it kept me warm all week", "your advice changed how I prepared").
5. **Close with warmth and a forward look.** "It was so thoughtful of you. I hope to see you soon." Then sign off. → *Expect:* three sentences total is plenty; you do not owe them a page.
6. **Prefer handwriting when it carries weight.** For gifts, condolences, weddings, and mentors, handwritten on paper signals effort that email cannot. → *Expect:* the medium matches the occasion (handwritten for weighty thanks, email acceptable for casual/work-fast contexts).
7. **Address, stamp, and send.** Double-check the spelling of the name and the address. → *Expect:* note sealed, correctly addressed, and in the mailbox (or sent).

## Decision points

- Job interview → send within 24 hours, usually by email so it lands before a decision; reference one specific point from the conversation.
- Group gift → a note to each named contributor if feasible; at minimum a warm note to whoever organized it, acknowledging the group.
- You genuinely disliked the gift → thank the thought and the gesture ("so thoughtful of you to remember"), never the object's merits; do not lie about loving it.

## Failure modes & recovery

- **F1 Blank-page freeze:** the three-sentence skeleton (thanks + impact + warm close) breaks the paralysis; fill the blanks and stop.
- **F2 It has been a month:** send anyway with a light acknowledgment ("I'm sorry this is late, but I've thought of you every time I...") rather than skipping it.
- **F3 Sounds generic:** if you could swap in any gift or any name, add the missing specific detail; that one edit fixes most notes.
- **F4 Wrong medium:** a texted thanks for a wedding gift can read as careless; upgrade weighty thanks to handwritten even if slower.

## Verification

The note names the specific gift or act, states its impact in your own words, closes warmly, and is on its way to the correct address, ideally within the occasion's expected window.

## Variations

- `email-professional`: acceptable and often expected for interviews and work favors; keep the same specific-detail rule and send fast.
- `condolence-thanks`: acknowledging support after a loss; slower timing is fine and warmth matters more than polish.

## Safety & privacy

Low risk. Avoid putting sensitive details (amounts of monetary gifts, private circumstances) in a note that others may see. Confirm the mailing address is current before sending.
