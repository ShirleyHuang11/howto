## Goal

Request time off with enough notice, coverage, and written clarity that your manager can approve it without chasing details.

## Preconditions

- Your available leave balance or policy, team calendar, project deadlines, and any blackout periods.
- The dates or partial days you want off, including timezone if working across regions.
- A rough handoff plan for urgent work.

## Steps

1. **Check policy and balance.** Confirm whether the time is vacation, sick, personal, unpaid, bereavement, parental, or holiday time. → *Expect:* you know what category and amount you are requesting.
2. **Check the calendar.** Look for launches, deadlines, team coverage gaps, holidays, and your manager's absence. → *Expect:* you can flag conflicts before your manager does.
3. **Choose coverage.** Identify who can handle urgent issues, meetings, approvals, or on-call duties while you are out. [BRANCH: routine vacation | sensitive leave] give normal coverage for routine vacation; share only necessary details for medical or family leave. → *Expect:* the request includes a workable backup.
4. **Give notice early.** Ask as soon as dates are likely, especially around holidays or long absences. → *Expect:* the team has time to plan.
5. **Write the request plainly.** Include exact dates, return date, leave type if needed, coverage plan, and any deadlines you will finish before leaving. → *Expect:* the manager can approve from the message alone.
6. **Submit in the required system.** Use the HR tool, calendar, email, or manager channel your workplace requires. → *Expect:* the request exists in writing and in the official place.
7. **Prepare the handoff.** Before leaving, send a short note with status, owners, links, risks, and what can wait. → *Expect:* coworkers can cover without decoding your brain.
8. **Set boundaries while away.** Update calendar, status, autoresponder if appropriate, and notification settings. ⚠️ *Irreversible:* nonrefundable travel or commitments should wait until leave is approved unless you accept the risk. → *Expect:* people can see you are out and know who to contact.

## Decision points

- If the time conflicts with a major deadline → propose coverage, adjusted dates, or a specific mitigation.
- If the leave is legally protected or medically sensitive → share only what policy requires and use HR when needed.
- If the manager hesitates → ask what coverage gap they need solved.
- If plans are tentative → ask about holding dates before booking.

## Failure modes & recovery

- **F1 Request lacks dates:** detect follow-up questions about when you leave or return → resend with exact dates and workdays affected.
- **F2 Coverage is unclear:** detect manager concern about work stopping → name owners, links, and decision rules.
- **F3 You ask too late:** detect scheduling conflict → apologize, offer alternatives, and accept that approval may be harder.
- **F4 Work follows you on leave:** detect pings that ignore your status → redirect once to the coverage owner and mute where appropriate.

## Verification

The time off is approved or acknowledged in writing, appears on the relevant calendar or system, and has a visible coverage plan.

## Variations

- Hourly shift work: request through the scheduling system and arrange swaps according to policy.
- Extended leave: involve HR early and document milestones, benefits, and return planning.
- Emergency leave: prioritize a short notice with expected next update time; details can come later.

## Safety & privacy

Low risk for routine vacation, higher for protected or sensitive leave. Do not disclose medical, family, immigration, or legal details beyond what policy requires.
