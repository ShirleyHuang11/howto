## Goal

A recommendation letter that explains your relationship to the candidate, proves their strengths with specific examples, and helps the reader see the fit.

## Preconditions

- You can honestly recommend the person for the role, program, award, or opportunity.
- You know the deadline, submission method, recipient, and any required format.
- You have the candidate's resume, prompt, achievements, and points they hope you can address.

## Steps

1. **Confirm the ask and deadline.** Ask what the letter is for, where it goes, and when it is due. → *Expect:* the logistics are clear before you draft.
2. **Define your relationship.** Open with who you are, how long you have known the candidate, and in what capacity. → *Expect:* the reader understands your basis for judgment.
3. **Choose two or three strengths.** Pick qualities relevant to the opportunity, such as research judgment, leadership, care, reliability, or writing. → *Expect:* the letter has a focused argument.
4. **Use specific examples.** Give concrete projects, moments, numbers, or behavior that show the qualities in action. → *Expect:* praise is supported rather than generic.
5. **Explain the fit.** Connect the candidate's strengths to the role, program, lab, scholarship, or team they want to join. → *Expect:* the reader sees why this person belongs there.
6. **Close strongly.** State your recommendation plainly and offer to be contacted for more information. → *Expect:* the final paragraph leaves no doubt about your support.
7. **Check constraints before sending.** [BRANCH: confidential upload | candidate-visible letter] Upload directly if confidentiality is required; share a copy only if the process allows it. ⚠️ *Irreversible:* a submitted confidential letter usually cannot be edited or shown later, so verify names, dates, and recipient before submission. → *Expect:* the letter is sent through the correct channel.
8. **Keep a dated copy.** Save the final version with the candidate's name and opportunity. → *Expect:* you can reuse accurate facts if asked again.

## Decision points

- If you cannot write a supportive letter, decline quickly so the candidate can ask someone else.
- If the deadline is too close, be honest about whether you can do it well.
- If you know the candidate in a limited way, state that scope and focus on what you observed.
- If the prompt asks for weaknesses, answer fairly and briefly with growth context.
- If the candidate drafts material, verify every claim before using it.

## Failure modes & recovery

- **F1 Generic praise:** detect it when the letter could describe anyone → add one named project or observed behavior per claim.
- **F2 Wrong audience:** detect it when the examples do not match the opportunity → revise the fit paragraph around the selection criteria.
- **F3 Missed deadline:** detect it when submission confirmation is absent near due date → contact the candidate immediately and submit if the portal still accepts it.
- **F4 Overclaiming:** detect it when you cannot personally support a statement → soften or remove it.

## Verification

The submitted letter states your relationship, gives specific examples, explains fit, closes with clear support, and includes your contact information if appropriate.

## Variations

- Academic letter: emphasize intellectual contribution, independence, and comparison group if you can do so honestly.
- Employment letter: emphasize responsibilities, outcomes, teamwork, and reliability.
- Character reference: explain the context and avoid claims outside what you know.

## Safety & privacy

Low risk. Do not disclose protected, medical, immigration, family, or disciplinary details unless the candidate explicitly asks and the disclosure is appropriate.
