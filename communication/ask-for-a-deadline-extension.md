## Goal

You have secured a realistic new deadline from the person who owns the original one, through a request that owns the delay honestly, proposes a specific new date, and protects the relationship, and then you deliver by that date.

## Preconditions

- A commitment with a deadline you now believe you will miss, or realistically might.
- Knowledge of who actually owns the deadline (the person who set it or depends on it), not just who forwarded it.
- An honest internal estimate of when you can actually deliver, with buffer.

## Steps

1. **Decide early, the moment you doubt the date.** The value of an extension request drops fast as the deadline nears; a week out is a planning conversation, an hour out is an apology. → *Expect:* you are raising this while the owner still has room to adjust.
2. **Estimate the real new date, then add buffer.** Pick the date you can hit even if one more thing goes wrong, not your best case. → *Expect:* a specific date you would bet on, not a hopeful one.
3. **Choose the channel to match the stakes.** [BRANCH: routine or internal → a short written message | high-stakes, client, or a date others depend on → a call or in-person, followed by written confirmation] → *Expect:* the owner hears it through a channel that signals you take it seriously.
4. **Lead with the ask and the new date, not the backstory.** Open with "I need to move the X deadline to [date]" so they can react to the concrete request. → *Expect:* the owner immediately knows what you want and by when.
5. **Own the reason in one honest line, without over-explaining.** State the cause plainly; do not manufacture drama or bury it in excuses. → *Expect:* the reason is credible and brief; you have not shifted blame onto others without cause.
6. **Show what they get for the delay.** Offer a partial deliverable now, a status detail, or a scope trade so the extension buys quality, not just time. → *Expect:* the owner sees the new date as protecting the outcome, not just your comfort.
7. **Get the new date confirmed in writing.** A verbal yes on a call must be echoed in a message. ⚠️ *Irreversible:* do not treat silence as agreement; an unanswered request means the old deadline still stands. → *Expect:* an explicit written acknowledgment of the new date.
8. **Deliver on the new date, or earlier.** The whole request is only worth making if you hit the date you asked for. → *Expect:* the work arrives by the agreed date; your next extension request, if ever, is still credible.

## Decision points

- The deadline is externally fixed and truly immovable (legal filing, contractual, exam) → do not ask for an extension; ask instead what a partial or late submission costs and whether a formal exception process exists.
- The delay is caused by someone else's late input → say so factually and name what you need and by when, rather than absorbing blame that hides the real bottleneck.
- You are chronically late on this relationship → a single clean extension is fine; a pattern needs a workload conversation, not another ask.

## Failure modes & recovery

- **F1 Asked too late:** lead with a genuine apology, deliver the most complete partial you can by the original date, and give a firm short new date for the rest.
- **F2 Extension refused:** ask what the minimum viable version is that meets the original date, and negotiate scope down instead of time out.
- **F3 New date slips too:** the second ask is far more expensive; escalate proactively before it lands, propose a scope cut, and do not go silent.
- **F4 No response to the request:** the original deadline is still live; follow up once, and if still silent, deliver against the original date.

## Verification

The deadline owner has explicitly agreed in writing to a specific new date, and you delivered the committed work on or before that date.

## Variations

- `academic`: many institutions require a formal extension form or documented grounds (illness, bereavement); a professor's verbal yes may not bind the registrar, so use the official channel where one exists.
- Team or project setting: raise the slip in the standup or tracker where dependencies are visible, so downstream owners can replan rather than being surprised.

## Safety & privacy

Low risk. If the reason involves health or personal circumstances, you are not obliged to disclose specifics; "a personal matter" plus a firm new date is a complete and professional reason.
