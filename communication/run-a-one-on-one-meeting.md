## Goal

A one-on-one meeting gives the other person useful attention, surfaces issues early, and ends with clear actions.

## Preconditions

- You have a recurring or one-time meeting with a direct report, manager, mentor, collaborator, or teammate.
- Both people know the meeting is for discussion, not only status reporting.
- There is a shared place for agenda items and action notes.

## Steps

1. **Create a shared agenda.** Add a living document or note with sections for their topics, your topics, decisions, and actions. → *Expect:* both people can shape the meeting before it starts.
2. **Invite their topics first.** Ask what they most want to cover and start there unless something urgent overrides it. → *Expect:* the meeting is not only your broadcast.
3. **Separate status from substance.** Move simple updates to writing and use live time for blockers, decisions, feedback, growth, or relationship repair. → *Expect:* the conversation uses the value of being together.
4. **Listen before solving.** Ask follow-up questions, reflect what you heard, and avoid jumping to advice before the problem is clear. → *Expect:* the other person feels understood and the real issue is visible.
5. **Discuss feedback or support plainly.** [BRANCH: manager-led | peer-led] Managers should cover priorities, development, and support; peers should cover coordination, decisions, and friction. → *Expect:* the conversation matches the relationship.
6. **Capture actions live.** Write owner, action, and date for each commitment while both people can correct it. → *Expect:* next steps are not hidden in memory.
7. **Keep confidentiality boundaries.** ⚠️ *Irreversible:* sensitive personal or personnel information can spread through notes, so record only what belongs in a work system. → *Expect:* notes are useful without overexposing private details.
8. **Protect the cadence.** End by confirming the next meeting and whether the frequency still works. → *Expect:* one-on-ones remain a reliable channel.

## Decision points

- If there are no topics repeatedly, reduce frequency or improve async status updates.
- If a personal issue appears, ask what support or privacy they want before documenting.
- If performance concerns exist, be direct and keep separate formal records as required.
- If the meeting is with your manager, bring decisions and tradeoffs, not only updates.
- If action items repeat, discuss the blocker instead of recopying the task.

## Failure modes & recovery

- **F1 Status swamp:** detect it when the whole meeting becomes update reading → move updates async and ask for the hardest current blocker.
- **F2 Manager monologue:** detect it when the other person barely speaks → stop and ask what you should understand from their side.
- **F3 No actions:** detect it when the meeting ends with vague agreement → add owner, next step, and date before closing.
- **F4 Canceled cadence:** detect it when meetings are repeatedly skipped → shorten them or choose a protected time.

## Verification

The one-on-one used a shared agenda, covered the other person's topics first, captured action owners and dates, and confirmed the next cadence.

## Variations

- New direct report: spend more time on context, expectations, and working preferences.
- Senior teammate: focus on decisions, risks, and removing friction.
- Remote meeting: use a shared note and leave more pauses.

## Safety & privacy

Low risk, with confidentiality concerns. Keep sensitive health, family, compensation, and performance details only where policy and trust support them.
