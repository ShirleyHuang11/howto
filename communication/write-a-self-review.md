## Goal

Write a self-review that gives your manager concrete evidence of impact, names growth honestly, and aligns your work to the goals you were supposed to advance.

## Preconditions

- The review form, rubric, competencies, level expectations, and submission deadline.
- Your goals, project notes, metrics, feedback, shipped work, incidents, praise, and lessons from the review period.
- Enough time to draft, gather evidence, and revise before submitting.

## Steps

1. **Collect evidence before writing.** Pull calendar milestones, tickets, docs, dashboards, customer notes, peer feedback, and goal updates. → *Expect:* you have facts instead of memory fragments.
2. **Map accomplishments to goals.** For each goal, state what you did, why it mattered, and what changed because of it. → *Expect:* the review follows business or team priorities, not only busyness.
3. **Use impact and metrics.** Include numbers where honest: revenue, cycle time, defect rate, adoption, saved hours, risk reduced, users helped, launches completed. → *Expect:* claims are checkable.
4. **Name your role clearly.** Say whether you led, owned, contributed, unblocked, reviewed, coordinated, or supported. Avoid taking credit for others and avoid disappearing your own work. → *Expect:* your actual contribution is visible.
5. **Avoid the modesty trap.** Replace "just helped with" with precise verbs and outcomes. [BRANCH: individual contributor | manager] ICs emphasize shipped work and influence; managers include team outcomes, coaching, hiring, and operating rhythm. → *Expect:* the review is confident without being inflated.
6. **Write growth areas with ownership.** Pick one or two real areas, describe what you learned, and state the next behavior: "I need to escalate dependency risk earlier, so I am adding weekly owner check-ins." → *Expect:* growth reads as maturity, not self-sabotage.
7. **Connect to next goals.** Mention where you want to deepen scope, skills, leadership, quality, or customer impact next cycle. → *Expect:* the review gives your manager material for future planning.
8. **Revise for evidence and tone.** Remove vague adjectives, defensiveness, and surprises. ⚠️ *Irreversible:* once submitted, the review may become part of your performance record, so verify dates, claims, and sensitive details. → *Expect:* every major statement has evidence and fits the rubric.

## Decision points

- If a project failed → explain your role, lesson, and recovery without hiding the outcome.
- If metrics are unavailable → use concrete qualitative evidence, such as customer quotes, adoption examples, or before-and-after process changes.
- If you had conflict → focus on behavior, impact, and repair, not motives.
- If the form asks for ratings → anchor each rating to rubric language.

## Failure modes & recovery

- **F1 Review is a task list:** detect bullets with no outcome → add impact, stakeholder, or metric to each major item.
- **F2 Review is too modest:** detect passive language and missing ownership → replace with active verbs and specific scope.
- **F3 Growth section sounds dangerous:** detect confessions without recovery → add what you changed and how you will measure improvement.
- **F4 Claims are unverifiable:** detect unsupported numbers or credit → link or cite source material where the system allows.

## Verification

The self-review ties accomplishments to goals, includes concrete impact or metrics, names growth areas with next actions, and matches the review rubric.

## Variations

- Promotion packet: add scope, influence beyond role, and examples at the next level.
- Short form: use three impact bullets, one growth paragraph, and one next-cycle paragraph.
- Manager review: include team health, hiring, retention, delivery, and how you grew others.

## Safety & privacy

Medium career risk because reviews affect compensation, promotion, and records. Do not include confidential personnel details, private customer data, or unsupported claims that could be challenged later.
