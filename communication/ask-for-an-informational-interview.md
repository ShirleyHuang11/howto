## Goal

Ask someone for a short informational interview in a way that is warm, specific, respectful of their time, and likely to produce useful advice.

## Preconditions

- A person whose experience is relevant to your question, plus a reliable contact channel.
- A brief reason you are reaching out and what you hope to learn.
- Three to five specific questions that do not require confidential information.

## Steps

1. **Start with the warmest path.** Use a mutual introduction, alumni directory, event follow-up, or prior connection when possible. → *Expect:* the recipient can place you or the context quickly.
2. **Write the ask in one short paragraph.** Include who you are, why them, what you want to learn, and the time limit. → *Expect:* the message is understandable without scrolling.
3. **Respect their time explicitly.** Ask for 15 to 20 minutes, offer two or three windows, and make it easy to decline. → *Expect:* saying yes feels bounded.
4. **Make the questions specific.** Ask about decisions, skills, transitions, day-to-day work, and what they wish they had known. Avoid "Can I pick your brain?" → *Expect:* they can see the conversation will be focused.
5. **Avoid disguising a job ask.** Do not ask for a referral, opening, or internal contact in the first request. [BRANCH: student or career changer | peer in field] emphasize learning if exploring; emphasize targeted perspective if already in the field. → *Expect:* the meeting is informational, not a hidden transaction.
6. **Send a polished message.** Example: "I am exploring product roles in health tech and saw your path from nursing operations to product. Would you be open to 20 minutes next week so I can ask how you made that transition?" → *Expect:* the recipient knows exactly why they were chosen.
7. **Prepare before they answer.** Make a short agenda, read their public work, and decide which questions matter most. → *Expect:* if they say yes, you are ready.
8. **Thank and update.** After the conversation, send a same-day thank-you with one useful takeaway. Later, send a brief update if their advice helped. ⚠️ *Irreversible:* burning someone's time with an unprepared call damages trust, so prepare before booking. → *Expect:* the relationship ends warmer than it began.

## Decision points

- If they do not reply → one polite follow-up after a week is enough.
- If they offer less time than requested → accept the smaller window and prioritize questions.
- If they refer you to someone else → thank them and make the next ask even more specific.
- If they bring up jobs or referrals → answer honestly, but do not push beyond their invitation.

## Failure modes & recovery

- **F1 Ask is too vague:** detect broad phrasing like "learn about your career" → name the transition, role, skill, or decision you want to understand.
- **F2 Message is too long:** detect multiple paragraphs of background → cut to four sentences and link only if needed.
- **F3 You overrun the call:** detect the time limit approaching → ask one final question or offer to stop.
- **F4 You forget to follow up:** detect no thank-you within a day → send a brief late note owning the delay.

## Verification

The request states why this person, asks for a bounded 15 to 20 minute conversation, includes focused topics, and makes declining easy.

## Variations

- Internal company ask: mention your team and keep it on approved channels.
- Academic ask: include program, advisor, or research interest and ask about fit, not admission odds.
- Friend-of-friend ask: thank the introducer separately and keep both people out of awkward pressure.

## Safety & privacy

Do not request confidential salary bands, hiring plans, customer data, or internal strategy. Treat the person's time and reputation as the valuable thing they are offering.
