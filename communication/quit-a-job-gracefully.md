## Goal

A resignation is communicated clearly, professionally, and with a handover that protects your reputation and the team's continuity.

## Preconditions

- You have decided to leave and understand any contract, visa, benefits, bonus, repayment, or notice implications.
- You know your preferred final working day and whether you can offer standard notice.
- You have personal copies of documents you are allowed to keep, not confidential company files.

## Steps

1. **Review obligations first.** Check contract, employee handbook, offer letter, and local rules for notice, equipment, confidentiality, and repayment clauses. → *Expect:* you know the practical consequences before speaking.
2. **Tell your manager first.** Request a private conversation and say directly that you are resigning and when your last day will be. → *Expect:* your manager hears it from you, not gossip or a system notice.
3. **Keep the reason brief.** Share a simple explanation if you want, but do not litigate every frustration in the resignation meeting. → *Expect:* the conversation stays controlled.
4. **Send the letter.** Put resignation, final day, thanks, and willingness to support transition in a short written note. → *Expect:* HR and your manager have a clear record.
5. **Plan the handover.** [BRANCH: project role | people manager] List active work, deadlines, contacts, files, decisions, and risks; if you manage people, add review cycles and sensitive follow-ups. → *Expect:* the team can see what needs coverage.
6. **Stay professional through notice.** Finish agreed tasks, document status, and avoid badmouthing the company or recruiting coworkers into your exit mood. → *Expect:* your last weeks support your future references.
7. **Return property and access.** Confirm equipment, badges, accounts, expenses, and benefits paperwork. ⚠️ *Irreversible:* deleting files, taking confidential data, or burning access can create legal and career consequences, so separate personal materials carefully. → *Expect:* offboarding is clean.
8. **Say goodbye well.** Thank key people, share personal contact information selectively, and leave concise notes for whoever inherits the work. → *Expect:* relationships survive the departure.

## Decision points

- If the workplace is hostile or unsafe, prioritize legal advice, HR guidance, or a shorter written process.
- If your employer counteroffers, pause and compare the full situation, not only salary.
- If asked to leave immediately, ask about pay, benefits, equipment return, and communication to colleagues.
- If you hold critical knowledge, document it without accepting an unlimited transition burden.
- If you are going to a competitor, be extra careful with confidentiality and devices.

## Failure modes & recovery

- **F1 Manager reacts badly:** detect it in anger, threats, or guilt → repeat your final day calmly and move logistics to writing.
- **F2 Notice becomes overloaded:** detect it when new work exceeds time left → ask your manager to prioritize the handover list.
- **F3 Confidentiality slip:** detect it when you start copying or forwarding work files → stop and ask what you are allowed to keep.
- **F4 Exit gossip grows:** detect it when coworkers press for drama → use a neutral line and redirect to transition.

## Verification

Your manager and HR have written notice, final day is documented, handover materials exist, and company property and access are addressed.

## Variations

- New job lined up: avoid sharing employer details until you are ready and allowed.
- Retirement: include transition history and legacy contacts if useful.
- Short tenure: keep explanation simple and focus on clean closure.

## Safety & privacy

Medium risk because employment, money, immigration, benefits, and confidentiality may be involved. Do not take proprietary data or rely on verbal promises for important terms.
