## Goal

A formal email — to an official, professor, landlord, HR, customer service, or a stranger with power over your outcome — that gets read, understood, and acted on, in one send.

## Preconditions

- A clear ask or purpose you can state in one sentence (if you can't, that's the actual task — do it first).
- The right recipient's address, verified (the specific person or role inbox, not a guessed pattern).

## Steps

1. **Write the body before the To: field.** (Old professional armor: no premature sends.) Start from the ask, not the throat-clearing. → *Expect:* drafting happens with an empty recipient field.
2. **Subject line = topic + specificity.** "Repair request — Flat 3B bathroom leak" beats "Question"; include reference numbers where they exist ("Application #48213"). → *Expect:* a subject the recipient could file, forward, and find again.
3. **Open with the right register and name.** "Dear Dr. Lee," / "Dear Sir or Madam," (unknown) / "Hello Support team,". When unsure of formality, err formal — recipients downgrade gracefully, upgrades read as correction. → *Expect:* a greeting matched to the relationship, names spelled right (check twice; it's the cheapest respect).
4. **State who you are and what you want in the first two sentences.** "I'm the tenant at X. I'm writing to request Y." Context *after* the ask, not before — busy readers triage on sentence one. → *Expect:* a recipient who knows the ask before their first scroll.
5. **Give the supporting facts in scannable form.** Short paragraphs or a compact list: dates, amounts, reference numbers, what you've already tried. Attach evidence and *name the attachments in the text* ("photos attached"). → *Expect:* everything needed to act, nothing that pads; attachments actually attached (the classic — see F1).
6. **Close with the explicit next step and a deadline where legitimate.** "Could you confirm the repair date by Friday the 25th?" — a formal email without a requested action invites a filed-and-forgotten. Then the sign-off: "Kind regards, / Full Name / contact." → *Expect:* the recipient knows exactly what doing-their-part looks like.
7. **The pre-send pass.** Read once aloud-ish: tone (would you say this to their face?), typos, the name again, attachment present, recipient *now* added and correct. ⚠️ *Irreversible:* send is send — wrong-recipient and reply-all-with-heat are the two classic unrecoverables; this pass is where they die. → *Expect:* one clean send.

## Decision points

- Angry context (complaint, dispute) → write the hot draft if you must, then strip every adjective of grievance and keep the facts + ask; formal-and-cold reads as strong, heated reads as dismissible. Sleep on genuinely high-stakes sends.
- Long saga (fifth email in a thread) → top-post a two-line summary of the state before your new point; never assume they re-read the thread.
- Should this even be an email? → requests needing back-and-forth negotiation are calls/meetings (`communication/schedule-a-meeting`); records, requests, and formal notices are email — the medium *is* part of the message.

## Failure modes & recovery

- **F1 Sent without the attachment:** the universal fumble — reply within the same thread immediately: "Apologies — attachment now included." Zero drama; speed is the recovery.
- **F2 No response after a reasonable window (3–5 working days for most contexts):** one polite nudge replying to your own email ("following up on the below — grateful for an update"); silence after two nudges → escalate channel (phone, the role's manager, the portal) referencing the dated thread.
- **F3 Realized an error after sending (wrong date, wrong amount):** correct in-thread fast and plainly: "Correction: the date should read…" — corrections age well, discovered errors don't.
- **F4 Tone landed wrong (reply reads offended):** repair directly, briefly, without over-apologizing: "That came out more abrupt than intended — to clarify…" and return to substance.

## Verification

One send contained: findable subject, correct name and register, the ask in sentence two, scannable facts, named-and-present attachments, an explicit requested action — and the thread now serves as its own paper trail (`housing/report-a-repair-to-your-landlord` step 3's principle, generalized).

## Variations

- `de`/`jp` formal registers are heavier (Sehr geehrte…, お世話になっております) and the formality floor is higher — mirror the region's templates; `us` professional norms run shorter and first-name faster, but the structure holds.
- Role inboxes (support@, admissions@): reference numbers in the subject do the routing; individuals: one human touch ("thank you for your help with X last month") earns real goodwill.

## Safety & privacy

Low risk with two live wires: the recipient-last habit and the pre-send pass (step 7) prevent the unrecoverables; and email is discoverable/forwardable forever — write every formal email as if the recipient's boss reads it, because sometimes that's exactly the point.
