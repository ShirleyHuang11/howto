## Goal

You deliver a short presentation (roughly 5 to 15 minutes) that lands one clear message with an audience, delivered on time and without technical mishaps.

## Preconditions

- The audience, purpose, and time limit are known.
- The room/platform and equipment you will use (projector, laptop, clicker, or video call).
- Enough lead time to rehearse aloud at least twice.

## Steps

1. **Decide the one message.** Finish this sentence: "If they remember only one thing, it is ___." Everything serves that sentence. → *Expect:* a single message written in one line.
2. **Structure with the rule of three.** Three supporting points, no more; audiences hold three well and forget five. Open by stating the message, cover the three, close by restating it. → *Expect:* an outline of exactly three points bracketed by the message.
3. **Build spare slides (if any).** Roughly one idea per slide, large text, few words, a strong visual over a wall of bullets. Slides support you; they are not your script. → *Expect:* slides readable from the back row, none crammed.
4. **Rehearse aloud, out loud, on your feet.** Reading silently hides the stumbles; speaking reveals timing and awkward transitions. Time it. → *Expect:* two full run-throughs, and a measured runtime under your limit with margin.
5. **Cut to fit with margin.** Aim for ~80% of the allotted time; overruns are the most common and most resented failure. → *Expect:* rehearsed runtime comfortably inside the limit.
6. **Do a full tech check in the actual setup.** Connect your laptop to the real projector or join the actual call; test the adapter, clicker, sound, and screen-share. ⚠️ *Irreversible:* a live failure cannot be redone, so verify the adapter and have slides exported to PDF on a USB stick and emailed to yourself as backups. → *Expect:* your first slide displays on the real screen and the clicker advances it.
7. **Deliver: open strong, watch the audience, close on the message.** Make eye contact, slow down (nerves speed you up), and end by restating the one message rather than trailing into "so, yeah, that's it." → *Expect:* you finish on time with a clean restatement, then invite questions.

## Decision points

- No projector or it fails → present from the exported PDF on any screen, or deliver without slides using your three points as the spine.
- Remote/video audience → share the PDF, not a fragile live app; ask someone to confirm they can see and hear before you begin.
- Q&A included in the time limit → budget for it; a 15-minute slot with questions means ~10 minutes of talking.

## Failure modes & recovery

- **F1 Running over time:** you feel the clock and rush → skip to your closing restatement rather than racing through remaining slides; ending clean beats cramming.
- **F2 Slides won't display:** adapter or resolution mismatch → fall back to the PDF on a USB or your emailed copy; this is why you exported it.
- **F3 You lose your place or go blank:** pause, breathe, and return to the current one of your three points; the simple structure is your recovery map.
- **F4 A hostile or off-topic question:** acknowledge it, answer briefly or offer to follow up after, and steer back to your message rather than getting drawn into a debate.

## Verification

You delivered within the time limit, stated your one message at both the open and close, the visuals displayed correctly (or you adapted cleanly), and the audience can repeat your core message back.

## Variations

- `no-slides`: a short talk often lands harder without slides; use your three points and a single prop or story instead.
- `high-stakes-pitch`: rehearse in front of a person for feedback, prepare answers to the three likeliest questions, and time the Q&A too.

## Safety & privacy

Low risk. Avoid putting confidential data or personal information on slides that may be photographed or recorded. Confirm whether the session is being recorded before sharing anything sensitive.
