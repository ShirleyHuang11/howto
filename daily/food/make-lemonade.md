## Goal

A pitcher of lemonade that is balanced (tart but drinkable), with all sugar fully dissolved and no gritty layer at the bottom, chilled and ready to serve.

## Preconditions

- 4-6 lemons (about 1 cup / 240 ml juice), or bottled lemon juice as a fallback.
- Granulated sugar, water, and ice.
- A small saucepan or a microwave-safe cup (for the syrup), a pitcher of at least 1.5 L, and a juicer or a fork.

## Steps

1. **Make a simple syrup instead of adding sugar directly.** Combine 1 cup sugar with 1 cup water in a saucepan over medium heat (or microwave 90 seconds), stirring until clear. This is the whole trick: sugar barely dissolves in cold water and otherwise sinks into a sludge you re-stir all afternoon. → *Expect:* liquid goes from cloudy to fully transparent with no grains on the spoon.
2. **Juice the lemons.** Roll each lemon firmly under your palm for 5 seconds first, then halve and juice into a bowl or measuring cup. Fish out seeds; pulp is a taste choice. → *Expect:* about 1 cup of juice from 4-6 lemons; rolled lemons give up noticeably more.
3. **Combine at the 1:1:4 ratio.** In the pitcher: 1 cup lemon juice, 1 cup syrup, 4 cups cold water. Stir. → *Expect:* a uniform pale-yellow mix; nothing settling at the bottom.
4. **Taste and correct before chilling.** [BRANCH: too tart → add syrup 2 tablespoons at a time | too sweet → add lemon juice 1 tablespoon at a time | too strong overall → add water] → *Expect:* a sip that makes you want a second sip, slightly stronger than final target (ice will dilute it).
5. **Chill the pitcher, then add ice only at serving.** Refrigerate 30+ minutes if time allows. Ice melting in the pitcher is the classic failure: an hour later the batch tastes like lemon water. Put ice in the glasses instead. → *Expect:* cold lemonade in the pitcher, ice standing by in glasses, not swimming in the batch.
6. **Serve.** Pour over ice, garnish with a lemon slice if you like. → *Expect:* first glass tastes balanced even after the ice starts to melt.

## Decision points

- Serving over a long event (party table) → mix the batch ~20% stronger than tastes right, since standing ice dilution is unavoidable.
- No time to cool the syrup → use half the water in the ratio as extra ice-cold water to bring the temperature down after mixing.
- Bottled juice only → same ratio works; expect a flatter flavor, and a few strips of fresh lemon zest steeped in the warm syrup for 5 minutes recovers most of it.

## Failure modes & recovery

- **F1 Gritty sugar at the bottom:** sugar was added dry to cold liquid → pour everything back through a warm saucepan, stir until clear, cool, return to the pitcher.
- **F2 Bitter batch:** juiced too aggressively into the white pith, or zest steeped too long → dilute with water plus a pinch of salt, which masks bitterness surprisingly well; keep pressure moderate next time.
- **F3 Watery after an hour:** ice was left in the pitcher → stir in 2-3 tablespoons each of lemon juice and syrup to re-balance; move remaining ice to glasses.
- **F4 Seeds or pulp complaints:** strain the pitcher through a fine sieve into a second container; takes one minute.

## Verification

The pitcher shows no sediment when tilted, a taste from the top and a taste after stirring are identical (fully dissolved), and a glass poured over ice still tastes of lemon after five minutes.

## Variations

- Sparkling: use chilled soda water for the 4 parts water, added at serving so it keeps its fizz.
- `minted`: bruise a handful of mint leaves into the warm syrup, strain when cool.
- Pink lemonade: a splash of cranberry juice or a few crushed raspberries strained in; adjust sugar down slightly.

## Safety & privacy

Low risk. The syrup pan is briefly hot, and hot syrup clings to skin, so stir with a long spoon and cool before tasting. Wash hands after juicing: lemon juice on skin in strong sunlight can cause phytophotodermatitis burns.
