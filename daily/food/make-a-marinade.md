## Goal

Make a balanced marinade that seasons food without making it mushy or unsafe to cook.

## Preconditions

- Protein or vegetables to marinate, a bowl or zip-top bag, measuring spoons, and refrigerator space.
- Oil, salt, acid, and aromatics such as garlic, herbs, spices, mustard, soy sauce, or citrus zest.
- A plan for cooking the food after marinating.

## Steps

1. **Start with a ratio.** Mix about 3 parts oil, 1 part acid, 1 percent salt by food weight if measuring, and aromatics to taste. → *Expect:* marinade tastes bright, salty, and rounded rather than harsh.
2. **Choose the acid.** [BRANCH: vinegar | citrus | yogurt | wine] Pick one main acidic ingredient so the flavor stays focused. → *Expect:* acid is present but does not dominate the bowl.
3. **Add salt intentionally.** Use kosher salt, soy sauce, fish sauce, or miso, counting salty liquids as part of the seasoning. → *Expect:* a small taste of marinade is noticeably seasoned.
4. **Use the bag method for coverage.** Put food and marinade in a zip-top bag, press out air, seal, and set the bag in a bowl. → *Expect:* marinade contacts most surfaces and the bowl catches leaks.
5. **Refrigerate during marinating.** Place the covered bowl or sealed bag in the refrigerator, not on the counter. → *Expect:* food stays cold until cooking.
6. **Respect time limits.** Marinate fish 15 to 30 min, shrimp 15 to 20 min, chicken 2 to 12 h, pork or beef 2 to 24 h, vegetables 15 min to 2 h. → *Expect:* timer or reminder matches the food type.
7. **Remove food and pat if needed.** Lift food out, let excess drip off, and pat dry for browning. → *Expect:* surface is seasoned but not dripping heavily.
8. **Discard used marinade.** ⚠️ *Irreversible:* once marinade has touched raw meat, poultry, or seafood, throw it away unless boiled hard as a sauce. → *Expect:* used marinade is not served raw.
9. **Cook promptly.** Move marinated food to the grill, pan, oven, or broiler. → *Expect:* food starts cooking while still cold and safely handled.

## Decision points

- Need a sauce too → reserve some clean marinade before raw food touches it.
- Delicate seafood → keep acid low and time short to avoid ceviche-like texture.
- Want stronger flavor → add more aromatics or salt, not much more acid.
- Sugar in marinade → cook over moderate heat because sugar burns quickly.

## Failure modes & recovery

- **F1 Mushy surface:** detect pasty or chalky exterior, recover by shortening acidic marinating time next batch.
- **F2 Bland result:** detect seasoning only on the outside, recover by salting earlier or using thinner cuts.
- **F3 Leaking bag:** detect liquid in the refrigerator bowl, recover by transferring to a new bag or covered container.
- **F4 Unsafe reuse:** detect used marinade set aside as sauce, recover by boiling it thoroughly or discarding it.

## Verification

The food has been marinated under refrigeration for an appropriate time, excess marinade is removed, and any marinade that touched raw protein is discarded or boiled.

## Variations

- Dry rub plus oil: skip acid when you mainly want browning and spice crust.
- Yogurt marinade: useful for chicken and lamb, with salt and spices mixed in.
- Vegetables: use less time and more oil so they do not become limp.

## Safety & privacy

Medium risk from raw protein handling. Keep raw marinade away from ready-to-eat food, wash hands and tools, and do not marinate at room temperature.
