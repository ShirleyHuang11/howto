## Goal

A rested roast carved into clean slices across the grain, with juices retained and portions cut to the right thickness.

## Preconditions

- A cooked roast, carving board with juice groove, sharp slicing knife, carving fork, and platter.
- Roast has reached its target internal temperature and can rest safely.
- Board is stable; place a damp towel underneath if it shifts.

## Steps

1. **Rest the roast first.** Move it to the board and tent loosely with foil for 10 to 30 minutes depending on size. → *Expect:* juices settle and the roast feels firm, not actively steaming hard.
2. **Find the grain.** Look for the direction muscle fibers run across the cut face. → *Expect:* you can point to long lines that need to be cut across.
3. **Anchor with the fork.** Set the carving fork into the roast lightly, tines angled down and away from the slicing path. → *Expect:* the roast stays put without being crushed.
4. **Trim ties or bones.** [BRANCH: boneless | bone-in] Remove twine on boneless roasts; for bone-in, separate the meat from bone in one long cut first. → *Expect:* the knife has a clear slicing path.
5. **Slice against the grain.** Use long strokes with a sharp knife, letting the blade length do the work. → *Expect:* slices show short muscle fibers, not long stringy strands.
6. **Control thickness.** Cut 1/4 inch slices for tender roasts and thinner slices for tougher cuts. → *Expect:* slices bend without shredding.
7. **Transfer neatly.** Slide the knife or spatula under slices and move them to a warm platter. → *Expect:* slices stay intact and juices remain on the board or platter.
8. **Spoon juices back.** Pour resting juices over sliced meat or into sauce. → *Expect:* carved meat looks glossy, not dry.
   - ⚠️ *Irreversible:* cutting before the rest dumps juices; confirm the rest time is complete before carving.

## Decision points

- Tenderloin or prime rib → thicker slices showcase tenderness.
- Brisket, flank, or tri-tip → thinner slices across the grain prevent chewiness.
- Mixed grain directions → rotate the roast as needed and carve each muscle across its own grain.

## Failure modes & recovery

- **F1 Juices flood the board:** roast was carved too soon → pause 10 minutes, then resume with thinner slices.
- **F2 Meat shreds:** knife is dull or cut is with the grain → sharpen or switch angle across the fibers.
- **F3 Fork tears the crust:** anchor pressure is too heavy → use lighter pressure or hold with tongs outside the slice line.
- **F4 Slices cool fast:** platter is cold → warm the platter and shingle slices tightly.

## Verification

Slices are even, carved across the grain, still juicy, and served at the planned thickness.

## Variations

- Poultry roast: remove breast lobes first, then slice across them off the bird.
- Porchetta or crusted roast: use a serrated slicer if the crust crushes under a straight blade.
- Leftovers: chill first, then slice thinly for sandwiches.

## Safety & privacy

Medium risk from hot meat and a long sharp knife. Stabilize the board, use a fork anchor, keep fingers curled back, and never carve toward your hand.
