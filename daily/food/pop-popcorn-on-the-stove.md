## Goal

A pot of stovetop popcorn with nearly every kernel popped, none burnt, seasoned while hot — cheaper, better, and more theatrical than any bag, with the three-kernel test doing the physics.

## Preconditions

- Popcorn kernels (½ cup makes a big bowl for two or three), 2 to 3 tablespoons of a high-heat oil (coconut is the cinema classic; vegetable or peanut work; butter alone burns — it joins later), and salt.
- A large heavy pot with a lid (contents will expand roughly 30-fold; the pot everyone thinks is too big is correct), and oven mitts for the shake phase.

## Steps

1. **Run the three-kernel test.** Oil and exactly three kernels into the cold pot, lid on, medium-high heat. When all three pop, the oil is at temperature. → *Expect:* three pops within a few minutes; the calibration that separates this recipe from burnt-or-oily roulette.
2. **Add all the kernels off the heat for 30 seconds.** Pull the pot off, pour in the rest in one layer, swirl to coat, count thirty. This equalizes the kernels' temperature so they pop *together* instead of in a stragglers' war. → *Expect:* a single layer of oil-glossed kernels, briefly resting.
3. **Return to heat with the lid cracked.** Lid on but tilted a sliver open (or use a lid with a vent): steam escapes, popcorn stays crisp instead of steaming soggy. → *Expect:* popping beginning within a minute, accelerating to a full drumroll.
4. **Shake the pot every 10 to 15 seconds.** Mitts on, flat circular shakes across the burner: unpopped kernels fall to the heat, popped ones ride up away from scorching. → *Expect:* continuous rolling pops, no burning smell; the pot filling audibly.
5. **Kill the heat at the two-second gap.** When pops slow to one every two seconds, off the burner and lid fully on for the last stragglers. Waiting for total silence burns the bottom layer — the two-second rule is the whole endgame. → *Expect:* a final scattered percussion, then quiet; no smoke at any point.
6. **Season immediately in the bowl.** Pour into the big bowl, add fine salt (and melted butter now, if buttering) while hot and toss — seasoning sticks to warm oil and slides off cold popcorn. ⚠️ The pot and escaping steam are hot; pour away from your forearm. → *Expect:* evenly seasoned popcorn; a bottom-of-pot check showing few unpopped kernels and zero black ones.

## Decision points

- Flavors beyond salt: nutritional yeast (savory), cinnamon sugar (dessert), smoked paprika + salt, or a drizzle of honey-butter for camps that allow stickiness; fine-ground seasonings adhere, coarse ones sink.
- Movie-night scale: two batches beat one overfilled pot; the expansion math is unforgiving and a lid held on by popcorn pressure pops open theatrically at the worst moment.
- Kernel age matters: old dry kernels pop poorly (the pop is steam-powered); a jar sealed well lasts months, and chronic unpopped rates mean new kernels, not more heat.

## Failure modes & recovery

- **F1 Burnt layer at the bottom:** heat too high or the two-second rule ignored; the burnt smell taints the whole pot fast — decant the good top immediately and soak the pot (`daily/food/clean-as-you-cook` step 5).
- **F2 Half the kernels unpopped:** oil not at temperature (the three-kernel test was skipped) or old kernels; recalibrate next batch, and the survivors' second chance in fresh oil works better than folklore suggests.
- **F3 Soggy, chewy popcorn:** lid fully sealed trapped steam; the cracked-lid rule, plus serving promptly, restores the crunch next round.
- **F4 Oil smoking before the test kernels pop:** heat far too high or a fragile oil (butter, olive); off the heat, cool a minute, restart at medium-high with a proper high-heat oil.

## Verification

Nearly all kernels popped with none burnt, the popcorn is crisp not chewy, seasoning coats evenly, the pot bottom shows no scorch ring, and the batch survived less than fifteen minutes of contact with its audience — the traditional measure of success.

## Variations

- Kettle-corn style: 2 tablespoons of sugar in with the kernels and *constant* shaking (sugar burns in seconds of neglect) for the fair-ground result.
- The plain-paper-bag microwave method (kernels in a folded bag, no oil) is the honest diet version and the office-kitchen fallback.

## Safety & privacy

Medium risk: hot oil, a pressurized-adjacent lid, and steam at the pour — mitts, the cracked lid, and pouring away from yourself cover all three. Popcorn is also the canonical young-child choking food; small children get supervision and the unpopped kernels get filtered out of their bowls.
