## Goal

Vegetables are briefly cooked in salted boiling water, then chilled so they stay bright, tender-crisp, and ready to eat, freeze, or finish later.

## Preconditions

- Fresh vegetables, trimmed into evenly sized pieces.
- A large pot, colander or spider, bowl of ice water, salt, and a clean towel.
- Enough water that the vegetables can move freely instead of crowding.
- A timer or visible clock.

## Steps

1. **Cut evenly.** Trim tough ends, peel only if needed, and cut pieces to a matching thickness so they finish together → *Expect:* pieces look uniform, with no woody stems mixed in.
2. **Prepare the ice bath.** Fill a bowl with cold water and enough ice to keep it cold after hot vegetables go in → *Expect:* the bowl feels very cold and has room for the vegetables.
3. **Boil and salt the water.** Bring a large pot to a rolling boil, then salt it until it tastes lightly seasoned, not seawater-level salty → *Expect:* large bubbles break the surface continuously and the salt has dissolved.
4. **Cook in a small batch.** Add vegetables and stir once so the water returns to a boil quickly [BRANCH: tender greens | dense roots] → *Expect:* the water resumes bubbling within about 30 seconds.
5. **Taste early.** Start checking thin vegetables at 30 seconds and dense pieces after 2 minutes → *Expect:* the vegetable is brighter and barely tender at the center.
6. **Shock immediately.** Lift vegetables into the ice bath as soon as they are done → *Expect:* steam stops rising within seconds and the color stays vivid.
7. **Drain when cold.** Remove the vegetables once fully chilled, then drain well in a colander → *Expect:* the vegetables feel cold all the way through and no longer cook from carryover heat.
8. **Dry before storing or finishing.** Spread on a towel and pat dry, especially before sauteing or freezing → *Expect:* surfaces look matte rather than wet.

## Decision points

- Vegetable is very delicate, such as spinach or pea shoots → blanch for seconds, not minutes.
- Vegetable is dense, such as carrots or broccoli stems → cut smaller or cook longer before shocking.
- You want vegetables for freezing → blanch fully enough to soften enzymes, then dry thoroughly before bagging.
- You want vegetables for a hot dish later → stop at tender-crisp so the second cooking step does not turn them mushy.

## Failure modes & recovery

- **F1 Dull color:** detect olive or gray-green vegetables after cooking, recover by using more water, more salt, and a faster ice bath next time.
- **F2 Mushy texture:** detect limp pieces that collapse when pinched, recover by shortening the cook time and cutting pieces thicker next time.
- **F3 Raw center:** detect a chalky or fibrous middle, recover by returning the batch to boiling water for 30 to 90 seconds, then shocking again.
- **F4 Watery finish:** detect puddles under the vegetables, recover by draining longer and drying on towels before saucing, freezing, or sauteing.
- **F5 Ice bath warms up:** detect lukewarm bath water, recover by adding more ice or replacing half the water with cold water.

## Verification

A test piece is bright, cold, and tender enough to bite through while still holding its shape.

## Variations

- Asparagus: peel thick stalks if fibrous, then blanch until the green deepens.
- Green beans: cook until the bean bends slightly but still snaps.
- Broccoli: separate florets from thick stems, since stems usually need more time.
- No ice available: use the coldest running water, but expect slightly less color control.

## Safety & privacy

Medium risk from boiling water and steam. Keep pot handles turned inward, lift food with a spider or tongs, and move slowly between the stove, sink, and ice bath.
