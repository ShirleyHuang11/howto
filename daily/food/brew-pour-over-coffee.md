## Goal

A clean, sweet cup of pour-over coffee from a cone dripper, with the two variables that matter (grind and ratio) under control and the ritual under five active minutes.

## Preconditions

- A cone dripper (V60, Kalita, or similar), its matching paper filters, and a mug or server.
- Coffee at roughly 1:16 by weight (for one mug: about 15 g coffee to 240 g water), ground medium-fine like table salt. A scale makes this recipe repeatable; without one, use a measured scoop and accept drift.
- Water just off the boil, around 92 to 96 °C: boil, then wait 30 seconds (`daily/food/boil-water`).

## Steps

1. **Rinse the paper filter with hot water, then dump the rinse water.** This washes papery taste out and preheats the dripper and mug. → *Expect:* wet seated filter, warm vessel, rinse water discarded.
2. **Add the ground coffee and level the bed.** A gentle shake flattens it. → *Expect:* a level bed; scale zeroed with everything on it if you use one.
3. **Bloom: pour about twice the coffee's weight in water (30 g water for 15 g coffee) and wait 30 to 45 seconds.** The bed swells and bubbles as gas escapes; skipping this makes sour, uneven cups. → *Expect:* visible blooming and a swelling bed; all grounds wetted, none dry at the edges.
4. **Pour the remaining water in slow circles, in two or three stages.** Spiral from the center outward and back, keeping the water level steady rather than flooding to the rim; pause between stages to let it draw down. Avoid pouring directly on the filter walls. → *Expect:* controlled pours reaching the target weight; the slurry never near overflow.
5. **Let it draw down completely.** Total brew time from bloom to final drip should land around 2.5 to 3.5 minutes for a mug. → *Expect:* a flat bed of grounds and a finished drip inside that window. Much faster means too coarse; much slower means too fine (see F1 and F2).
6. **Remove the dripper, swirl the cup, and taste before adjusting anything.** ⚠️ hot vessel and dripper: set the dripper on a saucer, not the tablecloth. → *Expect:* a cup you can already evaluate: sour and thin, bitter and harsh, or balanced.

## Decision points

- Tasting sour or weak: grind finer or pour slower next brew (more contact time). Tasting bitter or harsh: grind coarser or pull the temperature down slightly. Change one variable per brew, or you learn nothing.
- No scale: one rounded scoop per mug and count the pour in Mississippi seconds; consistency of method still beats precision you do not have.
- Multiple cups: scale everything linearly, grind a touch coarser for larger doses, and expect a longer draw-down.

## Failure modes & recovery

- **F1 Draw-down finished in under two minutes and the cup is thin:** grind is too coarse for the dose. The current cup is what it is; log it and tighten the grind next round.
- **F2 Stalled bed, four-plus minutes, bitter cup:** too fine, or the pour disturbed the bed into a clogged slurry. Coarsen next time and pour more gently.
- **F3 Overflowed the dripper:** poured too fast past the safe level. Stop, let it draw down, continue humbly; the cup survives, the counter needs a cloth.
- **F4 Grounds in the cup:** filter collapsed or was folded wrong at the seam. Fold along the seam before seating, and rinse it into place.

## Verification

Bloom happened and the bed swelled, total brew time landed in the window, the bed finished flat, and the cup tastes balanced or you know which single variable you are changing next brew.

## Variations

- Flat-bottom drippers (Kalita) are more forgiving of pour technique; the recipe transfers with slightly coarser grind.
- `daily/food/brew-french-press-coffee` is the sibling for people who prefer body over clarity and zero pouring technique.

## Safety & privacy

Medium risk only from near-boiling water handled repeatedly in small pours: kettle spout control, dripper on a stable base, and the saucer landing zone for the dripping cone.
