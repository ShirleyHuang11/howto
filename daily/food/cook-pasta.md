## Goal

Pasta cooked to al dente, seasoned from the water, married to its sauce, and on the table without a starch-glued colander disaster.

## Preconditions

- Dried pasta (about 80 to 100 g per person), a large pot, plenty of water, and salt.
- A sauce planned and ideally already warming in its own pan; pasta waits for no sauce.
- A colander in the sink, and a cup for reserving pasta water (the step everyone skips and every kitchen swears by).

## Steps

1. **Boil abundant water.** Roughly a liter per 100 g of pasta; crowded pasta cooks unevenly and glues. Lid on for speed (`daily/food/boil-water`). → *Expect:* a full rolling boil before any pasta enters.
2. **Salt the water generously.** About a tablespoon per few liters, into the boil. Salted water is the only chance to season the pasta itself; the old line is it should taste like the sea. → *Expect:* visibly salted, briefly calmer, then boiling again.
3. **Add the pasta and stir within the first minute.** Long shapes fan into the pot and soften down; the first-minute stir is what prevents sticking, more than any oil (oil in the water mostly coats the drain). → *Expect:* pasta fully submerged and moving freely after the stir.
4. **Cook at a lively boil, stirring occasionally, and set a timer for two minutes under the packet time.** The packet number lands soft; the check starts early. → *Expect:* timer running; occasional stirs; foam managed by lowering heat slightly or resting a spoon across the pot.
5. **Test by biting.** From the timer's ring, fish a piece every minute: al dente means tender with a slight firm core, no white chalky center. → *Expect:* the bite test, not the clock, makes the call.
6. **Reserve a cup of the cooking water, then drain.** Cup first, always, because the colander move is irreversible. Drain promptly; do not rinse (the surface starch is what lets sauce cling; rinsing is only for cold pasta salads). ⚠️ *Irreversible:* the pour is a steam-and-boiling-water moment; pour away from yourself, face back from the steam. → *Expect:* pasta in the colander, starchy water saved, nobody steamed.
7. **Marry pasta and sauce in the pan, loosening with the reserved water.** Pasta into the sauce (never sauce ladled onto naked pasta on plates, ideally), a splash of the starchy water, and a minute of tossing over heat until the sauce coats and clings. → *Expect:* glossy, coated pasta; the water's starch visibly binding sauce to noodle.
8. **Serve immediately.** Pasta continues softening as it sits; the table waits for pasta, not the reverse. → *Expect:* plates out within minutes, cheese and pepper at the table.

## Decision points

- Fresh pasta: minutes, not packet-dried timelines; it cooks in two to four and the bite test starts almost immediately.
- Stuffed shapes (ravioli, tortellini): gentle boil rather than rolling, or they burst; lift out with a spider rather than colander-dumping.
- One-pot and absorption methods exist and work; this recipe is the classic separated method because it teaches the water, the bite, and the marriage that every variant reuses.

## Failure modes & recovery

- **F1 Glued clump of pasta:** the first-minute stir was skipped. Break it apart gently in the pot; next time, stir early. Deeply fused pasta is a frittata ingredient, not a loss.
- **F2 Overcooked to soft:** no rescue exists; sauce it and serve it humbly, and start the bite test earlier next round. Undercooked, by contrast, fixes in sixty more seconds.
- **F3 Forgot to reserve water before draining:** the sauce loosens acceptably with a splash of hot tap water plus a knob of butter, and the cup lives inside the colander from now on as a physical reminder.
- **F4 Boil-over:** the `daily/food/cook-rice` F4 move: heat down, spoon across, wipe later. Abundant-pot sizing in step 1 is the real prevention.
- **F5 Salted like the sea, tastes like the ocean floor:** sauce unsalted to compensate this time; calibrate the hand next time. Chronic over-salting usually means salting before measuring the water honestly.

## Verification

The pasta passes the bite test at al dente, tastes seasoned on its own, wears its sauce glossily thanks to the reserved water, reached the table promptly, and the stove shows no boil-over archaeology.

## Variations

- `it` practice in one line: more water, more salt, firmer bite, sauce finished in the pan, cheese matched to the sauce rather than universal.
- Gluten-free pasta: foams more and goes from firm to collapse faster; start the bite test three minutes early and stir more gently.

## Safety & privacy

Medium risk is the drain: a full pot of boiling water moving across a kitchen. Clear the path, pour away from the body, keep faces out of the steam column, and let the reserved-cup habit remove any reason to rush.
