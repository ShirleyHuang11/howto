## Goal

Slices of bread soaked through with custard and cooked to a golden-brown surface with a soft, set (not wet) center, served hot.

## Preconditions

- Day-old or stale bread, sliced about 2 cm thick: brioche, challah, or a sturdy white loaf. Staleness is a feature; dry bread drinks custard, fresh bread collapses into mush. If only fresh bread is available, see Decision points.
- Eggs, milk (or half milk, half cream), sugar, a pinch of salt; vanilla and cinnamon optional but standard.
- A wide shallow dish for soaking, a nonstick or well-seasoned pan (or griddle), butter, a spatula.

## Steps

1. **Mix the custard until fully blended.** Per 4 thick slices: 3 eggs, 180 ml milk, 1 tbsp sugar, pinch of salt, splash of vanilla, dusting of cinnamon. Whisk hard until no streaks of white remain; unblended white cooks into rubbery patches on the surface. → *Expect:* a uniform pale custard with cinnamon suspended, not floating in rafts.
2. **Pour the custard into the shallow dish.** It should sit about 1 cm deep so slices can lie flat. → *Expect:* dish wide enough for one or two slices lying fully flat.
3. **Soak each slice on both sides.** Lay a slice in, count to 15-20 per side for properly stale bread (shorter for fresher, up to 40 for very dry). The goal is custard to the center, not a saturated sponge. → *Expect:* slice feels heavy and looks wet through, but still holds its shape when lifted with support under it.
4. **Heat the pan to medium, then medium-low, with butter.** Melt a knob of butter over medium heat until foaming, then back off to medium-low. Medium is the whole game: too hot browns the outside while the center stays raw custard; too cool dries the slice out before it colors. → *Expect:* butter foaming and just fragrant, not browning.
5. **Cook 2-4 minutes per side, flipping once.** Lay slices in without crowding. Lift a corner to check color before flipping. → *Expect:* an even deep golden-brown on the first side; a gentle sizzle throughout, never a hard sear sound.
6. **Press-test the center before plating.** Press the middle lightly with the spatula: it should spring back, not squelch. If it squelches, give each side another minute on lower heat. → *Expect:* springy center, both faces golden, edges set.
7. **Serve immediately, or hold in a low oven.** Straight to plates with butter, syrup, or fruit; for a batch, hold finished slices on a rack in a 100 °C oven, never stacked (stacking steams them limp). → *Expect:* surfaces still slightly crisp at serving; center soft and custardy but fully set.

## Decision points

- Only fresh bread on hand → dry the slices first: 10 minutes in a 120 °C oven, or leave them out overnight. This one step prevents the classic soggy failure.
- Very thick or very dry bread (2.5 cm+, fully staled) → soak longer, up to a minute per side, and finish the batch in a 180 °C oven for 5 minutes to set the deep center.
- Batch for a crowd → use a griddle and the low-oven hold; cook order beats cooking speed.

## Failure modes & recovery

- **F1 Soggy, wet center:** bread too fresh, soak too long, or heat too high (surface done before center set). Return offending slices to a low pan or 180 °C oven for a few minutes; adjust bread dryness next batch.
- **F2 Dry, bready result:** soak too short or bread too thin. Nothing to fix in the pan; extra syrup today, longer soak tomorrow.
- **F3 Burnt outside, raw inside:** heat too high. Drop the burner a full setting, finish current slices in the oven.
- **F4 Rubbery white patches on the surface:** custard was under-whisked. Cosmetic only; whisk to full uniformity next round.
- **F5 Slice falls apart during the flip:** oversoaked or unsupported. Use two spatulas or your hand as a backstop on the next flip; broken pieces still cook fine.

## Verification

Every slice is golden-brown on both faces, the center springs back under a light press with no wet squelch, no slice is burnt or pale, and slices held for serving are still unstacked and slightly crisp.

## Variations

- `fr` pain perdu: the original stale-bread rescue; heavier on cream, often finished with caramelized sugar.
- Savory version: drop the sugar, vanilla, and cinnamon; add pepper and serve with cheese or ham.
- Custard ratio memory hook: about 1 egg per 60 ml of milk scales cleanly in either direction.

## Safety & privacy

Low risk: a moderately hot pan and butter that can spit if the bread is dripping wet (let excess custard drain off each slice before it goes in). Raw-egg hygiene applies to the soaking dish and your hands; wash both after the last slice goes in the pan.
