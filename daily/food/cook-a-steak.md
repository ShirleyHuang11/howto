## Goal

A steak with a deep brown crust and an evenly cooked interior at your target doneness, rested so it stays juicy when cut.

## Preconditions

- A steak at least 2.5 cm thick (thinner steaks overcook before they brown; see Variations).
- A heavy pan, ideally cast iron or carbon steel, and a high smoke-point oil (canola, grapeseed, avocado). Butter alone burns at searing temperature.
- Salt, tongs, an instant-read thermometer, a plate or rack for resting, and a ventilated kitchen: this method makes smoke.

## Steps

1. **Salt early and dry the surface.** Salt both sides generously, then leave the steak uncovered on a rack, in the fridge for 1-24 hours if you planned ahead, or at room temperature for 30-45 minutes if not. Just before cooking, pat every surface bone-dry with paper towels. A wet surface steams instead of searing; drying is the single highest-leverage step. → *Expect:* paper towels come away nearly dry on the final pat; the surface looks tacky, not glossy.
2. **Heat the pan properly.** Empty pan over medium-high for 3-4 minutes, then add a thin film of oil. → *Expect:* the oil shimmers and shows first wisps of smoke. If the oil smokes heavily on contact, the pan is too hot; pull it off heat for 30 seconds.
3. **Lay the steak in, away from you.** Place it down and leave it alone. Moving it early tears the developing crust. → *Expect:* an immediate loud, sustained sizzle. A quiet pan means it was not hot enough; take the steak out, reheat, and restart.
4. **Sear undisturbed, then flip once.** 2-4 minutes per side depending on thickness. Peek by lifting one corner; flip only when the crust releases and is deep brown. → *Expect:* a mahogany crust on the first side; the steak releases without sticking.
5. **Optionally baste after the flip.** Add a knob of butter, smashed garlic, and thyme; tilt the pan and spoon the foaming butter over the steak for the last 1-2 minutes. → *Expect:* foaming (not blackening) butter; the crust darkens and smells nutty.
6. **Pull by thermometer, not by clock.** Probe horizontally into the center. Pull at 48-50 °C for rare, 52-54 °C for medium-rare, 57-60 °C for medium; carryover adds roughly 3 °C while resting. ⚠️ *Irreversible:* overcooking cannot be undone; when in doubt pull early, you can always return it to the pan. → *Expect:* thermometer reads your pull target in the thickest part.
7. **Rest as long as you cooked.** Steak to a warm plate or rack, loosely tented, for about as many minutes as it spent in the pan (5-10 minutes). Cutting immediately dumps the juices onto the board. → *Expect:* only a small puddle on the plate after resting; slices stay juicy.
8. **Slice across the grain and serve.** Identify the direction of the muscle fibers and cut perpendicular to them. → *Expect:* short fibers in each slice; tender chew even on cheaper cuts.

## Decision points

- Steak thicker than 4 cm → reverse sear: 120 °C oven to about 8 °C below pull target, then a short hard sear. Pan-only will burn the crust before the center cooks.
- No thermometer → use the finger-poke comparison only as a fallback, and cut into the thickest part discreetly rather than serve unknown doneness.
- Smoke alarm territory → open windows and run the hood before the pan gets hot, not after.

## Failure modes & recovery

- **F1 Gray band, no crust:** pan was too cold or surface was wet. Finish cooking anyway, then note it: next time dry harder and preheat longer.
- **F2 Crust burning before the center cooks:** heat too high or steak too thick for pan-only. Drop the heat, or transfer the pan to a 180 °C oven to finish.
- **F3 Stuck to the pan:** flipped too early. Wait 30 more seconds; a properly seared steak self-releases. Prying early leaves the crust behind.
- **F4 Overshot the temperature:** slice thin, serve immediately, and lean on a pan sauce; do not try to "rest it back".
- **F5 Oil ignites:** kill the heat, slide a lid over the pan. Never water, never carry a flaming pan.

## Verification

Crust is deep brown on both faces, the center reads the target doneness on a thermometer (or shows the target color when cut), the steak rested at least as long as it seared, and slices are cut across the grain with juice retained in the meat rather than pooled on the board.

## Variations

- Thin steaks (under 2 cm): highest heat, about 1 minute per side, no rest longer than 3 minutes; accept edge-to-edge medium.
- Grill instead of pan: same drying, flipping, and thermometer rules; lid down between flips for thick cuts.
- `fr` butter-heavy bistro style: baste continuously from the flip onward and serve with the pan butter spooned over.

## Safety & privacy

Medium risk: splattering hot oil, a pan handle at 200 °C plus, and heavy smoke. Keep a lid within reach for a flare-up, use a dry towel or mitt on the handle every time (a habit, because you will forget once), and keep the raw-meat cutting board and tongs away from ready-to-eat food until washed.
