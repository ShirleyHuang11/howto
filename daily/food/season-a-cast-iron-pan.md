## Goal

A cast-iron pan carries a hard, even, black polymerized oil layer that resists rust and releases food, built up through repeated thin-coat oven bakes and maintained by daily care.

## Preconditions

- A cast-iron pan: new, stripped, or rusty-but-sound (deep pitting cannot be seasoned over).
- A neutral high-smoke-point oil: grapeseed, canola, or flaxseed. Not olive oil, not butter.
- An oven that reaches 230-250 °C, paper towels, dish soap, and a kitchen with some ventilation (the bake produces smoke and smell).

## Steps

1. **Scrub the pan to bare, clean metal.** Hot water, dish soap, and a stiff brush; for rust, scour with steel wool or coarse salt until the orange is gone. Soap is fine here and in daily care; the "no soap ever" rule dates from lye-based soaps. → *Expect:* uniform gray metal (or intact old seasoning), no orange patches, no food residue.
2. **Dry it completely on the stove.** Towel dry, then 2-3 minutes over medium heat until every trace of moisture flashes off. Water left in the pores becomes rust under the new oil. → *Expect:* pan visibly dry and warm to the touch through a towel.
3. **Apply oil, then wipe almost all of it off.** Rub a small spoonful of oil over every surface (inside, outside, handle), then take a clean paper towel and wipe as if you made a mistake and want it all back off. The film that remains is the correct amount. Thick oil bakes into sticky pooled patches, and this over-application is the single most common seasoning failure. → *Expect:* pan looks barely damp with a matte sheen; the towel comes away lightly oiled, no shiny streaks or drips on the pan.
4. **Bake upside down at 230-250 °C for 1 hour.** Pan inverted on the middle rack, foil or a tray on the rack below to catch any drips. Inverting prevents pooling in the cooking surface. → *Expect:* light smoke and a hot-oil smell partway through; this is polymerization working, not a problem, but run the vent fan.
5. **Cool in the oven, then inspect.** Turn the oven off and leave the pan 30+ minutes. → *Expect:* surface darker than before, dry and hard to the fingertip, not tacky.
6. **Repeat the oil-wipe-bake cycle 2-3 more times.** One coat is a primer, not seasoning. Each round adds depth and evenness. → *Expect:* a progressively darker, semi-gloss black surface after 3-4 total rounds.
7. **Adopt the daily routine that maintains it.** After each cooking use: rinse hot with a brush (soap if greasy), dry on the burner for a minute, wipe on a few drops of oil while warm. This 90-second habit does more than any oven session. → *Expect:* pan stored bone-dry with a whisper of oil, never wet, never soaking in the sink.

## Decision points

- Old pan with flaking or gummy patches → strip first (oven self-clean cycle, lye bath, or long scour), then season from bare metal; seasoning over flakes locks the problem in.
- Flaxseed oil chosen → hardest finish but known to flake if applied thick; be even stricter on the wipe-off step.
- Only a weak oven (< 200 °C) available → stovetop seasoning works: heat the wiped pan until it just smokes, cool, repeat; less even, acceptable in a pinch.

## Failure modes & recovery

- **F1 Sticky or tacky surface after baking:** the coat was too thick → scrub the sticky patches with a scouring pad, re-oil with an aggressive wipe-off, re-bake.
- **F2 Rust spots appearing in storage:** pan was put away damp → scour the rust out, dry on the burner, oil, and do one oven round; store with a paper towel inside if the cabinet is humid.
- **F3 Seasoning flaking into food:** coats were thick or the base was dirty → not harmful to eat, but scrub loose flakes off and rebuild with 2-3 thin rounds.
- **F4 Patchy dull spots after cooking acidic food:** tomato or wine simmer stripped young seasoning → normal; wipe, oil, one stovetop or oven round; avoid long acidic simmers until the seasoning is a few months old.

## Verification

Water beads on the cooled surface instead of soaking in, a fried egg releases with modest oil, the surface is uniformly dark with no tacky or orange spots, and a paper towel wiped across it comes away clean, not black or greasy.

## Variations

- Carbon-steel pans: identical chemistry and procedure; they season faster and strip faster.
- Grill pans and dutch-oven exteriors: same routine; enameled interiors need none of this.

## Safety & privacy

Medium risk: a 240 °C pan looks identical to a cold one, so leave a dry mitt draped over the handle as a reminder for the entire cool-down. The bake smokes; ventilate and expect smoke-detector sensitivity. Oil-damp paper towels can self-heat in a pile; spread them out or wet them before binning.
