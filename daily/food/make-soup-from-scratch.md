## Goal

A pot of well-seasoned soup built from raw ingredients: aromatics, a main vegetable or protein, and liquid, finished either blended-smooth or chunky.

## Preconditions

- A heavy-bottomed pot with a lid, cutting board, sharp knife, wooden spoon, ladle.
- Aromatics (onion at minimum; garlic, carrot, celery as available), a main ingredient (vegetables, beans, or meat), fat (oil or butter), and liquid (stock, broth, or water).
- Salt and pepper; acid on hand for finishing (lemon, vinegar).
- If blending: an immersion blender, or a countertop blender and patience.

## Steps

1. **Prep everything before heat.** Dice the aromatics small and uniform; cut the main ingredient into even bite-size pieces so they cook at the same rate. → *Expect:* all cutting done, board cleared, pot empty on the stove.
2. **Sweat the aromatics in fat over medium heat, 5-10 minutes.** Onion first with a pinch of salt to draw moisture; garlic only in the last minute (it burns fast and burnt garlic ruins the pot). Do not brown unless you want a darker, roastier soup. → *Expect:* onions translucent and soft, glossy, fragrant, not colored.
3. **Add hardy ingredients and stir 2-3 minutes.** Root vegetables, dried spices, tomato paste if using; a short fry in the aromatic fat wakes them up. → *Expect:* spices fragrant, paste darkened a shade, nothing sticking hard.
4. **Add liquid to cover by 2-3 cm and bring to a boil, then drop to a simmer.** Scrape the pot bottom as the liquid goes in; those browned bits are flavor. → *Expect:* contents submerged, first boil reached, then lazy bubbles only.
5. **Simmer, lid ajar, until the slowest ingredient is tender.** Roughly 20-30 min for vegetables, 45+ for dried legumes (pre-soaked), longer for tough meat cuts. Skim any grey foam early on. → *Expect:* a knife slides through the largest piece with no resistance.
6. **Choose the finish.** [BRANCH: blend smooth → next step | leave chunky → skip to step 8] → *Expect:* a decision matched to the ingredients (silky for squash or tomato, chunky for minestrone-style).
7. **Blend.** Immersion blender: kill the heat, keep the head fully submerged, work in slow passes. Countertop blender: ⚠️ *Irreversible:* hot liquid erupts in a sealed blender; blend in half-full batches, lid vent open, towel over the top. → *Expect:* uniform velvety texture, no eruption, no splatter burns.
8. **Season late, in rounds.** Now add most of the salt: taste, add, stir, wait 30 seconds, taste again; repeat until flavors pop. Salting fully at the start overshoots because the liquid reduces. Finish with black pepper and a small squeeze of acid. → *Expect:* each round tastes noticeably better; the acid makes it taste brighter, not sour.
9. **Adjust body and serve hot.** Too thick: loosen with hot stock or water. Too thin: simmer uncovered 10 min, or blend a cupful and stir it back. → *Expect:* the soup coats a spoon the way you want; ladled without drips down the pot side.

## Decision points

- Liquid is water, not stock → compensate: brown the aromatics harder, add a parmesan rind, bay leaf, or a bouillon cube, and expect to season more at the end.
- Delicate additions (greens, pasta, canned beans, dairy) → stir in only for the final 2-10 minutes; they disintegrate or curdle in a long simmer.
- Soup tastes flat despite enough salt → it needs acid or fat, not more salt: lemon, vinegar, or a swirl of cream/olive oil.

## Failure modes & recovery

- **F1 Burnt fond on the pot bottom:** black flecks and an acrid smell → do NOT scrape; immediately pour the soup into a clean pot, leaving the burnt layer behind, and taste before continuing.
- **F2 Oversalted:** taste is harsh → dilute with unsalted liquid and add a starchy ingredient (potato chunks, rice) to absorb; next time season late.
- **F3 Blender eruption:** hot soup on the ceiling → prevention is the fix (half batches, vented lid); if it happens, kill the blender, cool, wipe, continue in smaller batches.
- **F4 Mushy vegetables, chunky style intended:** simmer went too long → embrace it: blend half for body and call it rustic, or add a fresh quick-cooking batch for texture.

## Verification

The slowest ingredient is fork-tender, the seasoning was tasted and adjusted in at least two rounds, texture matches the intended branch (uniformly smooth or distinct pieces), and the pot is at a servable temperature with the burner off or on low.

## Variations

- Creamy finish: temper in cream or coconut milk off the boil to prevent curdling.
- Big-batch: soup improves overnight; cool it uncovered to warm, then refrigerate; most soups freeze well before dairy is added.
- Pressure cooker: same build order, ~1/3 of the simmer time; season entirely after release.

## Safety & privacy

Medium risk: knife work during prep, a heavy pot of scalding liquid, and the blender-eruption hazard called out in step 7. Move the destination bowl to the pot, never carry a full pot one-handed. Cool leftovers within 2 hours before refrigerating. No privacy exposure.
