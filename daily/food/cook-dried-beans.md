## Goal

A pot of dried beans cooked through to uniform creaminess in a seasoned broth, portioned for immediate use and storage.

## Preconditions

- 500 g dried beans, ideally from a bag less than a year or two old; ancient beans may never soften.
- A large heavy pot with a lid, salt, and optionally aromatics (halved onion, garlic cloves, bay leaf).
- A 2-15 hour lead time depending on the soak choice, plus 1-2.5 hours of mostly unattended simmering.

## Steps

1. **Sort and rinse.** Spread the beans on a tray, pick out stones, dirt clods, and shriveled or broken beans, then rinse in a colander. Small stones are genuinely common in dried beans and cost a tooth. → *Expect:* clean beans, a small reject pile, clear rinse water.
2. **Choose a soak.** [BRANCH: overnight soak: cover with 5+ cm of water, 8-15 h on the counter | quick soak: boil 2 minutes, lid on, off heat 1 h | no soak: proceed directly, add ~50% simmering time] All three produce good beans; soaking mainly buys time and evenness. → *Expect:* soaked beans visibly swollen to roughly double; no-soak path proceeds with dry beans and more patience.
3. **Salt the soak or the pot from the start.** About 1 tablespoon per liter in the soak water, and season the cooking water too. The old rule that salt toughens beans and must wait until the end is a myth; brined beans cook up with better-seasoned, more intact skins. What genuinely slows softening is acid (tomato, vinegar), which waits until the beans are tender. → *Expect:* cooking water that tastes pleasantly salty, like weak soup; no acidic ingredients in the pot yet.
4. **Bring to a boil, then drop to a bare simmer.** Fresh water to 3-4 cm over the beans, aromatics in, boil, then reduce until only a few lazy bubbles rise, lid ajar. For kidney beans (and other red beans), hold a hard rolling boil for the first 10 minutes before reducing; see Safety. → *Expect:* an occasional slow bubble, not a churn; hard-boiling churns beans into split skins and cloudy broth.
5. **Skim, then leave it mostly alone.** Skim the gray-white foam in the first 15 minutes, top up with hot water if beans breach the surface, stir rarely. → *Expect:* beans staying submerged, broth clearing after the skim.
6. **Start testing at 1 hour, then every 15 minutes.** Blow on a spooned bean (thin skin wrinkles when done), then eat it: creamy to the center with no chalky core. Age, variety, and hard water swing the total anywhere from 1 to 2.5+ hours. Judge by texture, never by the clock. → *Expect:* five tested beans in a row all creamy; one chalky bean means keep going.
7. **Finish in the broth.** Off heat, adjust salt, add any acid now if the recipe calls for it, and let the beans rest 15-30 minutes in their liquid; they season and firm slightly as they cool. ⚠️ *Irreversible:* draining the broth discards the best part; it is the base for the soup or the storage liquid, so decide before the colander. → *Expect:* seasoned beans resting submerged in tasty broth.
8. **Store submerged.** Refrigerate beans in their broth (4-5 days) or freeze in portions with enough broth to cover (3+ months). Beans stored dry of liquid crack and stale. → *Expect:* containers with beans fully under liquid, labeled and dated.

## Decision points

- Beans still hard after 3 hours → old stock or very hard water; a pinch (1/4 teaspoon) of baking soda in the pot speeds softening, or switch to a pressure cooker; note the supplier and buy fresher next time.
- Cooking for a recipe with tomatoes (chili, stew) → cook beans fully tender first, add the tomato element after.
- Pressure cooker available → unsoaked beans in 25-45 minutes at high pressure; never fill past half for beans (they foam).

## Failure modes & recovery

- **F1 Beans blown out and mushy outside, some still firm inside:** simmer too hard → drop the heat to barely-bubbling; uneven batches usually mean mixed-age beans, cook to the firmest one and use the soft for refried or soup.
- **F2 Scorched bottom:** liquid ran low → do not stir; pour the unstuck beans into a new pot, leave the burnt layer behind, add hot water and continue.
- **F3 Persistent chalky center after hours:** ancient beans or acid added early → baking-soda pinch and continued simmering may rescue; otherwise blend into soup where texture hides.
- **F4 Sour smell after a countertop soak in hot weather:** fermentation started → if faint, rinse thoroughly and cook normally; if plainly sour, discard and re-soak in the fridge next time.

## Verification

Five consecutive sampled beans are creamy to the center with intact skins, the broth tastes seasoned, no acid was added before tenderness, kidney beans got their 10-minute hard boil, and all stored portions sit submerged in liquid.

## Variations

- Black beans and chickpeas: same procedure; chickpeas trend longer and love the overnight brine.
- Lentils and split peas: no soak, 20-45 minutes; this recipe's timing does not apply.
- `mx` frijoles de olla: onion, garlic, and a sprig of epazote in the pot; serve beans in their broth.

## Safety & privacy

Medium risk for one specific reason: raw and undercooked kidney beans contain phytohaemagglutinin, and slow-cooking them below boiling (as in a low crockpot) can leave enough toxin to cause hours of vomiting. The fix is simple: boil kidney and other red beans hard for 10 minutes at the start. Beyond that, it is a long-running stove; keep the simmer attended enough that it never runs dry.
