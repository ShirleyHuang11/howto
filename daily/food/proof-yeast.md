## Goal

Check whether yeast is alive by hydrating it in warm sweetened water and watching for foam before mixing dough.

## Preconditions

- Active dry yeast or uncertain instant yeast is ready and smells fresh.
- 100 to 110 f water, sugar, bowl, and spoon are ready.
- A clean work surface is available.
- Storage or serving container is ready.

## Steps

1. **Warm water.** Use 100 to 110 F water that feels warm, not hot → *Expect:* water feels like a warm bath
2. **Add sugar.** Stir in a pinch or teaspoon of sugar → *Expect:* sugar dissolves
3. **Sprinkle yeast.** Scatter yeast over the surface → *Expect:* granules hydrate evenly
4. **Wait.** Let sit 5 to 10 minutes → *Expect:* bubbles begin forming
5. **Read foam.** Look for a creamy cap and yeasty smell → *Expect:* mixture is foamy and tan
6. **Use promptly.** Stir into recipe liquid → *Expect:* foam disperses
7. **Reject flat yeast.** If flat after 10 minutes, do not add it. ⚠️ *Irreversible:* dead yeast wastes the dough batch → *Expect:* failed cup stays still

## Decision points

- Instant yeast known fresh -> proofing is optional.
- Water over 120 F -> start over cooler.
- Cold room -> allow the full 10 minutes.
- No sugar recipe -> a pinch for proofing is fine.

## Failure modes & recovery

- **F1 Dead yeast:** detect no foam -> discard and open fresh yeast.
- **F2 Hot water:** detect wrist-hot water -> replace before adding yeast.
- **F3 Clumps:** detect dry pellets -> stir gently and wait.
- **F4 Weak foam:** detect tiny bubbles only -> use if it smells yeasty, otherwise replace.

## Verification

mixture is foamy and smells like bread or beer

## Variations

- Fresh yeast: crumble and expect faster dissolving.
- Instant yeast: mix with flour if fresh.
- Enriched dough: proof to avoid wasting rich ingredients.
- Sourdough: feed starter instead of proofing yeast.

## Safety & privacy

Low risk; the main cost is wasted ingredients and time.
Do not guess with old yeast when expensive flour, butter, or long rise time is involved.
