## Goal

A bowl of smooth, seasoned mashed potatoes: fluffy, not gluey, hot at serving time, with the dairy fully absorbed.

## Preconditions

- Starchy or all-purpose potatoes: russets mash fluffiest, Yukon Golds mash creamier. Avoid waxy salad potatoes (red, fingerling); they turn to paste.
- A pot large enough to cover the potatoes with water, a colander, a masher or ricer (never a blender or food processor), a small saucepan or microwave for the milk.
- Butter, milk (or cream), salt. Roughly 60 g butter and 120 ml milk per kilo of potatoes is a sane starting ratio.

## Steps

1. **Peel and cut evenly.** Peel (or leave skins on Yukon Golds if you like texture), cut into chunks of equal size, about 4 cm. → *Expect:* uniform chunks; no piece more than twice the size of another, so they finish together.
2. **Start in cold, well-salted water.** Potatoes into the pot, cold water to cover by 2 cm, salt until the water tastes like the sea (about 1 tbsp per 2 liters). Starting cold cooks them evenly; dropping into boiling water leaves hard centers. → *Expect:* chunks submerged in visibly salted cold water before the burner goes on.
3. **Simmer until knife-tender.** Bring to a boil, then reduce to a steady simmer. Hard boiling batters the outsides apart before the centers cook. Check at 15 minutes. → *Expect:* a paring knife slides into the largest chunk with no resistance, typically 15-20 minutes.
4. **Warm the milk and butter while they simmer.** Milk to steaming (not boiling) with the butter melting into it. Cold milk drops the potatoes' temperature and they absorb it badly. → *Expect:* steaming, buttery milk standing by when the potatoes are done.
5. **Drain thoroughly, then dry the potatoes in the hot pot.** Drain in the colander, return potatoes to the empty pot over low heat for 1-2 minutes, shaking. ⚠️ *Irreversible:* the drain is the scald hazard of this recipe: pour away from your body, colander seated in the sink first. → *Expect:* visible steam leaving the chunks; surfaces look chalky-dry. Wet potatoes make wet, bland mash.
6. **Mash off the heat, dairy in stages.** [BRANCH: ricer for the smoothest result | hand masher for rustic texture] Mash first, then fold in the warm milk-butter in two or three additions, stirring just until absorbed. Overworking ruptures starch and turns the mash to glue. → *Expect:* smooth mash that holds a soft peak; each milk addition disappears before the next.
7. **Season and taste.** Salt in pinches, taste after each; black pepper if wanted. → *Expect:* it tastes seasoned, not flat; a final knob of butter on top for serving is optional but correct.

## Decision points

- Potatoes must wait before serving → hold them covered in the pot over the lowest heat or over a pan of barely simmering water; loosen with a splash of warm milk at serving.
- Only waxy potatoes available → mash minimally by hand and accept a denser, chunkier result; do not rice waxy potatoes.
- Dairy-free requirement → warm olive oil plus some of the reserved starchy cooking water substitutes surprisingly well.

## Failure modes & recovery

- **F1 Gluey, elastic mash:** caused by overworking or by a blender. Not fully reversible; fold in more hot butter and serve, or repurpose as the topping for a shepherd's pie where texture matters less.
- **F2 Lumpy mash:** undercooked chunks. Fish out hard lumps, or press the whole batch through the ricer; next time cut evenly and knife-test the largest piece.
- **F3 Watery, bland mash:** skipped the drying step or under-salted water. Dry the finished mash over low heat while stirring, then re-season; salt added only at the end never penetrates like salted cooking water does.
- **F4 Cold mash at the table:** cold milk or a long unheld wait. Reheat gently with a splash of warm milk, stirring minimally.

## Verification

The mash is smooth (or intentionally rustic), holds a soft peak on a spoon without weeping liquid, tastes distinctly seasoned, and is steaming hot at serving. No hard lumps on a fork drawn through the center of the bowl.

## Variations

- `us-thanksgiving`: double the butter, use cream, and hold in a slow cooker on low for the crowd version.
- Garlic mash: simmer 3-4 peeled cloves with the potatoes; they mash in invisibly.
- Skin-on: Yukon Golds only, scrubbed well; russet skins are too papery to mash pleasantly.

## Safety & privacy

Medium risk from one step: draining a large pot of boiling water. Seat the colander in the sink before lifting the pot, pour away from your torso, and keep children clear of the path. Everything after the drain is low risk.
