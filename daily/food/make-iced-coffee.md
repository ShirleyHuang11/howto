## Goal

Make cold coffee that tastes strong enough after ice, milk, or sweetener are added.

## Preconditions

- Coffee, water, ice, a glass, and either a brewer for hot coffee or a jar for cold brew.
- Optional milk, cream, syrup, sugar, or sweetener.
- Clean ice that does not taste like freezer odors.

## Steps

1. **Choose the method.** [BRANCH: hot-bloom-over-ice | cold brew] Use hot-bloom-over-ice for coffee now, or cold brew for a smoother drink prepared 12 to 18 hours ahead. → *Expect:* method fits your timeline.
2. **Plan for dilution.** For hot coffee over ice, brew stronger by using about 1.5 times the usual coffee or replacing half the brew water with ice in the glass. → *Expect:* the recipe accounts for melting ice.
3. **Use good ice.** Fill the serving glass with fresh ice, or use coffee ice cubes if you have them. → *Expect:* glass is cold and ice smells neutral.
4. **Bloom hot grounds if brewing now.** Wet grounds with a small amount of hot water for 30 seconds, then finish brewing directly over ice or brew strong coffee into a separate cup. → *Expect:* coffee aroma opens up and hot liquid starts melting some ice.
5. **Strain cold brew if using it.** Mix coarse grounds with cold water, steep in the fridge, then strain through a filter or fine mesh. → *Expect:* concentrate is clear enough to pour without grit.
6. **Cool before adding milk when possible.** Pour hot-brewed coffee over ice and stir until the glass chills, then add milk or cream. → *Expect:* milk stays smooth and does not taste cooked.
7. **Sweeten in the form that dissolves.** Use syrup in cold coffee, or dissolve sugar in the hot coffee before it hits the ice. → *Expect:* sweetness is even, with no sugar pile on the bottom.
8. **Adjust strength at the end.** Add more coffee concentrate for stronger flavor or more cold water for a lighter drink. → *Expect:* drink tastes balanced after the final stir.

## Decision points

- Coffee tastes watery → reduce brew water, use more coffee, or freeze leftover coffee into cubes.
- Drink tastes bitter → use colder water for cold brew, shorten hot contact time, or add milk after chilling.
- No time to chill → brew a small strong amount directly over a full glass of ice.
- Adding dairy alternative → shake the carton first and add after the coffee is cold to reduce separation.

## Failure modes & recovery

- **F1 Watery coffee:** detect pale color and weak flavor, recover by adding concentrate or a small shot of strong brewed coffee.
- **F2 Grit in drink:** detect sediment on the tongue or bottom of glass, recover by filtering again through paper.
- **F3 Sour cold brew:** detect sharp under-extracted taste, recover by steeping longer or grinding slightly finer next batch.
- **F4 Melted all the ice:** detect lukewarm coffee, recover by chilling briefly or pouring over a fresh glass of ice.

## Verification

After stirring, the drink is cold, the coffee flavor is still distinct, and no undissolved sweetener or grit remains at the bottom.

## Variations

- Japanese-style iced coffee: brew hot coffee directly onto a measured amount of ice.
- Cold brew concentrate: use roughly 1 part coffee to 4 to 5 parts water by weight, then dilute to taste.
- No brewer: steep grounds in a jar, then strain carefully through a paper filter.

## Safety & privacy

Low risk. Handle hot water carefully, keep dairy refrigerated, and discard coffee with milk if it has sat out for more than 2 hours.
