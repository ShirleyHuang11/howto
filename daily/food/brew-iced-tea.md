## Goal

A pitcher of clear, full-flavored iced tea, properly chilled and not watered down, ready to pour over ice.

## Preconditions

- Tea (bags or loose leaf), kettle or pot for boiling water (hot method), a heatproof brewing vessel, a serving pitcher, plenty of ice.
- For the cold-brew branch: a fridge with room for the pitcher and 8+ hours of lead time.
- Optional: sugar or simple syrup, lemon.

## Steps

1. **Choose the method.** [BRANCH: hot-brew double strength (ready in ~30 min) | cold-brew (smoothest, near-zero bitterness, needs 8-12 h)] Cold-brew: skip to step 6. → *Expect:* method matched to the deadline.
2. **Brew at double strength.** Use twice the tea per volume of water, not double the steeping time: e.g. 4 bags per 500 ml where you would use 2 for hot tea. The ice in step 5 will dilute it back to normal. Longer steeping extracts bitterness, more leaf extracts flavor. → *Expect:* correct leaf-to-water ratio measured before pouring.
3. **Pour water just off the boil over the tea and steep 3-5 minutes.** Black tea: boiling water, 4-5 min. Green tea: let the water cool ~2 min first (80 °C) and steep 2-3 min, or it turns bitter. Set a timer; "a few extra minutes" is the classic bitterness mistake. → *Expect:* deep-colored concentrate at the timer, bags/leaves removed promptly, not squeezed hard (squeezing wrings out tannin).
4. **Cool the concentrate 10-15 minutes at room temperature.** If sweetening, stir sugar in now while it is hot; sugar will not dissolve in cold tea (or use simple syrup later). → *Expect:* concentrate warm, not steaming; sugar fully dissolved if added.
5. **Pour the concentrate over a pitcher packed with ice.** The melting ice performs the dilution to drinking strength; stir until the ice stops melting fast. Top up with cold water if it still tastes strong. → *Expect:* tea at drinking strength and cold, roughly doubling the volume; proceed to step 8.
6. **Cold-brew: combine tea and cold water at normal strength in the pitcher.** Regular ratio (1 bag or 1 tsp loose leaf per 250 ml), cold water, lid on. → *Expect:* leaves submerged, pitcher lidded and in the fridge.
7. **Steep refrigerated 8-12 hours, then strain.** Cold water never extracts the harsh tannins, so overshooting to 12+ hours is forgiving. → *Expect:* smooth, clear, aromatic tea; remove bags or strain leaves.
8. **Serve over fresh ice and refrigerate the rest.** Lemon or syrup to taste at the glass. → *Expect:* clear (not cloudy) tea in the glass; pitcher back in the fridge, best within 2-3 days.

## Decision points

- Needed in under 10 minutes → hot-brew triple strength over a full pitcher of ice; accept a slightly flatter flavor.
- Serving a crowd hours later → keep concentrate and dilution separate until serving; pre-diluted tea over-waters as ice melts in the pitcher.
- Herbal or fruit tisanes → no tannin to fear: steep hot 5-10 min or cold-brew, hard to ruin.

## Failure modes & recovery

- **F1 Bitter, mouth-drying tea:** oversteeped or water too hot (green tea) → dilute heavily with cold water and serve with lemon and sweetener, which mask tannin; next batch, more leaf, less time.
- **F2 Cloudy iced tea:** hot-brewed black tea shocked by rapid chilling; tannins precipitate → cosmetic only; a splash of boiling water stirred in re-clears it, or switch to cold-brew, which never clouds.
- **F3 Watery, thin tea:** brewed at single strength then iced → treat the pitcher as concentrate: brew a second double-strength batch and combine, or drop in more tea bags for a slow cold top-up.
- **F4 Undissolved sugar sludge at the bottom:** sugar added to cold tea → warm a cup of the tea, dissolve the sugar in it, stir back in; next time sweeten hot or keep simple syrup on hand.

## Verification

The tea tastes at drinking strength after the ice melt (not sharp, not watery), is clear in the glass, cold throughout, and the leaves/bags are out of the liquid so it will not keep extracting.

## Variations

- Sun tea: jar of tea and water a few hours in direct sun; pleasant but a mild food-safety gray zone (lukewarm water grows bacteria); refrigerate promptly and drink same-day.
- Sweet tea (US South): dissolve a heavy dose of sugar into the hot concentrate; syrupy by design.
- Sparkling: dilute concentrate with cold sparkling water over ice instead of still.

## Safety & privacy

Low risk; the only hazards are boiling water in the hot branch (see `daily/boil-water`) and holding brewed tea unrefrigerated for many hours. Never pour boiling concentrate into a cold glass pitcher; thermal shock cracks it. Use a heatproof vessel for the hot steps. No privacy exposure.
