## Goal

A smooth, seasoned chickpea and tahini dip is made with a creamy texture and balanced lemon, salt, garlic, and olive oil.

## Preconditions

- Cooked chickpeas, either canned and rinsed or home-cooked until very soft.
- Tahini, lemon juice, garlic, salt, olive oil, and ice water.
- Food processor or powerful blender.
- Bowl, spatula, and tasting spoon.

## Steps

1. **Soften the chickpeas if needed.** If canned chickpeas feel firm, simmer them in water for 10 minutes, then drain [BRANCH: canned | home-cooked] → *Expect:* a chickpea squashes easily between two fingers.
2. **Decide on peeling.** Peel chickpeas only if you want the smoothest result and have the patience; otherwise use them as-is → *Expect:* you know whether the batch is speed-first or silk-first.
3. **Whip tahini first.** Process tahini, lemon juice, garlic, and salt before adding chickpeas → *Expect:* the tahini lightens, thickens, and turns paler.
4. **Loosen with ice water.** With the machine running, drizzle in ice water 1 tablespoon at a time → *Expect:* the tahini mixture changes from pasty to fluffy and glossy.
5. **Add chickpeas gradually.** Add chickpeas in two or three additions, scraping the bowl between additions → *Expect:* the paste becomes uniform with no whole chickpeas at the edge.
6. **Blend longer than seems necessary.** Run the processor for 2 to 4 minutes, stopping to scrape as needed → *Expect:* the hummus looks smoother and slightly lighter after each minute.
7. **Adjust seasoning.** Add salt for flatness, lemon for heaviness, tahini for nuttiness, or ice water for looseness → *Expect:* a spoonful tastes bright, savory, and spreadable.
8. **Rest and finish.** Let the hummus sit 10 minutes, then spoon into a bowl and top with olive oil or spices → *Expect:* the flavor settles and the surface holds a shallow swirl.

## Decision points

- Hummus tastes bitter → your tahini may be harsh; add lemon, salt, and a little olive oil rather than more tahini.
- Hummus is too thick → add ice water in teaspoons, not oil, for a lighter texture.
- Garlic tastes sharp → use less raw garlic, remove the germ, or steep minced garlic in lemon juice before blending.
- You want extra smooth hummus → peel chickpeas or pass the finished hummus through a fine mesh sieve.

## Failure modes & recovery

- **F1 Grainy hummus:** detect sandy texture, recover by blending longer with more ice water and using softer chickpeas next time.
- **F2 Seized tahini:** detect a stiff paste after lemon is added, recover by slowly processing in ice water until it loosens.
- **F3 Bland flavor:** detect chickpea paste with no lift, recover with salt first, then lemon, then garlic.
- **F4 Watery hummus:** detect a loose dip that will not mound, recover by blending in more chickpeas or chilling uncovered for 20 minutes.
- **F5 Over-garlicked batch:** detect harsh heat after resting, recover by blending in more chickpeas, tahini, and lemon.

## Verification

A spoonful is smooth enough to spread without tearing bread, tastes seasoned, and leaves no dry chickpea bits on the tongue.

## Variations

- Cumin hummus: add a small pinch of ground cumin with the salt.
- Roasted garlic: replace raw garlic with roasted cloves for a sweeter flavor.
- Extra lemon: add zest as well as juice when the dip needs aroma without more acidity.
- Warm hummus: serve right after blending with warm chickpeas and a softer texture.

## Safety & privacy

Low risk. Refrigerate leftovers within 2 hours, use clean utensils for serving, and discard if it smells sour, fizzy, or visibly moldy.
