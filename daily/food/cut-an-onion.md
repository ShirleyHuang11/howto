## Goal

An onion cut into even dice (or slices) with all fingers intact, minimal tears, and the board clear.

## Preconditions

- An onion, a *sharp* chef's knife (dull knives slip — they cause more cuts than sharp ones), and a stable cutting board.
- Board stabilized: a damp cloth or paper towel underneath stops it skating.

## Steps

1. **Cut the onion in half pole-to-pole (through the root).** Stem end to root end, not around the equator. → *Expect:* two halves, each with the root intact at one end.
2. **Peel each half.** Trim the stem (non-root) tip, lift the papery skin plus the first leathery layer from that corner. Leave the root on — it holds the layers together for every following cut. → *Expect:* naked halves, roots still attached.
3. **Place a half flat-side down.** The flat face on the board is your stability; never cut a rounded, rocking piece. → *Expect:* half sits dead stable under light pressure.
4. **Make horizontal cuts toward the root (for a dice), stopping short of it.** Knife parallel to the board, 1–2 cuts through the half's height, fingertips of the holding hand *on top*, well above the blade path. → *Expect:* layered half, still hinged together at the root.
5. **Make vertical lengthwise cuts toward the root, again stopping short.** Spacing = your dice size. Holding hand in the **claw**: fingertips curled under, knuckles forward guiding the flat of the blade. → *Expect:* a fan of strips, root hinge intact, knuckles — not fingertips — nearest the blade.
6. **Slice across the fan to produce dice.** Claw hand walks backward; the blade's flat rides against the knuckles; rock the knife, tip staying low. → *Expect:* even dice falling away; the hand retreats ahead of every cut.
7. **Discard the root nub, sweep dice aside with the knife's *spine*** (edge-sweeping dulls it). → *Expect:* board holding a pile of even dice and one root nub for the compost.

## Decision points

- Slices/rings instead of dice → skip steps 4–5: cut crosswise off the fan (half-moons), or slice an unhalved peeled onion across (rings — mind the rocking, flat a side first).
- Tears getting bad → chill the onion 15 min beforehand, keep the root intact till last (most of the tear-gas enzymes live there), and cut with ventilation on; a sharp knife crushes fewer cells and gasses less.
- Very large batch → do all halves through step 5 first, then all the cross-cuts — batching stages is faster and steadier.

## Failure modes & recovery

- **F1 Board skating:** stop, wet-cloth under the board; a moving board is the setup for every knife accident.
- **F2 Layers slipping apart mid-cut:** the root was trimmed too early — regroup the layers, cut what holds, accept a rougher dice this round.
- **F3 Nicked a finger:** rinse, pressure with a clean towel until it stops, plaster; a cut that gapes or won't stop in 10 min needs medical care. Retire fatigue or a dull knife — one of them was the cause.
- **F4 Eyes streaming, can't see the blade:** stop cutting blind — step away, let eyes clear, apply the tear mitigations above before resuming.

## Verification

Dice are even at roughly the intended size, the root nub is discarded, all ten fingertips are accounted for, and the knife is cleaned and stored edge-safe (block/rail/sheath — never loose in a drawer).

## Variations

- Fine mince: after dicing, rock-chop the pile with the free hand flat on the spine.
- Shallots: identical geometry, smaller and layered tighter — the claw matters more, the horizontal cuts less.

## Safety & privacy

Medium risk fully concentrated in the blade: sharp knife, stable board, flat side down, claw grip, and never catching a falling knife — step back and let it drop.
