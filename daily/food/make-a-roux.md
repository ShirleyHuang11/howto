## Goal

A smooth cooked mixture of fat and flour is prepared as a thickening base for sauce, gravy, soup, gumbo, or stew.

## Preconditions

- Equal parts fat and all-purpose flour by weight, or roughly 1 Tbsp fat to 1 Tbsp flour for each cup of liquid.
- A heavy pan, whisk or flat-edged spoon, and measured liquid for the final sauce.
- Butter, oil, pan drippings, or another cooking fat.
- Moderate heat source and a clear stovetop workspace.
- Optional scale if precision matters for a large batch.

## Steps

1. **Measure the fat and flour.** Use a 1:1 weight ratio, or equal tablespoon volumes for a quick home sauce. → *Expect:* flour is measured before the pan gets hot.
2. **Melt or warm the fat.** Heat butter until foaming subsides, or warm oil until it shimmers lightly. → *Expect:* fat coats the pan and is hot but not smoking.
3. **Add flour all at once.** Sprinkle flour over the fat while whisking or stirring firmly. → *Expect:* the mixture becomes a thick paste with no dry flour islands.
4. **Cook out raw-flour taste.** Stir constantly over medium-low heat for at least 2-3 minutes for a pale roux. → *Expect:* the paste smells nutty instead of dusty or cereal-like.
5. **Choose the color stage.** [BRANCH: white roux for milk sauces | blond roux for gravy | brown roux for darker stews | dark roux for gumbo] → *Expect:* color matches the intended dish before liquid is added.
6. **Control darker roux carefully.** For brown or dark roux, stir without stopping and lower heat as color deepens. → *Expect:* color changes evenly with no black flecks.
7. **Prevent lumps before adding liquid.** Use hot roux with cool liquid, or cooled roux with hot liquid, and add the first liquid slowly. → *Expect:* the first splash forms a smooth paste instead of floating clumps.
8. **Whisk in liquid gradually.** Add liquid in several pours, fully smoothing after each addition. → *Expect:* sauce loosens evenly and thickens as it simmers.
9. **Simmer to finish thickening.** Bring the sauce to a gentle simmer for 1-3 minutes after all liquid is added. → *Expect:* spoon coating improves and no floury taste remains.
10. **Adjust final texture.** Thin with more liquid or reduce gently if too loose. → *Expect:* sauce flows at the thickness needed for the dish.

## Decision points

- Using butter → keep heat moderate because milk solids brown before flour does.
- Using pan drippings → skim excess burnt bits first so the roux does not taste scorched.
- Need gluten-free thickening → use a known gluten-free flour blend or another thickener, not this default ratio.
- Want maximum thickening → stop at white or blond; darker roux thickens less.
- Roux is for later → cool it in a shallow container and refrigerate.

## Failure modes & recovery

- **F1 Lumpy sauce:** liquid went in too fast; whisk hard, use an immersion blender, or strain if needed.
- **F2 Raw flour flavor:** roux was undercooked; simmer the finished sauce longer while stirring.
- **F3 Scorched roux:** black specks or acrid smell mean it is burnt; discard and restart because bitterness spreads.
- **F4 Greasy separation:** fat ratio is high or sauce is too hot; whisk in a small splash of liquid and simmer gently.
- **F5 Too thick after standing:** starch keeps hydrating; loosen with warm liquid while whisking.

## Verification

The roux or sauce is smooth, smells toasted rather than raw, has no black flecks, and thickens liquid without visible flour lumps.

## Variations

- Beurre manié: knead equal parts soft butter and flour, then whisk pieces into hot liquid without precooking.
- Oven roux: bake oil and flour in a heavy dish, stirring periodically, for a more even dark roux.
- Large batch: weigh ingredients and use a flat whisk to reach pan corners.

## Safety & privacy

Medium risk from hot fat and thick sauces that splatter. Keep stirring tools dry, use a stable pan, and avoid tasting until the sauce cools briefly on the spoon.
