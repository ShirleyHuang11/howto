## Goal

A plate of soft, glossy scrambled eggs in small creamy curds: fully set but not dry, rubbery, or weeping liquid.

## Preconditions

- 2-3 fresh eggs per person, butter, salt.
- A nonstick or well-seasoned pan, small relative to the egg quantity (8 eggs in a huge pan spread thin and overcook).
- A flexible spatula (silicone is ideal) and a bowl and fork or whisk for beating.
- A warm plate ready before you start; these eggs go from done to overdone in under a minute.

## Steps

1. **Beat the eggs thoroughly with salt.** Crack into a bowl, add a pinch of salt per two eggs, and beat until fully homogeneous, no ribbons of clear white remaining. Salting now (not in the pan) dissolves evenly and slightly tenderizes. → *Expect:* a uniform pale-yellow liquid with no streaks.
2. **Melt butter over low heat.** A generous knob in the pan on low. Low heat is the entire method: eggs set at around 63-70 °C and high heat blows straight past that. → *Expect:* butter melted and foaming gently, never browning. Browning butter means the pan is too hot; pull it off and let it cool 30 seconds.
3. **Add the eggs and start stirring immediately.** Pour in and stir slowly but constantly with the spatula, scraping the bottom and edges where the eggs set first. Constant motion is what makes small creamy curds; leaving them alone makes an omelet. → *Expect:* nothing happens for the first 30-60 seconds. This is correct; do not raise the heat.
4. **Keep stirring as curds form.** Continue steady scraping as the mixture thickens from liquid, to thick cream, to soft curds. If it ever starts setting faster than you can stir, lift the pan off the heat and keep stirring; the pan's stored heat keeps cooking. → *Expect:* gradual thickening over 3-5 minutes; small moist curds forming without any browning.
5. **Pull off the heat early, while still glossy and slightly underdone.** Remove when the eggs look about 80 percent done: mostly set but still visibly wet on top. Carryover heat from the pan finishes them. This off-heat finish is the difference between creamy and rubbery. → *Expect:* eggs continue to visibly set in the pan over the next 30-60 seconds with no burner under them.
6. **Stir once more and plate immediately.** A final fold off heat, then straight onto the warm plate; do not let them sit in the hot pan. Add pepper, chives, or a small knob of cold butter folded in for gloss. → *Expect:* soft, glossy curds that hold their shape but jiggle slightly; no browned bits, no pooled liquid.

## Decision points

- Cooking for a crowd (6+ eggs) → keep the heat low, expect 8-10 minutes, and pull even earlier; a big mass carries more residual heat.
- Firmer eggs preferred (common diner style) → same method, just carry them 30-60 seconds further on heat; still pull before they look fully dry.
- Adding cheese → fold it in off heat at step 5 so it melts without oiling out.
- Adding vegetables or meat → cook them separately first; raw additions leak water into the eggs.

## Failure modes & recovery

- **F1 Rubbery, browned eggs:** heat was too high or they stayed on it too long. Not recoverable; eat or chop into fried rice. Next run: lower heat, off-heat finish.
- **F2 Weeping liquid on the plate:** overcooked; the protein network tightened and squeezed water out. Same recovery as F1. Salting only at the very end can also contribute.
- **F3 One giant pancake instead of curds:** stirring stopped too long. Break it up and stir; texture is partly lost but it is still breakfast.
- **F4 Eggs stick and tear:** pan surface is worn or butter was insufficient. Finish with more frequent scraping, then retire or re-season the pan.
- **F5 Shell fragment in the bowl:** fish it out before beating using a larger shell half as the scoop; it attracts the fragment better than fingers.

## Verification

Eggs on the plate are fully set with no translucent raw patches, show no browning, form soft distinct curds rather than one dry mass, and no free liquid pools on the plate within a minute of serving.

## Variations

- `fr` style: near-continuous whisking over the gentlest heat (or a double boiler) for tiny custard-like curds, finished with cream.
- `us-diner` style: medium heat, larger fluffy curds, fully set; faster and drier by design.
- Splash of milk or cream: optional; it softens flavor but adds nothing the off-heat finish does not already achieve.

## Safety & privacy

Low risk. The pan is only moderately hot by design. Standard raw-egg hygiene applies: wash hands and the beating fork after contact, and cook fully for anyone immunocompromised, pregnant, or very young.
