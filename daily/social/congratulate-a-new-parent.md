## Goal

Congratulate a new parent in a way that makes their life easier, respects recovery and bonding, and avoids adding hosting work to an already full season.

## Preconditions

- News that a baby has arrived, been adopted, or joined the family, and enough relationship to contact the parent directly or through someone close.
- Any known preferences about visitors, photos, food restrictions, siblings, pets, or health precautions.
- A willingness to offer practical help without needing a long reply.

## Steps

1. **Send a short warm message.** Say congratulations, name the baby if the name has been shared, and keep it low-demand: "No need to reply." → *Expect:* the parent receives care without another task.
2. **Do not visit unannounced.** Ask once, or wait for an invitation. Hospitals and homes are not open houses after birth or placement. → *Expect:* the parent controls access to their space and baby.
3. **Offer practical help over cute gifts.** Suggest one useful action: meal drop, grocery run, dog walk, laundry pickup, sibling outing, pharmacy errand, or coffee delivery. → *Expect:* the offer solves a real problem.
4. **Make the offer specific.** Instead of "Let me know," say "I can drop dinner Tuesday at 5, no visit, just porch handoff. Any allergies?" [BRANCH: meal drop | errand help] choose meal drop if you know food needs; choose errands if you do not. → *Expect:* they can answer yes, no, or allergy details with little effort.
5. **Respect the doorway.** If dropping food, label it, include reheating notes, text when it is outside, and leave unless invited in. → *Expect:* the parent receives food without cleaning, dressing, or hosting.
6. **Compliment without evaluating.** Say the baby is beautiful, the family looks loved, or you are happy for them. Avoid comments about body, feeding, sleep choices, or whether they look tired. → *Expect:* your words do not open a parenting debate.
7. **Ask before sharing photos or news.** ⚠️ *Irreversible:* once baby photos, names, or birth details are posted, privacy cannot be fully restored. → *Expect:* nothing appears online or in group chats without parent permission.
8. **Follow up after the first rush.** Check in two or four weeks later with another concrete offer. → *Expect:* support continues after the announcement attention fades.

## Decision points

- If the baby is in NICU or the situation is medically complex → use congratulations plus support, and avoid demanding details.
- If there was loss, infertility, adoption complexity, or a hard birth → keep language tender and let the parent define the story.
- If you are sick or exposed to illness → do not visit, even if invited.
- If they decline help → accept warmly and try one low-pressure check-in later.

## Failure modes & recovery

- **F1 You ask for a visit too soon:** detect a slow reply or vague answer → say "No pressure at all. I'll drop food and head out."
- **F2 Gift creates work:** detect assembly, returns, storage, or washing required → switch to food, errands, or a gift card next time.
- **F3 You say something judgmental:** detect defensiveness or silence → apologize once and stop explaining your intent.
- **F4 You forget to follow up:** detect that weeks passed → send a late, specific offer without guilt-tripping yourself or them.

## Verification

The parent received a warm message, no unannounced visit, one specific practical offer, and no shared baby information without permission.

## Variations

- Coworker: coordinate a meal train, card, or leave coverage instead of direct visits.
- Far away: send delivery credit, diapers, a grocery order, or a voice note with no reply expected.
- Second or later child: offer sibling help and household tasks, not only baby items.

## Safety & privacy

Newborn and postpartum periods involve medical privacy, immune vulnerability, adoption privacy, and family boundaries. Follow parent rules on illness, photos, visits, and disclosure.
