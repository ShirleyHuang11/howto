## Goal

A new person's name survives the introduction, the conversation, and the week, because you ran the small encoding routine at the moment of meeting instead of hoping.

## Preconditions

- Honesty about the real failure: names are rarely forgotten; they are never encoded, because at introduction time you were rehearsing your own greeting. The recipe is an attention protocol, not a memory trick.

## Steps

1. **Actually receive the name.** At the handshake moment, make hearing the name the entire job; your own name says itself. Missed it or unsure: ask immediately, when it is free ("Sorry, was that Mara or Marta?"). → *Expect:* the name heard clearly, at worst at the cost of one instant, painless clarification.
2. **Say it back within seconds.** "Nice to meet you, Mara." One natural use, not a salesman's drumbeat. → *Expect:* your own voice has now produced the name once, which is worth five silent hearings.
3. **Attach one hook while they talk.** Link the name to something durable: a rhyme, a same-named friend, their standout feature or role ("Mara, marathon"), the place you met. Silly hooks work best precisely because they are vivid. → *Expect:* a private, instant association; nobody ever needs to know it.
4. **Use it once more at parting.** "Good to meet you, Mara — hope the move goes well." Parting use plus a detail doubles as warmth (`daily/social/greet-a-neighbor` banks the same detail). → *Expect:* second retrieval performed while the face is still present.
5. **Write it down within the hour.** Phone note: name, where met, one detail ("Mara — Lena's party, moving to Leiden, climbs"). For events: annotate as you go or immediately after. → *Expect:* an external record that turns next-encounter preparation into a ten-second lookup.
6. **Retrieve once before the next meeting.** Seeing them again on the calendar or across the room: glance at the note or self-test first. → *Expect:* the name arriving with the greeting, which is the entire visible magic of "good with names" people.

## Decision points

- Bulk introductions (a table of six): do not attempt all six at once; anchor two or three with the routine, and re-ask the rest individually as you talk to them ("remind me of your name?" lands fine within the first meeting).
- Unfamiliar names from other languages: ask for it again slowly and repeat it until they confirm; caring enough to pronounce it correctly is itself the best first impression, and shortening someone's name uninvited is the opposite.
- Name tags and video-call labels: use them shamelessly, but still run steps 2 and 3, since the tag disappears and the meeting ends.

## Failure modes & recovery

- **F1 Name gone mid-conversation:** engineer retrieval: introduce them to someone ("Have you two met?" — they will say their own names), ask for their contact info and watch them type it, or simply confess at a natural beat; confession within the first meeting is free.
- **F2 Name gone at the second meeting:** the one-time honest reset: "I'm terrible — remind me of your name? It won't happen again." Then run the full routine so the promise holds (`daily/social/greet-a-neighbor` F1).
- **F3 Called them the wrong name:** correct instantly and lightly ("Sorry — Mara, of course"), do not over-apologize, and strengthen the hook now; the error just gave you a vivid one.
- **F4 The notes habit lapsed and the backlog is faceless:** restart from today; the protocol only ever needs to work forward.

## Verification

You said the name at meeting and parting, a hook exists, a note exists within the hour, and at the next encounter the name arrived before they finished turning around. The compounding proof: people start remarking that you remember them.

## Variations

- Professional contexts wire the note into the tools you already use (CRM, calendar-event descriptions, the `communication/schedule-a-meeting` invite body).
- Teachers, hosts, and managers meeting groups: seating charts and roster self-tests are this recipe at scale.

## Safety & privacy

Low risk. Keep the notes factual and kind; write every entry as if its subject might one day see it, because a phone left unlocked has made that happen.
