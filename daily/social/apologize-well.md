## Goal

A real apology delivered for something you actually did: one that names the harm, skips the self-defense, repairs what it can, and leaves the other person freer rather than obligated.

## Preconditions

- Something to apologize for that you genuinely regret. If on reflection you do not think you were wrong, the honest conversation is a different one (a clarification or a disagreement), and dressing it as an apology makes things worse.
- Enough calm to speak without relitigating. Apologizing mid-anger usually appends a fresh offense.
- The right channel: in person or a call for anything that mattered; text only for small things or as the opener requesting a talk.

## Steps

1. **Name what you did, concretely.** "I'm sorry I made that joke about your work in front of the team." Naming it proves you know what happened; vagueness ("sorry about yesterday") makes them do the work of prosecuting it. → *Expect:* the specific act stated in your words, without euphemism.
2. **Acknowledge the impact on them.** "That undermined you in a meeting where you needed the room's confidence." Impact, not intent: what it did to them is the subject. → *Expect:* their experience reflected accurately enough that they feel seen rather than managed.
3. **Do not attach the defense.** No "but", no "I was stressed", no "you know I didn't mean it", and never "I'm sorry you feel that way" (which apologizes for their feelings instead of your act). Explanations can come later if they ask; welded to the apology they read as retraction. → *Expect:* a full stop where the excuse wanted to go.
4. **Offer repair where repair exists.** Correct the record with the people who heard it, fix or replace what broke (`daily/social/borrow-and-return-an-item`'s broke-it protocol is this step in miniature), redo the work, cover the cost. Ask if unsure: "What would help?" → *Expect:* a concrete offer, or a sincere question, not just sentiment.
5. **State the change, modestly.** "I'll keep that stuff out of group settings." One credible behavioral sentence beats grand reform speeches; the future is where apologies are actually judged. → *Expect:* a specific, keepable commitment.
6. **Let them respond without steering.** They may accept, need time, or still be angry; all are legitimate. Do not extract absolution ("so are we good?") on the spot; the apology is a gift with no receipt required. → *Expect:* their response received as given; pressure absent.

## Decision points

- Public offense, public repair: harm done in front of others often needs its correction visible to the same audience; private apologies for public harms leave the record standing.
- Timing: soon beats perfect, but their readiness outranks your urge to be forgiven today; "when you're up for it, I'd like to apologize properly" respects both.
- Repeat offense territory: a third apology for the same behavior is worth less than one visible month of the changed behavior; consider leading with the change and letting the apology follow it.

## Failure modes & recovery

- **F1 The "but" escaped:** catch it live: "No, let me stop. No but. I was wrong to do it." Recovering mid-sentence reads as more sincere than a polished script.
- **F2 They bring up more than you expected (older grievances surface):** listen; the apology opened a door. Address what you can honestly, and ask for a separate conversation for the rest rather than triaging their history on the spot.
- **F3 Apology rejected or met coldly:** their right. Restate the standing offer of repair once, then give it time; pursuing warmth on a schedule converts the apology into pressure.
- **F4 You apologized to smooth things over but privately disagree:** it will leak, and the wobble costs more than the disagreement would have. Return to the preconditions: have the honest conversation instead.

## Verification

The act was named, the impact acknowledged, no defense rode along, repair was offered or asked about, one credible change was stated, and the other person's response was left entirely theirs. The long-run verification is the only one that counts: the behavior changed.

## Variations

- Professional settings: the same skeleton, tighter language, and repair leans on correcting records and workload; `communication/write-a-formal-email`'s tone rules apply if it must be written.
- Cultural registers vary in formality and ritual (a bow, a gift, a letter), but the load-bearing parts (specificity, no defense, repair, change) survive translation everywhere.

## Safety & privacy

Low risk socially, high value: the discipline of impact-over-intent and no-defense is the whole craft. One boundary: apologize for your acts, not for other people's, and not as a tool to reopen contact someone has asked you not to make.
