## Goal

A short toast delivered at a dinner, wedding, or send-off that lands warm, earns one genuine laugh or one genuine mist of the eyes, and ends before anyone checks the time — the whole craft being that it is about them, not you.

## Preconditions

- An occasion and an honoree (a person, a couple, a milestone), and you with a reason to speak: invited to, hosting, or the moment clearly calling for someone.
- Ninety seconds of material prepared, even for "spontaneous" toasts; the formula in step 2 assembles in the hallway if needed.
- A glass in hand with something in it (anything; the liquid is ceremonial), and a moment when the room can actually attend: between courses, not mid-service.

## Steps

1. **Claim the room gently.** Stand if the group is larger than a table's worth, raise the glass to shoulder height, and use the opener that always works: "If I could have everyone for just a moment." Wait out the settling; starting over chatter buries the first line. → *Expect:* faces turned, conversations paused, your glass visibly the flag.
2. **Run the three-part formula: connection, story, wish.** One line on who you are to the honoree ("I've sat next to Ana for six years of Mondays"); one *short specific* story or trait that only you would tell, aimed at their best qualities ("the year my project collapsed, she rebuilt my spreadsheet and my morale, in that order"); one forward-looking wish ("here's to the next chapter being even kinder to her than she is to everyone else"). → *Expect:* 60 to 90 seconds total; the story concrete enough to be about no one else on earth.
3. **Aim the warmth, not the roast.** One gentle tease is seasoning; embarrassment, in-jokes the room cannot parse, exes, and anything the honoree would wince at are banned regardless of how funny the memory is to you. The test: would they be glad their grandmother heard it? → *Expect:* the honoree smiling at their own story, not surviving it.
4. **Land the ending cleanly.** Signal the close ("So please raise your glasses—"), deliver the wish in one sentence, name them ("—to Ana!"), and drink. The named toast is the period; do not resume talking after it. → *Expect:* the room echoing the name, glasses up, a clean end.
5. **Clink and eye-contact per local custom, then sit down.** Sitting down promptly is the second most appreciated part of any toast. → *Expect:* the moment returned to the table; the honoree's thanks caught with a nod, not a second speech.

## Decision points

- Nerves: write the three parts on a card and hold it without shame; rooms trust a prepared toaster more than a rambling confident one. Rehearse once aloud; the mouth finds the potholes the mind skips.
- Humor calibration: one real laugh beats three attempted ones; when in doubt, sincerity is the higher-percentage play and ages better in the wedding video.
- Multiple toasters queued (weddings): coordinate beforehand, keep to your lane of the honoree's life, and the later your slot, the shorter your text.
- Drinking contexts vary: toasting with water is fine nearly everywhere despite folklore; in cultures with formal toast rituals (`cn` rounds of ganbei, `ge` supra with a tamada, `jp` kanpai openings), follow the local order and let the host's lead set the cadence.

## Failure modes & recovery

- **F1 Lost the thread mid-toast:** skip straight to the wish and the name; the formula's parts are detachable, and nobody mourns a missing middle.
- **F2 The joke landed on silence:** do not explain it; half-smile, "well, *I* laughed," and proceed to sincerity, which is waiting patiently.
- **F3 Went long (the room's energy sagging):** the emergency exit is always open: "—which is all to say: to Ana!" Any sentence can become the last one.
- **F4 Sudden emotion (your own voice breaking):** pause, breathe, say "she means a lot to me" as the bridge; rooms are entirely on your side at this moment, and it often becomes the toast people remember.

## Verification

Under two minutes, three parts present, the story specific to the honoree, nothing they would wince at, ended on their name with glasses raised, and you sat down while the room still wanted one more sentence.

## Variations

- Work farewells: same formula, story from the trenches, wish aimed at the next role; keep confidential projects out even in jest.
- Memorial toasts: the formula holds with the wish becoming remembrance ("to the way he made Tuesdays feel like Fridays"); shorter, softer, and the glass raise slower.

## Safety & privacy

Low risk socially, governed by one rule: the honoree's dignity outranks your material. Stories told in a toast are published — treat private confidences as off-limits exactly like `daily/social/comfort-someone`'s confidentiality line.
