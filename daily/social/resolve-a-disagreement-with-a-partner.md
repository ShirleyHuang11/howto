## Goal

A disagreement with a romantic partner is cooled, understood, and turned into one practical next step without winning becoming the point.

## Preconditions

- Both people are physically safe and able to pause if voices rise.
- The issue is suitable for direct conversation, not an emergency, threat, or pattern of coercion.
- You can name the disagreement in one sentence without insulting your partner.

## Steps

1. **Cool down first.** Take at least twenty minutes if either person is flooded, and agree when to return rather than disappearing. → *Expect:* bodies and voices are calmer before content resumes.
2. **State the topic narrowly.** Say "I want to talk about how we handle weekends with family," not a lifetime indictment. → *Expect:* one solvable issue is on the table.
3. **Use I-statements.** Describe your feeling, the concrete event, and the effect on you: "I felt dismissed when plans changed after I had arranged childcare." → *Expect:* your partner hears impact before defense.
4. **Invite their version.** Ask what they experienced, then repeat back the strongest version before responding. → *Expect:* they can say "yes, that's what I mean" or correct you.
5. **Find the shared goal.** Name what both of you want, such as less stress, more respect, fair time, or better follow-through. → *Expect:* the conversation shifts from opponent to problem.
6. **Choose one next step.** [BRANCH: behavior change | information needed] Agree on a specific action if the facts are clear; agree on what to learn or decide later if they are not. → *Expect:* the outcome can be observed.
7. **Protect the relationship while deciding.** Avoid threats, name-calling, scorekeeping, and bringing in unrelated old fights. ⚠️ *Irreversible:* cruel lines can be apologized for but not unheard, so pause before saying the thing meant to wound. → *Expect:* the disagreement stays repairable.
8. **Confirm and schedule a check-in.** Summarize the agreement and choose when to revisit it. → *Expect:* both people know what happens next.

## Decision points

- If either person feels unsafe, stop the conversation and seek trusted support or professional help.
- If the issue repeats often, discuss the pattern and consider couples counseling.
- If facts are disputed, agree on what evidence matters instead of arguing memory forever.
- If one person needs time, set a return time within a reasonable window.
- If the conversation becomes circular, write down the shared goal and next step.

## Failure modes & recovery

- **F1 Voices escalate:** detect it when volume, speed, or insults rise → call a timed pause and physically separate if helpful.
- **F2 Blame spiral:** detect it when sentences start with "you always" → restate one current behavior and one feeling.
- **F3 Fake agreement:** detect it when one person says "fine" but withdraws → ask what part still does not work.
- **F4 No next step:** detect it when the talk ends in feelings only → propose one small experiment before closing.

## Verification

Both partners can state the issue, the other person's concern, the shared goal, and one agreed next step without insults.

## Variations

- Long-distance relationship: use video or voice for sensitive topics and write the agreement afterward.
- Parenting conflict: separate child logistics from couple feelings so neither gets lost.
- Money conflict: use numbers and dates, not character judgments.

## Safety & privacy

Medium risk because intimate conflict can involve safety and power. Do not use this recipe to negotiate with someone who threatens, controls, stalks, or punishes you for speaking.
