## Goal

You get the help you need in a store — finding an item, checking stock, or understanding a product — quickly and pleasantly for both sides.

## Preconditions

- You're in a store with a concrete need you can articulate (item, category, or problem).
- You've given the obvious a 60-second try (aisle signs, the section where it plausibly lives) — or you've decided your time beats your pride, which is also valid immediately.

## Steps

1. **Identify actual staff.** Uniform, name badge, lanyard, or behind a counter; restocking carts are a giveaway. → *Expect:* a person who works here, not a fellow shopper in an unlucky shirt (if wrong: everyone laughs, see F1).
2. **Open with a polite interrupt at a good moment.** Wait for a pause if they're mid-customer; then: "Excuse me — could you help me find something?" → *Expect:* attention turned to you; nobody's transaction was trampled.
3. **State the need concretely.** Name + use beats vague category: "AAA batteries" beats "batteries stuff"; unknown name → describe the job: "the sticky strips for hanging posters without nails." Show a phone photo if you have one. → *Expect:* recognition, or a clarifying question you can answer.
4. **Accept the help in the form offered.** [BRANCH: verbal aisle pointer → repeat it back ("aisle 7, by the tape — thanks") | they walk you there → follow and let them lead | they check the system → wait] → *Expect:* you end up at the item or at a definitive answer.
5. **Handle "not stocked" usefully.** Ask the two follow-ups that staff can actually answer: "Will it come back / can you order it?" and "Who nearby would carry it?" → *Expect:* a next action, not just a dead end.
6. **Close with thanks.** Brief and genuine; if they went notably out of their way, say so. → *Expect:* interaction ends warm; you're a pleasant memory, which matters in stores you frequent.

## Decision points

- Staff member visibly slammed (line of askers, urgent task) → self-serve harder first or find another employee; the checkout cashier can point but can't walk.
- Product *advice* (not location) needed → ask for the department's person ("anyone from electronics?"); floor staff route, specialists advise.
- Complaint or return rather than help → that's the service desk's script, not the aisle-staff's — go there directly.

## Failure modes & recovery

- **F1 Asked a non-employee:** instant light apology — "Sorry, thought you worked here!" — universal, forgiven, occasionally they help anyway.
- **F2 Directed to the wrong aisle:** benefit of the doubt (planograms move); one re-ask of anyone nearer, mentioning what you already tried.
- **F3 Frozen because interrupting feels rude:** the reframe that fixes it: helping customers *is their job's core*, and a found item is a sale — you are the point of the store, not an intrusion on it.

## Verification

You hold the item or a definitive answer (out of stock + next action), the interaction cost the staff member under a minute of friction, and it ended with thanks.

## Variations

- Big-box self-serve cultures: staff are sparse — the store app's item locator may genuinely be the fastest "staff member."
- `jp` department stores: staff proactively approach; your job flips to accepting help gracefully rather than seeking it.
- Language barrier: the phone-photo method (step 3) becomes primary; product names travel badly, pictures don't.

## Safety & privacy

Essentially risk-free. The entire skill is two sentences of social courage and one concrete noun.
