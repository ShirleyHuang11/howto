## Goal

The conversation ends within a few minutes, both people feel the exchange was complete, and the other person is left with a warm final impression rather than the sense of being escaped from.

## Preconditions

- You are in a live conversation (party, hallway, networking event, chance encounter) that has run its course or that you need to leave.
- You know at least roughly why you are leaving (another commitment, another person to see, a drink to refill, simple fatigue). A true reason, even a small one, beats an invented one.

## Steps

1. **Wait for a natural seam.** Let the current thought finish: a laugh, a story landing, a brief pause. Do not cut someone off mid-sentence to leave. → *Expect:* a beat of silence or a topic that has clearly resolved.
2. **Deploy a bridge phrase.** Signal the ending before it happens: "Before I let you go...", "I should wrap up in a minute, but first...", or "One last thing I wanted to ask you." The bridge gives the other person a few seconds to adjust. → *Expect:* their posture or tone shifts to wrap-up mode; nobody is surprised by what comes next.
3. **State a concrete exit.** Name the real, specific thing you are doing next: "I need to catch Maria before she leaves", "I'm going to grab food before they clear it", "I have a call at the top of the hour." Vague exits ("well, anyway...") stall; concrete ones move. → *Expect:* the reason lands as information, not rejection.
4. **Close warm with a specific callback.** Reference something they actually said: "Good luck with the kitchen renovation", "I'm going to try that podcast you mentioned." Use their name if you have it. [BRANCH: you want future contact → add one concrete follow-up ("I'll email you that article tonight") | you don't → keep it to the warm callback alone, no fake "let's get coffee"] → *Expect:* a smile or reciprocal closing line from them.
5. **Physically leave on the close.** Handshake, wave, or step away immediately after the closing line. Lingering after a goodbye restarts the conversation. → *Expect:* you are three steps away within seconds of the last word.

## Decision points

- The other person launches a new topic right after your bridge → answer in one sentence, then repeat the concrete exit verbatim; the repetition, delivered kindly, is the signal.
- Group setting and they will be stranded alone → hand them off first: introduce them to someone nearby with a one-line connector ("You two both work in logistics") before you exit.
- You promised a follow-up in step 4 → do it the same day; an unkept "I'll send you that" quietly erases the warm close.

## Failure modes & recovery

- **F1 The restart loop:** you said goodbye but are still talking five minutes later → stop adding new content, physically angle your body toward the exit, and re-close with a shorter version ("Really, great to see you") plus immediate movement.
- **F2 Exit read as rejection:** their face falls at your exit line → the callback was missing or generic; add a specific one now ("Seriously, the story about the ferry made my night") before stepping away.
- **F3 No seam ever appears (a monologuer):** wait through two full minutes, then use their inhale: touch of the arm or a raised hand, warm tone, "I'm so sorry to cut in, I have to run to X, but I loved hearing about Y."
- **F4 You invented a reason and got caught:** they see you standing alone by the wall two minutes after "I have to find my friend" → this is why the reason must be true; recover with honesty and humor if confronted, and do not repeat the move.

## Verification

The conversation is over, you have physically moved on to the stated next thing, the other person got a specific personal callback in the final exchange, and no goodbye was said more than twice.

## Variations

- Phone calls: the bridge phrase carries all the weight since there is no body language; "Before we hang up..." then close within one minute of saying it.
- Video meetings: state the exit against the clock ("We're at time, so last thoughts?"), which is impersonal and therefore easier; the warm callback still applies.
- High-context cultures: extend the close; an abrupt exit after one closing line can read as rude where farewells are expected to take several exchanges.

## Safety & privacy

Low risk. The only real hazard is social: an exit that skips the bridge or the callback can read as a snub, which is exactly what the sequence above prevents. Do not fabricate checkable reasons.
