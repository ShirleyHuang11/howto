## Goal

Someone in distress (bad news, a loss, a hard day) feels less alone after your presence, because you offered attention and warmth without trying to fix, minimize, or redirect their feelings.

## Preconditions

- Someone visibly or reportedly struggling, and enough of a relationship that your presence is welcome (a colleague's watery eyes may want privacy, not company; offering is fine, insisting is not).
- The core stance loaded in advance, because instinct fights it: comfort is presence, not solutions. The urge to fix, explain, or cheer up is about your discomfort, not their need.

## Steps

1. **Show up and open gently.** "Hey — I heard. I'm so sorry." or "You seem really down. Want company?" The offer includes a live exit for them. → *Expect:* an invitation in, or a "not now" that you honor gracefully (honoring it *is* comfort).
2. **Let them talk, and listen without steering.** Attention on them, phone away, nods and small acknowledgments ("that's so hard", "of course you're upset"). Silence is allowed; they fill it when ready. → *Expect:* them doing most of the talking, at their pace, including quiet stretches you do not rush to fill.
3. **Validate before anything else.** Their feeling is legitimate by definition; say so plainly: "Anyone would be gutted." Strike the minimizers from your vocabulary for this hour: "at least...", "everything happens for a reason", "others have it worse", "you'll be fine". → *Expect:* zero sentences that begin with "at least".
4. **Resist the fix, unless asked.** Advice arrives only through the door they open ("what do you think I should do?"), and even then lightly. Uninvited solutions convert their grief into your project. → *Expect:* your suggestions held; if the urge wins, at most: "Do you want thoughts, or do you want me to just listen?" — the question itself is excellent comfort.
5. **Offer concrete, small help instead of "let me know if you need anything".** The open offer transfers work to them; the specific one does the work: "I'm dropping dinner off tomorrow — any allergies?", "I'll take the airport run", "Want me to sit with you while you make that call?" → *Expect:* one real offer they can accept with a single word (`daily/social/host-a-guest-at-home`'s food instinct often applies).
6. **Close with continuity, and mean it.** "I'll check on you Thursday." Then check on them Thursday. Distress outlives the sympathy window; the second and third check-ins are worth more than the first. → *Expect:* a follow-up promised at a named time, and a reminder set so it happens.

## Decision points

- Touch: read the person and the relationship; "do you want a hug?" respects both answers. Some people are held, some are sat-next-to, some are made tea (`daily/food/make-tea` as pastoral care).
- Distraction vs. depth: some people want to talk about anything else; follow their lead — comfort is their menu, not your script.
- Serious territory: talk of self-harm, hopelessness that frightens you, or a crisis beyond your depth means you stay warm *and* widen the circle: encourage professional support, share a crisis line, loop in family where appropriate. Presence does not mean carrying it alone.

## Failure modes & recovery

- **F1 You fixed, minimized, or "at least"-ed:** catch it live: "Sorry — that wasn't what you needed. Tell me more." The repair is returning to listening, not apologizing at length.
- **F2 They do not want to talk at all:** presence without content is a real mode: sit with them, watch something, handle a chore in their house quietly. "I'll just be here" is a complete sentence.
- **F3 Their distress activates yours (you start crying, or unloading your parallel story):** brief shared feeling humanizes; taking over the emotional floor does not. One sentence of your own, then back to them; process your load elsewhere, later.
- **F4 The follow-up lapsed:** late beats never, named plainly: "I said Thursday and it's Sunday — I'm sorry. How are you doing?"

## Verification

They talked (or chose quiet) at their own pace, heard validation and no minimizers, received one concrete offer, and a follow-up happened at the named time. The honest metric arrives later: they come to you the next time it is heavy.

## Variations

- By message when distance forces it: the same skeleton compressed — name it, validate, one concrete offer, a scheduled check-in; voice notes carry warmth text cannot.
- Grief's long tail: anniversaries and firsts (first birthday, first holiday after a loss) are when everyone else has moved on; a calendar note there makes you the person who remembered.

## Safety & privacy

Low risk with two lines: the crisis-escalation rule in the decision points, and confidentiality — what they shared in distress is not conversation material elsewhere, full stop.
