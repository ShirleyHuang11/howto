## Goal

A price negotiated at a market, bazaar, or flea market where haggling is the norm: a fair deal reached with good humor, or a clean walk-away, without either party feeling robbed.

## Preconditions

- A venue where bargaining is actually expected. Fixed-price shops, supermarkets, and most Western retail are not it; souks, flea markets, street vendors in much of the world, and second-hand negotiations (`shopping/buy-second-hand` step 4) are. When unsure, watch other buyers or ask lightly: "Is the price fixed?"
- A sense of the fair range, gathered before wanting anything visibly: comparable stalls' prices, or a local's rule of thumb. Your anchor is worthless without it.
- Your walk-away number decided in advance, and cash in small denominations (paying a haggled 70 from a 100 note weakens every future round).

## Steps

1. **Browse with warmth and a poker face.** Greet the seller, handle goods respectfully, and keep your longing invisible; the item you love is "interesting, maybe." → *Expect:* friendly rapport established; your target item one of several you have touched.
2. **Let them open, or ask casually.** "How much for this?" Their first number is the ceiling and it is decorated for tourists where you look like one. → *Expect:* an opening price, received with mild, good-natured surprise rather than outrage.
3. **Counter well below, inside the plausible.** In heavy-haggling cultures, 40 to 60 percent of the opening is a normal first counter; in casual flea markets, 60 to 75 percent. Deliver it with a smile and a reason where one exists ("there's a small chip here"). → *Expect:* laughter or theatrical pain from the seller, then a lower number; the dance is on.
4. **Converge in shrinking steps.** Move less each round than they do; use silence after their offers (it works); bundle to break deadlocks ("both for 50?"); keep the tone light throughout — the moment it feels like combat, the deal and the fun are both dying. → *Expect:* the gap narrowing toward the fair range you scouted.
5. **Play the walk-away only if it is real.** A warm "thank you, maybe next time" and genuinely leaving is the strongest move in the room, and it only works because you mean it. → *Expect:* either a called-back better price within ten steps, or confirmation your number was truly below their floor.
6. **Close cleanly and pay with grace.** Agree the number, pay near-exact, thank them warmly, and stop negotiating the moment hands shake; retrading after agreement is the one real taboo. ⚠️ *Irreversible in effect:* market sales are final; inspect the actual item that goes in the bag (not the display twin) before money moves (`shopping/buy-second-hand` step 5's inspection instinct). → *Expect:* both parties smiling; the item you examined in your bag.

## Decision points

- Small amounts and poor vendors: the sport has a floor — grinding a farmer over the last coin buys nothing but shame. Decide the sum where you stop caring and pay it cheerfully.
- Love it and it is rare: secure it near the fair range instead of gambling the walk-away on a one-of-a-kind piece; the recipe serves the acquisition, not the theater.
- Taxi fares, tours, and services in haggling regions: same skeleton, but agree the price *before* the service starts, always; after is not a negotiation, it is a dispute.

## Failure modes & recovery

- **F1 You anchored first and too high:** the scouting (preconditions) was skipped; recover with slower concessions, or walk and re-scout before the next stall.
- **F2 Overpaid, discovered later:** tuition, not tragedy; the vendor was better at their daily craft than you at your holiday one. Adjust the mental map and enjoy the item.
- **F3 The tone soured (offense taken or given):** de-escalate immediately: smile, soften, or exit warmly; no object at a market is worth an ugly scene, and `daily/social/apologize-well`'s one-line version repairs most of it.
- **F4 Called back after your walk-away and the price still is not right:** hold your number kindly once more; being called back moved them, not you.

## Verification

The final price landed inside the range you scouted (or you walked cleanly), the tone stayed warm through the close, the exact item was inspected before payment, and no negotiating happened after the handshake.

## Variations

- Heavy-ritual bazaars (`mena`, parts of `asia`): tea may be offered and rounds may be many; accepting the rhythm is part of the respect.
- Flea markets in fixed-price cultures (`us`, `eu` yard sales): one polite counter ("would you take 15?") is the whole dance; declining gracefully is expected either way.

## Safety & privacy

Low risk. Keep the wallet flash minimal (small notes, split pockets), agree service prices upfront, and remember the sport's ethics: the goal is a deal both sides retell cheerfully, not a conquest.
