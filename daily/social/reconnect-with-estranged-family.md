## Goal

A first attempt to reconnect with an estranged family member is made gently, with boundaries, and without demanding a particular answer.

## Preconditions

- Contact is physically and emotionally safe enough to attempt, and no court order or safety plan forbids it.
- You have a low-pressure channel such as a letter, text, email, or message through a trusted intermediary.
- You are prepared for no response, a slow response, or a response you do not like.

## Steps

1. **Clarify your reason privately.** Write why you want contact now and what kind of relationship you can actually sustain. → *Expect:* the outreach is not driven only by panic, guilt, or a holiday deadline.
2. **Choose a low-pressure opening.** Use a short message that does not require immediate conversation: "I've been thinking of you and would be open to talking if you ever want to." → *Expect:* the recipient has room to decide.
3. **Avoid blame in the first contact.** Do not litigate the whole history, assign motives, or demand an apology in the opener. → *Expect:* the message can be read without a defensive essay forming.
4. **Name a small next step.** [BRANCH: direct contact | mediated contact] Suggest a brief call or coffee if direct contact is safe; suggest writing through a mediator if trust is low. → *Expect:* the ask is specific and small.
5. **State boundaries kindly.** Say what you are available for and what you are not, such as "I want to talk, but I am not ready to discuss money." → *Expect:* reconnection does not mean open access to every topic.
6. **Send once, then wait.** ⚠️ *Irreversible:* repeated messages can feel like pressure or harassment, so send one clean opening and give them time. → *Expect:* the choice clearly belongs to them.
7. **Accept any answer.** Treat yes, no, silence, anger, or hesitation as information, not a verdict on your worth. → *Expect:* you do not escalate because the answer hurts.
8. **Build in small steps if they respond.** Keep the first exchange brief, avoid surprise guests, and end while both people are regulated. → *Expect:* contact has a chance to become repeatable.

## Decision points

- If there was abuse, addiction, stalking, or coercion, consult a therapist, advocate, or legal adviser before contact.
- If the person asks for no contact, stop unless there is a genuine legal or safety reason.
- If you want an apology, decide whether you can reconnect before receiving one.
- If another family member pressures you, separate their urgency from your readiness.
- If contact resumes, keep expectations modest for the first several interactions.

## Failure modes & recovery

- **F1 No response:** detect it after the time window you set privately → do not chase; consider one later message only after months, not days.
- **F2 Angry response:** detect it in accusations or insults → acknowledge receipt, pause, and decide later whether any reply is safe or useful.
- **F3 Boundary push:** detect it when they demand topics, money, access, or secrecy → restate the boundary and end the exchange if needed.
- **F4 Fantasy reunion:** detect it when you imagine instant closeness → return to the next small contact only.

## Verification

You sent one respectful, low-pressure message, stated or preserved boundaries, and did not demand a response or immediate reconciliation.

## Variations

- Letter: write warmly, keep it under one page, and avoid surprise enclosures meant to force emotion.
- Through a relative: ask them to pass only the message, not argue your case.
- After a funeral or illness: acknowledge timing without using crisis as pressure.

## Safety & privacy

Medium risk. Protect addresses, children, finances, immigration status, medical details, and safety plans; do not reconnect privately with someone who has harmed or threatened you.
