## Goal

A baby shower that makes the parent or parents feel supported, keeps guests comfortable, and does not turn the host into a frantic stage manager.

## Preconditions

- The guest of honor wants a shower and has shared any preferences about date, size, gifts, games, food, and photos.
- You have a budget, a location, and at least one helper for setup or cleanup.
- You know dietary restrictions, accessibility needs, and whether children or partners are invited.

## Steps

1. **Confirm the guest list and date.** Ask the parent for must-invite names, contact details, and no-invite boundaries, then choose a date about one to two months before the due date or adoption arrival when possible. → *Expect:* a list and date approved by the guest of honor.
2. **Pick the format.** [BRANCH: games | no games] Choose two short games if the parent likes playful group energy; choose mingling, advice cards, or a meal if they prefer calm. → *Expect:* the shower has a shape that fits the parent rather than a default script.
3. **Send simple invitations.** Include date, time, address or link, RSVP deadline, registry link if there is one, parking, dress expectations, and who to contact with questions. → *Expect:* guests can decide and respond without extra messages.
4. **Plan food that holds well.** Use brunch, snacks, desserts, or a light meal that can sit safely and does not require constant cooking during the party. → *Expect:* the host can join the event instead of watching the kitchen.
5. **Share the registry without pressure.** Put the registry link in the invitation or follow-up, and include a line that presence and notes are welcome too. → *Expect:* gift guidance is available without making the shower feel transactional.
6. **Prepare a gift log.** Assign someone to record giver, gift, and any card details as presents are opened or dropped off. → *Expect:* thank-you notes are possible without memory work later.
7. **Set the room for low stress.** Make places to sit, a visible gift area, water within reach, trash bags hidden but available, and a quiet spot for the parent if needed. → *Expect:* guests know where to go and the parent can rest.
8. **Check consent before public moments.** Ask before belly touching, surprise speeches, birth predictions, or posting photos. ⚠️ *Irreversible:* public photos and intimate comments can spread fast, so confirm before sharing. → *Expect:* the parent stays in control of personal attention.
9. **Close with practical help.** Pack gifts, send food home if wanted, collect the gift log, and make cleanup someone else's job if the parent is tired. → *Expect:* the guest of honor leaves with support, not chores.

## Decision points

- If the parent dislikes being watched while opening gifts, set gifts aside and send thanks later.
- If family groups are tense, use assigned helpers, separate seating clusters, or a shorter schedule.
- If budget is tight, choose a home location, potluck snacks, and one decorative focal point.
- If the shower is virtual, mail game materials early and keep the call under 75 minutes.
- If there is pregnancy loss history in the group, avoid intrusive questions and miracle language.

## Failure modes & recovery

- **F1 Too many guests:** detect it when RSVPs exceed space or budget → ask the parent about splitting events or moving to a simpler venue.
- **F2 Food shortage:** detect it when serving trays empty early → open backup snacks, order simple delivery, and stop apologizing.
- **F3 Game falls flat:** detect it when guests disengage → end it quickly and move to food, gifts, or conversation.
- **F4 Gift log incomplete:** detect it when cards separate from gifts → photograph each gift with its card before packing.

## Verification

The parent approved the guest list and format, guests were fed, gifts were tracked, and cleanup did not fall on the guest of honor.

## Variations

- Sprinkle for a later child: smaller guest list, fewer gifts, and more practical support.
- Coed shower: invite by relationship, not gender, and use games or activities that suit the actual room.
- Workplace shower: keep it short, voluntary, and modest about money.

## Safety & privacy

Low social risk. Protect medical details, fertility history, adoption status, address information, and photos of children or guests who did not consent to being posted.
