## Goal

Support a child through homework by setting up the conditions, guiding their thinking, and preserving the teacher's view of what the child can do.

## Preconditions

- The assignment, due date, and teacher instructions are available.
- The child has needed materials, such as paper, pencil, device, charger, calculator, book, or login.
- A reasonably quiet work area and a predictable time window are available.
- You are prepared to help with process and questions, not complete the work for the child.

## Steps

1. **Set the environment.** Clear the table, reduce screens or noise that are not part of the task, and put supplies within reach. → *Expect:* the child can start without searching for basics.
2. **Read the assignment together.** Ask the child to explain what is due and what the teacher wants. → *Expect:* both of you can name the task in one sentence.
3. **Choose the first piece.** Break the work into small parts, such as read directions, do problems 1 to 5, draft one paragraph, or review notes. → *Expect:* the child has a next action that feels possible.
4. **Guide, do not take over.** Ask questions, model one similar example if needed, and have the child do the assigned item. → *Expect:* the child's marks, words, or reasoning remain their own.
5. **Use short work blocks.** [BRANCH: continue | take a break] work for 10 to 20 minutes depending on age, then pause for movement, water, or a reset. → *Expect:* attention improves or at least stops getting worse.
6. **Praise effort and strategy.** Name the behavior you want repeated, such as checking directions, trying a second method, or explaining a mistake. → *Expect:* the child hears what they controlled, not just whether the answer was right.
7. **Check for understanding.** Ask the child to teach back one problem or explain the next step without your prompt. → *Expect:* you can tell whether they understand or are copying.
8. **Stop before a meltdown.** If frustration rises, pause, mark the stuck place, and return after a short break or the next day if appropriate. → *Expect:* the child is not learning that homework equals a fight.
9. **Pack and communicate.** Put completed work where it belongs and email the teacher if directions are unclear, the workload is consistently too much, or the child cannot do it without heavy help. → *Expect:* the assignment is ready or the teacher has useful information.

## Decision points

- If the child does not understand the underlying lesson → write a note to the teacher rather than quietly reteaching the whole unit every night.
- If homework regularly takes far longer than expected → track time for several days and ask the teacher what amount of help is appropriate.
- If a digital platform or login fails → document the issue with a screenshot or note and contact the teacher.

## Failure modes & recovery

- **F1 Adult does the work:** answers sound like the adult or the child cannot explain them → erase or label the assisted part and tell the teacher what support was used.
- **F2 Child shuts down:** crying, anger, or refusal replaces work → pause, validate frustration, and restart with a smaller first step.
- **F3 Directions unclear:** neither of you can tell what is expected → send a concise teacher email with the assignment name and exact question.
- **F4 Rushing errors:** careless mistakes appear near the end → take a break, then review only the directions and a small sample.

## Verification

The child can explain what was completed, the work is packed or submitted, and any unresolved confusion has been sent to the teacher.

## Variations

- `early-reader`: read directions aloud, but let the child choose answers or dictate their own ideas.
- `middle-school`: shift toward planning, checking portals, and helping the child write the teacher email.
- `online-homework`: confirm submission status, not just that the screen looked finished.

## Safety & privacy

Low risk, with privacy centered on school accounts, grades, and messages to teachers. Do not share a child's login broadly, and do not disguise adult work as the child's independent ability.
