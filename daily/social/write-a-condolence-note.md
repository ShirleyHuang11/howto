## Goal

A short, sincere note reaches a grieving person and does its one job: they feel their loss was seen and they are not alone. It does not try to fix, explain, or brighten anything.

## Preconditions

- You know of the loss reliably (told directly, or through an obituary or mutual contact), and you know the bereaved at least somewhat.
- A card or plain paper and a pen; handwritten is the norm for this genre, though a message is better than nothing when that is your real relationship channel.
- Willingness to sit with discomfort for twenty minutes. The urge to avoid writing because "I don't know what to say" is the main enemy; nobody knows what to say, and the note works anyway.

## Steps

1. **Write now, or whenever you can. Late is fine.** The only true deadline is never. Notes arriving weeks or months later often land hardest, because the first-wave support has receded by then. → *Expect:* you have stopped waiting for the perfect moment; pen is on paper.
2. **Open by naming the loss and the person.** "I was so sorry to hear about your father, Daniel." Use the deceased's name; the bereaved does not get to hear it enough, and vagueness ("your loss") reads as distance. → *Expect:* first sentence contains both the relationship and, where you know it, the name.
3. **Give one specific memory or true observation.** The heart of the note, one to three sentences: "I still think about him teaching half the neighborhood to fix bikes" or, if you never met the deceased, something true about the bereaved: "the way you talked about her every Monday told me what she meant to you." → *Expect:* a sentence that could not have been written about anyone else's loss.
4. **Say the plain hard thing without repair.** "There's nothing I can say that makes this better. I'm so sorry." Do not add a lesson, a comparison to your own losses, or a bright side. → *Expect:* no sentence in the note starts with "at least," and nothing explains, ranks, or silver-lines the death.
5. **Make any offer of help specific, or leave it out.** "Let me know if you need anything" transfers work to the griever and yields nothing. Instead: "I'll text next week about dropping off dinner" or "I can take the school run any morning." Only write what you will actually do. → *Expect:* either a concrete, initiated-by-you offer, or no offer at all.
6. **Close warmly and briefly, then stop.** "With love," "Thinking of you and your family." Sign and send to the best address you have; a mutual friend can supply one. Total length: 4-8 sentences is right. → *Expect:* the finished note fits on one card face, maybe two; brevity here is a kindness, not a failure of effort.
7. **Follow up past the early wave.** Grief outlasts the casseroles. A short check-in a month or two later, and a line on the first anniversary or the deceased's birthday if you know it, is worth more than the note itself. → *Expect:* a reminder set for the later check-in; no reply to any of it is expected or owed (see F4).

## Decision points

- Death by suicide or overdose → same structure, extra care: never speculate about circumstances or causes; "I'm so sorry about Sam" with a good memory of Sam is exactly right.
- You knew the deceased well but the bereaved barely → introduce the connection in one line ("I worked with your mother for ten years"), then a memory; these notes are treasured because they carry stories the family never heard.
- Estranged or complicated relationship between the bereaved and the deceased → do not assume simple sadness; "I'm thinking of you; this must be a lot to carry" honors complexity without probing.
- The bereaved is a colleague you are not close to → shorter and more formal, same rules: name the loss, no clichés, no pressure to respond.
- Religious language → mirror their framework, not yours; "she's in a better place" comforts only people who already believe it, and stings people who do not.

## Failure modes & recovery

- **F1 Silver-lining reflex:** drafts containing "at least she lived a long life," "everything happens for a reason," "he's no longer in pain," "God needed another angel" → strike the sentence entirely; these minimize the loss to soothe the writer, and grieving people report them as the lines that hurt.
- **F2 Centering yourself:** the note drifts into your own grief or a comparable loss of yours ("I know exactly how you feel, when my dog died...") → one clause of shared sorrow is fine ("we will miss him too"); more than that, cut. The note is about them.
- **F3 Paralysis and silence:** weeks pass, shame compounds, you say nothing forever → invoke step 1: open with "I'm sorry this note comes late; you have been on my mind." The lateness apology takes one sentence and dissolves the problem; silence is the only real failure.
- **F4 Expecting a reply:** you read nothing back as offense → grieving people owe no correspondence; many read every note and answer none. Follow up later anyway, expecting nothing.
- **F5 Practical-matters creep:** the note mentions the shared project deadline, the borrowed ladder, the rent → condolences travel alone; any logistics go in a separate message, later.

## Verification

The finished note names the loss (and the deceased where known), contains at least one sentence specific to this person, contains zero silver-lining or explanatory phrases, makes either a concrete offer or none, runs 4-8 sentences, and has actually been sent, however late. A later check-in is scheduled.

## Variations

- Message or email instead of a card: acceptable where that is the genuine channel of the relationship; same content rules, and do not send it in a group thread.
- Sympathy flowers or donations: check the obituary first; "in lieu of flowers" instructions override your defaults, and the note still travels with or precedes the gesture.
- Loss of a pet: real grief, same structure, shorter; naming the animal matters just as much.

## Safety & privacy

Low material risk; the stakes are emotional. Do not share news of a death onward without knowing it is public, do not post condolences on social media before the family has announced, and never include details of the death you learned secondhand. When in doubt, say less about the death and more about the person.
