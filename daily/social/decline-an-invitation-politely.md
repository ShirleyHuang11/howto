## Goal

An invitation you cannot or do not want to accept is declined promptly and warmly, keeping the relationship intact and your calendar honest, without a fabricated excuse waiting to unravel.

## Preconditions

- An actual decision made. The worst state is the limbo "maybe" you already know is a no; this recipe starts once you know.
- Clarity on which kind of no this is: this-time-no (you want future invitations) or this-kind-no (you never want the sales dinner), because the wording differs.

## Steps

1. **Reply promptly, by the invitation's own channel.** A fast no is a courtesy; a late no costs the host planning options. Formal invitations with RSVP mechanics get the mechanic used (`communication/schedule-a-meeting`'s decline button exists for this). → *Expect:* a response within a day or two of deciding, not at the reminder.
2. **Thank first, and mean it.** "Thanks for thinking of me" is true even when the answer is no; being invited is a small gift. → *Expect:* warmth up front, so the no lands on a cushion.
3. **Decline clearly, without a fog of maybes.** "I can't make it" or "I'm going to pass on this one." Clear beats soft-but-ambiguous ("I'll try to swing by"), which the host must interpret and cater for. → *Expect:* a no the host can plan around.
4. **Give a reason only at the honesty level you can sustain.** A true brief reason is fine ("we have family visiting"); no reason is also fine ("I can't make it that night" is complete). Fabricated specifics are the trap: they demand memory and invite follow-up questions. → *Expect:* nothing said tonight that becomes a lie by Saturday.
5. **Signal the relationship's future, matched to which no this is.** This-time-no: attach a real alternative ("gutted to miss it; can we grab lunch next week?") and then actually propose it. This-kind-no: warmth without a counter-offer, so the pattern quietly ends. → *Expect:* the follow-up matches your actual intent; fake "let's do something soon" is the small lie that erodes.
6. **Close warm and wish the event well.** "Hope it's a great one; send photos." → *Expect:* the exchange ends with the relationship exactly where it was or better.

## Decision points

- Recurring invitations you always decline (the weekly game, the monthly dinner): one honest meta-conversation ("evenings are impossible for me this season; don't stop inviting me, but expect a lot of nos" or its opposite) beats a year of individual declines.
- Big-ticket invitations (weddings, milestone birthdays): decline extra early, warmly, and with the RSVP mechanics respected; consider a card or gift where customary, since the cost to the host of your seat is real.
- Pressure after a clear no: one cheerful repetition ("really can't, but have a blast") and then a subject change; a no does not require winning an argument.

## Failure modes & recovery

- **F1 Said maybe to avoid the moment, now it is the day before:** decline now anyway with an apology for the lateness; late-honest beats no-show, and no-show is the one unrecoverable version.
- **F2 The fabricated excuse got caught (you were seen elsewhere):** own it plainly and briefly: "Honestly, I just needed a night off and should have said so." The recovery for a small lie is a small truth.
- **F3 The host took the no personally:** reaffirm the relationship separately from the event: a message, a coffee proposal, some visible warmth on another channel; actions repair what wording bruised.
- **F4 You declined something you actually wanted once the day came:** note the pattern; if reflexive no-saying is the habit, try the delayed reply ("can I confirm tomorrow?") to give your genuine preference time to surface before the reflex answers.

## Verification

The host got a prompt, clear, warm no through the right channel, nothing untrue was said, the follow-up signal matched your real intent, any proposed alternative was actually pursued, and the next invitation's arrival (or intentional non-arrival) matches what you wanted.

## Variations

- Professional declines (meetings, speaking, committees): the same skeleton plus an offer of an alternative resource where real ("I can't, but Sam knows this area well"); `communication/write-a-formal-email` carries the formal version.
- Cultures where direct nos are impolite lean on established softeners (scheduling difficulty, family obligations) that both sides understand as complete answers; use the local idiom, keep the internal rule of not inventing checkable facts.

## Safety & privacy

Low risk. The craft is entirely in honesty-at-a-sustainable-level and matching signals to intent; the only real hazards are the fabricated excuse and the insincere counter-offer, both of which are self-armed traps.
