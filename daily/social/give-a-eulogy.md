## Goal

Deliver a brief, truthful eulogy that helps mourners remember the person with love, without pretending they were perfect or making the service about your grief.

## Preconditions

- Permission from the family, organizer, clergy, or funeral director to speak.
- The service length, order, microphone situation, and any religious or cultural expectations.
- A written copy, because grief changes memory and breath.

## Steps

1. **Choose one theme.** Pick a sentence that can hold the eulogy: "She made people feel expected," "He repaired what others threw away," or "They loved by showing up." → *Expect:* every story you choose can point back to that theme.
2. **Gather a few stories.** Select two or three short stories from different parts of their life, including one small everyday detail. Ask close family if any story is off-limits. → *Expect:* the eulogy shows a full person through concrete moments.
3. **Honor, do not perfect.** Name complexity gently if needed: "Life with him was not always easy, and his love was still real." Avoid score-settling, public revelations, or forced sainthood. → *Expect:* mourners hear truth without feeling ambushed.
4. **Keep it short.** Aim for 3 to 5 minutes, about 450 to 700 spoken words. Cut anything that exists mainly to explain your relationship to the deceased. → *Expect:* the eulogy can be read slowly without rushing the service.
5. **Make an emotion plan.** Print the eulogy in large type, mark pauses, bring water, and ask one person to stand ready to finish if you cannot. [BRANCH: continue | hand off] continue after a breath if words return; hand off if you cannot speak. → *Expect:* tears are allowed without the service losing its path.
6. **Practice aloud once, then stop polishing.** Read it standing, with the first and last sentences exactly as written. Do not keep revising into the night. → *Expect:* your voice has met the hardest lines before the service.
7. **Deliver to the room slowly.** Start with your name and relationship, then the theme, stories, and a final line of gratitude or farewell. ⚠️ *Irreversible:* public accusations, secrets, and family conflicts cannot be unsaid at a funeral, so remove them before speaking. → *Expect:* the room has room to grieve, smile, and breathe.
8. **Return the focus after speaking.** Step away, sit down, and let the service continue. Save extra memories for the reception or condolence line. → *Expect:* the eulogy closes cleanly instead of becoming a second service.

## Decision points

- If the person was difficult or harmful → speak only what you can truthfully honor, or decline to speak.
- If several people are speaking → coordinate stories so the same anecdote is not repeated three times.
- If young children are present → avoid graphic medical or death details.
- If a faith tradition has set wording → ask where personal memory belongs and follow the order.

## Failure modes & recovery

- **F1 Too emotional to continue:** detect losing breath or words → pause, sip water, say "I need a moment," or hand the page to the backup reader.
- **F2 Eulogy turns into biography:** detect a timeline of dates and jobs → cut to the theme and keep only stories that reveal character.
- **F3 Family disagreement over content:** detect requests to remove a story → remove it unless keeping it is clearly necessary and welcome to the primary mourners.
- **F4 You go blank at the microphone:** detect silence and panic → read the next printed sentence; nobody expects memorization.

## Verification

The eulogy has one clear theme, two or three approved stories, a 3 to 5 minute length, and a backup reader or printed copy ready.

## Variations

- Very short service: use one story and one closing sentence, under two minutes.
- Memorial gathering: slightly more conversational tone is fine, but the same privacy rules apply.
- Written-only tribute: send the full text to family afterward if they want a copy.

## Safety & privacy

Funerals gather people in acute grief. Avoid medical specifics, family secrets, blame, and surprise disclosures; the ethical test is whether the closest mourners feel held rather than exposed.
