## Goal

An item is borrowed from a neighbor, friend, or colleague and returned in equal-or-better condition, on time — leaving the lending relationship stronger, not spent.

## Preconditions

- A genuine short-term need for something you don't own (a drill, a ladder, a cup of sugar, a charger).
- A lender for whom this is a small ask — the item is not their livelihood, irreplaceable, or something they visibly treasure.

## Steps

1. **Ask with an easy out and a defined term.** "Any chance I could borrow your drill for an hour this afternoon? Totally fine if not." The escape hatch and the timeframe are what make it a small ask. → *Expect:* a genuine yes (or a no you accept gracefully at zero cost to either side).
2. **Receive it with a condition glance.** Note the state together, casually: "Great — battery's about half, cool." Not an inspection ritual; one shared sentence that prevents later ambiguity. → *Expect:* both parties hold the same picture of the item's condition and accessories (case, bits, charger).
3. **Use it within the asked scope.** The borrowed drill hangs one shelf; it does not renovate the bathroom or get sub-lent to your cousin. Scope creep is a new ask — make it explicitly if needed. → *Expect:* usage matches what was described.
4. **Return it better than borrowed, on or before the term.** Charged, cleaned, refilled (fuel, tape, sugar replaced with a fresh bag), accessories complete. ⚠️ *Irreversible-ish:* late unprompted returns quietly re-price every future ask — the *lender having to ask for it back* is the relationship failure, not damage. → *Expect:* item back in their hands by the stated time, in better shape than it left.
5. **Close with thanks + the report.** "Lifesaver — worked perfectly, I topped up the charge." Small reciprocity where natural (the beer, the returned favor, your own stuff lent freely). → *Expect:* the lender ends *gladder* they lent it; the channel stays open both ways.

## Decision points

- You broke or lost it → tell them *immediately and first*, with the replacement already offered/in motion: "The bit snapped — I've ordered the same set, arrives Tuesday." Speed and initiative convert an incident into credibility.
- Term needs extending → ask before it lapses, never after: "Still OK if I keep the ladder till the weekend?"
- You're the lender being asked → decide by replaceability + your relationship, say no cleanly when it's no ("that one I don't lend out — but here's my other one"), and note-to-self who returns things well.

## Failure modes & recovery

- **F1 You forgot to return it (found it a month later):** return it now, named plainly: "I'm so sorry — this hid in my trunk for a month." Late-with-ownership beats never-with-shame; add the reciprocity gesture.
- **F2 It came back to *you* (as lender) damaged, unmentioned:** one matter-of-fact raise — "hey, the blade's chipped, was that from the weekend?" — settles it or teaches you the lending list needs editing.
- **F3 Chronic borrower dynamics (you're always asking the same person):** buy your own X once the asks recur — borrowing is for spikes, not baseline; or formalize sharing ("splitting the cost of a shared ladder?").

## Verification

The item is back with its owner on time, complete and equal-or-better, they learned about any incident from you first, and the honest test: they'd say yes faster next time.

## Variations

- Cup-of-sugar class (consumables): the return is a replacement of equal-or-nicer kind, at the next natural moment — not the same three eggs back.
- Workplace equipment: the same recipe plus the org's sign-out rules; the shared-sentence condition check (step 2) does double duty as due diligence.

## Safety & privacy

Low risk. The whole recipe is trust mechanics: defined term, scope honesty, better-than-borrowed, bad-news-first. Money is the exception — lending money is a different act with different failure modes, and it is deliberately not this recipe.
