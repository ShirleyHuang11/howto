## Goal

Create a surprise party that feels loving instead of stressful, with the guest of honor arriving at the right place, surrounded by the right people, and still able to enjoy the evening.

## Preconditions

- A guest of honor who would actually like a surprise, not someone who dislikes attention or disrupted plans.
- A trusted co-planner who can help with invitations, timing, and the ruse.
- A date, location, budget, and one person who knows the guest of honor's calendar well enough to avoid conflicts.

## Steps

1. **Confirm the surprise is kind.** Quietly check with a partner, close friend, sibling, or coworker whether the person enjoys surprises and public attention. → *Expect:* a clear yes, a smaller-plan suggestion, or a warning to make it private.
2. **Build the guest list around safety and warmth.** Invite people the honoree would be happy to see, not everyone who might feel entitled to be included. Keep the list small if secrecy matters. → *Expect:* a guest list with no known conflicts, exes, strained relatives, or awkward work overlaps unless the honoree would choose them.
3. **Choose one secrecy channel.** Use one group text, email thread, or event page, and title it blandly if shared screens are likely. Tell guests not to mention it to the honoree or post public hints. → *Expect:* every invitee has the same date, time, address, arrival window, and secrecy rule.
4. **Design the ruse.** Pick a believable reason for the honoree to arrive dressed appropriately and on time: dinner reservation, help with an errand, casual drinks, or a work pretext. [BRANCH: close friend drives | honoree arrives alone] choose a driver when timing must be controlled; send a simple calendar hold when independence matters. → *Expect:* the honoree has a normal reason to go there without sensing a production.
5. **Set the arrival choreography.** Ask guests to arrive 30 minutes early, park away from the entrance, silence phones, and keep coats or bags out of sight. Assign one person to watch the door. → *Expect:* the room is ready before the honoree is nearby, with no visible cars or names giving it away.
6. **Plan the reveal.** Decide whether people shout, clap, sing, or simply greet them warmly. Avoid jumping out close to their body. → *Expect:* the first five seconds feel celebratory, not frightening.
7. **Prepare the leak escape.** If the surprise leaks, pivot to "we wanted to gather everyone for you" and keep the party. Do not interrogate the leaker during the event. → *Expect:* the plan still works as a planned celebration if the surprise part fails.
8. **Protect the honoree after the reveal.** Give them water, a place for their things, and a quiet minute if they look overwhelmed. ⚠️ *Irreversible:* once the reveal happens, it cannot be made private again, so check their face before pulling them into speeches or photos. → *Expect:* the honoree is smiling, settled, and not trapped at the center of attention.
9. **End with cleanup and thanks.** Assign cleanup before the party begins, then thank the host, driver, and secret-keepers within a day. → *Expect:* the host is not left alone with the mess, and helpers feel appreciated.

## Decision points

- If the honoree hates surprises → make it a known dinner or small gathering and call it thoughtful, not failed.
- If the guest list grows past the secrecy limit → split into a small surprise and a later open celebration.
- If timing depends on transit, childcare, or work → put one trusted person in charge of moving the honoree, not the whole group chat.
- If someone asks to bring an uninvited guest → allow only if the honoree would be glad to see them and capacity allows.

## Failure modes & recovery

- **F1 Surprise leaks:** detect it when the honoree hints or asks directly → admit only the least necessary truth, keep the gathering warm, and stop treating secrecy as the point.
- **F2 Honoree arrives early:** detect it from the door watcher or driver → turn lights and music to normal, greet them calmly, and let late guests join without dramatics.
- **F3 Guest causes tension:** detect visible avoidance, sharp comments, or the honoree withdrawing → move the guest into a task, change seating, or ask a co-host to walk them out kindly.
- **F4 Reveal overwhelms them:** detect tears, silence, anger, or frozen body language → lower the volume, give them a private minute, and let them choose whether to continue.

## Verification

The honoree arrived at the intended place, understood the celebration was for them, and had at least one comfortable way to stay, pause, or leave.

## Variations

- Workplace version: clear it with a manager or close colleague, avoid alcohol as the central plan, and keep speeches brief.
- Restaurant version: confirm cake, seating, arrival time, and bill handling with the restaurant before guests arrive.
- Home version: hide supplies outside normal sight lines and keep one room clean enough for an early arrival.

## Safety & privacy

Surprise plans expose calendars, relationships, addresses, and sometimes sensitive family dynamics. Share only what guests need, avoid public event pages when secrecy matters, and do not pressure the honoree to perform delight for photos.
