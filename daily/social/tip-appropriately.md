## Goal

Choose a reasonable tip for a service situation while accounting for country norms, service type, payment method, and when tipping is not expected.

## Preconditions

- You know what service was provided and whether a service charge is already included.
- You have the bill total, local currency, and a way to pay.
- You can check local norms when traveling.

## Steps

1. **Identify the country norm.** Before applying a percentage, ask whether tipping is standard, optional, or unusual in that country. → *Expect:* you avoid importing the wrong custom.
2. **Check for included service.** Look for "service charge," "gratuity," "cover," or local equivalents on the bill. → *Expect:* you know whether a tip has already been added.
3. **Match the service type.** Use table service, bar, delivery, taxi, hotel, salon, or counter service as the category. → *Expect:* the tip decision fits the work performed.
4. **Estimate the local range.** In the United States, table service is commonly around 15 to 20 percent before tax, with more for exceptional service. → *Expect:* you have a starting number rather than guessing under pressure.
5. **Adjust for service quality and constraints.** Reduce for serious service failures, not for kitchen delays the server did not control. → *Expect:* the amount reflects the service fairly.
6. **Choose cash or card.** [BRANCH: cash | card] leave cash when you want the worker to receive it directly, or add it on card when that is the available method. → *Expect:* the tip is visible and not accidentally skipped.
7. **Handle counter prompts.** For quick counter service, choose a small amount, custom amount, or no tip based on local norm and effort. → *Expect:* the screen prompt does not force a decision you do not mean.
8. **Do the math.** Move the decimal for 10 percent, double it for 20 percent, and round to a simple amount. → *Expect:* the final total is legible and affordable.
9. **Close the transaction.** Write the tip and total clearly, or hand cash directly with thanks. → *Expect:* the worker and payment system can read your intent.

## Decision points

- Traveling where tipping is rare or rude → follow local guidance and do not insist on tipping.
- A mandatory gratuity is included → add extra only for truly extra service.
- Service was unsafe or abusive → do not reward it; ask for a manager if needed.
- You cannot afford the usual percentage → tip what you can, but avoid high-service settings you cannot support regularly.

## Failure modes & recovery

- **F1 Double tipping:** detect an included gratuity plus a suggested tip screen, recover by tipping only extra if you choose.
- **F2 Math mistake:** detect a total that looks too high or low, recover by recalculating from the pre-tax subtotal when appropriate.
- **F3 Country mismatch:** detect uncertainty while traveling, recover by checking a current local guide or asking hotel staff discreetly.
- **F4 Cash left unnoticed:** detect a busy table or windy outdoor setting, recover by handing the cash to the server or weighting it under a dish.

## Verification

The final paid amount reflects local custom, service type, any included charge, and your intentional choice.

## Variations

- United States: tipping is expected in many personal-service settings, especially restaurants.
- Japan: routine tipping is generally not part of the custom and may confuse the transaction.
- Large groups: restaurants may add automatic gratuity, so read the bill before adding more.

## Safety & privacy

Low risk. Keep card receipts private, do not flash large cash amounts, and remember that norms change by country and service category.
