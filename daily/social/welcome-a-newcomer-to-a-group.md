## Goal

A newcomer enters a group with names, context, and at least one easy way to participate.

## Preconditions

- You are already part of the group or have permission to host introductions.
- You know the newcomer's name and, if possible, pronunciation and basic connection to the group.
- The setting allows brief introductions without interrupting something urgent.

## Steps

1. **Greet them promptly.** Use their name, say yours, and make the first minute simple: "I'm glad you made it." → *Expect:* they are no longer standing unnoticed.
2. **Orient them to the space.** Point out where coats, bathrooms, food, seats, chat links, or materials are. → *Expect:* they can meet basic needs without asking publicly.
3. **Introduce them around.** Start with two or three friendly people and include a useful hook, not a biography. → *Expect:* names come with reasons to talk.
4. **Give the unwritten rules.** Explain norms kindly, such as how turns work, whether people bring snacks, or where beginners usually start. → *Expect:* hidden expectations become visible.
5. **Include them in the next activity.** [BRANCH: structured group | casual group] In a structured group, give them a role or partner; in a casual group, invite them into a conversation with an easy topic. → *Expect:* they are participating, not only watching.
6. **Check consent around attention.** Ask before making them introduce themselves to everyone or answer personal questions. ⚠️ *Irreversible:* a forced spotlight can make a first visit feel unsafe, so do not surprise them with public attention. → *Expect:* attention level matches their comfort.
7. **Keep an eye on the edges.** Notice if they drift alone, cannot follow jargon, or miss a transition. → *Expect:* you can re-include them before awkwardness hardens.
8. **Check back before they leave.** Ask how it went, answer questions, and tell them the next meeting or channel if they want it. → *Expect:* they know how to return.

## Decision points

- If the newcomer is shy, pair them with one warm person instead of a group introduction.
- If the group uses acronyms or inside jokes, translate once without making them feel behind.
- If someone asks intrusive questions, redirect to the shared activity.
- If the group is busy, assign a specific buddy rather than assuming everyone will help.
- If they do not return, send one low-pressure message, then leave the choice with them.

## Failure modes & recovery

- **F1 Name forgotten:** detect it when you hesitate → ask again plainly and use it soon after.
- **F2 Overwhelmed newcomer:** detect it when they give short answers or retreat → reduce attention and offer a quieter entry point.
- **F3 Clique closes back up:** detect it when old members resume private talk → bridge with a shared topic or rotate partners.
- **F4 Wrong assumption:** detect it when your introduction misstates them → correct yourself quickly and let them define their own context.

## Verification

The newcomer knows at least three names, understands the basic norms, joins one activity or conversation, and knows how to come back.

## Variations

- Online group: greet in chat, explain channels, and tag one helpful person.
- Volunteer group: add safety procedures and who supervises new tasks.
- Friend gathering: avoid over-hosting after the first bridge.

## Safety & privacy

Low risk. Do not share the newcomer's personal history, contact details, identity labels, or reason for joining unless they volunteer it.
