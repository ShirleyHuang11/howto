## Goal

The other person understands exactly which behavior you are flagging and why it matters, agrees on a concrete change going forward, and the relationship is intact or stronger afterward.

## Preconditions

- You observed the behavior yourself (secondhand reports change the script: you can only discuss what was reported, not assert it happened).
- The event is recent, ideally within the last 48 hours; stale feedback reads as a stored grievance.
- A private setting is available. Praise can be public; corrective feedback in front of others becomes about the audience, not the behavior.
- You are calm. If you are still angry, wait. Feedback delivered hot is venting with a cover story.

## Steps

1. **Prepare one specific instance.** Write down the situation, the observable behavior, and its impact before you talk. One issue per conversation; a list of three becomes an indictment. → *Expect:* you can state the behavior in one sentence a video camera would have captured ("you interrupted the client twice"), not a trait ("you're dismissive").
2. **Ask first.** "Do you have a few minutes? I'd like to share some feedback on yesterday's meeting. Is now okay?" The ask transfers a little control and takes the ambush out of it. [BRANCH: they say yes → proceed | they say not now → get a concrete time within a day or two, and hold it] → *Expect:* explicit consent and their attention, or a scheduled slot.
3. **State the situation and behavior, factually.** "In yesterday's client call, when Sam was presenting the timeline, you cut in twice before he finished." No "always", no "never", no adjectives about them as a person. → *Expect:* they can locate the exact moment; a fact-check ("was it twice?") is fine, an identity defense means you drifted into character territory.
4. **State the impact.** The consequence you observed or felt: "Sam lost his thread, and the client started directing questions only to you, which undercuts the team." Impact is what makes it feedback instead of a preference. → *Expect:* a shift in their face from defense to processing; this is usually the moment it lands.
5. **Pause and genuinely listen.** Ask "how did it look from your side?" and then actually stop talking. Their context may change your read (maybe Sam asked them beforehand to jump in). → *Expect:* their account, which you can honestly engage with; feedback that survives their context is stronger, feedback that doesn't should be withdrawn on the spot.
6. **Agree on the forward-looking change.** Co-author it: "What would work better next time?" beats dictating. Settle on one observable behavior ("let Sam finish sections, add your points at the transitions"). → *Expect:* a specific next-time behavior both of you could verify, said back in their words.
7. **Close with real regard.** One true sentence about why the feedback was worth giving: "I'm raising it because clients should see how good this team is." Skip the compliment sandwich; a bolted-on opening compliment teaches people to flinch at praise. → *Expect:* a normal-temperature end to the conversation, ideally with thanks.

## Decision points

- The behavior involves ethics, safety, or policy violations → this recipe does not apply; use your organization's reporting channel instead of a private chat.
- Power differential (they outrank you) → same structure, softer frame: lead step 3 with "I might be missing context" and weight step 5 heavily.
- They get visibly upset at step 3 or 4 → slow down, acknowledge ("I can see this lands hard"), and offer to continue later; forcing through tears or anger destroys the content.

## Failure modes & recovery

- **F1 Character defense ("so you think I'm rude"):** detect the leap from behavior to identity → return them to the tape: "I'm only talking about the two interruptions in that call, nothing broader."
- **F2 Kitchen-sinking:** you notice yourself adding "and another thing" → stop, name it ("that's a separate topic, not for today"), and re-anchor on the single instance.
- **F3 The context flip:** their side (step 5) shows your read was wrong → say so plainly ("that changes it, I withdraw the point"), which costs nothing and buys enormous credibility for next time.
- **F4 Agreement without change:** two weeks later the behavior recurs → one brief follow-up referencing the co-authored agreement, not the original offense; if it recurs again, the conversation changes from feedback to expectations.

## Verification

The other person can restate the specific behavior and its impact in their own words, a single forward-looking change was agreed and is observable, no character labels were used by either side, and a follow-up moment (if needed) has a date.

## Variations

- Written feedback (async/remote): same SBI order, but always offer a call first; text strips tone and doubles perceived harshness.
- Peer-to-peer with no authority: frame as an observation plus a question ("I noticed X, and it seemed to cause Y. How do you see it?") rather than a request.
- Upward feedback: anchor to their stated goals ("you said you want the team speaking up more; X had the opposite effect").

## Safety & privacy

Low risk, but words are not retractable: what you say in step 3 will be remembered verbatim, so script it beforehand. Keep the conversation private afterward too; retelling it to colleagues converts feedback into gossip.
