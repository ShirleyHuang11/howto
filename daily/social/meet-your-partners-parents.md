## Goal

Meet your partner's parents or guardians in a way that shows respect, curiosity, and steadiness without performing a version of yourself you cannot maintain.

## Preconditions

- Your partner has told you names, pronouns, family structure, basic etiquette, and any sensitive topics to avoid.
- A clear plan for time, place, dress, transportation, and whether the meeting includes a meal.
- Agreement with your partner about signals if either of you needs help changing the subject or leaving.

## Steps

1. **Ask your partner for the field guide.** Learn how their parents like to be addressed, what they care about, and what topics create tension. → *Expect:* you know at least three safe topics and three avoid zones.
2. **Bring a small gift when appropriate.** Choose something modest: flowers, dessert, coffee, fruit, or a host item your partner confirms fits. Do not make the gift expensive or attention-seeking. → *Expect:* the gift says "thank you for having me" without creating obligation.
3. **Arrive ready to listen more than talk.** Put your phone away, greet each person by name, and let the first minutes be about them and the setting. → *Expect:* they experience you as present, not auditioning.
4. **Use safe topics with real curiosity.** Ask about food, neighborhood, family stories your partner has mentioned, hobbies, pets, travel, or the house. [BRANCH: formal family | casual family] use more titles and restraint for formal families; relax only after they do. → *Expect:* conversation moves without forcing politics, money, religion, or old conflicts.
5. **Help out without taking over.** Offer to set the table, carry dishes, pour water, or clear plates. Accept a no graciously. → *Expect:* you contribute to the household rhythm instead of sitting like a guest of honor.
6. **Let your partner be known.** Tell one kind, specific thing you appreciate about your partner, but do not reveal private conflicts or overly intimate details. → *Expect:* parents hear affection and respect, not a performance.
7. **Exit while energy is still good.** Thank them directly, gather your things without lingering at the door forever, and let your partner handle any family debrief first. → *Expect:* the meeting ends warmly before fatigue turns into strain.
8. **Send follow-up thanks.** Within a day, send a short message through your partner or directly if that is normal: "Thank you for dinner. I loved hearing about the garden." ⚠️ *Irreversible:* first impressions cannot be fully redone, so repair small missteps quickly and plainly. → *Expect:* the hosts receive a specific thank-you, not a generic formality.

## Decision points

- If family customs differ from yours → follow your partner's guidance over your instinct.
- If they ask a too-personal question → answer lightly, then redirect: "We're still figuring that out, but I heard you just renovated the kitchen."
- If a tense topic starts → let your partner lead unless they signal for help.
- If alcohol is offered → drink less than your comfort limit, or decline without a long explanation.

## Failure modes & recovery

- **F1 You say something awkward:** detect a pause, correction, or confused look → acknowledge briefly, clarify, and move on without making them soothe you.
- **F2 They criticize your partner:** detect old family patterns surfacing → do not join in; say something steady and positive, then change the topic.
- **F3 Conversation stalls:** detect repeated short answers → ask about something visible in the room or a story your partner pre-cleared.
- **F4 You overstay:** detect yawns, cleanup, or repeated time checks → thank them and leave.

## Verification

You greeted people respectfully, listened more than you talked, avoided known sensitive topics, helped where welcome, and sent a specific thank-you within 24 hours.

## Variations

- Video call: check lighting and sound, keep it shorter, and still send a thank-you afterward.
- Large family gathering: learn five names in advance and focus on being kind, not memorable to everyone.
- Restaurant meeting: offer to contribute or thank the person paying, based on your partner's cultural guidance.

## Safety & privacy

Family meetings can expose religion, politics, finances, immigration status, health, and relationship history. Let your partner decide what is shareable and do not trade private couple information for approval.
