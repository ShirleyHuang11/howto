## Goal

A compliment is received with a plain thank-you and warmth, leaving the giver glad they said it, instead of being argued with, deflected, or awkwardly boomeranged.

## Preconditions

- Someone has just complimented you (work, appearance, cooking, a skill).
- Nothing else: this recipe exists because the required move is tiny and most adults still fumble it.

## Steps

1. **Let it land.** Half a second of pause, eye contact if in person. Resist the reflex to start talking before they finish. → *Expect:* the compliment was fully spoken; you did not talk over its ending.
2. **Say "thank you," full stop.** The complete move is two words plus a smile. Not "thanks, but," not "oh, this old thing," not "it was nothing." The period is the technique. → *Expect:* you have stopped speaking and the silence feels longer to you than to them (it always does).
3. **Do not deflect, discount, or rebut.** Deflection ("it was all luck," "the team did everything," "I got it on sale") reads as arguing with their judgment: they said something kind and you told them they were wrong. → *Expect:* nothing you said contradicted the compliment.
4. **Optionally add one sentence of warmth or truth.** [BRANCH: earned achievement → own it briefly ("thanks, I worked hard on that") | genuinely shared credit → share specifically ("thank you, and Maya built the hard half") | small thing → warmth only ("thanks, that's kind of you")] One sentence, then done. → *Expect:* the addition is a single sentence and none of it shrinks the thank-you.
5. **Return warmth, not a rebuttal and not an instant counter-compliment.** A reflexive "no YOU look great" reads as a formality, not a gift. If you genuinely have a compliment for them, bank it and deliver it later or on a different topic, where it stands on its own. → *Expect:* the exchange ends warm; no compliment was traded back within the same breath.
6. **Move the conversation forward.** A natural pivot ("thanks! How was your weekend?") releases any lingering awkwardness for both of you. → *Expect:* conversation continues normally; nobody is stranded in the moment.

## Decision points

- Compliment on written work or a deliverable, in front of others → same plain thank-you; a public forum raises the deflection urge and the cost of indulging it. Credit specific collaborators if real, then stop.
- The compliment feels like flattery with an agenda (softening you up before an ask) → still just "thank you," then let their ask arrive and evaluate it on its own merits. The thank-you concedes nothing.
- A workplace comment on your appearance that feels off → a flat, brief "thanks" with no warmth added, then an immediate subject change. Escalate only if it repeats or sharpens.

## Failure modes & recovery

- **F1 Deflection already left your mouth:** "oh, it was noth..." Recover mid-sentence; it works: "...actually, thank you, I'm really happy with how it came out." The correction itself lands charmingly.
- **F2 The compliment-boomerang:** you instantly complimented them back and it hung there sounding hollow. Recover: let it go, don't stack more compliments to fix it; return to normal conversation.
- **F3 Compliment on something you privately think failed:** the urge to explain everything wrong with it is strong. Recover: their experience of it was good, and that is what they compliment; "thank you, glad it worked for you" is fully honest.
- **F4 Freezing (just going red and silent):** a beat of silence plus a smile already reads fine from outside. Recover: a late "thank you, sorry, that caught me off guard" is endearing, not a failure.

## Verification

You said "thank you" without any qualifier that shrank it, said at most one added sentence, gave no instant counter-compliment, and the conversation moved on with the giver visibly at ease. If you can recall your exact words and none argue with the compliment, it succeeded.

## Variations

- High-modesty cultures: in some settings (notably parts of East Asia), plain acceptance can read as immodest among older or traditional company; a soft partial deflection is the local norm. Read the room; with international or younger company, the plain thank-you wins.
- Text and chat: "thank you!" plus at most one emoji-free warm sentence; over-typed gratitude reads as fishing for more.

## Safety & privacy

Low risk. The only real hazards are social: repeated boundary-crossing "compliments" are not compliments and this recipe does not apply to them; name the behavior or involve the appropriate channel instead of thanking it.
