## Goal

Get through a toddler tantrum safely while holding the boundary, naming the feeling, and reconnecting after the storm passes.

## Preconditions

- The child is a toddler or young child whose tantrum is not caused by an immediate medical emergency.
- You can move hazards, reduce audience pressure, or leave the area if needed.
- You know the boundary you need to hold, such as no hitting, no candy before dinner, or leaving the playground.
- You can stay close enough to supervise without arguing every sentence.

## Steps

1. **Check safety first.** Move the child away from traffic, stairs, sharp objects, hot surfaces, or breakable items. → *Expect:* the tantrum can continue without immediate physical danger.
2. **Lower your voice and body.** Speak slowly, unclench your hands, and get near the child's level without crowding them. → *Expect:* you are not adding more intensity to the room.
3. **Acknowledge the feeling.** Say the feeling and the limit in one short phrase, such as "You are mad. I will not let you hit." → *Expect:* the child hears that the feeling is accepted and the unsafe behavior is not.
4. **Hold the boundary.** Do not give the denied item or cancel the required transition because of screaming. → *Expect:* the rule stays the same without a long debate.
5. **Offer a small choice.** [BRANCH: choice accepted | choice ignored] give two acceptable options, such as walk or be carried, blue cup or green cup. → *Expect:* the child has control inside the boundary.
6. **Use few words.** Repeat the same calm limit if needed and stop explaining once the child is too upset to process language. → *Expect:* the conflict does not grow through negotiation.
7. **Block unsafe behavior.** Hold hands gently away from hitting, move objects, or carry the child if leaving is required. ⚠️ *Irreversible:* never shake, throw, or strike a child, because injury and fear cannot be undone. → *Expect:* everyone remains physically safe.
8. **Wait for the wave to pass.** Stay nearby if safe, or give space while watching, and let crying slow before teaching. → *Expect:* breathing, volume, and body tension start to come down.
9. **Reconnect after.** Offer comfort, restate the limit briefly, and guide the next action without a lecture. → *Expect:* the child can rejoin the day and knows the relationship is intact.

## Decision points

- If the tantrum happens in public → move to a quieter safe spot and keep the same boundary.
- If hunger, tiredness, or overstimulation is driving repeated tantrums → adjust routine after this episode, not in the middle of the demand.
- If tantrums include injury, breath-holding with fainting, developmental regression, or extreme frequency → discuss it with the pediatrician.

## Failure modes & recovery

- **F1 Boundary collapses:** the child gets the denied item after screaming → reset next time with a smaller, clearer limit and expect protest.
- **F2 Adult escalates:** yelling, threats, or rough handling starts → put distance between you and the child if safe, call another adult, and repair afterward.
- **F3 Child hurts someone:** hitting, biting, or throwing continues → block access, remove objects, and leave the situation if needed.
- **F4 Tantrum restarts:** a new demand appears after calming → acknowledge once, repeat the limit, and move to food, sleep, or quiet.

## Verification

The child and others are safe, the core boundary was held, and the child has reconnected or moved to a calmer next activity.

## Variations

- `transition-tantrum`: use warnings, visual timers, and a consistent goodbye ritual before the next transition.
- `sensory-overload`: reduce noise, lights, clothing irritation, or crowding before expecting cooperation.
- `shared-care`: agree on one phrase and boundary so caregivers do not undercut each other.

## Safety & privacy

Medium risk comes from unsafe surroundings, rough adult reactions, and public shame. Protect the child from injury and humiliation, and seek pediatric or behavioral support if tantrums feel beyond ordinary development.
