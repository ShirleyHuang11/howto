## Goal

Write vows that sound like you, honor your partner specifically, and give the ceremony a clear promise instead of a speech that wanders.

## Preconditions

- Agreement with your partner and officiant on whether personal vows are part of the ceremony.
- Any ceremony limits: length, religious language, legal wording, microphone setup, and whether vows are private or public.
- Enough quiet time to draft, revise, and practice aloud before the wedding day.

## Steps

1. **Set the container together.** Agree on approximate length, tone, and whether you will share drafts or keep them private. [BRANCH: matching tone | surprise tone] choose matching if one partner fears imbalance; choose surprise only if both are comfortable. → *Expect:* both people know whether the vows are 1 minute, 3 minutes, formal, funny, spiritual, or plainspoken.
2. **Use a promise structure.** Draft in four parts: what I see in you, one story that shows it, what our life has taught me, and three promises I can actually keep. → *Expect:* the draft has a spine and does not become a biography.
3. **Choose specific stories.** Pick one or two moments with concrete details: the bus stop in the rain, the hospital chair, the terrible first apartment, the way they label leftovers. → *Expect:* a stranger could understand why this person is loved, and your partner could recognize themselves instantly.
4. **Make promises behavioral.** Replace "I promise to love you forever" alone with actions: listen before defending, make coffee on early flights, protect our jokes, ask for repair after hard days. → *Expect:* the vows include promises that would change a normal Tuesday.
5. **Cut private material.** Remove sexual details, family secrets, financial history, medical specifics, exes, and jokes that make your partner the punchline. ⚠️ *Irreversible:* once spoken in a ceremony or recorded, private details cannot be fully taken back. → *Expect:* every line could be heard by the room without your partner flinching.
6. **Practice aloud with a timer.** Read slowly, standing up, at least three times. Mark breaths and hard words. → *Expect:* the vows fit the agreed length and your mouth can say every sentence naturally.
7. **Prepare the backup card.** Print or handwrite the final version in large type on a card, not a phone if avoidable. Give a spare copy to the officiant or best person. → *Expect:* you can read through tears, wind, dead batteries, or shaking hands.
8. **Deliver to your partner, not the audience.** Hold the card low, look up at the starts and endings of sentences, and slow down when emotion rises. → *Expect:* your partner receives the vows as a promise, while guests simply witness it.

## Decision points

- If your partner writes shorter than you → cut yours to meet them instead of making them feel outperformed.
- If humor is central to you both → keep it affectionate and let the last third turn sincere.
- If you cry easily → build in a short sentence you can say while breathing, such as "I am here, and I mean every word."
- If the ceremony has religious or cultural vows → ask the officiant where personal vows fit and what wording must remain unchanged.

## Failure modes & recovery

- **F1 Draft sounds generic:** detect phrases that could fit anyone → add one sensory detail, one shared place, and one promise tied to daily life.
- **F2 Vows become too long:** detect a reading over the agreed time → keep the strongest story, keep three promises, and cut setup.
- **F3 You freeze during delivery:** detect blankness or panic → look at the card, take one breath, and read the next line without apologizing.
- **F4 Partner feels exposed:** detect discomfort during review or after ceremony → apologize plainly, avoid defending the joke, and protect future versions from being posted.

## Verification

The final vows fit the agreed length, include at least one specific story, contain concrete promises, and exist on a readable backup card.

## Variations

- Private vows: exchange the intimate version before the ceremony and use a shorter public version at the altar.
- Renewal vows: include what you now know about keeping the promise, not only how the relationship began.
- Multilingual ceremony: keep sentences simple and give translators or readers the final version early.

## Safety & privacy

Vows can expose private history in front of family, coworkers, and cameras. Treat your partner's dignity as the boundary, and get explicit consent before posting full vows online.
