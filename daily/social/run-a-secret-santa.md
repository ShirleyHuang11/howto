## Goal

A Secret Santa exchange where every participant has one recipient, understands the budget and rules, and no one is pressured into awkward spending.

## Preconditions

- A group that wants to participate, with opt-in consent rather than automatic enrollment.
- A deadline, exchange date, and organizer who can keep names private.
- A way to communicate rules and collect wish lists.

## Steps

1. **Invite people to opt in.** Send the date, budget, exchange format, and RSVP deadline before collecting names. → *Expect:* only willing participants are included.
2. **Set a realistic budget.** Choose a maximum amount that fits the group, and say whether handmade, thrifted, or joke gifts count. → *Expect:* participants know the spending ceiling and tone.
3. **Collect wish lists and restrictions.** Ask for three to five ideas, sizes if relevant, allergies, dislikes, and delivery address if remote. → *Expect:* givers have enough guidance to avoid guessing badly.
4. **Decide exclusions before the draw.** [BRANCH: exclusions | open draw] Exclude partners, managers and direct reports, close relatives, or last year's match if that makes the group more comfortable; otherwise keep the draw fully random. → *Expect:* the rules are clear before names are assigned.
5. **Draw names privately.** Use slips, a spreadsheet, or a reputable online draw tool, then send each giver only their assigned recipient. → *Expect:* every participant has exactly one recipient and secrecy is intact.
6. **Send the rules in one message.** Include budget, wish list link, gift deadline, reveal plan, wrapping instructions, and who to contact if there is a problem. → *Expect:* nobody has to search old messages for the basics.
7. **Track completion quietly.** Check in one week before the exchange and offer help for late shipping, illness, or confusion. → *Expect:* missing gifts are caught before the reveal.
8. **Run the reveal.** [BRANCH: public reveal | anonymous drop] Let recipients guess and thank their giver if the group enjoys that; allow anonymous gifts if privacy or workplace hierarchy matters. → *Expect:* the exchange ends with thanks, not interrogation.
9. **Protect the draw.** ⚠️ *Irreversible:* revealing assignments early can spoil the exchange, so do not forward draw results or share the master list. → *Expect:* surprises remain intact until the agreed reveal.

## Decision points

- If there is a manager in the group, make participation optional and avoid gifts flowing upward as an expectation.
- If some people cannot afford the budget, lower it or use a no-purchase theme.
- If a participant joins late, redraw only if everyone agrees and no gifts have been bought.
- If shipping is involved, set a mailing deadline earlier than the party date.
- If the group prefers practical gifts, ask for wish lists instead of joke themes.

## Failure modes & recovery

- **F1 Someone forgot:** detect it when they have no gift near the deadline → help arrange a digital gift card or delayed handoff with a clear apology.
- **F2 Duplicate assignment:** detect it when one person has two givers or none → redo the draw only for affected people if secrecy allows.
- **F3 Budget mismatch:** detect it when a gift is far above the cap → remind the group next time and do not shame the giver publicly.
- **F4 Unwanted gift:** detect it when a recipient is uncomfortable → let them thank briefly and move on, then improve wish list guidance next round.

## Verification

Each opted-in participant received one assignment, the budget and rules were shared, and every recipient got a gift or a clear recovery plan.

## Variations

- Remote exchange: use mailed gifts, digital wish lists, and a video reveal with shipping buffers.
- White elephant: buy for the game, not a person, and publish swap rules separately.
- Family exchange: add exclusions for households or siblings if needed.

## Safety & privacy

Low risk, with some money and privacy concerns. Keep addresses, sizes, allergies, and family exclusions visible only to people who need them.
