## Goal

Decline or renegotiate a work request while preserving the relationship and protecting agreed priorities.

## Preconditions

- You understand the request well enough to assess time, deadline, and owner.
- You know your current priorities or can point to the person who sets them.
- The request is not an emergency requiring immediate action.

## Steps

1. **Pause before answering.** Say you need to check timing or priorities before committing. → *Expect:* you avoid an automatic yes.
2. **Clarify the ask.** Ask what outcome is needed, by when, and what done means. → *Expect:* the request becomes a task, not a vague pressure cloud.
3. **Compare against priorities.** Check your calendar, deadlines, and commitments. → *Expect:* you can name the conflict concretely.
4. **Decline the task, not the person.** Use a sentence like "I cannot take this on by Friday because X." → *Expect:* the no is clear without insulting the requester.
5. **Offer one alternative.** Suggest a later date, smaller scope, different owner, template, or tradeoff. [BRANCH: time conflict | scope conflict] → *Expect:* the requester has a path besides repeated asking.
6. **Escalate priority tradeoffs.** If a manager wants it anyway, ask what should move or stop. → *Expect:* the decision returns to the priority owner.
7. **Put it in writing.** Summarize the decision and any alternative in chat or email. → *Expect:* there is a record of what you did and did not accept.
8. **Hold the boundary.** Repeat the same answer if the request comes back unchanged. → *Expect:* the boundary remains stable without a longer debate.

## Decision points

- Your manager directly reprioritizes → accept the new priority and document what moved.
- The requester is blocked by a tiny question → answer the question without accepting the whole task.
- The work is outside your role but important → route it to the right owner instead of silently absorbing it.
- The request is urgent and safety-critical → help stabilize first, then sort ownership.

## Failure modes & recovery

- **F1 Soft no heard as yes:** detect when the requester thanks you for doing it, recover by restating "I am not able to take this on."
- **F2 Overexplaining:** detect a long defense of your workload, recover by shortening to conflict, no, alternative.
- **F3 Hidden resentment:** detect when you say yes but plan to sacrifice core work, recover by reopening the tradeoff quickly.
- **F4 Public cornering:** detect pressure in a meeting, recover with "I need to check priorities and will respond after."

## Verification

The requester knows you are not taking the original task as stated, and any alternative or priority tradeoff is explicit.

## Variations

- Peer request: be warmer and more collaborative, but keep the no clear.
- Manager request: focus on tradeoffs between assigned priorities.
- Client request: use scope, timeline, and approval language rather than personal workload.

## Safety & privacy

Medium risk from job expectations and reputational effects. Avoid sharing confidential workload details, blaming coworkers, or inventing excuses that can be checked later.
