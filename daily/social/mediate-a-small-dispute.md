## Goal

Two people in a low-stakes conflict (roommates, coworkers, friends) leave with one small concrete change agreed, and neither believes you took the other's side.

## Preconditions

- Both parties accept you as a neutral, or at least neither objects to you. Self-appointed mediators fail; get a nod from each first.
- The dispute is genuinely small: chores, noise, credit for work, a perceived slight. Not money above trivial amounts, not anything involving safety, contracts, or HR-level conduct.
- You can give it a quiet hour without an audience.

## Steps

1. **Get both parties' consent to your role.** Privately, to each: "Want me to help sort this out?" → *Expect:* a yes or shrug from both. A hard no from either means you are a messenger at most, not a mediator; stop here.
2. **Hear each side separately first.** One conversation each, private, no time pressure. Your only jobs: listen, ask what happened and what they actually want, and restate their view back until they say "yes, that's it." Promise nothing, agree with nothing, share nothing from the other conversation yet. → *Expect:* each person confirms your restatement of their position; you have made zero commitments.
3. **Find the common ground underneath the positions.** Positions conflict ("the kitchen is always a mess" vs "I'm nagged constantly"); the interests underneath usually don't (both want a livable home and less friction). Write down, for yourself, one sentence of shared interest and the one or two factual disagreements. → *Expect:* you can state what both people want in words each would endorse.
4. **Convene the joint conversation with ground rules.** Neutral place, no audience. State the rules upfront: one person speaks at a time, describe behavior and its effect, not character ("dishes sat for three days" not "you're a slob"). → *Expect:* both nod to the rules before anyone starts.
5. **Open by stating the common ground, then let each speak.** Start with your shared-interest sentence from step 3; it reframes the meeting from contest to joint problem. Then each party gets a few uninterrupted minutes. Enforce the rules gently but immediately. → *Expect:* both have spoken; interruptions were caught within a sentence.
6. **Have each restate the other's view.** "Before solutions: say back what you heard them say." This is the hinge step; disputes at this scale are mostly two people who have never felt heard by each other. → *Expect:* each produces a restatement the other accepts, possibly after one correction round.
7. **Converge on one small concrete fix.** Not a grand reconciliation: one behavior, one owner, one timeframe ("dishes done by end of each day for two weeks, and asks go in the group chat, not in person at 7am"). Let them propose; you only narrow and concretize. ⚠️ *Irreversible:* do not push either party into a public apology they don't mean; a coerced apology poisons the fix and cannot be retracted. → *Expect:* both say yes to the same sentence, stated aloud.
8. **Set a follow-up and close.** Name a check-in point ("I'll ask you both in two weeks") and end the meeting; do not relitigate the history after agreement. → *Expect:* meeting ends on the agreement, not on a reopened wound; the check-in actually happens.

## Decision points

- One party wants a verdict, not mediation ("just tell them they're wrong") → decline the judge role explicitly; offer the process or nothing.
- Mid-joint-session escalation (shouting, character attacks that won't stop) → pause and split back to separate conversations; reconvene another day. One retreat is normal.
- You discover the "small" dispute has deep roots (long betrayals, money, anything HR- or safety-shaped) → stop mediating and say so; recommend the appropriate channel. Amateur mediation of deep conflict makes it worse.

## Failure modes & recovery

- **F1 Perceived bias:** one party mutters that you're on the other's side. Detect: they go quiet or sarcastic toward you. Recover: name it directly ("it might sound like I'm taking sides; I'm not, and tell me if I do"), then visibly restate that person's view next.
- **F2 Agreement without intention:** quick "fine, whatever" acceptance to end the meeting. Detect: no eye contact, no counter-proposals during step 7. Recover: slow down and ask that person to modify the fix ("what would make this actually workable for you?"); ownership requires authorship.
- **F3 The fix decays after a week:** old pattern returns. Recover: at the follow-up, treat it as calibration, not betrayal; shrink the fix further rather than escalating rhetoric.
- **F4 Triangulation:** one party keeps messaging you privately to relay complaints. Recover: redirect every relay to the joint channel; the moment you carry messages, you are part of the dispute.

## Verification

Both parties separately agreed to the same one-sentence fix with a named owner and timeframe, the joint meeting ended without a walkout, and at the scheduled follow-up both report the fix held (or you re-ran step 7 with a smaller fix). Neither party has stopped speaking to you.

## Variations

- Workplace flavor: same steps, but know the line: harassment, discrimination, or anything reportable goes to HR, not to you. Peer mediation covers friction, not misconduct.
- Kids or siblings: compress steps 2-6 into minutes, keep step 7 literal and tiny ("you get it Mondays, you get it Tuesdays"), and enforce turn-taking firmly.

## Safety & privacy

What each party told you separately is confidential by default; ask before carrying any of it into the joint session. Never mediate anything involving violence, threats, or intimidation. If a "small dispute" reveals one, step out and involve the appropriate authority.
