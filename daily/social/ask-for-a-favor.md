## Goal

You have asked someone for a specific favor in a way that made yes easy and no comfortable, and afterward you closed the loop so they'd gladly say yes again.

## Preconditions

- A favor you can state in one sentence with a size and a deadline ("can you drive me to the airport Saturday at 7am," not "could you maybe help me sometime with a thing").
- A person plausibly able to do it without hardship. Right-sizing the person is half the ask: airport rides go to friends with cars, not to a colleague you met twice.
- You would be willing to do a favor of similar size for them. If not, notice that before asking.

## Steps

1. **Define the exact ask first.** Write the one sentence: what, when, how long it will take them, and the deadline. Vague asks get vague maybes. → *Expect:* a single sentence a stranger could act on, with an honest time estimate.
2. **Pick the channel by size.** [BRANCH: small favor (minutes of their time) → text message, which lets them answer on their own clock | larger favor (hours, money-adjacent, or emotionally loaded) → ask in person or by call, then follow up with details in writing] → *Expect:* channel matched to size; big asks never arrive as a wall of text out of nowhere.
3. **Ask directly, with minimal preamble.** One line of context, then the ask. Skip the long apology-ramp ("I hate to ask, I know you're so busy, I feel terrible...") which forces them to manage your feelings before they can even hear the request. Avoid the contentless "can I ask you a favor?" opener; it extracts a blind yes and people resent it. → *Expect:* your ask was fully stated within the first two sentences.
4. **Give an explicit easy out.** Say the actual words: "and honestly, no worries at all if you can't, I have a backup." An escape hatch stated by you is worth ten implied ones; it converts pressured yeses into real ones. → *Expect:* the out is in the same message as the ask, and you genuinely have (or will find) the backup you claimed.
5. **Make the yes cheap to execute.** Bundle everything they need: the address, the file, the dates, the login already shared, the form pre-filled. Every question they'd have to ask you is a tax on their yes. → *Expect:* they can complete the favor without a single clarifying question.
6. **Accept the answer at face value.** Yes: thank them once, confirm logistics, stop selling. No or hesitation: "totally fine, thanks for considering it," and mean it; do not negotiate a no. A no gracefully received keeps the relationship fully intact for the next ask. → *Expect:* no repeated asking; hesitation was treated as a no.
7. **Thank them promptly after the favor happens.** Same day, proportional to size: a warm text for small favors, something tangible (meal, small gift, conspicuous public credit) for large ones. → *Expect:* thanks delivered within 24 hours of the favor.
8. **Close the loop with a report.** Tell them how it turned out: "made the flight with 20 minutes to spare," "the intro you made turned into an interview." The report is the underused half of gratitude; it shows the favor mattered and is the single best predictor they'll say yes next time. → *Expect:* they know the outcome of what they helped with, not just that you were grateful.

## Decision points

- The favor involves lending money or their name (references, guarantees, vouching) → treat as a large favor regardless of amount; ask in person, volunteer terms in writing yourself, and make the out extra explicit.
- You've asked this person for the last two favors → find someone else or offer something first; ledgers are unspoken but everyone keeps one.
- They owe you a favor → still ask cleanly, never invoke the debt ("after all I've done..."); invoked reciprocity spends the relationship, voluntary reciprocity builds it.

## Failure modes & recovery

- **F1 No reply:** silence for days on a small ask. One nudge after 2-3 days ("no pressure, just need to sort it by Friday either way"), then activate the backup without resentment; silence usually means overwhelmed, not hostile.
- **F2 Yes, then no follow-through:** the deadline nears and nothing happened. Recover: one concrete reminder tied to the deadline, and quietly run your backup in parallel; never let a favor become the single point of failure for something important.
- **F3 The resentful yes:** they agreed but sound put-upon. Recover: offer the out again once, sincerely ("really, I can ask Sam instead"); one re-offer, then take whatever they choose as final.
- **F4 Scope creep by you:** the "quick airport ride" grows a stop at the pharmacy and a bag drop. Detect: you're about to say "while we're at it." Recover: don't; new needs are new asks, made separately and declinable separately.

## Verification

The ask was one specific sentence with a stated easy out, the answer (either way) was accepted without pressure, and, if they did it: thanks landed within a day and they later heard the outcome. Test of success: you could comfortably ask this person for something again, and they'd be glad to hear from you.

## Variations

- Workplace: frame the favor with the deadline and effort estimate upfront, and give public credit in the channel or meeting where it counts; visible credit is workplace currency.
- Asking a stranger or weak tie: shrink the ask, raise the context ("I got your name from Ana..."), and make the out even easier; expect and respect a higher no-rate.

## Safety & privacy

Low risk. Don't put someone's credentials, keys, or finances inside a "small favor" without naming what you're really asking. If a favor requires them to bend rules at work, say so explicitly and let them refuse cleanly; hiding the risk inside a friendly ask is how favors end friendships.
