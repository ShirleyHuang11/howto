## Goal

A short wedding speech that honors the couple, gives the room one or two clean stories, and ends in a toast before attention thins.

## Preconditions

- You know your speaking slot, time limit, microphone setup, and whether anyone else is giving a formal toast.
- You have permission to mention the stories you plan to tell, or you can confidently say they would not embarrass the couple.
- You know the couple's names, pronunciation, key family sensitivities, and any topics the wedding party wants avoided.

## Steps

1. **Set a firm length.** Aim for three to five minutes, roughly 450 to 700 spoken words, and write the limit at the top of your draft. → *Expect:* a draft target that prevents rambling before you start.
2. **Open with your relationship.** Say who you are, thank the hosts briefly if appropriate, and name the honor of standing beside the bride, groom, or couple. → *Expect:* guests who do not know you understand why you are speaking.
3. **Choose one or two stories.** Pick stories that show kindness, steadiness, humor, courage, or how the couple works together. [BRANCH: one story | two stories] Use one strong story if it says enough; use two only if the second adds a new quality. → *Expect:* the stories point toward the marriage, not your own greatest hits.
4. **Keep every story clean.** Cut exes, drunken disasters, private family pain, money trouble, and inside jokes that only one table understands. ⚠️ *Irreversible:* once said into a microphone, the line becomes part of the wedding memory, so remove anything the couple would have to forgive. → *Expect:* nothing in the speech makes either partner brace.
5. **Add the partner warmly.** If you know the spouse well, include a specific example; if you do not, say what you have seen them bring out in your friend. → *Expect:* both halves of the couple feel included.
6. **Write the toast last.** Close with a single sentence wish, ask everyone to raise a glass, and name the couple clearly. → *Expect:* a clean ending the room can repeat.
7. **Rehearse aloud with a timer.** Practice standing, mark breath points, and trim every sentence that feels hard to say naturally. → *Expect:* the speech fits the time limit and sounds like you, only steadier.
8. **Bring a readable copy.** Print large text or use note cards, number the pages, and keep a backup on your phone. → *Expect:* you can recover if nerves, lighting, or a dropped page interfere.
9. **Deliver and stop.** Speak slower than conversation, look at the couple for the toast, then sit down after the glass raise. → *Expect:* applause or warmth arrives at the end, not a drift into another anecdote.

## Decision points

- If the couple requested no roast, treat that as a boundary, not a challenge.
- If many speeches are scheduled, choose one story and make yours shorter.
- If you become emotional, pause, breathe, and continue from the next bold line on your notes.
- If a joke only works with explanation, remove it.
- If alcohol is involved, finish the speech before you start drinking heavily.

## Failure modes & recovery

- **F1 Speech runs long:** detect it when rehearsal exceeds five minutes → cut setup, side characters, and any story that does not reveal the couple.
- **F2 Story embarrasses someone:** detect it when a trusted listener winces → replace it with a trait-based memory.
- **F3 Lost your place:** detect it when the page blurs or the sentence disappears → say "what I mean is" and jump to the toast.
- **F4 Room is noisy:** detect it when people keep talking → wait silently with the microphone close before restarting the current sentence.

## Verification

The final speech is under five minutes, includes one or two respectful stories, mentions both partners, and ends with a clear toast naming the couple.

## Variations

- Co-speech: divide sections before writing, avoid duplicate stories, and rehearse the handoffs.
- Very formal wedding: reduce teasing, thank hosts by title or relationship, and keep the toast traditional.
- Tiny reception: skip the microphone voice and speak as if to one table.

## Safety & privacy

Low risk, but emotionally permanent. Protect private histories, immigration or health details, family conflict, fertility, sexuality, finances, and anything shared with you in confidence.
