## Goal

A person who asked the way leaves with directions they can actually follow — or with an honest referral to a better source.

## Preconditions

- Someone has asked you how to get somewhere.
- You genuinely know the route — the first decision is whether you do.

## Steps

1. **Confirm the destination and their mode.** "The central library — walking or driving?" Mode changes the route (one-ways, shortcuts, distance tolerance). → *Expect:* destination and mode pinned; if the name is ambiguous ("the Starbucks" — which?), disambiguate now.
2. **Honestly gate yourself.** [BRANCH: you know it cold → step 3 | you half-know → say so and offer only the certain part | no idea → "I'm not sure — sorry" and suggest maps/a shop to ask] A confident wrong answer costs them 20 minutes; "I don't know" costs seconds. → *Expect:* you're only proceeding on solid knowledge.
3. **Orient them physically first.** Turn them the right way before any words: "So, facing this way—" (gesture down the street). Directions given against their current facing fail at the first turn. → *Expect:* their body points along leg one.
4. **Give the route in 3 chunks maximum, landmark-anchored.** People hold ~3 instructions. "Straight to the church (2 blocks) — left there — it's on your right past the pharmacy." Landmarks beat street names for strangers; distances in blocks/minutes beat meters. → *Expect:* a route of ≤3 legs, each ending at something visible/checkable.
5. **Give the overshoot tell.** "If you reach the river, you've gone too far." → *Expect:* they have a failure detector, not just a plan.
6. **Have them confirm the first leg back to you.** "So I go...?" or simply watch that they set off the right way; re-anchor if the readback is off. → *Expect:* correct readback or correct departure heading.

## Decision points

- Route too complex for 3 chunks → route them to an intermediate anchor everyone can find ("get to the station, ask again there") or offer the maps app spelling of the destination.
- Language barrier → fewer words, more pointing; draw it (paper or their phone map); numbers by fingers.
- They're clearly walking somewhere far/unsafe → mention the transit option even though they asked walking.

## Failure modes & recovery

- **F1 You realize mid-explanation you've mixed up two streets:** say so plainly and restart the leg — "wait, I'm wrong, let me redo that" beats letting a bad leg stand.
- **F2 Their eyes glazed at leg two:** you overloaded — collapse to leg one plus "then ask anyone." Delivering less, reliably, is the job.
- **F3 They walk off the wrong way despite it all:** one calling-out correction ("Other way!") is friendly; chasing them down is not required.

## Verification

They departed along the correct first leg carrying ≤3 landmark-anchored instructions and an overshoot tell — or they left with an honest "don't know" and a better source instead of a guess.

## Variations

- Drivers asking from a car window: compress harder (they can't take notes) — first two turns only, then "signs for X".
- Address-grid cities (`us`): counting blocks and cardinal directions work; medieval-street cities: landmarks are everything.

## Safety & privacy

Low risk. Don't volunteer your own address patterns to strangers as reference points; and the honesty gate (step 2) is the whole ethics of the task.
