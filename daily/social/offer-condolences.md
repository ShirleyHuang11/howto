## Goal

Offer condolences that make a grieving person feel remembered and supported, without forcing them to comfort you or process a platitude.

## Preconditions

- Reliable knowledge that someone has died or that the person is grieving a loss.
- The deceased person's name if it has been shared and is appropriate to use.
- A channel that fits your relationship: in person, card, text, email, call, or meal note.

## Steps

1. **Say their name and say you are sorry.** Use plain words: "I'm so sorry about Maria" or "I was heartbroken to hear that your dad died." → *Expect:* the loss is named instead of tiptoed around.
2. **Keep the first message short.** Do not ask for details, explanations, or a reply. → *Expect:* the grieving person receives care without homework.
3. **Avoid platitudes.** Skip "everything happens for a reason," "they are in a better place" unless you know that belief comforts them, and "at least" in any form. → *Expect:* nothing minimizes the loss.
4. **Add one true memory if you have one.** A small specific memory is often better than a grand statement: "She always saved me the corner brownie." → *Expect:* the person hears that their loved one mattered in the world.
5. **Make a concrete offer.** [BRANCH: close relationship | distant relationship] offer a task if close, such as food, rides, childcare, calls, or errands; send a card or donation if distant. → *Expect:* your help has a clear action, not "anything."
6. **Follow their lead in person.** If they want to talk, listen. If they are greeting a line of people, be brief. If they cry, stay calm. → *Expect:* your presence matches their capacity.
7. **Follow up later.** Put a reminder for two weeks, one month, or a meaningful date. Grief gets quieter around the person before it gets easier. → *Expect:* support reaches beyond the first wave.
8. **Protect private grief.** ⚠️ *Irreversible:* once death details, photos, or family updates are shared publicly, they cannot be fully pulled back. → *Expect:* you do not post, forward, or announce anything before the family does.

## Decision points

- If you did not know the deceased → comfort the living person: "I know how much she meant to you."
- If the loss is complicated → avoid judging the relationship and say, "I'm thinking of you in all of this."
- If you share the same grief → offer one sentence of shared feeling, then return the focus to them.
- If you are unsure whether to contact them → a short card or text with no reply expected is usually safer than silence.

## Failure modes & recovery

- **F1 You use a platitude:** detect discomfort or your own realization → send a repair: "I am sorry. I meant that I care about you and I miss him too."
- **F2 Offer is too vague:** detect "thanks" with no follow-up → make one specific offer with date and task.
- **F3 You ask for details:** detect the conversation turning into an interview → stop and say they do not need to explain anything.
- **F4 You disappear after the funeral:** detect that the sympathy window passed → send a simple check-in without making them reassure you.

## Verification

Your condolence names the loss, avoids minimizers, includes either a memory or a concrete offer, and preserves the family's privacy.

## Variations

- Work setting: keep it brief, cover practical duties, and do not ask medical or family questions.
- Card: write two or three sincere sentences by hand if possible.
- Online message: comment only after the family has posted, and keep it respectful.

## Safety & privacy

Grief often includes medical, financial, religious, and family information. Do not share details, speculate about cause of death, or turn condolences into your own story.
