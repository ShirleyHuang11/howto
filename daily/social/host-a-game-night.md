## Goal

Four to eight people spend an evening at your place actually playing games (not watching one person read a rulebook), and leave wanting a repeat.

## Preconditions

- A table that seats your headcount with elbow room, and enough chairs.
- Two or three games you have personally played, not just own. Never debut a game you'd have to learn live.
- Guests invited with a firm start time and a soft end time.

## Steps

1. **Invite 5-8 people and get real confirmations.** Expect one drop-out per five invitees; over-invite by one. Ask about food constraints in the same message. → *Expect:* a confirmed count 48h out, so you can pick games for the actual number.
2. **Pick games by confirmed count, not preference.** [BRANCH: 3-4 players → almost anything works, include one mid-weight game | 5-6 → one party game plus one that genuinely supports 6 (check the box, many "2-6" games drag past 4) | 7+ → team games, social deduction, or plan two tables] → *Expect:* a lineup of 2-3 games: light opener, main event, short closer.
3. **Set up before anyone arrives.** Opener game laid out on the table, other boxes staged nearby, rulebook skimmed that day, scorepad and pens found. → *Expect:* the first game is playable within 10 minutes of the last arrival.
4. **Stage snacks for snack physics.** Dry, one-hand, non-greasy: pretzels, popcorn, cut vegetables, candy. No powdered cheese, no salsa near the board. Small bowls distributed around the table beat one central bowl everyone reaches across. Drinks get a side table or coasters well away from the play area. → *Expect:* nothing on the table that leaves residue on fingers; no open cup uphill of the board.
5. **Start the opener on time with whoever is present.** Waiting for stragglers kills momentum; light games absorb late joiners. → *Expect:* play begins within 15 minutes of the stated start.
6. **Teach rules fast, in this order:** the goal of the game in one sentence, what a turn looks like, then start playing immediately with open hands and take-backs for the first round. Never read the rulebook aloud; look up edge cases only when they occur. → *Expect:* teaching takes under 5 minutes; everyone learns by round two.
7. **Run the main game, managing pace.** Nudge slow turns gently ("you're up"), keep score visible, and side-conversation is fine between turns. → *Expect:* the game ends with energy left in the room.
8. **Read the room before the closer.** [BRANCH: energy high → short closer game | energy fading → stop while it's fun] Ending 30 minutes early beats a death-march finish. → *Expect:* people leave during the fun part, saying "next time."

## Decision points

- Someone arrives who has never gamed → seat them next to you, pick the opener for them, and never open with social deduction (bluffing against strangers is the worst first experience).
- Group splits into gamers and chatters mid-evening → let it happen; run the game at one end of the table rather than policing attention.
- 9+ people actually show → two tables from the start, with a planned mid-evening reshuffle.

## Failure modes & recovery

- **F1 Rules dispute stalls the game:** two people arguing interpretations while everyone waits. Detect: rulebook open longer than 2 minutes. Recover: the host rules it for tonight ("house rule, we'll look it up later") and play resumes; correctness is not the product, the evening is.
- **F2 The alpha gamer:** one player dictating everyone's moves "to help." Recover: a light "let them make their own mistakes" the first time, a private word if it repeats.
- **F3 Greasy-hand contamination:** orange fingerprints heading for your cards. Recover: napkins preemptively at every seat; quarantine the offending snack to the side table mid-game without commentary.
- **F4 Main game running way over:** past the point of fun with no end in sight. Recover: declare a final round openly, tally, and call it; most modern games score fine at an agreed stopping point.
- **F5 Spill on the board:** cloth or paper towels within arm's reach of the table before the night starts, not in the kitchen. Blot, don't wipe, card surfaces.

## Verification

At evening's end: at least two games were completed, no single rules explanation exceeded ten minutes, the components are dry and grease-free going back in their boxes, and at least one guest asked about the next one unprompted.

## Variations

- Video game night: same structure, but rotate controllers on short matches so no one spectates longer than 10 minutes.
- Kids in the mix: run the light opener as the whole-family game, then split tables at bedtime.
- Potluck format: assign categories (drinks, dry snacks, dessert) or you will receive four bags of chips.

## Safety & privacy

Low risk. Confirm allergies before buying snacks (nuts are the classic ambush). If alcohol is present, watch that competitive banter stays banter, and make sure drivers taper early or have another way home.
