## Goal

A book club meeting where people know what to read, have room to talk, and leave with a next book and date.

## Preconditions

- A group of readers, a meeting location or video link, and a communication channel.
- A book selected far enough ahead for people to borrow, buy, or listen to it.
- Agreement on whether unfinished readers are welcome and whether spoilers are allowed.

## Steps

1. **Pick the book and date together.** Choose a book length and genre the group can realistically finish, then set the meeting date before enthusiasm fades. → *Expect:* members know what to read and when to show up.
2. **Send one clear reminder.** Include title, author, format options, start time, address or link, food plan, and spoiler policy. → *Expect:* logistics are in one findable message.
3. **Prepare discussion questions.** Write five to eight open questions about characters, structure, themes, favorite passages, and disagreements. → *Expect:* conversation can restart if the room goes quiet.
4. **Choose food that supports talking.** [BRANCH: full meal | snacks] Serve a meal if gathering is the main social event; use snacks and drinks if the focus is discussion. → *Expect:* food does not swallow the meeting.
5. **Open with a quick round.** Ask for a rating, favorite moment, or one word reaction so every person speaks early. → *Expect:* quieter members have an easy first entry.
6. **Keep it on topic without policing.** Let brief tangents breathe, then bring the group back with a prepared question or a passage. → *Expect:* the meeting feels social but still about the book.
7. **Make space evenly.** Invite people who have not spoken, interrupt monologues kindly, and avoid treating one interpretation as the answer. → *Expect:* the discussion has several voices.
8. **Pick the next host and book.** Rotate hosts, nominations, or genres, and record the next date before people leave. → *Expect:* continuity is decided while the group is present.
9. **Handle spoilers clearly.** ⚠️ *Irreversible:* once a twist is said, it cannot be unheard, so confirm the spoiler rule before discussing endings. → *Expect:* unfinished readers know whether to stay, pause, or listen anyway.

## Decision points

- If few people finished, discuss the first half, adaptations, or reasons people stalled.
- If debate gets sharp, ask each person what text evidence shaped their view.
- If one member dominates, thank them and invite a named quieter person in.
- If nobody likes the book, use that as the discussion rather than pretending.
- If attendance drops, shorten the book or rotate lighter choices.

## Failure modes & recovery

- **F1 Conversation dies:** detect it when answers become single words → use a passage, quote, or "who changed their mind" question.
- **F2 Meeting becomes gossip only:** detect it when the book disappears for ten minutes → bridge back with "this reminds me of the narrator because..."
- **F3 Food delays discussion:** detect it when serving takes half the meeting → pause discussion until plates are set, then simplify next time.
- **F4 Next plan is vague:** detect it when people leave without a date → send a poll the same day with three specific options.

## Verification

The group discussed the book, multiple people spoke, and the next host, book, or date was recorded before or immediately after the meeting.

## Variations

- Virtual club: use a visible queue, chat for quotes, and a shorter meeting.
- Library club: pick books with many available copies and avoid expensive new releases.
- Workplace club: schedule within work norms and avoid mandatory personal disclosure.

## Safety & privacy

Low risk. Respect members who do not want alcohol, photos, personal stories, or political arguments attached to a leisure group.
