## Goal

A neighbor encounter — hallway, elevator, sidewalk, or a first meeting after moving in — ends with goodwill maintained or built, in proportion to what both people wanted from it.

## Preconditions

- Physical proximity to a neighbor: passing encounter, shared wait (elevator/mailboxes), or you've just moved in / they have.

## Steps

1. **Read the encounter's size.** [BRANCH: passing at distance → nod/wave/"morning" | shared confined wait (elevator, laundry room) → greeting + optional small exchange | first meeting after a move → introduction, see step 4] → *Expect:* you've matched your opener to the container — a passing nod needs no conversation; a 40-second elevator ride tolerates one.
2. **Deliver the greeting with eye contact and a smile.** "Morning." / "Hi." / a raised-hand wave across the street. That's a complete, successful interaction by itself. → *Expect:* acknowledgment returned — nod, greeting, smile.
3. **Optionally extend one beat — and read the response.** One low-stakes line: weather, the building ("elevator's back, finally"), their dog. Their answer's length tells you everything: short + closed → warm exit ("have a good one"); engaged → a brief chat is welcome. → *Expect:* conversation sized to the least-interested participant, ending on time.
4. **First-meeting version: introduce with name + unit.** "Hi — I'm Sam, just moved into 3B." Offer a handshake where that's local custom. Reciprocal intro usually follows; a practical question makes a natural second beat ("Is Tuesday really bin day?"). → *Expect:* names exchanged; you can now greet by name forever, which compounds.
5. **Bank the details.** Name, unit/house, dog's name, anything they mentioned. Next encounter opens with it ("How was the trip?"). → *Expect:* recorded (mentally or literally); future greetings get warmer at zero cost.

## Decision points

- Neighbor visibly rushed, on a call, or arms full of groceries → nod-and-step-aside *is* the courteous version; offering a hand with a door outranks any conversation.
- Culture/building norms vary hard → mirror the local baseline: in some places neighbors chat, in some a nod is the whole institution; two weeks of observation calibrates you.
- The relationship has an open friction (noise complaint, parking) → the greeting stays warm and *separate*; don't smuggle disputes into hallway hellos — raise issues purposefully, at doors, at chosen times (a knock and "got a minute?").

## Failure modes & recovery

- **F1 Blanked on their name after they used yours:** confess at the second meeting, not the tenth: "I'm so sorry — remind me of your name?" One-time cost, permanent fix.
- **F2 Greeting not returned:** headphones, preoccupation, or shyness — assume noise, not hostility; keep your baseline nod on future passes. Consistency, not pursuit, is how cold starts thaw.
- **F3 Trapped in an over-long chat:** the honest exit is standard-issue and offense-free: "I've got to run — good to see you!"

## Verification

The encounter ended at or above its starting warmth, sized to what both parties wanted, and (first meetings) you can address them by name next time.

## Variations

- Dense-anonymity cities: the norm may be mutual non-acknowledgment in transit but friendliness at building facilities — container-reading (step 1) does the work.
- `jp`: a slight bow + "konnichiwa" is the complete form; moving-in custom historically includes a small introductory gift to adjacent units.
- Rural/small-town: greetings are near-mandatory and skipping them is the marked behavior.

## Safety & privacy

Low risk. Share your unit number and rhythms at the pace trust earns — friendliness and open-book are different settings; both are fine, chosen deliberately.
