## Goal

A new colleague ends their first week knowing the practical basics, having eaten lunch with people instead of alone, and knowing exactly who to ping with dumb questions.

## Preconditions

- You know the new person's name, role, and start date (ask the hiring manager if unsure).
- You work near them or on their team; a welcome from a random stranger three floors away lands oddly.
- Their first day overlaps yours in person or on the team chat.

## Steps

1. **Prepare the day before.** Learn how to pronounce their name, skim their role, and note the two or three practical things nobody documents (where the good coffee is, which printer works, which meetings are skippable). → *Expect:* you can greet them by name without reading it off a screen.
2. **Greet them within their first hour.** Walk over (or send a chat message for remote starts), introduce yourself with name and what you do in one sentence, and say you're a go-to for questions. → *Expect:* they know one concrete person, you, by name.
3. **Give the two-minute practical tour.** Bathrooms, kitchen, exits, where their team sits, and the unwritten norms (headphones etiquette, meeting-room booking). Skip org-chart lore; it does not stick on day one. → *Expect:* they can find coffee and a bathroom unassisted.
4. **Invite them to lunch on day one, as a group.** Say the time and place, and recruit one or two teammates so it is not a one-on-one interview. [BRANCH: they accept → go | they decline or brought food → offer a coffee walk that afternoon instead] → *Expect:* the new person does not eat alone on day one.
5. **Hand over the tribal knowledge.** Send links to the team chat channels, the docs people actually use, and a one-line "who to ask for what" list. → *Expect:* they acknowledge receipt; the list is short enough to actually read.
6. **Make the question channel explicit.** Say the exact words: "Ping me anytime, especially with questions that feel too dumb to ask in the channel." → *Expect:* they ping you at least once in the first days. Silence for a full week is a signal, not a success (see F2).
7. **Check in at the one-week mark.** A short, private conversation: what's confusing, what's blocking, who have they not met yet that they should. Fix one concrete blocker on the spot if you can. → *Expect:* at least one specific confusion surfaced and addressed, not just "everything's fine."

## Decision points

- Remote or hybrid start → replace the tour with a 20-minute video call and make the lunch a scheduled virtual coffee; the one-week check-in matters even more.
- You are their manager rather than a peer → still do all of this, but also recruit a peer buddy; some questions will never be asked upward.
- They join mid-crunch → protect step 4 anyway. "Too busy for lunch" on day one sets the culture they will assume forever.

## Failure modes & recovery

- **F1 Lunch invite declined twice:** stop pushing meals; some people guard lunch. Switch to a low-commitment format (10-minute coffee, walking them to a meeting) and let frequency do the work.
- **F2 No questions all week:** they are stuck and hiding it, or drowning in silence. Ask a specific question yourself ("did you get access to X yet?") because "any questions?" reliably returns "no."
- **F3 You forget their name or details after day one:** admit it plainly and re-ask; faking it and avoiding their name for weeks is the worse outcome everyone notices.
- **F4 Welcome committee pile-on:** five people independently giving tours exhausts the newcomer. Coordinate in the team channel so one person owns day one.

## Verification

By end of week one: the new colleague has eaten lunch or coffee with teammates at least once, has asked you (or the buddy) at least one logistical question, and can name who to ask for equipment, code access, and HR matters. If any of the three is false, the welcome is not done.

## Variations

- Large enterprise with formal onboarding: the official program covers HR and equipment; your job narrows to lunch, tribal knowledge, and the check-in.
- Small team or startup: you are probably also the one requesting their accounts and desk; do that before day one, not during it.

## Safety & privacy

Low risk. Do not probe personal history, previous salary, or reasons for leaving the last job. Keep the one-week check-in private; surfacing their confusions in front of the team punishes honesty.
