## Goal

You have one or two people who reliably want to spend time with you outside a structured activity, built from strangers over roughly two to three months.

## Preconditions

- A weekly time slot you can protect for 8+ weeks; consistency is the entire mechanism.
- Willingness to be the initiator. Adult friendship stalls because everyone waits; assume you will do the first two invitations.
- Basic solvency for a recurring activity fee if the activity has one (classes, club dues, gym).

## Steps

1. **Pick one recurring shared activity, not events.** Choose something that meets weekly with the same people: climbing gym, run club, board game night, language class, rec sports, choir. One-off meetups and parties shuffle the deck every time; repetition is what converts strangers to familiar faces. → *Expect:* a specific activity with a fixed weekly slot on your calendar.
2. **Attend every week for four to six weeks before judging anything.** Show up even when tired. Proximity plus repetition does the early work; you are becoming "the person who's always here." → *Expect:* by week four, several people greet you unprompted.
3. **Learn names immediately and use them.** Ask, repeat the name back, and use it next session. → *Expect:* you can name five regulars; they use your name back.
4. **Small-talk toward overlap, not biography.** During natural gaps, ask what people do outside the activity and volunteer your own interests. You are scanning for a second shared interest, which is the hook for step 5. → *Expect:* at least one person where you know a concrete overlap (same neighborhood, same sport, same taste in food or films).
5. **Make the first outside invitation, tied to the activity.** Lowest-friction version: extend the existing session ("a few of us are getting food after, join?") or propose an adjacent event ("that climbing film is showing Thursday, want to go?"). Concrete time and place, never "we should hang out sometime." → *Expect:* a yes, or a no with a counter-offer. A bare no is fine; note it and continue.
6. **Initiate a second time without waiting for reciprocation.** This is the step most people skip. Non-initiation is usually passivity or busyness, not rejection; the second invite is what signals you are serious. Wait one to three weeks, vary the format slightly. → *Expect:* a yes, and often visible relief that someone else is driving.
7. **Escalate gradually across invitations.** Activity-adjacent → generic one-on-one (meal, walk, coffee) → into your life (your other friends, your home, helping each other with real things like moves and airport runs). One rung per few weeks; jumping rungs feels intense. → *Expect:* they start initiating or reciprocating (hosting, inviting you into their circles) by roughly the third or fourth hangout.
8. **Put the friendship on a light cadence.** A standing thing (monthly dinner, weekly session plus occasional extras) beats sporadic scheduling from scratch. → *Expect:* meetups happen without a multi-week text negotiation each time.

## Decision points

- Two or three invitations in with zero reciprocation and lukewarm yeses → stop escalating with that person; stay friendly at the activity and start step 5 with someone else. Redirect, don't audit.
- The activity's regulars already form a closed clique → try a different session time or a different venue before abandoning the activity type.
- You moved to a new city → run two activities in parallel for the first months; the funnel is wide and slow.

## Failure modes & recovery

- **F1 Serial flaking:** plans made, always cancelled. Two flakes without a counter-proposal from them means deprioritize; a flake plus their concrete alternative date means genuinely busy, continue.
- **F2 Mistaking proximity for friendship:** months of pleasant sessions but you'd have nothing to do together elsewhere. Detect: every conversation is about the activity. Recover: force step 4's overlap-scan or accept it as an activity-friendship, which is also fine.
- **F3 The activity dissolves (class ends, club folds):** friendships at the acquaintance stage evaporate with the venue. Recover: within a week, invite the one or two best candidates to something specific; the ending itself is a natural excuse ("we should still get that dinner").
- **F4 Burnout from over-initiating:** you resent being the only driver. Recover: name it lightly once ("you pick the next one"), then apply the decision-point triage rather than dropping everyone at once.

## Verification

After two to three months: at least one person from the activity has initiated contact or accepted two invitations with visible enthusiasm, you have each other's numbers, and you have met at least once with no connection to the original activity. If all your interactions still require the activity's room, you are at step 4, not done.

## Variations

- Remote-first life: the same loop works in online communities with regular sessions (gaming groups, book clubs); escalation runs voice chat → one-on-one calls → meeting in person if geography allows.
- Parents of young children: kid-adjacent recurring activities (playgroups, school pickup) are the natural venue; escalation is family-to-family, which moves slower but bonds harder.

## Safety & privacy

Low risk. Escalate location intimacy in step 7 with ordinary judgment: public venues before homes, especially for connections made online. Don't lend meaningful money at the acquaintance stage; it converts a forming friendship into a creditor relationship.
