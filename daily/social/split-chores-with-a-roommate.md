## Goal

Create a chore system with a roommate that covers visible mess and invisible work, so the home runs without one person becoming the unpaid manager.

## Preconditions

- A shared living space and at least one roommate willing to discuss upkeep.
- A way to track tasks: paper chart, shared note, calendar, spreadsheet, or chore app.
- Basic agreement that cleanliness standards affect everyone and should be negotiated, not guessed.

## Steps

1. **Name the problem without accusation.** Start with the home, not character: "I want us to make chores clearer so neither of us has to keep score in our heads." → *Expect:* the conversation opens as a shared logistics problem.
2. **List visible and invisible work.** Include dishes, trash, bathroom, floors, mail, supplies, rent reminders, repair calls, guests, pet care, and noticing when things are low. → *Expect:* the list shows both scrubbing and mental tracking.
3. **Set minimum standards.** Define what "done" means for each task: trash bag replaced, sink wiped, bathroom mirror cleaned, supplies added to the list. → *Expect:* fewer arguments about whether a chore counts.
4. **Choose rotate or divide.** [BRANCH: rotate | divide] rotate disliked tasks weekly for fairness; divide stable tasks if schedules and preferences make ownership easier. → *Expect:* every recurring task has one owner at a time.
5. **Build the shared tracker.** Put tasks, owners, due days, and supply needs somewhere both people can see without asking. → *Expect:* the system remembers, not one roommate.
6. **Add exceptions before they happen.** Decide what happens during travel, exams, overtime, illness, or hosting guests. → *Expect:* temporary overload has a swap rule instead of resentment.
7. **Schedule a check-in.** Pick a 15-minute check-in after two weeks, then monthly if useful. Keep it about the system: what is unclear, too frequent, or unfair. → *Expect:* there is a named time to adjust, not a fight at midnight.
8. **Use repair language when it slips.** Say "The bathroom was due yesterday. Can you do it tonight or trade for trash?" not "You never clean." ⚠️ *Irreversible:* angry texts and public shaming change the roommate relationship, so pause before sending them. → *Expect:* problems turn into actions, not character trials.

## Decision points

- If cleanliness standards differ sharply → agree on minimum shared-space standards and leave private rooms out unless pests, smells, or safety are involved.
- If one roommate is home far more → split by impact and capacity, not only equal task count.
- If money replaces labor → make the amount explicit and confirm both people can afford it.
- If chores involve pets or guests → assign them to the person who brought the pet or guest unless agreed otherwise.

## Failure modes & recovery

- **F1 Invisible work remains hidden:** detect one person still buying, reminding, and scheduling everything → add those tasks to the tracker with owners.
- **F2 Tracker is ignored:** detect tasks overdue without comment → simplify it, add reminders, or move it to a more visible place.
- **F3 Standards argument repeats:** detect the same chore being redone or criticized → rewrite the definition of done with observable criteria.
- **F4 Resentment spikes:** detect sarcasm, avoidance, or blowups → pause the chore talk and schedule a calmer check-in with notes.

## Verification

Every recurring shared chore has an owner, a due rhythm, a definition of done, and a check-in date visible to all roommates.

## Variations

- Three or more roommates: assign zones or weeks, and make supply purchasing a rotating role.
- Couples living with roommates: do not let the couple vote as a bloc on shared standards.
- Professional cleaners: still assign tidying, dishes, trash, supplies, and booking responsibilities.

## Safety & privacy

Low risk, but roommate conflict affects housing stability. Keep notes factual, avoid recording private messes for leverage, and treat shared purchases and keys as trust-sensitive.
