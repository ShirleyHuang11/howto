## Goal

A minor cut or scrape is cleaned, protected, and healing, and you know the specific signs that mean it needs professional care instead.

## Preconditions

- A minor wound: shallow, edges close together, bleeding that pressure controls within minutes.
- **Not minor, seek medical care instead:** spurting or soaking bleeding, deep or gaping cuts that may need stitches, wounds on the face where scarring matters, punctures from rusty or dirty objects, animal or human bites, or anything with something embedded in it. Embedded objects stay in place; removal is for professionals.
- Supplies: clean running water, mild soap, sterile gauze or a clean cloth, adhesive bandages, and ideally an antibiotic ointment or plain petroleum jelly.

## Steps

1. **Wash your hands before touching the wound.** (`daily/self-care/wash-hands`.) → *Expect:* clean hands; gloves if treating someone else and they are available.
2. **Stop the bleeding with direct pressure.** Press a clean cloth or gauze firmly on the cut for several minutes without peeking. Lifting the cloth to check restarts the clock. → *Expect:* bleeding stopped or reduced to a slow ooze after 5 minutes of uninterrupted pressure. Still soaking through after 10 minutes of firm pressure is a seek-care sign.
3. **Rinse the wound under cool running water.** Let the stream flush it for a minute or two; wash the skin *around* it with mild soap. Soap, alcohol, and hydrogen peroxide inside the wound itself damage tissue and slow healing; water does the cleaning. → *Expect:* visible dirt and debris flushed out; the wound looks clean.
4. **Remove small debris the water missed.** Tweezers cleaned with alcohol, working gently at the surface only. Anything that resists a gentle pull stays for a professional. → *Expect:* no visible grit; no digging.
5. **Dress it.** A thin layer of antibiotic ointment or petroleum jelly (keeps the wound moist, which heals faster than drying out), then a bandage sized so the pad covers the wound and the adhesive grips dry skin. → *Expect:* wound covered, pad not stuck to the cut itself.
6. **Change the dressing daily and keep it dry.** Re-dress after showers, or sooner if wet or dirty. Each change is a ten-second inspection. → *Expect:* day by day, the wound looks calmer: less red, drier edges, then a scab or closed line.
7. **Watch for infection over the next days.** Increasing redness spreading outward, swelling, warmth, throbbing pain, pus, or fever mean the recipe is over and a clinician is next. → *Expect:* none of those; steady quiet healing.

## Decision points

- Tetanus: a dirty or puncture wound with your last tetanus shot more than 5 to 10 years ago is worth a same-week call to a clinic. This is the question everyone forgets to ask.
- Scrapes (road rash): same recipe with more emphasis on the flushing step, since embedded grit tattoos the skin if left in.
- Blood thinners or diabetes: lower the threshold for professional care by a lot; slow healing and quiet infections are the pattern.

## Failure modes & recovery

- **F1 Bandage stuck to the wound at changing time:** soak it off with water rather than ripping; the moist-healing ointment in step 5 prevents this.
- **F2 Cut reopens:** re-clean briefly, pressure again, and close with a fresh bandage or butterfly strips pulling the edges together. Repeated reopening in a moving area (knuckle, joint) may mean it needed stitches on day one; a clinician can still help within a day or two.
- **F3 Signs of infection appear:** stop home treatment and get it seen. Photograph the redness border with a pen line around it; growth past the line is exactly what the clinician wants to know.
- **F4 It was deeper than it looked (fat visible, edges gape when moved):** stitches have a time window of roughly 12 to 24 hours. Go today, not next week.

## Verification

Bleeding stopped with pressure, the wound was flushed clean with water only, it is dressed moist and covered, dressings are changed daily, and the infection watch-list is being checked and staying negative.

## Variations

- Fingertip cuts from kitchen work (`daily/food/cut-an-onion` F3 hands off to this recipe): pressure with the hand raised, and a fingertip bandage wrapped so it does not slide off.
- Kids: same steps, calmer narration than the wound deserves, and a bandage they like doubles compliance.

## Safety & privacy

Medium risk lives entirely in the triage: knowing what is not minor (preconditions) and what infection looks like (step 7). Everything between those two checkpoints is simple and forgiving.
