## Goal

Athlete's foot is treated with dryness, antifungal medicine, and habits that reduce spread and recurrence.

## Preconditions

- Typical signs include itchy, peeling, cracking, burning skin between toes or on soles.
- Seek medical care for diabetes, poor circulation, immune suppression, spreading redness, swelling, pus, fever, severe pain, or open sores.
- Call emergency services for rapidly spreading infection with fever, confusion, or feeling very ill.
- Supplies: soap, towel, clean socks, breathable shoes, antifungal cream or spray, and optional antifungal powder.
- Do not share towels, socks, or shoes.

## Steps

1. **Wash and dry feet.** Clean feet daily and dry carefully between toes. → *Expect:* skin is clean and moisture is removed.
2. **Apply antifungal treatment.** [BRANCH: cream | spray or powder] apply an over-the-counter antifungal as labeled to affected skin and nearby area. → *Expect:* medicine covers the rash without heavy buildup.
3. **Continue long enough.** Use treatment for the full label course, often 1 to 4 weeks, and a bit after symptoms clear if the label says so. → *Expect:* fungus is less likely to return.
   - ⚠️ *Irreversible:* Do not cut away deep cracked skin or open blisters at home.
4. **Change socks daily.** Use clean dry socks and change sooner after sweating. → *Expect:* feet stay drier through the day.
5. **Rotate shoes.** Let shoes dry fully between wears and choose breathable footwear. → *Expect:* damp shoe interiors are reduced.
6. **Treat shoes if needed.** Use antifungal powder or spray in shoes as labeled. → *Expect:* reinfection from footwear is less likely.
7. **Protect shared spaces.** Wear sandals in locker rooms, pools, and shared showers. → *Expect:* spread to and from surfaces is reduced.
8. **Avoid sharing towels.** Launder towels and socks after use. → *Expect:* fungus is not passed to others.
9. **Reassess after treatment.** Check whether itching, scaling, and cracking are clearly improving. → *Expect:* lack of improvement triggers medical advice.

## Decision points

- Diabetes, poor circulation, immune suppression, open sores, or spreading redness → see a clinician.
- No improvement after 2 weeks of correct treatment → ask a clinician or pharmacist.
- Toenails are thick, yellow, or crumbly → ask about nail fungus treatment.
- Rash is painful, one-sided, blistering, or oozing → get medical advice because it may not be athlete's foot.
- Symptoms resolve → keep dryness habits to prevent recurrence.

## Failure modes & recovery

- **F1 Stopping early:** detect treatment stopped when itching improves, recover by completing the label course.
- **F2 Reinfection from shoes:** detect recurrence after wearing same damp shoes, recover by drying and treating footwear.
- **F3 Skin breakdown:** detect cracks, bleeding, or open sores, recover by seeking care if deep, spreading, or high risk.
- **F4 Wrong diagnosis:** detect no response or unusual rash, recover by seeing a clinician.

## Verification

Feet are dry, antifungal treatment is being used as labeled, towels are not shared, and rash is improving without infection signs.

## Variations

- Sweaty feet: change socks mid-day and use breathable shoes.
- Public showers: wear shower sandals and dry feet afterward.
- Sensitive skin: ask a pharmacist for a formulation less likely to sting.

## Safety & privacy

Low to medium risk because most cases are minor, but diabetes, poor circulation, infection signs, or fever need medical care. Avoid sharing personal towels or footwear.
