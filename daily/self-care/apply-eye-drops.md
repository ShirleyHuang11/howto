## Goal

Eye drops enter the lower-lid pocket cleanly, stay on the eye long enough to work, and the bottle tip stays uncontaminated.

## Preconditions

- The correct eye-drop bottle, readable label, and dose instructions.
- Clean hands and a mirror if needed.
- No chemical splash, eye injury, sudden vision loss, severe eye pain, or new light sensitivity. Those need urgent medical care.

## Steps

1. **Read the label.** Confirm the medication, eye side, dose, and expiration date. → *Expect:* you know exactly which eye and how many drops.
2. **Wash your hands.** Use soap and water, then dry with a clean towel. → *Expect:* clean, dry fingers before touching your face.
3. **Prepare the bottle.** Shake it only if the label says to, remove the cap, and place the cap on a clean surface. → *Expect:* open bottle ready without touching the tip.
4. **Make a lid pocket.** Tilt your head back, look up, and pull the lower lid down gently. → *Expect:* a small pocket forms between the lower lid and eyeball.
5. **Place one drop.** Hold the bottle above the pocket and squeeze one drop without touching the eye, lashes, skin, or lid. → *Expect:* one drop lands in or near the pocket.
6. **Close and press.** Close the eye gently, then press the inner corner near the nose for 1 minute unless your clinician told you not to. → *Expect:* less liquid drains into the throat and the drop stays on the eye.
7. **Blot excess.** Dab extra liquid from the cheek with a clean tissue without rubbing the eye. → *Expect:* the eye is moist, not dripping.
8. **Space multiple drops.** If using another drop, wait at least 5 minutes before the next one. → *Expect:* the first drop is not washed out.
9. **Recap cleanly.** Replace the cap without touching the bottle tip. → *Expect:* the bottle is closed and the tip remains clean.

## Decision points

- Contact lenses are in → remove them unless the label or clinician says the drops are safe with contacts.
- The tip touched your eye or skin → wipe the outside if needed, recap, and ask a pharmacist whether replacement is needed for prescription drops.
- Burning is severe, vision changes, rash, swelling, or breathing symptoms occur → seek medical advice promptly.

## Failure modes & recovery

- **F1 Drop misses the eye:** try once more after repositioning the lower lid pocket.
- **F2 Too many drops come out:** do not add extra medicine; blot and resume the next scheduled dose.
- **F3 Reflex blinking pushes it out:** lie down or use a mirror, then close the eye gently after the drop lands.
- **F4 Eye becomes painful or very red:** stop nonessential drops and contact a clinician, especially after surgery or injury.

## Verification

The ordered number of drops reached the correct eye, the eye stayed closed with tear-duct pressure, and the bottle tip did not touch any surface.

## Variations

- Ointment: pull the same lower-lid pocket and lay a thin ribbon inside it.
- Child or shaky hands: have the person lie down and stabilize the bottle hand against the forehead.
- Glaucoma drops: tear-duct pressure is especially useful because it reduces whole-body absorption.

## Safety & privacy

Medium risk because contaminated drops can infect an eye and some drops affect the whole body. Seek urgent care for sudden vision loss, severe pain, chemical exposure, eye trauma, or a red painful eye with light sensitivity.
