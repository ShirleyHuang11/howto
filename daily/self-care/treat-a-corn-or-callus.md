## Goal

A corn or callus is softened, cushioned, and prevented from recurring by reducing pressure or friction.

## Preconditions

- This is for thickened skin from pressure or rubbing, not infected wounds or unexplained growths.
- Seek professional foot care if you have diabetes, poor circulation, neuropathy, immune suppression, or foot ulcers.
- Call a clinician for redness, swelling, warmth, drainage, bleeding, severe pain, or skin that opens.
- Supplies: warm water, towel, pumice stone or foot file, moisturizer, protective pad, and better-fitting footwear.
- Do not use medicated corn removers if you have diabetes, poor circulation, or reduced sensation unless a clinician approves.

## Steps

1. **Inspect the skin.** Check for open skin, redness, drainage, bleeding, or severe tenderness. → *Expect:* only intact thickened skin is treated at home.
2. **Soak the area.** Soak the foot or hand in warm water for 10 to 15 minutes. → *Expect:* thick skin feels softer.
3. **File gently.** [BRANCH: foot callus | hand callus] rub lightly with a pumice stone or file, removing only softened surface skin. → *Expect:* the area is smoother without pain or bleeding.
   - ⚠️ *Irreversible:* Do not cut corns or calluses with a blade, razor, or sharp tool.
4. **Moisturize.** Apply a plain moisturizer to intact skin after drying. → *Expect:* skin feels less dry and cracked.
5. **Cushion pressure.** Use a non-medicated pad, moleskin, or gel cushion around the corn or callus. → *Expect:* walking or gripping causes less rubbing.
6. **Address footwear or tools.** Switch to wider shoes, thicker socks, gloves, or adjusted handles that remove the pressure point. → *Expect:* the same spot is not repeatedly compressed.
7. **Repeat gradually.** Soak, file lightly, and moisturize a few times per week. → *Expect:* thick skin reduces slowly without injury.
8. **Monitor for injury.** Stop if pain, bleeding, redness, or drainage appears. → *Expect:* complications trigger medical care.
9. **Get help if persistent.** See a clinician or podiatrist if pain continues or the cause cannot be corrected. → *Expect:* structural or footwear issues are assessed.

## Decision points

- Diabetes, neuropathy, poor circulation, immune suppression, or foot ulcer history → see a professional before treating.
- Skin is open, bleeding, red, warm, swollen, or draining → do not file; seek medical care.
- Pain is sharp or centered under a wart-like spot → ask a clinician because it may not be a callus.
- Callus comes from shoe pressure → replace or stretch shoes and use padding.
- Hand callus comes from tools or sports → use gloves, grip tape, or technique changes.

## Failure modes & recovery

- **F1 Over-filing:** detect raw skin, pain, or bleeding, recover by stopping, washing, covering, and seeking care if infection signs appear.
- **F2 Pressure unchanged:** detect quick recurrence, recover by changing shoes, pads, or tool grip.
- **F3 Medicated pad burn:** detect white, painful, or broken skin after acid pad, recover by stopping and calling a clinician.
- **F4 High-risk foot:** detect diabetes or poor sensation, recover by stopping home trimming and seeing a podiatrist.

## Verification

Only intact thickened skin was gently filed, the area is cushioned, and the pressure source has been reduced.

## Variations

- Toe corn: use non-medicated pads that do not squeeze toes together.
- Runner's callus: assess shoe size, socks, mileage changes, and friction points.
- Hand callus: reduce friction with gloves and moisturize after washing.

## Safety & privacy

Medium risk for people with diabetes, poor circulation, or reduced sensation because small foot injuries can become serious. Seek care for infection signs, open skin, severe pain, or uncertain diagnosis.
