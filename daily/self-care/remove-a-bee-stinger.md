## Goal

A bee stinger is removed quickly, the sting is cleaned and cooled, and allergic reaction signs are monitored.

## Preconditions

- Move away from the bees or nest before treating the sting.
- ⚠️ Call emergency services for trouble breathing, throat tightness, swelling of lips or tongue, widespread hives, vomiting, dizziness, fainting, confusion, or known severe allergy.
- Use an epinephrine auto-injector immediately if prescribed for anaphylaxis, then call emergency services.
- Seek medical care for stings in the mouth or throat, many stings, eye stings, infection signs, or worsening swelling after 24 to 48 hours.
- Supplies: clean water, soap, flat-edged card or fingernail, cold pack, cloth, and bandage if needed.

## Steps

1. **Check for allergy symptoms.** Look for breathing trouble, throat tightness, widespread hives, vomiting, dizziness, or fainting. → *Expect:* anaphylaxis signs are ruled in or out immediately.
2. **Use epinephrine if prescribed.** [BRANCH: severe allergy signs | local sting only] use the auto-injector and call emergency services for severe signs; continue local care only if symptoms are local. → *Expect:* emergency treatment starts when needed.
   - ⚠️ *Irreversible:* Do not wait to see if anaphylaxis improves on its own.
3. **Scrape the stinger out fast.** Use a credit card edge, fingernail, or other flat edge to scrape across the skin. → *Expect:* the stinger is removed quickly.
4. **Avoid pinching the venom sac.** Do not squeeze with tweezers if a sac is visible. → *Expect:* less venom may be pushed into the skin.
5. **Wash the area.** Clean with soap and water. → *Expect:* surface germs and venom residue are reduced.
6. **Apply cold.** Wrap a cold pack in cloth and apply for 10 to 15 minutes at a time. → *Expect:* pain and swelling decrease.
7. **Elevate if possible.** Raise the stung limb above heart level. → *Expect:* swelling is limited.
8. **Use symptom relief if safe.** Consider an oral antihistamine for itching or acetaminophen or ibuprofen for pain as labeled. → *Expect:* discomfort is controlled without exceeding label doses.
9. **Monitor for delayed problems.** Watch for spreading redness, warmth, pus, fever, or swelling that worsens after 24 to 48 hours. → *Expect:* infection or delayed reaction triggers medical care.

## Decision points

- Any anaphylaxis sign → use prescribed epinephrine and call emergency services.
- Sting inside mouth or throat → call emergency services because swelling can block breathing.
- More than a few stings, or stings in a small child, older adult, or medically fragile person → call a clinician or poison control.
- Redness, warmth, pus, fever, or worsening pain → seek medical care.
- Local swelling only and improving → continue cold compresses and monitoring.

## Failure modes & recovery

- **F1 Stinger remains:** detect a dark splinter-like point, recover by scraping again with a flat edge.
- **F2 Pinching first:** detect tweezers squeezing the sac, recover by switching to scraping and washing.
- **F3 Anaphylaxis delayed:** detect new hives, breathing symptoms, vomiting, dizziness, or swelling, recover by using epinephrine if prescribed and calling emergency services.
- **F4 Cold injury:** detect numb white skin from direct ice, recover by removing ice and using a cloth barrier.

## Verification

The stinger is out, the area is washed and cooled, and emergency allergy symptoms are absent or emergency services have been called.

## Variations

- Known bee allergy: carry two epinephrine auto-injectors and follow the personal action plan.
- Child: remove the stinger the same way and use age-appropriate medicine only.
- Outdoor work: leave the area calmly and check clothing for additional insects before treatment.

## Safety & privacy

Medium risk because anaphylaxis can progress quickly. Call emergency services for breathing trouble, throat tightness, lip or tongue swelling, widespread hives, vomiting, dizziness, or fainting.
