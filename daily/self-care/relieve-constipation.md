## Goal

Mild constipation is relieved with fluids, fiber, movement, toilet positioning, and gentle medicine when appropriate.

## Preconditions

- This is for mild constipation without severe pain or emergency symptoms.
- Call emergency services for severe abdominal pain with a rigid belly, fainting, confusion, or signs of shock.
- Seek urgent medical care for severe or worsening belly pain, vomiting, swollen abdomen, inability to pass gas, blood in stool, black stool, fever, unexplained weight loss, or constipation after surgery.
- Ask a clinician before laxatives for pregnancy, kidney disease, inflammatory bowel disease, older frailty, or chronic opioid use.
- Have water, fiber foods, and a bathroom available.

## Steps

1. **Drink fluids.** Sip water or warm fluids through the day unless a clinician has restricted fluids. → *Expect:* urine is not dark and hard stools may soften.
2. **Add fiber gradually.** [BRANCH: low fiber diet | already high fiber] add fruits, vegetables, beans, oats, or psyllium slowly; avoid sudden large increases if bloated. → *Expect:* stool bulk improves without major gas or cramping.
3. **Move your body.** Walk or do gentle activity if safe. → *Expect:* bowel movement signals may increase.
   - ⚠️ *Irreversible:* Do not ignore severe abdominal pain, vomiting, or inability to pass gas while taking laxatives.
4. **Use toilet posture.** Sit with knees higher than hips using a small footstool, lean forward, and relax the belly. → *Expect:* pushing requires less strain.
5. **Respond to urges.** Use the bathroom when the urge appears and allow unhurried time. → *Expect:* stool is not held until harder.
6. **Avoid straining.** Breathe out gently instead of bearing down hard. → *Expect:* hemorrhoid and fainting risk is reduced.
7. **Try a gentle laxative if needed.** Use an over-the-counter osmotic laxative, stool softener, or fiber supplement as labeled. → *Expect:* relief occurs within the label timeframe.
8. **Review constipating medicines.** Note opioids, iron, calcium, antacids, antihistamines, and some antidepressants. → *Expect:* a clinician or pharmacist can advise safer adjustments.
9. **Escalate if not improving.** Contact a clinician if constipation lasts more than several days despite care or keeps recurring. → *Expect:* obstruction, medication effects, and other causes are considered.

## Decision points

- Severe pain, vomiting, swollen belly, inability to pass gas, fever, or blood → seek urgent medical care.
- New constipation after starting a medication → ask the prescriber or pharmacist.
- Chronic constipation or frequent laxative need → schedule clinician evaluation.
- Dehydration or fluid restriction → ask a clinician how much fluid is safe.
- Child, pregnancy, or older adult → use lower thresholds for medical advice.

## Failure modes & recovery

- **F1 Fiber too fast:** detect gas, bloating, or cramps, recover by reducing dose and increasing gradually with fluids.
- **F2 Laxative overuse:** detect repeated dosing beyond label, recover by stopping and calling a clinician.
- **F3 Straining injury:** detect hemorrhoid pain, bleeding, or dizziness, recover by stopping strain and seeking care for bleeding.
- **F4 Obstruction signs:** detect vomiting, swollen abdomen, severe pain, or no gas, recover by urgent medical evaluation.

## Verification

A bowel movement occurs without severe straining, or medical care is arranged because red-flag symptoms or persistent constipation are present.

## Variations

- Travel: resume fluids, movement, regular meals, and bathroom routine.
- Opioid use: ask the prescriber about a bowel regimen early.
- Low appetite: use tolerated fluids and small fiber additions rather than a sudden high-fiber load.

## Safety & privacy

Medium risk because bowel obstruction and bleeding need urgent care. Discuss stool changes honestly with clinicians, and avoid sharing bathroom details beyond what care requires.
