## Goal

You leave the house dressed so that the day's actual weather — including its swings — is comfortable, not a negotiation.

## Preconditions

- A weather source checked for *today at your location*: temperature now, high/low, rain chance, and wind — the "feels like" number where available.
- A wardrobe with basic layers: base tops, a mid layer (sweater/fleece), a shell/coat, and rain options.

## Steps

1. **Read four numbers, not one.** Current temp, today's high, rain probability, wind. The gap between now and the high is the layering plan; wind and rain modify everything ("feels like" already blends them). → *Expect:* you can say "cold start, mild afternoon, dry, breezy" — a sentence, not a number.
2. **Dress for the day's shape with removable layers.** Big now-to-high gap → base + removable mid; flat cold day → warmer fixed layers; flat hot day → light, breathable, sun-considered. The test: every layer you might not need must be carryable. → *Expect:* an outfit that works at *both* ends of the day's range by adding/removing pieces.
3. **Apply the rain rule by probability × plans.** >40–50% and you'll be outside → rain shell or umbrella, water-tolerant shoes; a shower-proof jacket covers ambiguity cheaply. → *Expect:* rain answered in proportion — not drenched, not hauling an umbrella through a clear day (a compact one in the bag dissolves this tradeoff).
4. **Handle the extremities where comfort actually lives.** Cold: hat, gloves, warm socks move the needle more than a second sweater. Hot/sunny: hat, sunglasses, sunscreen on exposed skin. Wet: the shoes decision outranks the jacket decision. → *Expect:* head/hands/feet matched to conditions.
5. **Do the 30-second door test.** Step outside or open the window fully dressed. Slightly cool standing still = right for a day involving walking (you warm up moving). → *Expect:* a confirming shiver-or-not; adjust one layer now, not three blocks away.
6. **Stage the shed-layer logistics.** Where does the coat go at the destination? Bag fits the folded mid-layer? → *Expect:* removing a layer later has a plan, so you'll actually wear it now.

## Decision points

- Long indoor-outdoor mix (commute + office) → dress for the indoors and make the *outer* layer do all the weather work at the transitions.
- Kids → same layers, plus the truth that they run warmer moving and complain at the extremities first: their gloves/hat outrank their second sweater too.
- Trust the forecast? → within ~12 hours, mostly yes; the sky out the window right now wins any disagreement about the next hour.

## Failure modes & recovery

- **F1 Freezing by mid-morning (underdressed):** indoor refuge + hot drink short-term; the lesson is usually a skipped door test or dressing for the high instead of the now.
- **F2 Sweltering and carrying a parka (overdressed):** shed and carry; recalibrate toward the "cool standing still" standard — moving bodies need less than intuition says.
- **F3 Soaked (rain rule lost):** dry socks/shirt if available beat enduring wet ones (carry spares in a commute bag during rainy seasons); wet phone/bag triage first.
- **F4 Same argument with the weather every day (chronic miscalibration):** log three days of "what I wore / what I wished" — personal bias (runs hot/cold) is stable and correctable once written down.

## Verification

Through the day's full range — coldest start, warmest peak, any rain — you were comfortable or one carried-layer adjustment away from it, and nothing you brought went unused *and* regretted.

## Variations

- Four-seasons-in-a-day climates (mountain, maritime): the shell + layers doctrine is mandatory, not seasonal; a packable shell lives in the bag permanently.
- Extreme cold (`-15°C and below`): layering becomes technical (base/insulation/windproof shell, no exposed skin, cotton stops being acceptable) — a distinct discipline beyond this recipe.

## Safety & privacy

Low risk in temperate life; the edges are real though — sun (burn on cloudy-bright days too) and genuine cold (wet + wind + cold compounds fast). The door test and the extremities rule are the daily workhorses.
