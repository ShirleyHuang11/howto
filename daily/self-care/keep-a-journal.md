## Goal

A journal entry gets written most days with minimal friction, sustained for weeks rather than abandoned after an ambitious first week, and missed days cost nothing.

## Preconditions

- One capture medium chosen and staged: a notebook with a pen clipped to it, or a single notes app; not both.
- A daily anchor moment identified (with morning coffee, or in bed before lights out) that already happens reliably.
- Acceptance up front that the journal is for you: ugly handwriting, fragments, and repetition are all valid.

## Steps

1. **Lower the bar before you start.** Define the minimum entry as one true sentence, dated. "Tired. Long day, shipped the report." is a complete entry. Ambitious daily-page quotas are the number one reason journals die in week two. → *Expect:* a written-down personal rule: one sentence counts.
2. **Stage the journal at the anchor location.** Notebook on the pillow or next to the coffee machine; app icon on the phone's first screen. The journal must be closer to hand than the distraction that replaces it. → *Expect:* starting an entry requires zero searching.
3. **Write at the same time every day.** Attach it to the anchor: pour coffee, open journal; or get into bed, write, then lights out. Consistency of the trigger matters far more than the length of the entry. → *Expect:* within a week the anchor itself reminds you; no alarm needed.
4. **Start each entry with the date, then the first true thing.** The date makes the notebook a record; the first sentence is whatever is actually on your mind, however mundane. → *Expect:* the blank-page pause lasts seconds, not minutes.
5. **When stuck, use a rotating prompt.** [BRANCH: nothing comes → pick one: what drained me today, what surprised me, what am I avoiding, what would I tell a friend in my situation | words are flowing → skip the prompt] Keep three or four favorite prompts written inside the front cover. → *Expect:* even a flat day yields a few sentences.
6. **Stop while it is still easy.** End at the natural pause, even after two sentences. Ending mid-thought at "still easy" makes tomorrow's entry inviting; writing to exhaustion makes it a chore. → *Expect:* closing the journal feels slightly early, not relieved.
7. **When you miss days, resume without backfilling.** ⚠️ *Irreversible:* days not written stay unwritten; that is fine. Write today's date after the gap and continue. Reconstructing missed days from memory produces homework, guilt, and quitting. → *Expect:* a visible date gap in the notebook and a normal entry after it, with no apology paragraph.

## Decision points

- Two weeks in and entries feel like a status report → switch the anchor entry to one prompt-driven paragraph; prompts break the log-of-events rut.
- Worried someone might read it → journal candor depends on believed privacy; use a drawer, a bag pocket, or an app with a passcode before censoring yourself.
- The same worry appears in entries for a week straight → that is the journal doing its job as a detector; act on the worry or talk to someone, rather than re-transcribing it nightly.

## Failure modes & recovery

- **F1 Streak breaks and restarting feels pointless:** all-or-nothing framing → count "days written this month" instead of streaks; 20 of 30 is a thriving journal practice.
- **F2 Entries balloon and start taking 40 minutes:** scope creep → set a soft cap (one page or ten minutes) and stop there; overflow topics get one line and "more tomorrow".
- **F3 Blank page paralysis despite prompts:** the bar has silently risen again → write literally "one sentence: " and finish it; re-read the rule from step 1.
- **F4 Notebook keeps being elsewhere:** the staging broke (bag swap, travel) → keep a fallback: any dated scrap or a phone note counts and gets taped or copied in later, no penalty.

## Verification

Over the trailing two weeks: entries exist for most days (10 of 14 is strong), every entry is dated, at least one gap was resumed with no backfilled entries, and the median entry took under ten minutes.

## Variations

- Morning pages style: write immediately on waking before any screen, stream-of-consciousness; pairs badly with a bedtime anchor, pick one.
- Digital-first: a notes app with a daily template and passcode; enable the app's daily reminder as the anchor and disable its word-count display.
- Gratitude variant: the prompt is fixed (three specific things from today); specificity is the trick, "good coffee with Sam" not "friends".

## Safety & privacy

Low physical risk. The real exposure is the content: an honest journal contains material you may not want read, so control access (drawer, passcode) rather than writing a censored journal. If entries persistently document harm to yourself or others, that is a signal to seek human help, not just to keep writing.
