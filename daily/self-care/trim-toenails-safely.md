## Goal

All toenails are trimmed straight across, left long enough to protect the toe tip, and smoothed without cutting skin or encouraging ingrown edges.

## Preconditions

- Clean toenail clippers, a nail file, soap, water, and a towel.
- Good light and a stable seat where you can reach your feet without straining.
- Nails are not actively bleeding, draining pus, or severely painful.
- If you have diabetes, poor circulation, numb feet, or very thick nails, plan professional foot care instead of self-trimming.
- Enough time to soften, trim slowly, and clean the tools after.

## Steps

1. **Soften the nails.** Wash your feet or trim after a shower, then dry between the toes. → *Expect:* nails feel slightly more flexible and the skin is clean.
2. **Inspect each toe.** Look for redness, swelling, drainage, dark discoloration, numbness, or nails too thick for the clipper. → *Expect:* you know which nails are safe to trim and which should be left for a clinician or podiatrist.
3. **Position the clipper straight across.** Hold the blade nearly perpendicular to the nail, not wrapped around the corners. → *Expect:* the planned cut line is flat across the nail tip.
4. **Take small straight cuts.** Clip from one side toward the other while leaving a small white edge beyond the skin. → *Expect:* the nail is shorter but not cut into the corner or pink tissue.
5. **Leave corners visible.** Do not round down the sides or chase the nail into the groove. → *Expect:* both corners still reach past the skin fold.
6. **Smooth sharp points.** File only the front edge and any rough snag, keeping the shape straight across. → *Expect:* socks slide over the nail without catching.
7. **Clean up.** Discard clippings, wash or wipe the clippers, and put on roomy socks or shoes. → *Expect:* the area is clean and no toe feels pinched.

## Decision points

- Nail is thick, curved, crumbly, or too hard to clip → stop and book a podiatrist or clinician.
- You have diabetes, poor circulation, neuropathy, or a foot ulcer history → use professional foot care.
- Skin is red, hot, swollen, draining, or very tender → do not trim into it; see a doctor.
- Nail edge is already digging into skin → switch to `daily/self-care/treat-an-ingrown-toenail` if mild, or seek care if infected.
- You cannot see or reach safely → ask for help from a trained caregiver or clinician.

## Failure modes & recovery

- **F1 Cut too short:** tenderness or exposed pink skin → rinse, dry, cover if rubbing, and leave it alone while it grows.
- **F2 Corner rounded down:** nail side now sits inside the skin fold → stop cutting the side and let the corner grow out straight.
- **F3 Skin nicked:** bleeding or stinging → rinse, press with clean gauze, apply a small bandage, and watch for infection.
- **F4 Nail splits:** a crack catches on socks → file the loose edge smooth; see a clinician if the split reaches the nail bed.
- **F5 Painful toe after trimming:** throbbing, swelling, pus, spreading redness, fever, or worsening pain → see a doctor promptly.

## Verification

Each toenail has a straight front edge with visible corners, no bleeding, no skin cut, and no shoe pressure pain when you stand.

## Variations

- Large toenail clippers: use them for big toes, but still take small cuts.
- Nail scissors: cut straight across with short snips, then file the front edge.
- Pedicure appointment: tell the provider not to cut the corners or trim too short.
- Thick nails: a podiatrist may thin or trim them with safer tools.

## Safety & privacy

Low risk for healthy feet, medium risk for people with diabetes, poor circulation, neuropathy, immune suppression, or recurrent ingrown nails. Do not share clippers casually. Seek medical care for pus, spreading redness, fever, severe pain, black discoloration after injury, numbness, or any wound that is not improving.
