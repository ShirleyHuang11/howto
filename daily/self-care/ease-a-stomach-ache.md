## Goal

A mild stomach ache is managed with rest, fluids, heat, and trigger avoidance while red flags are recognized.

## Preconditions

- Pain is mild to moderate, familiar, and not linked to major injury, pregnancy concern, or known serious condition.
- Water, bland food or fluids, a heat source, and a way to contact care are available.
- Seek professional or emergency care when pain is severe, persistent, localized with fever, chest pain, black or bloody stool, repeated vomiting, fainting, stiff abdomen, or pregnancy concern.
- This does not diagnose the cause of abdominal pain.

## Steps

1. **Pause and rest.** Sit or lie in a comfortable position. → *Expect:* movement no longer worsens the ache.
2. **Rate the pain.** Note location, intensity, timing, and whether it is cramping, burning, or sharp. → *Expect:* you can tell if it changes.
3. **Sip fluids.** Take small sips of water, broth, or oral rehydration solution. → *Expect:* fluids stay down.
4. **Choose bland intake.** If hungry, try crackers, toast, rice, banana, or applesauce. → *Expect:* food does not worsen nausea or pain.
5. **Use gentle heat.** Place a warm, not hot, pad or bottle over clothing on the abdomen. → *Expect:* cramping may ease.
6. **Avoid triggers.** Pause alcohol, heavy meals, spicy food, greasy food, and too much caffeine. → *Expect:* the stomach is not irritated further.
7. **Do not overuse medicine.** Use over-the-counter products only as labeled and avoid aspirin or ibuprofen if they worsen stomach irritation. → *Expect:* symptom relief does not create new risk.
8. **Use the bathroom if needed.** Do not strain, but respond to diarrhea, constipation, or gas cues. → *Expect:* pressure may improve.
9. **Recheck after an hour.** Compare pain, nausea, fever, hydration, and location. → *Expect:* symptoms are improving or escalation is clear.
10. **Seek care for red flags.** Treat severe, persistent, or worsening pain as more than a simple stomach ache. → *Expect:* serious symptoms are not delayed.

## Decision points

- Pain is severe, one-sided, or moves to the lower right abdomen with fever or vomiting → seek urgent care.
- Vomiting prevents fluids or there are signs of dehydration → seek medical advice.
- [BRANCH: mild improving ache | severe or persistent pain] improving symptoms can be monitored, while severe or persistent pain needs care.
- ⚠️ *Irreversible:* Do not ignore severe abdominal pain because some causes need time-sensitive treatment.

## Failure modes & recovery

- **F1 Heavy meal too soon:** detect worse pain after eating, recover by returning to small sips and bland foods.
- **F2 Heat too hot:** detect skin redness or burning, recover by removing heat and using a cloth barrier.
- **F3 Red flag dismissed:** detect severe, persistent, or localized pain, recover by seeking care.
- **F4 Dehydration:** detect dizziness, very dark urine, or dry mouth, recover with oral fluids or medical care if not tolerated.

## Verification

Pain is mild and improving, fluids are tolerated, and no abdominal red flag is present.

## Variations

- Gas-like cramps: walking gently may help if it does not worsen pain.
- Nausea: prioritize small sips before food.
- Known condition: follow the clinician's plan for that condition.

## Safety & privacy

Medium risk because abdominal pain can reflect urgent illness. Seek professional or emergency care when pain is severe, persistent, worsening, localized with fever, or paired with bleeding, fainting, chest pain, or dehydration.
