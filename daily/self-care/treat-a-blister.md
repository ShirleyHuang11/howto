## Goal

A friction blister is protected, pain is reduced, and infection risk is minimized while the skin heals.

## Preconditions

- Soap and water, clean towel, blister bandage or moleskin, and optional sterile gauze.
- Clean needle and antiseptic only if draining is truly necessary.
- Ability to stop the rubbing source, such as changing socks or shoes.
- Medical help available if signs of infection or high-risk health conditions are present.
- This recipe is for typical friction blisters, not burns or severe wounds.

## Steps

1. **Wash hands and the area.** Clean with soap and water, then dry gently. → *Expect:* skin is clean and grit is removed.
2. **Identify blister type.** [BRANCH: intact and tolerable | intact but painful or likely to burst | already torn] → *Expect:* the covering skin condition is clear.
3. **Leave intact blisters closed when possible.** Do not pop a blister that is small, clean, and not limiting movement. → *Expect:* fluid cushion remains under unbroken skin.
4. **Pad around pressure.** Use a donut of moleskin or a blister bandage so shoe pressure lands around the blister. → *Expect:* walking pressure decreases immediately.
5. **Drain only if necessary.** If large, painful, or certain to tear, sterilize a needle, puncture near the edge, and leave roof skin in place. → *Expect:* fluid drains while the protective top skin remains attached.
6. **Cover the blister.** Apply a hydrocolloid blister bandage or sterile gauze with light tape. → *Expect:* dressing stays put without squeezing the blister.
7. **Reduce friction source.** Change socks, loosen laces, add padding, or stop the activity. → *Expect:* the same spot is no longer being rubbed.
8. **Change dressing as needed.** Replace loose, wet, or dirty coverings with clean ones. → *Expect:* wound stays covered and dry enough to heal.
9. **Watch for infection.** Check daily for spreading redness, warmth, pus, red streaks, fever, or worsening pain. → *Expect:* either normal healing or a clear reason to seek care.
10. **Let dead skin release naturally.** Trim only loose, clean dead skin after healing begins. → *Expect:* tender new skin remains protected.

## Decision points

- Diabetes, poor circulation, immune suppression, or neuropathy → seek medical guidance early.
- Blister is from a burn, frostbite, chemical, or allergic rash → do not treat as simple friction.
- Blood blister → protect and avoid draining unless advised by a clinician.
- Blister is on the face, genitals, or near an eye → seek medical advice.
- Event must continue → prioritize friction control and clean coverage over speed.

## Failure modes & recovery

- **F1 Blister tears open:** wash, keep the skin flap if clean, apply ointment if appropriate, and cover sterile.
- **F2 Dressing sticks:** soak with clean water before removal rather than ripping new skin.
- **F3 Pain increases:** pressure is still present or infection may be starting; offload and reassess.
- **F4 Skin turns macerated white:** dressing trapped too much moisture; air briefly in a clean setting and redress.
- **F5 Infection signs appear:** stop self-treatment and contact a clinician urgently, especially with fever or red streaks.

## Verification

The blister is clean, covered or padded, protected from further rubbing, and shows no spreading redness, pus, fever, or worsening pain.

## Variations

- Hiking: apply moleskin or tape at the first hot spot before fluid forms.
- Hand blister: use gloves or grip tape to remove the rubbing source.
- Hydrocolloid bandage: leave it in place until it loosens unless leakage or infection signs appear.

## Safety & privacy

Medium risk because opening skin can introduce infection. Use clean materials, avoid unnecessary draining, and seek professional care for high-risk health conditions or infection signs.
