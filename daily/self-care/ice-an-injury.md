## Goal

A minor acute sprain, strain, or bruise is cooled safely with rest, compression, and elevation while signs needing medical care are recognized.

## Preconditions

- Ice pack, bag of ice, or frozen vegetables.
- Thin towel or cloth barrier.
- Elastic bandage if compression is appropriate.
- Place to rest and elevate the injured area.
- Emergency care access if the injury may be serious.

## Steps

1. **Stop the activity.** Move out of the situation that caused the injury. → *Expect:* the injured part is no longer being loaded or struck.
2. **Screen for serious injury.** Check for deformity, inability to bear weight, numbness, severe swelling, open wound, or head, neck, chest, belly, or spine injury. → *Expect:* either minor-injury care is reasonable or urgent care is needed.
3. **Rest the area.** Keep the injured part still and supported. → *Expect:* pain decreases when movement stops.
4. **Wrap ice in a barrier.** Place ice or a cold pack inside a towel. → *Expect:* cold reaches the skin without direct ice contact.
5. **Ice for 15-20 minutes.** Apply cold over the sore area, checking skin every few minutes. → *Expect:* area feels cold and numb but not burning, white, or painful from cold.
6. **Compress if suitable.** Wrap an elastic bandage snugly from farther out toward the body, avoiding tightness. → *Expect:* swelling support improves without tingling or color change.
7. **Elevate above heart level.** Prop the injured area on pillows when possible. → *Expect:* swelling pressure feels reduced.
8. **Pause between icing rounds.** Wait at least 40-60 minutes before icing again. → *Expect:* skin returns to normal temperature and color.
9. **Reassess function.** After the first rounds, check pain, swelling, movement, and weight bearing. → *Expect:* mild injuries improve or remain stable.
10. **Seek care when the pattern is not minor.** Call a clinician or emergency services for severe symptoms or worsening function. → *Expect:* serious injuries are not delayed by home icing.

## Decision points

- Injury involves head, neck, spine, chest, abdomen, or loss of consciousness → this is not a routine RICE injury; seek urgent help.
- Bone looks deformed or weight bearing is impossible → get medical evaluation.
- Skin has poor sensation or circulation → avoid unsupervised icing.
- Pain is from chronic stiffness rather than acute swelling → heat or movement guidance may be more appropriate.
- Swelling worsens despite rest → seek care.

## Failure modes & recovery

- **F1 Ice burn:** skin becomes white, waxy, or painfully cold; remove ice and warm gently, then stop icing.
- **F2 Compression too tight:** fingers or toes tingle, pale, or swell; unwrap and rewrap looser.
- **F3 Masked serious injury:** numbness from ice hides worsening pain; reassess after skin warms.
- **F4 Reinjury:** activity resumed too soon; rest and protect the area until function returns.
- **F5 No improvement:** pain or swelling persists beyond 24-48 hours; arrange medical advice.

## Verification

The injured area has been rested, iced through a cloth for no more than 20 minutes at a time, optionally compressed without circulation changes, elevated, and screened for urgent warning signs.

## Variations

- Bruise: compression may be minimal, but cold and elevation can still reduce swelling early.
- Ankle sprain: use support and avoid weight bearing if walking changes sharply.
- No ice pack: use a bag of frozen vegetables wrapped in a towel.

## Safety & privacy

Medium risk because serious injuries can look mild at first. Do not apply ice directly to skin, do not sleep with ice on, and call emergency services for severe or suspicious injuries.
