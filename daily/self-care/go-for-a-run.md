## Goal

A run completed at the right effort for its purpose, over a sensible route, with the traffic, weather, and body signals all handled, and recovery started when it ends.

## Preconditions

- Running shoes with life left in them (dead cushioning is a quiet injury source) and weather-matched clothing (`daily/self-care/dress-for-the-weather`).
- A route or distance in mind, and a purpose: easy run, long run, or workout. Most runs should be easy; that single fact prevents most running injuries.
- Water beforehand; phone or ID pocketed for longer or remote routes.

## Steps

1. **Warm up.** Three minutes of brisk walking plus leg swings (`daily/self-care/warm-up-before-exercise`), then start the run itself slow. → *Expect:* first kilometer noticeably slower than the run's average; that is correct, not weak.
2. **Settle into the day's effort using the talk test.** Easy runs: full sentences possible. Steady: phrases. Hard workout intervals: words. If the plan says easy and you cannot chat, slow down regardless of pace numbers. → *Expect:* breathing matched to the run's stated purpose.
3. **Run the route with traffic logic.** Sidewalks and paths where they exist; facing oncoming traffic where they do not; `embodied/mobility/cross-a-street` at every crossing with headphones low or one ear out; visible colors or lights at dusk and dark. → *Expect:* every junction treated as a junction, not as pace interruption.
4. **Check form when it gets hard.** Shoulders down, gaze ahead, quick light steps rather than reaching strides. Form decays with fatigue and shortening the stride restores it cheaply. → *Expect:* periodic thirty-second form scans, especially in the final third.
5. **Distinguish discomfort from pain.** Effort, burning legs, and general tiredness are the sport. Sharp, localized, one-sided, or gait-changing pain is a stop signal: walk, cut the route short, and let it be assessed by rest and, if persistent, a professional. → *Expect:* honest triage in the moment; no limping through kilometers to satisfy a number.
6. **Finish with a ramp down, not a dead stop.** Last minutes easier, then a few minutes of walking; gentle stretching after is where the static holds belong. → *Expect:* breathing recovered during the walk; legs looser after the stretch.
7. **Start recovery immediately.** Water, food within the hour for longer runs, sweaty kit into the wash cycle (`daily/home/do-the-laundry`), and the run logged if you track. → *Expect:* rehydrated, fed, and the kit not fermenting in a bag (`daily/self-care/pack-a-gym-bag` F2).

## Decision points

- Building up from nothing: run-walk intervals (for example one minute running, ninety seconds walking, repeated) are the legitimate on-ramp, and progressing them weekly beats heroic continuous efforts that end in three-week injuries.
- Heat and cold: shift effort expectations down in heat and hydrate more; in cold, longer warm-up and layers you can shed (`dress-for-the-weather`'s removable-layer doctrine applies at running temperatures, which feel roughly ten degrees warmer than standing ones).
- Weekly structure: increasing total distance by roughly ten percent a week, with most runs easy and at most one hard day, is the boring formula that keeps runners running.

## Failure modes & recovery

- **F1 Went out too fast and blew up mid-route:** walk until the talk test passes again, finish easy, and start the next run slower on purpose. The first kilometer sets the whole run's fate.
- **F2 Sharp pain mid-run:** stop and walk home or call the ride; ice and rest, and if it alters walking the next day or persists a week, get it assessed. Running through sharp pain converts days off into months off.
- **F3 Chafing or blisters discovered mid-run:** adjust what you can (socks, laces), finish short if needed; afterwards fix the cause (anti-chafe balm, better socks, shoe fit) rather than re-testing the same failure.
- **F4 Motivation collapse (skipped a week or three):** restart smaller than pride suggests, schedule runs like appointments, and let the streak rebuild; the injury-free comeback is the slow one.

## Verification

The run matched its stated purpose on the talk test, junctions were crossed properly, no sharp pain was run through, it ended with a walk and stretch, and recovery (water, food, laundry, log) happened within the hour.

## Variations

- Treadmills remove traffic and weather and add a belt: slight incline (one percent) approximates outdoors, and the stop-button replaces route-shortening decisions.
- Trail running adds ankles and navigation to the risk list: sturdier shoes, more attention to footing, and telling someone the route on remote trails.

## Safety & privacy

Medium risk split between traffic and overuse. Traffic is handled at every junction with full attention; overuse is handled by the unglamorous trio of easy pace, gradual volume, and respecting sharp pain. Route-tracking apps share your regular paths and times if left public; set that to private once.
