## Goal

Poison ivy, oak, or sumac exposure is washed off early, itching is controlled, and serious reactions are escalated.

## Preconditions

- This covers skin exposure to plants that contain urushiol oil.
- Call emergency services for trouble breathing, swelling of lips or tongue, faintness, or a rapidly spreading severe allergic reaction.
- Seek medical care for rash on the face, eyes, genitals, widespread rash, pus, fever, severe swelling, or symptoms that keep spreading.
- Supplies: soap, cool water, laundry access, calamine or hydrocortisone, oral antihistamine if safe for you, and clean clothing.
- Avoid burning poison ivy plants because inhaled smoke can cause serious airway irritation.

## Steps

1. **Wash skin quickly.** Within the first hour if possible, wash exposed skin with soap and cool running water. → *Expect:* plant oil is removed before more binds to skin.
2. **Clean under nails.** Scrub gently under fingernails with soap and water. → *Expect:* oil is less likely to spread by scratching.
3. **Remove contaminated clothing.** [BRANCH: washable items | gear or shoes] wash clothing in detergent; wipe shoes, tools, and pet fur with appropriate protection. → *Expect:* oil is not reintroduced to skin.
   - ⚠️ *Irreversible:* Do not burn contaminated plants or brush because smoke exposure can injure the airway.
4. **Use cool compresses.** Apply cool wet cloths for 15 to 30 minutes several times daily. → *Expect:* itching and heat decrease.
5. **Apply itch relief.** Use calamine lotion or hydrocortisone cream as labeled on intact skin. → *Expect:* itching becomes more manageable.
6. **Consider antihistamine.** Use an oral antihistamine as labeled if itching disrupts sleep and it is safe with your conditions and medicines. → *Expect:* scratching is reduced, especially at night.
7. **Avoid scratching.** Keep nails short and cover weepy areas with clean loose gauze. → *Expect:* skin breaks and infection risk are reduced.
8. **Monitor spread and severity.** Check daily for face or eye involvement, severe swelling, pus, fever, or rapidly expanding rash. → *Expect:* serious symptoms trigger medical care.
9. **Call if not improving.** Contact a clinician if rash is severe, widespread, or not improving after about a week. → *Expect:* stronger treatment is considered when needed.

## Decision points

- Breathing trouble, facial swelling, faintness, or severe allergic symptoms → call emergency services.
- Rash near eyes, on face or genitals, or widespread over the body → seek medical care.
- Pus, fever, warmth, or increasing pain → call a clinician for possible infection.
- Exposure was within an hour → washing is the priority.
- Pet or gear exposure → clean items because urushiol can remain active on surfaces.

## Failure modes & recovery

- **F1 Oil remains on gear:** detect rash after reusing shoes or tools, recover by cleaning or isolating contaminated items.
- **F2 Scratching opens skin:** detect bleeding or broken skin, recover by washing, covering, and watching infection signs.
- **F3 Eye involvement:** detect rash, swelling, or irritation near the eye, recover by seeking medical care.
- **F4 Sedation from antihistamine:** detect drowsiness, recover by avoiding driving and asking a pharmacist about options.

## Verification

Exposed skin and contaminated items have been washed, itching is controlled, and no emergency or see-a-doctor thresholds are present.

## Variations

- Known severe reactions: contact a clinician early after exposure.
- Children: use age-appropriate itch medicine only and prevent scratching with trimmed nails.
- Outdoor workers: wash tools, gloves, boots, and pet fur after likely exposure.

## Safety & privacy

Medium risk because severe allergy, infection, and eye involvement need medical care. Call emergency services for breathing trouble or swelling of lips, tongue, or throat.
