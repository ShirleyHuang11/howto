## Goal

You, your home, and the vulnerable people around you get through a multi-day heat wave without heat illness: the home stays as cool as physics allows, hydration stays ahead of losses, and warning signs are caught early.

## Preconditions

- A heat warning or forecast of unusually high temperatures for your area, ideally noticed 1-2 days ahead.
- Water supply, window coverings of some kind, and at least one fan or one room that can be closed off.
- Knowledge of who nearby is high-risk: people over 65 living alone, infants, pregnant people, outdoor workers, and anyone on diuretics, beta blockers, or psychiatric medications that impair heat regulation.

## Steps

1. **Stock up before the peak.** Water, electrolyte options (oral rehydration salts or sports drinks), food that needs no cooking, and any prescription refills. → *Expect:* no errands required during the hottest days.
2. **Start hydrating the day before.** Drink regularly without waiting for thirst; thirst lags dehydration, and older adults barely feel it at all. Cut back alcohol; it works against you. → *Expect:* pale-yellow urine going into day one of the heat.
3. **Run the night-flush cycle.** Each night when outside air drops below inside temperature, open windows on opposite sides of the home for cross-flow; a fan blowing hot air out one window accelerates it. → *Expect:* indoor temperature drops several degrees by morning.
4. **Seal the home at dawn.** Close all windows and cover them, outside shading (shutters, awnings) beating inside curtains, before outdoor temperature crosses indoor. Keep everything shut through the day. → *Expect:* the home stays cooler than outside well into the afternoon; opening a window "for air" at 2 pm imports heat.
5. **Designate and defend one cool room.** Usually the lowest floor or the north-facing room. Close its door, and everyone sleeps or rests there during the peak if the rest of the home overheats. → *Expect:* one room measurably cooler than the others.
6. **Use fans correctly.** A fan on your skin cools by evaporation and works better with a spritz of water or a damp shirt. [BRANCH: indoor temperature under about 35 °C / 95 °F → fan on people helps | above that → fans on dry skin can add heat; switch to damp cloths, cool showers, forearms in cool water] → *Expect:* skin feels cooler, not blasted with hot air.
7. **Shift activity to the edges of the day.** Exercise, errands, and cooking (which heats the home) before 10 am or after sunset. → *Expect:* no strenuous outdoor effort between late morning and early evening.
8. **Check on high-risk neighbors daily, in person or by call.** Ask specifically: are you drinking, is your home cooler than outside, are you dizzy or confused. Twice daily during the peak. → *Expect:* a direct answer each day; no answer for a day triggers a visit.
9. **Watch each other for heat illness.** Heavy sweating, cramps, nausea, headache: heat exhaustion. Confusion, hot dry or clammy skin, fainting: possible heat stroke. → *Expect:* everyone in the household can name the difference (see F3, F4).

## Decision points

- Home has air conditioning → run it early rather than trying to catch up; a reasonable setpoint sustained beats blasting a sweltering room. If cost is the barrier, cool just the one designated room.
- No way to keep home under roughly 32 °C / 90 °F for days → plan daytime hours in public cooled spaces: library, mall, cooling center. Several hours of cooling per day substantially cuts risk.
- Power outage during the peak → the home becomes untenable faster than people expect; move to a cooled public space, and treat car air conditioning as a stopgap, never a place to leave anyone.
- Working outdoors is unavoidable → shift hours, enforce shade breaks every hour, and pair up; solo outdoor work in peak heat is how people are found too late.

## Failure modes & recovery

- **F1 Windows open all day:** the home tracks outdoor temperature by afternoon → close and cover at dawn; verify with a thermometer inside vs outside rather than by feel.
- **F2 Hydration by thirst alone:** headache and dark urine by afternoon → schedule drinking (a glass every hour awake); add electrolytes if sweating heavily, since plain water alone can dilute sodium over multi-day heavy sweating.
- **F3 Heat exhaustion:** heavy sweat, weakness, nausea, near-fainting → stop activity, move to the coolest spot, loosen clothing, sip fluids, cool the skin with water and airflow; improvement should start within 30 minutes, otherwise escalate.
- **F4 Heat stroke:** confusion, slurred speech, fainting, skin hot to touch → emergency services immediately; while waiting, cool aggressively (water over skin, fanning, ice packs to neck, armpits, groin). ⚠️ *Irreversible:* untreated heat stroke causes organ damage and death within hours; never wait to "see if they perk up," and never leave the person alone.
- **F5 Forgotten occupants:** children or pets left in cars, pets on balconies without water → a hard rule of checking the back seat every exit; car interiors reach lethal temperatures in minutes even with windows cracked.

## Verification

Through the final day of the warning: indoor peak temperature stayed below the outdoor peak, everyone's urine stayed pale and nobody progressed past mild heat symptoms, every high-risk contact was reached daily, and no person or animal was ever in a parked car or sealed hot room.

## Variations

- Humid climates: sweat evaporates poorly, so fans and damp cloths help less and rest breaks matter more; heat index, not raw temperature, is the number to watch.
- Dry climates: evaporative tricks (damp sheets, swamp coolers) work unusually well; night flushing is very effective given large day-night swings.

## Safety & privacy

Medium risk: heat waves kill more people than storms, mostly isolated elderly people indoors. The load-bearing behaviors are night-flush plus daytime sealing, scheduled hydration, and the daily in-person check on neighbors. Checking on a neighbor requires no personal data beyond a knock and a question.
