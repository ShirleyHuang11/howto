## Goal

You get through a common cold with the least misery and lost time, avoid infecting others, and recognize early if what you have is not actually a cold.

## Preconditions

- Symptoms consistent with a cold: gradual onset, sore or scratchy throat first, then congestion and runny nose, mild cough, little or no fever in adults.
- Access to fluids, basic supplies (tissues, saline spray or rinse, honey, an over-the-counter pain reliever), and the ability to reduce your workload for 2-3 days.
- Not applicable as-is to infants, the immunocompromised, or people with serious lung or heart disease; those cases go to a clinician earlier.

## Steps

1. **Confirm it looks like a cold, not something else.** Sudden onset with high fever, body aches, and exhaustion points to flu; loss of smell or known exposure suggests testing for COVID if tests are available. → *Expect:* gradual onset and throat-first symptoms; anything else, see Decision points.
2. **Cancel what can be canceled for the first 48-72 hours.** The early phase is when rest changes the trajectory and when you are most contagious. → *Expect:* calendar cleared of gyms, gatherings, and visits to elderly or infant relatives.
3. **Drink fluids on a schedule, not by thirst.** Water, broth, warm tea; a practical target is pale-yellow urine. Warm liquids also loosen congestion. → *Expect:* urinating regularly, urine pale; throat feels less raw after warm drinks.
4. **Treat the symptoms that block sleep, and only those.** Sore throat or headache: paracetamol/acetaminophen or ibuprofen at label doses. Blocked nose: saline rinse or spray first; a decongestant only if saline is not enough. Night cough in adults: a spoonful of honey is as good as most syrups. → *Expect:* enough relief to sleep; you are not stacking multi-symptom products (see F2).
5. **Sleep more than usual, with the head slightly elevated.** An extra pillow reduces postnasal drip coughing. → *Expect:* fewer night wakings from cough; mornings are the worst point and that is normal.
6. **Protect other people.** Hand washing, sneezing into the elbow, a mask if you must share indoor space, own towel and cup at home. → *Expect:* housemates not sick a week later.
7. **Return to activity gradually after day 3-5.** The rule of thumb: symptoms above the neck only (sniffles, mild sore throat) permit light activity; anything with fever, chest involvement, or body aches means keep resting. → *Expect:* light activity does not worsen next-day symptoms.
8. **Ride out the tail.** A dry cough and fatigue commonly linger 1-2 weeks after everything else clears. → *Expect:* slow steady improvement; the pattern to fear is improvement followed by a new decline (see F4).

## Decision points

- Fever above 39 °C / 102 °F, or any fever lasting more than 3 days → this is beyond a routine cold; contact a clinician.
- Severe one-sided facial pain or tooth pain with thick discharge after a week → possible bacterial sinusitis; clinician.
- Shortness of breath, wheezing, chest pain, confusion, or inability to keep fluids down → urgent care now, not watchful waiting.
- Severe sore throat with fever but little or no cough → possible strep; worth a test rather than assuming a cold.
- Symptoms identical every spring or around cats/dust, no fever ever → likely allergy, not infection; antihistamines, not this recipe.

## Failure modes & recovery

- **F1 Demanding antibiotics:** colds are viral; antibiotics do nothing and cost you side effects → save the clinician visit for the red flags above, where antibiotics may actually be indicated.
- **F2 Medication stacking:** two multi-symptom products both containing paracetamol → read active ingredients on every box; never exceed the daily paracetamol maximum, and prefer single-ingredient products.
- **F3 Decongestant rebound:** oxymetazoline-type nasal sprays used beyond 3 days cause worse congestion when stopped → hard 3-day limit; switch to saline, which has no limit.
- **F4 Double worsening:** you improve around day 5-7, then fever and fatigue return or the cough turns productive and worse → classic sign of a secondary bacterial infection; see a clinician promptly.
- **F5 Training through it:** hard exercise during the feverish phase prolongs illness and (rarely) risks myocarditis → no intense exercise with fever, full stop.

## Verification

By day 10: no fever, congestion largely cleared, energy near baseline, and any residual cough clearly diminishing week over week. Nobody in your household caught it after your day-2 precautions began, and no red-flag symptom from the Decision points ever appeared unaddressed.

## Variations

- Children: dosing is weight-based and honey is forbidden under age 1; fever thresholds for seeking care are lower, especially under 3 months (any fever: clinician immediately).
- Zinc lozenges started within 24 hours of onset may shorten a cold slightly; vitamin C after onset does not.

## Safety & privacy

Low risk when it is actually a cold. The two hazards worth respecting: paracetamol overdose from stacked products (F2) and missed red flags (Decision points). Telling an employer "I am sick" requires no diagnosis details in most places; share only what you must.
