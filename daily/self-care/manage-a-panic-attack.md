## Goal

A panic attack is managed with grounding, slower breathing, and aftercare while urgent medical or safety concerns are not ignored.

## Preconditions

- You are in a place where you can sit, slow down, or step away from immediate demands.
- A support person, crisis line, clinician, or emergency number is available if symptoms feel unsafe.
- Seek professional or emergency care when chest pain is new or severe, fainting occurs, breathing trouble is not improving, there is risk of self-harm, or symptoms are unlike previous panic attacks.
- This is coping support, not a substitute for mental health or medical care.

## Steps

1. **Name what is happening.** Say to yourself, this may be panic and it can pass. → *Expect:* the experience has a label instead of feeling mysterious.
2. **Sit or steady yourself.** Place both feet on the floor or lean against a stable surface. → *Expect:* falling risk is reduced.
3. **Use five senses.** Name 5 things you see, 4 you feel, 3 you hear, 2 you smell, and 1 you taste. → *Expect:* attention shifts toward the present room.
4. **Slow the exhale.** Breathe in gently and make the exhale longer than the inhale. → *Expect:* breathing feels less rushed after several cycles.
5. **Release muscle tension.** Drop shoulders, unclench jaw, and soften hands. → *Expect:* the body is not bracing as hard.
6. **Reduce stimulation.** Step away from noise, bright screens, crowds, or conflict if safe. → *Expect:* fewer inputs compete for attention.
7. **Use a support script.** Text or call a trusted person and say you are having panic and need calm company. → *Expect:* support is specific and practical.
8. **Let the wave pass.** Remind yourself that panic peaks and settles even when it feels intense. → *Expect:* the goal becomes riding it out, not fighting every sensation.
9. **Do gentle aftercare.** Drink water, eat something light if needed, and avoid judging yourself. → *Expect:* recovery continues after symptoms drop.
10. **Plan follow-up.** If attacks are new, frequent, or disruptive, contact a clinician or therapist. → *Expect:* ongoing panic is not managed alone.

## Decision points

- Symptoms could be a medical emergency, especially new chest pain, fainting, or severe shortness of breath → seek emergency care.
- Thoughts of self-harm or inability to stay safe → contact emergency services or a crisis line now.
- [BRANCH: familiar panic pattern | new or different symptoms] familiar panic can use coping steps, while new symptoms need medical caution.
- ⚠️ *Irreversible:* Do not drive, use tools, or make major decisions during peak panic if attention is impaired.

## Failure modes & recovery

- **F1 Breath forced:** detect dizziness from deep or fast breathing, recover by breathing gently with a longer exhale.
- **F2 Sensations fought:** detect escalating fear from checking symptoms repeatedly, recover by returning to five-senses grounding.
- **F3 Isolation:** detect being alone and scared, recover by contacting a trusted person or crisis support.
- **F4 Medical sign missed:** detect new severe chest pain, fainting, or unusual symptoms, recover by seeking emergency care.

## Verification

Breathing and attention are steadier, immediate safety is maintained, and medical or self-harm warning signs have been addressed.

## Variations

- Public place: move to a wall, restroom, hallway, or quieter corner.
- Night panic: use dim light, slow exhale, and a brief note about what happened.
- Recurring attacks: build a clinician-approved panic plan.

## Safety & privacy

Medium risk because panic can resemble medical emergencies and may involve self-harm risk. Seek professional or emergency care when symptoms are new, severe, unsafe, or connected to thoughts of self-harm.
