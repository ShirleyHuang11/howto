## Goal

The tongue surface is gently cleaned from back to front to reduce coating and help freshen breath without irritating tissue.

## Preconditions

- A clean tongue scraper, sink, running water, and optional toothbrush or mouth rinse.
- You can breathe comfortably through your nose for a few seconds.
- No open sores, fresh oral surgery site, unexplained bleeding, or severe tongue pain.
- The scraper has smooth edges and is not cracked or rusty.
- You are ready to use gentle pressure only.

## Steps

1. **Wash the scraper.** Rinse it under running water before use. → *Expect:* the scraper surface is visibly clean.
2. **Stick out your tongue.** Relax your jaw and hold the scraper with both hands if it is U-shaped. → *Expect:* you can see the coated part of the tongue.
3. **Start as far back as comfortable.** Place the scraper near the back of the tongue without forcing a gag. → *Expect:* you feel pressure but not choking or pain.
4. **Scrape back to front.** Pull the scraper forward in one smooth pass with gentle pressure. → *Expect:* coating collects on the scraper edge.
5. **Rinse between passes.** Rinse the scraper after each pass before touching the tongue again. → *Expect:* removed coating is washed away instead of spread around.
6. **Repeat a few times.** Make 3 to 5 gentle passes, moving across the tongue surface. → *Expect:* the tongue looks less coated and feels cleaner.
7. **Rinse and store.** Rinse your mouth and wash the scraper before drying or storing it. → *Expect:* the tool is clean and ready for the next use.

## Decision points

- You gag easily → start closer to the middle of the tongue and move back gradually over days.
- Tongue hurts, bleeds, or has ulcers → stop and see a dentist or clinician if it persists.
- Bad breath continues despite daily cleaning, brushing, and flossing → consider dental disease, dry mouth, tonsil stones, reflux, or medication effects.
- White patches do not scrape off or the tongue is very red and sore → see a clinician or dentist.
- You are sick with a contagious mouth infection → clean the tool carefully and do not share it.

## Failure modes & recovery

- **F1 Gagging:** gag reflex starts before a pass finishes → move the scraper forward, breathe through the nose, and use shorter passes.
- **F2 Too much pressure:** tongue feels raw or scraped → stop for the day and use lighter pressure next time.
- **F3 Bleeding:** blood appears on the scraper → stop, rinse gently, and seek care if bleeding repeats.
- **F4 Tool contamination:** scraper falls on a dirty surface → wash with soap and water before reuse.
- **F5 Breath not improving:** odor returns quickly → add brushing, flossing, hydration, and dental evaluation if persistent.

## Verification

After scraping, the tongue has less visible coating, the scraper is rinsed clean, and there is no pain, bleeding, or raw feeling.

## Variations

- Plastic scraper: replace it when edges become rough or warped.
- Metal scraper: wash and dry it well to prevent residue buildup.
- Toothbrush backup: brush the tongue lightly from back to front if no scraper is available.
- Morning routine: use daily before or after brushing based on comfort.

## Safety & privacy

Low risk when gentle. Do not share tongue scrapers. See a dentist or clinician for persistent bad breath, painful tongue changes, unexplained bleeding, mouth sores lasting more than 2 weeks, white patches that do not wipe away, or trouble swallowing.
