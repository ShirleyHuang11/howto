## Goal

Lower body heat during hot weather and know when heat illness needs urgent help.

## Preconditions

- Access to drinking water or an electrolyte drink.
- Shade, air conditioning, fan, cool shower, or another cooler place nearby.
- A phone or nearby person if symptoms are severe or getting worse.

## Steps

1. **Move out of direct heat.** [BRANCH: outdoors | indoors] outdoors: go to shade, a cooled building, or an air-conditioned vehicle. indoors: move away from sun-facing windows and turn on a fan or AC. → *Expect:* sun is no longer on your skin and the air feels cooler or moving.
2. **Stop exertion.** Sit or lie down with feet slightly raised if lightheaded. → *Expect:* breathing and heart rate begin settling within a few minutes.
3. **Hydrate before thirst gets intense.** Sip water steadily; add electrolytes or a salty snack if you have sweated heavily. → *Expect:* you can drink without nausea and your mouth starts feeling less dry.
4. **Cool pulse points with water.** Put a wet cloth, wrapped ice pack, or cool bottle on the neck, wrists, armpits, or groin for 5 to 10 minutes. → *Expect:* skin feels cooler at the contact points and sweating may ease.
5. **Loosen and lighten clothing.** Remove hats, heavy layers, packs, and tight collars; switch to loose light clothes if available. → *Expect:* air reaches more skin and fabric no longer traps heat.
6. **Add active cooling.** [BRANCH: shower available | no shower] use a cool shower or sponge bath, or mist skin and sit by a fan. → *Expect:* body temperature and discomfort continue dropping.
7. **Check for heat-exhaustion red flags.** Watch for heavy sweating, weakness, dizziness, headache, nausea, cramps, fast pulse, or fainting. → *Expect:* symptoms are identified as mild, improving, or concerning.
8. **Check for heat-stroke red flags.** Call emergency services now if there is confusion, collapse, seizure, very hot skin, loss of consciousness, or symptoms that keep worsening after cooling. → *Expect:* emergency help is requested before the person is left alone.
9. **Keep cooling while waiting or recovering.** Continue wet cloths, shade or AC, and small sips if awake and not vomiting. → *Expect:* the person is monitored and not sent back into the heat.
10. **Avoid a quick rebound.** Stay in the cooler place for at least 30 minutes after feeling better. → *Expect:* dizziness, nausea, and overheating do not return when standing.

## Decision points

- Symptoms improve within 15 to 30 minutes → rest, rehydrate, and avoid more heat that day.
- Symptoms include confusion, collapse, seizure, or loss of consciousness → call emergency services immediately.
- Vomiting or unable to keep fluids down → treat as urgent because hydration by mouth may not work.
- No cool place is available → ask a nearby business, station, library, clinic, or neighbor for cooling space.

## Failure modes & recovery

- **F1 Drinking too fast:** nausea or stomach sloshing appears, recover by pausing 2 minutes and restarting with small sips.
- **F2 Cooling only the face:** face feels better but body stays overheated, recover by cooling neck, armpits, wrists, and groin.
- **F3 Returning too soon:** dizziness returns when walking, recover by sitting down, cooling again, and delaying exertion.
- **F4 Heat stroke missed:** confusion, collapse, seizure, or unconsciousness appears, recover by calling emergency services and cooling continuously.

## Verification

The person is in shade or AC, has cooled skin, can think and speak normally, can sip fluids, and has no worsening heat-stroke red flags.

## Variations

- Humid heat: sweat evaporates poorly, so use AC, cool showers, and wet cloths more aggressively.
- Older adult, infant, pregnancy, or chronic illness: use a lower threshold for medical help.
- Outdoor work or sports: schedule cooling breaks before symptoms start, not after collapse.

## Safety & privacy

High risk because heat stroke can cause organ damage or death. Do not give fluids to an unconscious, confused, or vomiting person. Share location and symptoms clearly when calling emergency services.
