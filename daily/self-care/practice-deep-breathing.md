## Goal

Your heart rate and sense of urgency drop measurably within a few minutes, using a breathing pattern matched to the situation: a physiological sigh for acute spikes, box breathing for sustained calm.

## Preconditions

- Anywhere you can breathe through your nose; no equipment, no seated posture required (both patterns work standing, walking, or mid-meeting).
- Not currently in a task that demands full attention on something dangerous (driving in traffic: sighs only, no counted patterns).
- Know your baseline: if you already feel lightheaded or short of breath for medical reasons, this recipe does not apply. See Safety.

## Steps

1. **Name the situation.** [BRANCH: acute spike (about to speak, just got bad news, flash of anger) → physiological sigh, steps 2-3 | sustained stress (racing mind, can't settle, pre-sleep) → box breathing, steps 4-6] → *Expect:* you have picked one pattern; do not improvise a hybrid.
2. **Physiological sigh: double inhale through the nose.** One normal inhale, then a second short sniff stacked on top to fully inflate the lungs. → *Expect:* a slight stretch at the top of the chest; the second sniff is small, not a full breath.
3. **Long slow exhale through the mouth.** Exhale until the lungs feel empty, longer than both inhales combined. Repeat 1-3 times total, then stop. → *Expect:* a noticeable drop in tension by the second repetition; more than 3 repetitions adds nothing.
4. **Box breathing: settle the count.** Inhale through the nose for a 4 count, hold 4, exhale 4, hold empty 4. Count in your head, not aloud. → *Expect:* the first box feels awkward; that is normal.
5. **Run boxes for 2-5 minutes.** Keep the exhale unhurried; if 4 feels strained, drop to a 3 count rather than gasping through 4. → *Expect:* by minute two, shoulders drop and the counting itself crowds out the racing thoughts.
6. **Exit gently.** Let breathing return to automatic; do not evaluate whether it "worked" mid-pattern. → *Expect:* breathing continues slow on its own for a while after you stop counting.

## Decision points

- Lightheaded or tingling fingers during either pattern → you are overbreathing; stop counting, breathe normally, resume later with smaller breaths (see F1).
- Need calm while appearing normal in public → physiological sighs are nearly invisible; box breathing with eyes open works but counting shows on some faces.
- Pattern needed nightly for sleep → box breathing works, but bias the exhale longer than the inhale (4 in, 6-8 out) which is more sedating than a symmetric box.
- Panic attack rather than ordinary stress → skip counting entirely; long exhales only, plus grounding (name 5 things you can see). Counted holds can feed the panic.

## Failure modes & recovery

- **F1 Dizziness or tingling:** classic overbreathing from too-deep inhales → stop the exercise, breathe normally and shallowly for a minute; when resuming, make every breath about 70% of maximum.
- **F2 Air hunger during holds:** the 4-count hold feels like suffocating → shorten all four sides to 3, or drop the empty hold entirely (4-4-4-0); the benefit survives.
- **F3 Chest-only breathing:** shoulders rise but belly stays still, so breaths stay shallow → place a hand on the abdomen and push it out on each inhale; the hand moving is the check.
- **F4 It becomes another performance:** you monitor whether you are calm yet, which keeps you activated → move attention fully onto the count or the sensation at the nostrils; calm is a side effect, not a target to watch.

## Verification

After the chosen pattern: exhales are audibly slower than before you started, shoulders are lower, and you can hold attention on one thing for ten seconds without it bouncing. If you wear a heart rate monitor, expect a drop of roughly 5-15 bpm from the stressed reading.

## Variations

- 4-7-8 breathing (inhale 4, hold 7, exhale 8): a popular pre-sleep alternative; same overbreathing cautions.
- Walking version: pace box breathing to steps (4 steps per side) instead of a mental count.

## Safety & privacy

Low risk. Two real cautions: never practice breath holds in or near water (shallow-water blackout kills), and people with cardiovascular or respiratory conditions, panic disorder, or pregnancy should skip long holds and use gentle extended exhales instead. No data or money involved.
