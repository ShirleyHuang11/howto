## Goal

Calm a minor bug bite, reduce itching and swelling, and recognize signs that need medical help.

## Preconditions

- The bite is minor, with no trouble breathing, facial swelling, or widespread hives.
- Soap, water, a clean cloth, cold pack, and anti-itch product are available.
- The person can avoid scratching or cover the bite if needed.

## Steps

1. **Move away from insects.** Leave the area or cover exposed skin before treating the bite. → *Expect:* no new bites are happening.
2. **Check for a stinger or tick.** [BRANCH: visible stinger | attached tick | simple bite] scrape a stinger away, remove a tick with fine tweezers, or continue for a simple bite. → *Expect:* no insect parts remain on the skin surface.
3. **Wash the bite.** Clean with soap and running water, then pat dry. → *Expect:* dirt, saliva, and surface irritants are removed.
4. **Apply a cold compress.** Wrap ice or a cold pack in cloth and hold it on for 10 minutes. → *Expect:* swelling and heat start to decrease.
5. **Use anti-itch treatment.** Apply hydrocortisone cream, calamine, or an oral antihistamine according to the label. → *Expect:* itching becomes less intense within the product's expected time.
6. **Avoid scratching.** Trim nails, cover the bite with a bandage, or tap around it instead of scratching. → *Expect:* the skin stays unbroken.
7. **Repeat comfort care.** Reapply cold compresses and labeled anti-itch treatment as needed. → *Expect:* symptoms remain manageable and localized.
8. **Watch for allergic red flags.** Look for trouble breathing, lip or tongue swelling, dizziness, vomiting, or hives away from the bite. ⚠️ *Irreversible:* severe allergic reactions can progress quickly; call emergency services immediately if these appear. → *Expect:* either no red flags are present or urgent help is contacted.
9. **Watch for infection.** Check over the next 24 to 48 hours for spreading redness, warmth, pus, fever, or worsening pain. → *Expect:* the bite improves or stays stable rather than spreading.
10. **Seek care when needed.** Contact a clinician for infection signs, a bite near the eye, a tick bite concern, or symptoms lasting more than a few days. → *Expect:* concerning cases are assessed instead of ignored.

## Decision points

- Person has known severe insect allergy → use prescribed epinephrine as directed and call emergency services.
- Bite is from a tick → save the tick if possible and follow local tick-borne disease guidance.
- Itching prevents sleep → ask a pharmacist or clinician about an oral antihistamine choice.

## Failure modes & recovery

- **F1 Scratched open:** detect broken or bleeding skin, recover by washing, applying a clean bandage, and monitoring for infection.
- **F2 Swelling grows fast:** detect swelling beyond the nearby area, recover by elevating if possible and seeking medical advice.
- **F3 Allergic reaction:** detect breathing trouble, facial swelling, dizziness, or widespread hives, recover by using prescribed emergency medication and calling emergency services.
- **F4 Infection signs:** detect pus, fever, warmth, or spreading redness, recover by contacting a clinician promptly.

## Verification

The bite is clean, itch is controlled without scratching, and no allergic-reaction or infection red flags are present.

## Variations

- `child`: use age-appropriate anti-itch medicine and avoid topical products not labeled for children.
- `outdoor-trip`: mark the bite edge with a pen so spreading redness is easier to detect.

## Safety & privacy

Medium risk because allergic reactions and infections can become urgent. Follow medicine labels, avoid using adult doses on children, and do not post identifiable bite photos when asking for informal advice.
