## Goal

Mild heartburn is relieved with posture, trigger control, and appropriate over-the-counter medicine while dangerous chest symptoms are recognized.

## Preconditions

- Heartburn often feels like burning behind the breastbone after meals or when lying down.
- Call emergency services for chest pressure, pain spreading to arm, jaw, back, or neck, shortness of breath, sweating, fainting, or nausea with chest pain.
- Seek medical care for trouble swallowing, vomiting blood, black stools, unexplained weight loss, persistent vomiting, severe abdominal pain, or symptoms more than twice weekly.
- Ask a clinician before frequent antacid use if pregnant, kidney disease, on blood thinners, or taking many medications.
- Have the medicine label available before taking any dose.

## Steps

1. **Sit upright.** Stay seated or stand and loosen tight waist clothing. → *Expect:* reflux pressure may ease within minutes.
2. **Avoid lying down.** Wait at least 2 to 3 hours after eating before lying flat. → *Expect:* stomach contents are less likely to reflux.
3. **Take an antacid if appropriate.** [BRANCH: occasional symptoms | frequent symptoms] use an antacid as labeled for occasional symptoms; contact a clinician for frequent or persistent symptoms. → *Expect:* burning improves within the expected label timeframe.
   - ⚠️ *Irreversible:* Do not assume new chest pain is heartburn when emergency heart symptoms are present.
4. **Sip water.** Drink small sips of water if tolerated. → *Expect:* throat irritation feels less intense.
5. **Avoid triggers today.** Skip large meals, alcohol, peppermint, chocolate, caffeine, acidic foods, spicy foods, and high-fat meals if they trigger symptoms. → *Expect:* symptoms are less likely to flare again.
6. **Raise the head for sleep.** If nighttime symptoms recur, elevate the head of the bed or use a wedge. → *Expect:* nighttime reflux is reduced without bending only at the waist.
7. **Review medicine timing.** Separate antacids from other medicines when the labels or pharmacist advise spacing. → *Expect:* interactions are avoided.
8. **Track recurrence.** Note food, timing, position, medicine used, and response. → *Expect:* patterns become clear for prevention or clinician review.
9. **Escalate persistent symptoms.** Call a clinician if symptoms last despite labeled medicine, occur often, or are new after age 40 to 50. → *Expect:* serious causes are considered.

## Decision points

- Chest pressure, radiating pain, shortness of breath, sweating, fainting, or severe weakness → call emergency services.
- Trouble swallowing, blood in vomit, black stools, weight loss, or persistent vomiting → seek medical care.
- Symptoms happen more than twice per week or require medicine often → schedule clinical evaluation.
- Symptoms started after a new medication → ask a pharmacist or clinician.
- Occasional mild burning after a known trigger → posture and labeled antacid may be enough.

## Failure modes & recovery

- **F1 Heart attack mistaken for reflux:** detect chest pressure, radiation, sweating, breathlessness, or fainting, recover by calling emergency services.
- **F2 Overusing antacids:** detect daily use or exceeding label dose, recover by stopping excess and calling a clinician.
- **F3 Lying down too soon:** detect symptoms after reclining, recover by sitting upright and delaying bedtime after meals.
- **F4 Interaction risk:** detect other medicines taken near antacid, recover by checking labels or asking a pharmacist.

## Verification

Burning improves with upright posture and labeled treatment, and no emergency chest-pain or gastrointestinal red flags are present.

## Variations

- Pregnancy: ask a clinician or pharmacist which medicines are appropriate.
- Nighttime reflux: use a wedge or bed elevation rather than stacking soft pillows.
- Frequent reflux: clinician-guided acid reducers may be needed instead of repeated antacids.

## Safety & privacy

Medium risk because dangerous heart, lung, or gastrointestinal problems can mimic heartburn. Call emergency services for chest pain with breathing trouble, sweating, fainting, or pain spreading beyond the chest.
