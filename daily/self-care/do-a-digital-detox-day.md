## Goal

One full day passes without social media, news feeds, or recreational screen time, while the phone stays usable for calls, maps, and emergencies, and the return to normal use the next day is deliberate rather than a binge.

## Preconditions

- A chosen day with no obligations that require being online (check the calendar first; a workday is a setup for failure).
- The people who might need you that day know how to reach you by call or SMS.
- At least two planned analog activities written down before the day starts.

## Steps

1. **Announce the day in advance.** Tell the household and one or two frequent contacts the day before: "Offline tomorrow, call if urgent." Set an auto-reply on work chat if the tool supports it. → *Expect:* nobody will interpret silence as an emergency; the social pressure now works for you instead of against you.
2. **Remove the apps, not the phone.** The evening before, delete or sign out of social, news, and video apps, and move any survivors off the home screen. Keep the phone itself: calls, SMS, maps, camera, and payment still work. Going fully phoneless fails the first time you need directions. → *Expect:* home screen shows only utility apps; muscle-memory taps land on nothing.
3. **Kill the notification pull.** Enable do-not-disturb with exceptions for calls from favorites. Notifications are the reacquisition mechanism; silence them and the phone becomes boring. → *Expect:* screen stays dark unless someone actually calls.
4. **Plan the analog day the night before.** Write two or three concrete activities with times: a long walk, cooking a real meal, a paper book, seeing someone in person. Unstructured empty time is what drives reinstalling by 10 a.m. → *Expect:* a short written plan exists on paper, not in a notes app.
5. **Start the day without the phone in the first hour.** Leave it charging in another room; use an alarm clock or the phone's alarm from across the room. The first hour sets the day's default. → *Expect:* breakfast and the first activity happen before the phone is touched at all.
6. **Ride out the itch with substitution, not willpower.** Each urge to check gets redirected to the written plan or a pocket notebook for capturing "must look up later" items. The urge peaks in the first half of the day and fades. → *Expect:* the later-list grows; actual screen time does not.
7. **Handle genuinely necessary lookups narrowly.** [BRANCH: a real need arises (directions, a reservation, a call) → do exactly that task and put the phone down | it can wait → write it on the later-list] → *Expect:* any phone use is under two minutes and single-purpose.
8. **Plan re-entry before bed.** Decide which apps return tomorrow and which stay deleted, and pick a first-check time (after breakfast, not on waking). Reinstall nothing tonight. → *Expect:* a written re-entry decision; the phone goes to its overnight spot outside the bedroom.
9. **Re-enter on your terms the next morning.** Reinstall only the apps you decided to keep, check messages once at the chosen time, and reply to what needs replying. → *Expect:* the backlog is smaller and less urgent than it felt; at least one app stays uninstalled.

## Decision points

- On-call for work or family caregiving that day → do not detox fully; keep the required channel open with its own loud ringtone and detox everything else.
- The urge to check becomes anxiety about a specific person or event → make a phone call about it; calls are allowed, feeds are not.
- Halfway through the day and it collapses (an hour of scrolling happened) → do not write off the day; restart at the next planned activity. Half a detox day still counts.

## Failure modes & recovery

- **F1 Reinstalled an app by reflex within hours:** app-store muscle memory → also sign out and turn off saved passwords for those accounts the night before, so reinstalling costs a login.
- **F2 Boredom turns into irritability by afternoon:** the plan was too thin → add a physical, hands-busy activity (cooking, cleaning, repair, exercise); idle hands are the relapse vector.
- **F3 Someone urgent could not reach you:** the announcement missed them → after the day, add them to the do-not-disturb exception list for next time; the fix is a better allowlist, not abandoning detox days.
- **F4 Next-day binge undoes the calm:** re-entry was unplanned → the first check must be time-boxed (15 minutes, timer set) and happen after a meal, never in bed on waking.

## Verification

The day ended with zero minutes on social, news, and video feeds; the phone was used only for calls, SMS, maps, camera, or payments; the later-list and re-entry decision exist on paper; and the next morning's first check happened at the planned time, not on waking.

## Variations

- Detox weekend: same structure, but announce a week ahead and plan one anchor activity per half-day; the second morning is markedly easier than the first.
- Family version: everyone's apps come off together and the analog plan is shared; a lone detoxer in a scrolling household relapses fast.

## Safety & privacy

Low risk. Keep calls reachable for genuine emergencies; never detox away a channel someone depends on for urgent contact (elder care, on-call duty). Announcing the day reveals only that you are offline; share the reason at your own discretion.
