## Goal

Five to ten minutes that take your body from resting to ready for the session ahead, raising temperature and rehearsing the movements, so the workout starts at minute one instead of minute fifteen.

## Preconditions

- A planned session whose main movements you can name (run, lifting day, sport, class), because the warm-up rehearses precisely those.
- Enough space to move, and the session's gear already on (`daily/self-care/pack-a-gym-bag` delivered).

## Steps

1. **Raise the pulse gently for 3 to 5 minutes.** Brisk walk, easy jog, cycling, rowing, or jumping jacks at conversational effort. The goal is warmth and light sweat onset, not fatigue. → *Expect:* breathing elevated but easy, body noticeably warmer.
2. **Move the joints through their ranges, dynamically.** Leg swings, arm circles, hip circles, torso rotations, ankle rolls: 8 to 10 controlled reps each, flowing rather than held. Static stretching (long holds) belongs after the session, not here. → *Expect:* joints moving freely through full range with no pinching.
3. **Rehearse the session's actual movements at low intensity.** Runners: drills and a few strides building speed. Lifters: the first exercise with an empty bar or light dumbbells for 1 to 2 sets. Sports: the sport's motions at half speed (easy rallies, light dribbling). → *Expect:* the exact patterns of the day grooved with light loads or speeds.
4. **Ramp, do not jump.** Bridge the last gap gradually: the lifter adds weight in steps toward the working sets; the runner's first kilometer is the slowest; the class's first round is deliberately easy. → *Expect:* working intensity reached through 2 or 3 rising steps.
5. **Do a readiness check before the real work.** Anything painful (sharp, joint-specific, one-sided) during the warm-up gets respected: modify the session around it rather than through it. General stiffness that faded is normal; pain that sharpened is information. → *Expect:* a green light, or a consciously modified plan.

## Decision points

- Short on time: shrink the warm-up, never delete it. Three minutes of steps 1 and 3 beat zero; the first working sets or kilometers become part of the ramp.
- Cold environments and mornings: double step 1. Bodies straight from bed or from winter need more temperature before ranges open up.
- Older, returning from injury, or high-intensity days planned: extend everything and lean on step 3; the more explosive the session, the longer the runway it needs.

## Failure modes & recovery

- **F1 Skipped it and the first sets felt awful or tweaked something:** stop escalating, drop the load, and effectively run the warm-up now (light ramping sets); assess the tweak honestly before continuing (`daily/self-care/treat-a-minor-cut`'s triage spirit applied to strains: sharp or worsening means stop).
- **F2 Warm-up so long it eats the workout:** cap it at ten minutes; a warm-up that fatigues is a workout with bad branding.
- **F3 Static-stretched cold muscles because it felt traditional:** move that habit to the cool-down where it helps; cold long holds do not reduce injury and can blunt power.
- **F4 The same niggle appears at every warm-up:** that is a pattern, not noise. It is the cheapest possible early warning; a professional look beats waiting for the session where it becomes a real injury.

## Verification

By the end you are warm, lightly sweating, joints moving freely, the day's specific movements have been rehearsed and ramped, and any pain signal was caught and acted on before working intensity, with the whole thing inside ten minutes.

## Variations

- Desk-to-exercise transitions (evening gym after sitting all day): add extra hip and upper-back work in step 2; chairs leave a signature.
- Pre-run version condenses to: 3 minutes brisk walk, leg swings, then run with the first kilometer as the ramp.

## Safety & privacy

Low risk; the warm-up is itself the safety mechanism for the riskier thing that follows. Its two jobs are temperature and rehearsal, and its one rule is that pain discovered here changes the plan.
