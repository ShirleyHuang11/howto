## Goal

Ten minutes of seated breath-focused meditation are completed: a settled posture held, attention repeatedly returned to the breath, the session ended by a timer rather than restlessness.

## Preconditions

- A spot where you can sit undisturbed for ten minutes: a chair, cushion, or floor.
- A timer with a gentle end sound (phone timer is fine; put the phone on Do Not Disturb so notifications do not become the distraction).
- No requirement to "clear your mind" or feel calm; those are common misconceptions, not the task.

## Steps

1. **Set the timer and remove interruptions.** Ten minutes, gentle chime, Do Not Disturb on. → *Expect:* countdown running; no notifications will fire.
2. **Take the posture.** Sit with hips slightly above knees (sit on the front edge of a chair or a folded blanket), spine tall but not rigid, shoulders dropped, hands resting on thighs. Imagine a string lifting the crown of your head. → *Expect:* upright and stable, able to breathe fully, no strain in the low back.
3. **Settle the eyes and take three anchoring breaths.** Close the eyes, or soften the gaze downward. Breathe in slowly through the nose, out slowly, three times, letting the body settle a little more on each exhale. → *Expect:* a small drop in shoulder and jaw tension.
4. **Choose one breath anchor and stay with it.** Pick a single point where you feel the breath most clearly: the nostrils, the chest, or the belly rising and falling. Let the breath be natural, not controlled; just feel it at that one place. → *Expect:* attention resting on the sensation of one breath, then the next.
5. **Notice when the mind wanders, and return.** It will wander into thoughts, plans, sounds, within seconds. Noticing that you have wandered is not failure; it is the whole exercise. The moment of noticing is one repetition, like a rep in the gym. → *Expect:* you catch yourself mid-thought and gently move attention back to the anchor, without commentary or self-criticism.
6. **Repeat the return for the full ten minutes.** Wander, notice, return, hundreds of times if needed. Do not count how many times, do not grade the session by how "calm" it felt. Label a persistent pull ("thinking", "hearing") softly and come back. → *Expect:* a repeating cycle of drifting and returning; each return is the work being done.
7. **End at the chime, not before.** When the timer sounds, keep the eyes closed for a few breaths, notice how the body and mind feel now versus at the start, then open the eyes and stretch gently before standing. → *Expect:* session closed deliberately; a brief pause before re-entering activity.

## Decision points

- Strong drowsiness → open the eyes to a soft downward gaze, straighten the spine, or stand and do it upright; sleepiness usually means posture collapsed or you are under-rested.
- Physical pain (not mild discomfort) → adjust position; meditation is not an endurance test, and shifting mindfully is allowed.
- Restlessness spikes and ten minutes feels impossible → do not quit early; drop to focusing only on the very next single breath, then the next.

## Failure modes & recovery

- **F1 "I can't stop thinking, so I'm bad at this":** you judge a wandering mind as failure → reframe; a wandering mind is the raw material, and each noticing-and-return is the exact skill being trained. Everyone's mind wanders constantly.
- **F2 Turning it into breath control:** you feel lightheaded or breathing gets forced → you are steering the breath instead of observing it; let it go fully natural, even if that means shallow.
- **F3 Timer anxiety (peeking to check time):** you keep wondering how long is left → the timer's job is to track time so yours is not; trust it, that is why it exists.
- **F4 Never getting started:** you keep waiting for a calm moment or the "right" setup → sit anyway; a distracted ten minutes still counts, and the perfect conditions never arrive.
- **F5 Rare strong distress surfaces:** difficult emotions or memories arise and intensify → open the eyes, ground in the room and your feet on the floor, and stop; for a trauma history, guided or trauma-informed practice is safer than silent solo sitting.

## Verification

The timer ran the full ten minutes and ended the session; you maintained an upright seated posture; you noticed wandering and returned to the breath anchor repeatedly (many times is normal and good); you did not end early out of restlessness or grade the session by how calm it felt.

## Variations

- Guided track: a ten-minute guided audio replaces silent self-direction; useful for the first weeks while the return habit forms.
- Body-scan anchor: instead of the breath, move attention slowly through the body head to toe; same wander-and-return mechanic.
- Walking version: for restless days, walk slowly and anchor on the sensation of the feet contacting the ground.
- Longer sits: the exact method scales to 20+ minutes; extend the timer, keep everything else identical.

## Safety & privacy

Low risk. Do not meditate while driving or operating anything requiring attention. If you have a trauma history or a mental-health condition, intensive or silent practice can occasionally surface difficult material; start short, keep the eyes-open grounding option ready, and consider a trauma-informed teacher or clinician rather than practicing through acute distress.
