## Goal

Once per work hour, a short neck-shoulders-hips sequence is completed at the desk, leaving muscles noticeably looser and posture reset, with no strain from overstretching.

## Preconditions

- A timer or calendar that can fire an hourly reminder (phone, watch, or OS focus timer).
- Enough floor space to stand and take a half step back from the chair.
- Clothing that allows raising the arms overhead; no acute neck or back injury (if injured, follow medical guidance instead of this recipe).

## Steps

1. **Set the hourly trigger once, at the start of the day.** A repeating alarm labeled "stretch" beats intending to remember; snoozing is allowed, skipping twice in a row is the failure signal. → *Expect:* a recurring reminder exists for every working hour.
2. **When it fires, stand up and step away from the chair.** Standing is half the benefit; the sequence done seated is the fallback, not the default. → *Expect:* standing, weight even on both feet, screen out of reach.
3. **Neck: slow half-circles and side holds.** Drop chin to chest, roll the ear toward one shoulder, hold 15 seconds, roll to the other side, hold 15 seconds. Move only through the front half circle; never roll the head backward in a full circle. → *Expect:* a mild pull along the side of the neck, no pinching or tingling.
4. **Shoulders: rolls, then a chest opener.** Five slow shoulder rolls backward, then clasp hands behind the back, straighten the arms, and lift the knuckles an inch while squeezing the shoulder blades. Hold 20 seconds, breathing normally. → *Expect:* the front of the shoulders and chest feel a gentle stretch; shoulders sit lower than before.
5. **Hips: standing hip-flexor lunge or seated figure-4.** [BRANCH: space and privacy allow → half-kneel or split-stance lunge, back leg's hip pressed gently forward, 20 seconds per side | open-plan office → seated, ankle over opposite knee, hinge forward from the hips, 20 seconds per side] → *Expect:* stretch felt in the front of the hip (lunge) or outer hip and glute (figure-4).
6. **Hold everything static; never bounce.** Each position is a steady hold at mild tension, roughly 3 of 10 discomfort. Bouncing (ballistic stretching) triggers the muscle's protective contraction and is how desk stretchers strain themselves. → *Expect:* tension eases slightly during each hold instead of building.
7. **Reset posture and sit back down.** Roll shoulders back once, sit with hips against the chair back, screen at eye height. → *Expect:* sitting feels noticeably different for the first minute; timer still armed for the next hour.

## Decision points

- Reminder fires mid-meeting or mid-flow → do the 30-second seated subset (neck holds plus shoulder rolls) now and the full sequence at the next natural break; do not silently dismiss.
- Sharp pain, numbness, or tingling in any position → stop that stretch entirely for the day; persistent symptoms mean a clinician, not a deeper stretch.
- Standing desk user → the hip work still matters; standing all day shortens calves instead, so add a 20-second calf stretch against the desk edge.

## Failure modes & recovery

- **F1 Reminders get ignored within two days:** the trigger is invisible or embarrassing → tie it to an existing anchor instead (every coffee refill, every meeting end) and make the first move trivially small (just stand up).
- **F2 Soreness the next day:** stretches were held too deep or bounced → drop intensity to a barely-there pull and keep holds under 30 seconds; mild is effective.
- **F3 Neck feels worse after stretching:** almost always the backward head roll or an overdeep side hold → keep the chin slightly tucked, front half-circle only.
- **F4 Sequence takes so long it gets skipped:** trim to one item per body region, 90 seconds total; a short sequence done hourly beats a long one done never.

## Verification

At the end of the workday: the hourly reminder fired and was answered (full or fallback version) at least 5 of 8 hours, each stretch was a static hold with no bouncing, and there is no new pain in neck, shoulders, or hips.

## Variations

- Home office: swap the seated figure-4 for a floor-based hip-flexor half-kneel and add a doorway chest stretch, both easier without an audience.
- Long-haul flights and trains: the same seated subset (neck holds, shoulder rolls, seated figure-4) works in an economy seat, once per hour awake.

## Safety & privacy

Low risk. The two rules that prevent injury: no bouncing in any hold, and no full backward neck circles. Nothing here exposes personal data; the only social cost is being seen stretching, which the seated subset solves.
