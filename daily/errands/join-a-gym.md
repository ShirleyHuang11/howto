## Goal

You hold an active gym membership whose real monthly cost, commitment length, and exact cancellation procedure you understood before signing.

## Preconditions

- A shortlist of gyms within a realistic commute (the best gym you won't travel to loses to the adequate one nearby).
- Photo ID and a payment method; some gyms also want proof of address for direct-debit setup.
- A clear idea of what you actually need: free weights, classes, pool, or just cardio machines.

## Steps

1. **Visit at the hour you will actually train.** A gym at 2pm tells you nothing about the 6pm squat-rack queue. Walk in and ask for a tour; any decent gym gives one free. → *Expect:* a tour granted during your real workout window.
2. **Run the tour checklist.** Count people waiting for the equipment you care about; check machines for out-of-order tags and frayed cables; inspect locker rooms and showers for cleanliness; note ventilation and whether staff are on the floor. → *Expect:* mental notes on crowding, maintenance, and hygiene; more than one or two dead machines is a maintenance-budget signal.
3. **Ask for a trial pass before any contract talk.** [BRANCH: free day/week pass offered → train once at your normal hour before deciding | no trial at all → treat as a yellow flag and raise your contract scrutiny] → *Expect:* either a trial workout completed or a conscious decision to proceed without one.
4. **Get the full price in writing, itemized.** Monthly fee, joining/initiation fee, any "annual maintenance fee" (the classic hidden charge, often billed in month 3), and class or towel add-ons. → *Expect:* a single total figure for year-one cost, not just the headline monthly rate.
5. **Read the cancellation clause before anything else in the contract.** How many days' notice, from what date, delivered how (many chains still require certified mail or an in-person form, not email), and what an early exit from a fixed term costs. → *Expect:* you can state the exact steps and cost of leaving; if the rep cannot show you the clause, do not sign today.
6. **Check the other standard traps.** Auto-renewal into a new fixed term versus rolling monthly; price-increase clauses; whether "freeze" (pause) options exist for travel or injury; transfer rules if you move. → *Expect:* each answered from the contract text, not from the rep's verbal assurances.
7. **Negotiate before signing.** Initiation fees are waived routinely near month-end when reps chase quotas; ask directly. Month-to-month at a slightly higher rate usually beats a 12-month commitment unless you have trained consistently for a year already. → *Expect:* at least one concession, or confirmation the price is genuinely fixed.
8. **Sign, keep the counter-signed copy, and confirm billing.** ⚠️ *Irreversible:* a fixed-term direct-debit agreement commits real money; verify the amount and the term on the paper matches the verbal offer before signing. → *Expect:* your own copy of the contract, membership card or app access active, and first billing date noted in your calendar.

## Decision points

- New to training → month-to-month first, even at a premium; most lapsed memberships were 12-month contracts signed in January.
- The rep pressures with "today-only pricing" → the discount almost always survives a day; walking out is the strongest negotiating move you have.
- Two comparable gyms → pick the closer one; adherence beats amenities.

## Failure modes & recovery

- **F1 Surprise annual fee billed:** an unexpected charge appears months in → it was in the contract's fee schedule; check your copy, and if it genuinely was not disclosed, dispute with the gym in writing and then your bank.
- **F2 Cancellation letter "never received":** billing continues after you cancel → this is why notice goes by certified mail or an in-person form with a stamped copy; resend with proof of delivery and dispute charges after the notice period with your bank.
- **F3 Auto-renewed into a new term:** the contract renewed for another fixed year → calendar the renewal notice window (often 30-60 days before term end) on the day you sign, not later.
- **F4 Gym closes or changes hands:** access stops but billing does not → membership debts usually transfer to the new owner; cancel the payment mandate at your bank and keep the closure notice as evidence.

## Verification

You can enter the gym with your card or app, your contract copy states the same monthly price and term you agreed verbally, the exact cancellation procedure is written in your notes or calendar, and the first billing matches the itemized total from step 4.

## Variations

- Budget chains: cheap and contract-light but often no classes, no pool, and kiosk-only support; cancellation is frequently app-based and easier.
- Council/community and university gyms: usually the fairest contracts (monthly, easy exit) and fine equipment; check public access hours.
- Boutique studios (climbing, CrossFit, spin): class-pack pricing avoids contracts entirely; a fair first test of whether you will actually attend.

## Safety & privacy

Medium risk, financial rather than physical: the money is at stake in auto-renewals, hidden annual fees, and hard-to-execute cancellation terms, so keep the counter-signed contract and proof of any cancellation notice. Gyms collect ID, banking mandates, and sometimes biometric entry data (fingerprint or face scan); you can usually decline biometrics and use a card instead.
