## Goal

Your document carries a valid notarial certificate (stamp/seal and the notary's signature), executed correctly for the certificate type the receiving party requires, and it is accepted on submission.

## Preconditions

- The complete, final document: every page present, no blank fields (a notary can refuse a document with blanks, and blanks invite later tampering).
- Valid, unexpired government photo ID for every person who must sign, with a name matching the name on the document.
- You know which act the receiving party wants: an acknowledgment (you confirm you signed it) or a jurat (you swear to the contents and sign in front of the notary). When in doubt, ask the receiving party, not the notary; notaries cannot choose for you in many jurisdictions.

## Steps

1. **Confirm the document actually needs notarization and which type.** Read the receiving party's instructions or the document's pre-printed certificate wording. Many forms only need a witness or a certified copy, which are different services. → *Expect:* you can name the required act (acknowledgment, jurat/affidavit, certified copy, or signature witnessing).
2. **Do not sign the document yet.** [BRANCH: jurat required → the signature must happen in the notary's presence, full stop | acknowledgment required → pre-signing is technically allowed in many places, but signing in front of the notary is always safe] The universal rule: arrive with it unsigned. → *Expect:* signature lines still blank when you leave home.
3. **Find a notary and confirm scope before traveling.** Options: your bank (often free for account holders), shipping/office stores, courthouses and law offices, mobile notaries (they come to you, at a travel premium), or remote online notarization where the receiving party accepts it. Call ahead: confirm they can handle your document type, all signers can attend, and the fee. → *Expect:* an appointment or confirmed walk-in window, quoted fee, and confirmation they will notarize this document class (some refuse wills or immigration forms).
4. **Bring every required signer, their IDs, and the whole document.** All signers appear in person with unexpired photo ID; bring all pages, not just the signature page, since the notary may need to see what is being signed. → *Expect:* everyone present, IDs in hand, complete document on the table.
5. **Present ID and sign exactly as the document names you.** The notary checks each ID against the document. Sign in the notary's presence, matching the printed name form (middle initial included if the document prints one). → *Expect:* signatures placed while the notary watches; names on ID, document, and signature consistent.
6. **The notary completes the certificate, seals, and records the act.** They fill the certificate block (venue, date, names, act type), sign, apply the stamp or embossed seal, and log it in their journal. Verify the certificate wording matches what the receiving party specified and that the date and names are correct before you leave. ⚠️ *Irreversible:* an executed and submitted notarization cannot be quietly amended; errors caught after submission mean redoing the document. → *Expect:* legible seal, notary signature, correct date and names, and the certificate type you needed.
7. **Pay and take copies.** Fees are commonly per signature or per seal and are capped by law in many jurisdictions; mobile travel fees are separate. Photograph or scan the executed document before submitting the original. → *Expect:* receipt, plus a scan of the sealed document in your files.

## Decision points

- The document has no pre-printed certificate wording → the notary can attach a loose certificate, but you must tell them which type; if you cannot find out, contact the receiving party before the appointment.
- Document is going to another country → you may need an apostille or consular legalization on top of the notarization; that is a separate government-office step after the notary, so plan the extra days.
- A signer cannot appear in person → remote online notarization (video, where accepted) or a mobile notary at their location; never sign on someone's behalf without formal authority.

## Failure modes & recovery

- **F1 ID rejected:** expired ID, or the name does not match the document (maiden vs. married name) → bring an unexpired ID; for name mismatches ask the receiving party whether an "also known as" form or a corrected document is required before rebooking.
- **F2 You signed it at home before a jurat:** the notary cannot notarize an already-signed jurat → print a fresh copy and sign again in their presence; this is the single most common wasted trip.
- **F3 Notary refuses the document:** blanks in the document, a signer who seems unwilling or confused, or a document class they will not handle → fill every blank (or strike through with "N/A"), and find a specialist (attorney-notary) for refused classes.
- **F4 Receiving party rejects the notarization:** wrong certificate type or missing commission-expiry text → return to the same notary with the exact required wording from the receiving party; correcting a certificate typically costs little or nothing if it was the notary's error.

## Verification

The document bears a legible notary seal and signature, a fully completed certificate block of the type the receiving party specified, dates and all names are correct and consistent with the IDs shown, every signer signed in the required manner, and you hold a scan plus the fee receipt.

## Variations

- Bank branches: often free for customers but commonly refuse high-liability documents (wills, trusts, real-estate deeds); call first.
- Remote online notarization: video session with an online-commissioned notary, ID verified digitally; check the receiving party accepts it before paying, as some courts and foreign authorities do not.
- Civil-law countries: "notaries" are trained legal professionals who draft and authenticate documents; the quick-stamp errand described here maps to a different, longer appointment there.

## Safety & privacy

Medium risk: the document and your ID details enter the notary's journal (a legal record; this is normal and protective), and notarized documents often carry high stakes (property, powers of attorney, sworn statements). Never sign anything you have not read in full, never leave blanks, and treat a notarization request on a document you do not understand as a reason to consult a lawyer first, not a formality.
