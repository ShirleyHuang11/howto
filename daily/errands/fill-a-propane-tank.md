## Goal

Get a propane cylinder safely refilled or exchanged, then transport it home upright without leaks.

## Preconditions

- You have a refillable propane cylinder within its stamped certification date.
- The cylinder valve is closed and the cap or plug is available if fitted.
- You can travel to a propane refill station, hardware store, gas station, or exchange cage.
- The vehicle has a ventilated passenger or cargo area where the tank can stand upright.

## Steps

1. **Inspect the cylinder.** Check for rust, dents, bulges, damaged threads, an expired date stamp, or a missing collar. → *Expect:* the tank looks intact and the date is acceptable to the attendant.
2. **Choose refill or exchange.** [BRANCH: refill | exchange] refill if your tank is in good shape and you want to keep it, or exchange if it is expired, nearly empty, or inconvenient to refill. → *Expect:* you know which counter or cage to use.
3. **Keep ignition sources away.** Turn off cigarettes, grills, heaters, and vehicle engines near the filling area. ⚠️ *Irreversible:* propane can ignite explosively, so do not create flames or sparks around the tank. → *Expect:* the area around the cylinder has no open flame.
4. **Hand the tank to staff.** Let trained staff fill, weigh, or swap the cylinder instead of opening fill valves yourself. → *Expect:* the attendant controls the filling equipment or unlocks the exchange cage.
5. **Confirm the fill limit.** Know that portable cylinders are filled to about 80 percent liquid capacity so vapor has expansion space. → *Expect:* the tank is weighed or filled by an overfill prevention device, not topped to full liquid volume.
6. **Check the valve and leak screen.** Make sure the valve is closed, the outlet is capped if provided, and no hissing or propane smell is present. → *Expect:* the cylinder is quiet and odor-free at the valve.
7. **Pay and get the receipt.** Confirm the price, any exchange deposit, and the cylinder size before paying. → *Expect:* the receipt matches the service and tank size.
8. **Transport upright and ventilated.** Place the tank upright, secure it from tipping, open a window if inside the cabin, and drive directly home. → *Expect:* the cylinder stays vertical and does not roll or heat up.

## Decision points

- The tank is expired or badly rusted → choose exchange or ask where to recertify it.
- You smell propane at any point → move away from ignition sources and ask staff to inspect it.
- The attendant tries to overfill by feel only → ask for weighing or a proper fill-stop method.
- You only need barbecue fuel quickly → exchange may cost more per pound but avoids waiting.
- You need a specific tank you own, such as an RV bottle → use a refill station.

## Failure modes & recovery

- **F1 Rejected cylinder:** detect by staff refusing to fill because of age or damage, recover by exchanging it or getting a certified replacement.
- **F2 Valve leak:** detect by hissing, frost, bubbles from leak solution, or propane odor, recover by keeping it outside and asking staff to bleed or replace it.
- **F3 Tank tips in vehicle:** detect by rolling or clanking during transport, recover by stopping safely and wedging it upright.
- **F4 Wrong size exchange:** detect by a cage cylinder that does not match your appliance connection, recover before leaving by swapping it at the counter.

## Verification

The cylinder is upright, valve closed, leak-free by smell and sound, and ready to connect only when outdoors at the appliance.

## Variations

- Exchange cage: the store may require paying inside before the cage is unlocked.
- Refill station: staff may weigh the tank before and after filling to charge by volume or flat rate.
- RV or forklift cylinder: orientation and valve type may differ, so follow the cylinder label.

## Safety & privacy

Propane is flammable gas under pressure. Keep cylinders upright, ventilated, away from heat, and never in a closed trunk or near flames.
