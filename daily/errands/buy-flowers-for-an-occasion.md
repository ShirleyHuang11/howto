## Goal

You hand over (or have delivered) a bouquet that fits the occasion and the recipient's culture, arrives fresh, and comes with what the recipient needs to keep it alive.

## Preconditions

- You know the occasion, the recipient's rough cultural background, and where the flowers must be at what time.
- A florist, supermarket flower stand, or delivery service reachable in time; for peak dates (Valentine's Day, Mother's Day) this means ordering days ahead.
- Awareness of hard constraints: recipient allergies, hospital ward rules (many ICUs and oncology wards ban flowers), pets at home (lilies are lethally toxic to cats).

## Steps

1. **Decode the occasion.** Common codes: red roses read as romance almost everywhere (avoid them for colleagues); white lilies and white chrysanthemums are funeral flowers in much of Europe and East Asia; yellow roses mean friendship in some cultures and jealousy or infidelity in others; bright mixed seasonal bouquets are the safe congratulations/birthday default. When unsure, tell the florist the occasion and relationship and let them steer. → *Expect:* a flower-and-color direction that cannot be misread as romantic, funereal, or accusatory.
2. **Check the number rules.** Stem-count customs are real: in Russia and much of Eastern Europe, even counts are strictly for funerals, so give odd numbers; in parts of East Asia the number 4 is avoided (homophone of death) and 8 is favored; a dozen red roses is the Western romantic standard. When the recipient's culture is unknown, an odd count under ten offends nobody. → *Expect:* a stem count that is deliberate, not accidental.
3. **Choose the source.** [BRANCH: hand delivery today → local florist (best quality and advice) or supermarket (cheaper, fine for casual occasions) | remote recipient → a delivery service or, better, phone a florist local to the recipient directly, which skips relay markups | peak date → order 3+ days ahead and expect surge pricing on roses] → *Expect:* a source that can meet the deadline at your budget.
4. **Select for freshness, not fullest bloom.** Buy blooms one-third to half open: fully open flowers are at peak now and fade in days. Freshness tells: firm upright heads, green (not slimy or brown) stem ends, no translucent or dropping petals, buds with color showing. Sniff the water in the bucket; sour means old stock. → *Expect:* a bouquet that looks slightly "young", which is correct.
5. **Have it finished for the occasion.** Ask for a hand-tied wrap for casual gifting, an arrangement in wet foam or a vase if the recipient will not have one (hospitals, offices), and a written card; dictate the message and check the spelling of names. → *Expect:* wrap or vessel matched to where the flowers will sit, card attached and correct.
6. **Attach or request the care card.** ⚠️ *Irreversible:* skipped care halves vase life and cannot be recovered. The card (most florists include one, or write it yourself): recut stems 2 cm at an angle under running water, use the food sachet in clean water, refresh water every 2 days, keep away from direct sun, heaters, and the fruit bowl (ripening fruit emits ethylene, which ages flowers fast). → *Expect:* care instructions and a food sachet physically with the bouquet.
7. **Transport flat and cool, deliver on time.** Lay the bouquet flat in the car, out of sun; in hot weather go directly, no errands en route. For services, confirm the delivery window lands when someone is there to receive. → *Expect:* flowers handed over unwilted, at the planned moment.

## Decision points

- Funeral or condolence → whites and greens, restrained arrangement, and check the family's wishes first: many obituaries specify "no flowers" or a charity donation instead, and some traditions (for example Jewish funerals) do not use flowers at all.
- Recipient in hospital → call the ward before ordering; if banned, send to their home for discharge day instead.
- Recipient has cats → no lilies of any kind, including in mixed bouquets; every part of a true lily is toxic to cats. Say "no lilies, cat household" to the florist explicitly.
- Budget tight → fewer stems of one good flower beats a thin mixed bouquet; a generous bunch of single-variety tulips or sunflowers reads as intentional, not cheap.

## Failure modes & recovery

- **F1 Wilted on arrival (delivery):** photograph immediately in the packaging and contact the service the same day; reputable ones redeliver free, but only against day-of photos.
- **F2 Wrong cultural read discovered late:** white bouquet en route to an East Asian birthday → call the florist to swap ribbon and add warm-colored stems before dispatch; after handover, a light apology and no repeat beats drawing attention to it.
- **F3 Peak-date sellout:** roses gone before Valentine's → pivot variety, not occasion: red tulips or ranunculus carry the same message and are often fresher than the last picked-over roses.
- **F4 Card mix-up:** delivery services transcribe; a wrong or missing name on the card is the most common complaint → always have the message read back or shown on screen before paying.

## Verification

The bouquet reached the recipient on time and unwilted, its flowers, colors, and stem count fit the occasion and culture (or were florist-vetted for it), the card carries the intended message with names spelled right, and the food sachet plus care instructions traveled with it.

## Variations

- East Asia: chrysanthemums are for graves and funerals regardless of color for many families; avoid entirely for celebrations.
- Russia and Eastern Europe: odd stem counts only for the living; this rule is strong enough that florists count aloud.
- Middle East and conservative contexts: flowers from an unrelated man to a woman can itself carry meaning; sending in a group's name defuses it.

## Safety & privacy

Money risk is peak-date surge pricing and relay-service markups (order early, call local). Privacy: the card is read by everyone who handles and sees the bouquet, so keep intimate messages inside a sealed envelope. Physical: lilies and cats do not mix, and pollen stains; florists can strip lily anthers on request.
