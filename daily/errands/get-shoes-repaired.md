## Goal

Your shoes are assessed, left with a cobbler under an agreed price and pickup date, and come back wearable for another season, or you have made an informed decision to replace them instead.

## Preconditions

- The shoes, reasonably clean and dry (cobblers can refuse or surcharge mud-caked or wet footwear, and dirt hides the damage they need to see).
- A cobbler / shoe repair shop located; look for one attached to a dry cleaner, transit station, or old shopping arcade if no standalone shop exists.
- A rough idea of the shoes' replacement price, for the repair-versus-replace call.

## Steps

1. **Triage repairability at home.** Repair-friendly: leather or suede uppers, stitched (welted) soles, worn heel tips, broken zippers on boots, stretched or loose stitching, detached but intact soles. Repair-hostile: crumbling foam midsoles (hydrolyzed PU turns to powder with age), fast-fashion shoes where glue is the only thing holding them together, knit sneaker uppers torn at the flex point. → *Expect:* a genuine candidate, or an early honest decision to replace.
2. **Do the cost math before you go.** Rules of thumb: heel tips are cheap and always worth it; half-soles and heels cost a mid-double-digit amount; a full resole on welted shoes can approach half the price of new. Repair is usually rational when the uppers are sound and the quote lands under about half of replacement cost. → *Expect:* a walk-away price in your head before anyone quotes you.
3. **Brief the cobbler specifically.** Point at each problem and say what outcome you want: "new heel tips", "restitch this seam", "resole, keep the leather sole" (or "switch to rubber"). Also ask what *they* see; a good cobbler will flag the failing counter or dried-out sole you missed. → *Expect:* a per-item quote and their read on whether the shoe is worth it. A cobbler who says "not worth repairing" is giving you free, reliable advice.
4. **Agree the full terms before handing them over.** Price, pickup date, and any materials choice (leather vs rubber sole, hardware color). [BRANCH: quote under your walk-away price → proceed | over it → thank them and take the shoes; no partial commitments] → *Expect:* one number, one date, no "we'll see when we open it up" without a cap.
5. **Get the claim ticket.** ⚠️ *Irreversible:* many shops release shoes only against the physical ticket, and unclaimed pairs are disposed of or donated after a posted period (often 30 to 90 days). Photograph the ticket immediately. → *Expect:* ticket in hand and photographed, pickup date written on it.
6. **Pick up on time and inspect at the counter.** Check the specific repairs, flex the sole, verify heel tips are seated flush, look inside for stray nails on heel work. Raise anything before paying, while the shoes are still their responsibility. → *Expect:* the briefed items visibly fixed; pairs match in color after any refinishing.
7. **Pay and break them back in gently.** Resoled shoes are stiff for the first few wears; wear them dry days first. → *Expect:* payment done, shoes home, first wear planned indoors or on a short day.

## Decision points

- Sentimental or discontinued shoes → the cost math changes; a repair above half of replacement can still be right when the shoe cannot be rebought.
- Sneakers → most cobblers will not resole cemented athletic soles, but sneaker-specific services exist for premium pairs; ordinary gym shoes with worn midsoles should be replaced (cushioning failure is invisible and injures joints).
- Only the heel tips are gone → this is a while-you-wait job at many shops; ask, and keep wearing exposed metal heel pins no further (they wreck the heel block, turning a cheap fix into an expensive one).
- Structural leather damage (torn upper at a seam, ripped eyelet) → repairable; a hole mid-panel in the leather usually is not, only patchable.

## Failure modes & recovery

- **F1 Scope creep at pickup ("we also had to..."):** unagreed extra charges → point to the quoted number from step 4; reputable shops call before doing extra work, and you should pay only what was agreed unless they did call.
- **F2 Repair fails within weeks:** a sole edge lifting or heel tip lost → return with the receipt; nearly all cobblers redo their own recent work free, but only if you come back promptly rather than months later.
- **F3 Lost claim ticket:** bring ID and the ticket photo, describe the shoes, and expect to sign a release; this is exactly what the step 5 photograph is for.
- **F4 Shop misses the date:** call before traveling on pickup day; heel bars run same-day but resoles queue behind material orders, and a courtesy call saves the wasted trip.

## Verification

The specific faults you briefed are fixed and pass a counter inspection (flush heel tips, continuous sole bond, matching finish), you paid the pre-agreed price, and the shoes survive their first full day of wear without the repair lifting.

## Variations

- No local cobbler: mail-in resole services handle welted shoes and premium boots well; the tradeoff is weeks of turnaround and shipping both ways.
- Preventive track: putting rubber sole guards and new heel tips on quality shoes when nearly new roughly doubles sole life for a small fraction of resole cost.

## Safety & privacy

Low risk. Money at stake is the repair fee, protected by agreeing price and date up front and keeping the ticket. The one physical note: do not keep running on athletic shoes with collapsed midsoles while deciding; that is the injury path.
