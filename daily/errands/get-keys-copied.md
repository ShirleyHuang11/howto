## Goal

You leave the shop with a working duplicate key that has been proven in the actual lock it is meant to open.

## Preconditions

- The original key, on hand and working (a copy of a copy compounds cutting errors; always duplicate from the original if one exists).
- A hardware store, locksmith, or self-service kiosk within reach, plus a few dollars per key.
- Knowledge of, or access to, the lock the copy must open, for the test in step 6.

## Steps

1. **Identify what kind of key you have.** [BRANCH: standard house/padlock key (plain brass/nickel, no electronics) → any hardware store or kiosk | car key with a plastic head → likely contains a transponder chip; a plain metal copy will turn the door but not start the engine, see Decision points | key stamped "DO NOT DUPLICATE" or a patented/restricted profile → locksmith with your authorization, see Decision points | USPS cluster mailbox key → cannot be legally duplicated; replacements go through the postal service] → *Expect:* you know which counter to walk to before leaving home.
2. **Go to the cutting station and hand over the original.** At a hardware store, ask staff; at a kiosk (MinuteKey, KeyMe and similar), insert the key as prompted. → *Expect:* the machine or clerk finds a matching blank; "we don't have that blank" means a locksmith trip instead, not a "close enough" blank.
3. **Choose the blank.** Plain brass is fine; decorated blanks work identically. If offered a thickness or headshape choice, match the original. → *Expect:* blank and original side by side with identical profile (the ridged silhouette along the blade).
4. **Have it cut.** Duplication takes about a minute per key: the original guides a stylus while a wheel cuts the blank. → *Expect:* a freshly cut key, warm, with visible burrs on the teeth.
5. **Ask for deburring.** Most cutters brush the key automatically; if the teeth feel sharp or gritty, ask for a pass on the wire wheel. Burrs are the top cause of a new key sticking. → *Expect:* teeth smooth to the thumb.
6. **Pay, then test immediately in the real lock.** ⚠️ *Irreversible:* discovering a bad copy at your door at midnight is the failure this whole recipe prevents; do not skip the test. Insert fully, turn both directions, repeat three times, and also re-test the original. [BRANCH: lock is at home → test the same day before you need the key | lock is nearby → test before leaving the area] → *Expect:* the copy turns as smoothly as the original.
7. **Label and distribute.** Mark the copy (tag or paint pen, never the address it opens) and hand it to its intended holder. → *Expect:* copy identifiable in a drawer six months from now, and not traceable to your door if lost.

## Decision points

- Copy turns stiffly but works → return to the cutter within the return window; a light recut or deburr is free at most shops.
- Transponder car key → hardware kiosks cannot program chips; use a locksmith or dealer, expect a programming step and a much higher price, and note that a cheap "shell copy" is still useful as a door-only backup.
- "DO NOT DUPLICATE" stamp → the stamp is a request, not a law, in most jurisdictions, but chains will refuse; a locksmith will usually cut it for the person who can show they control the lock. Restricted (patented) keyways genuinely require the registered owner's authorization card.
- High-security dimple or laser-cut keys → specialist locksmith only; call ahead to confirm they carry the blank.

## Failure modes & recovery

- **F1 Copy doesn't turn at all:** wrong blank or worn original → have the shop cut a fresh copy against the original while you watch; if the original itself is worn, a locksmith can decode the lock and cut to factory spec instead of tracing the wear.
- **F2 Copy works in one lock but not another keyed-alike lock:** the locks have drifted with wear → have the copy cut to whichever lock matters most, or get the locks re-pinned to match.
- **F3 Kiosk rejects the key:** unsupported blank or a bent original → straighten trivially bent keys only by hand pressure at the shop's vise, then fall back to a human cutter.
- **F4 Copy of a copy fails:** each generation drifts a few thousandths → find the original, or have a locksmith cut by code from the lock.

## Verification

The duplicate has been inserted and turned in the target lock at least three times without force, the original still works, and the copy is labeled without revealing what address it opens.

## Variations

- Kiosk vs counter: kiosks are faster and fine for standard blanks; a human cutter handles worn originals, odd blanks, and free recuts better.
- Mail-order duplication apps: photo-based copies work for standard keys but eliminate the immediate test; only use them for spares of already-proven copies.

## Safety & privacy

A key photo is functionally the key: never post images of keys, and never label a key with its address or unit number. Cutting cost is small (a few dollars) but a lost labeled key can force a full lock change.
