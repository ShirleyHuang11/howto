## Goal

The pet has been examined by a veterinarian, you leave with a clear diagnosis or plan, written care instructions, and a booked follow-up if one is needed.

## Preconditions

- A vet appointment booked by phone or online, or a known walk-in/emergency clinic for urgent cases.
- A secure carrier (cats, small animals) or a well-fitting harness and leash (dogs); no pet rides loose in a car.
- Vaccination records or prior clinic name if this vet has not seen the pet before.

## Steps

1. **Start carrier acclimation days ahead, not the morning of.** Leave the carrier open in the living space with bedding and treats inside so it stops predicting doom. A carrier that only appears for vet trips guarantees a fight. → *Expect:* the pet enters the carrier voluntarily at least once before the appointment day.
2. **Write symptom notes with dates and specifics.** When it started, frequency, appetite, water intake, stool/urine changes, current medications with doses. Film any intermittent behavior (limping, coughing, seizure-like episodes) on your phone; symptoms hide in exam rooms. → *Expect:* a dated note and any video ready on your phone.
3. **Confirm pre-visit instructions when booking.** Ask whether to withhold food (possible bloodwork or sedation) and whether to bring a fresh stool sample. → *Expect:* fasting yes/no and sample yes/no explicitly answered.
4. **Load and secure the pet for transport.** Cat: into the carrier rear-first or lowered in top-down, door latched, a towel over the carrier to cut visual stress. Dog: harness clipped, carrier or seat-belt tether in the car. → *Expect:* carrier latched and seat-belted flat on the seat or footwell; no loose animal in the car.
5. **Check in and keep the pet contained in the waiting room.** Carrier stays closed and off the floor if possible; keep dogs at leash length from other animals, many of whom are sick or scared. → *Expect:* checked in; pet contained and reasonably settled until called.
6. **During the exam, restrain only as the staff direct.** Trained staff handle the actual restraint; your job is a calm voice and steady hands where asked. Never grab a panicking animal's scruff or muzzle on your own initiative. → *Expect:* the vet completes the exam; you have relayed the symptom notes and shown any video.
7. **Ask the closing questions before anyone reaches for the door.** What is the diagnosis or the differential? What does this medication do, dose and duration, and key side effects? What signs mean come back immediately? What is the total cost of the proposed plan? → *Expect:* answers you could repeat back; written or printed instructions in hand.
8. **Pay, book the follow-up, and get records.** ⚠️ *Irreversible:* consenting to surgery, euthanasia, or other major procedures cannot be walked back; ask for the estimate in writing and take time to decide unless it is a true emergency. → *Expect:* receipt, follow-up date if needed, and copies of new records or vaccine certificates.

## Decision points

- Pet is in acute distress (labored breathing, collapse, suspected toxin, bloated hard abdomen) → skip routine booking and go straight to an emergency clinic; call ahead while en route.
- Quoted plan is expensive or invasive but not urgent → asking for a written estimate and a second opinion is normal, not rude.
- Highly fear-aggressive pet → tell the clinic when booking; they can prescribe pre-visit sedation and schedule a quiet slot.

## Failure modes & recovery

- **F1 Pet refuses the carrier on the day:** chase and cram raises everyone's stress → close off escape rooms, wrap a cat in a towel (burrito hold) and lower it into a top-loading carrier; next time start acclimation earlier.
- **F2 Symptoms absent at the clinic:** the limp or cough vanishes in the exam room → this is why step 2's video exists; show it rather than describing from memory.
- **F3 Escape attempt in the parking lot:** door or carrier opened too early → open carriers and unclip leashes only inside closed rooms; a lost pet at a strange location rarely comes when called.
- **F4 You forgot what the vet said:** instructions blur the moment you leave → ask for printed discharge notes before paying; call back the same day for clarification rather than guessing at doses.

## Verification

The pet is back home and settled, you hold written instructions you can act on (or a clean bill of health), any medication schedule is understood with first dose timed, and a follow-up appointment exists if the vet said one was needed.

## Variations

- Exotics (birds, reptiles, rabbits): need an exotics-qualified vet; a dog-and-cat clinic may not treat them. Transport warm and covered.
- Mobile/house-call vets: good for multi-pet homes and carrier-phobic cats; procedures needing equipment still route to a clinic.
- Telehealth triage: useful for "is this urgent" questions; not a substitute for hands-on exams and often legally unable to prescribe without one.

## Safety & privacy

Medium risk: a stressed animal can bite or bolt (restrain only as staff direct, doors closed), and costs can escalate quickly (get estimates in writing before consenting). Clinic forms collect your contact and payment details; that is normal, but records of the pet's chip number and your address should go only to the clinic itself.
