## Goal

You leave a barbershop/salon with a haircut you asked for, having communicated it well enough that "what you asked for" and "what you wanted" were the same thing.

## Preconditions

- A shop chosen (walk-in barbershop or appointment salon — know which yours is) and, for salons, an appointment booked.
- Reference material if you have a specific goal: a photo beats every verbal description ever attempted.
- Payment plus tip where customary.

## Steps

1. **Arrive on time (appointment) or check in and take the queue (walk-in).** Give your name at the desk; waiting is `daily/wait-in-line` with magazines. → *Expect:* you're in the system; rough wait known.
2. **Brief the cutter before any water or clippers touch you.** The three-part brief: overall goal ("shorter but still combable"), the numbers/lengths where you know them ("2 on the sides, scissor top"), and the photo if you have one. State the *don'ts* too ("keep the fringe long enough to sweep"). → *Expect:* they play it back or ask sharpening questions ("skin fade or leave weight?") — a cutter who asks questions is a good sign.
3. **Answer the process questions honestly.** Wash first? Product you normally use? How long since the last cut? These calibrate them. → *Expect:* brief agreed; cape on.
4. **Stay still, and speak up early — not at the end.** Head where they place it; phone down (it tilts your head). If something looks shorter/different than briefed *say it at the first sign*: "That's about the shortest I'd want on the sides." Mid-cut corrections are cheap; end-state ones are haircuts you wear for a month. → *Expect:* course corrections absorbed while they still matter.
5. **Use the mirror check as a real checkpoint, not a formality.** When shown the back with the hand mirror: actually look — back hairline, sideburn evenness, the blend. Request touch-ups now: "Could you square the neckline a bit more?" → *Expect:* touch-ups done; you've approved the actual cut, not performed approval.
6. **Pay and tip per local custom, and log the result.** Note (in your phone, honestly) what you asked for in whose language it worked: "Maria — 3 side, finger-length top, thinned." → *Expect:* paid; the successful spec recorded so next time starts from a known-good state.

## Decision points

- New shop and unsure of quality → book a modest cut first (a trim reveals skill with low stakes) rather than a restyle.
- Cutter proposes something ("I'd take more weight out") → their expertise is real; accept if it serves your brief, decline cleanly if it doesn't: "Let's keep it conservative today."
- Big change (long → short) → say the number *less* short than you think you want; you can always go shorter next visit — the reverse takes months.

## Failure modes & recovery

- **F1 The language gap (numbers, "fade", "layers" mean different things across shops):** the photo again — it is the interlingua; and clipper guard numbers are near-universal where words aren't.
- **F2 It came out too short despite everything:** it's hair; it grows ~1 cm/month. Ask how to style it in the meantime — cutters are good at rescue-styling their own work.
- **F3 Genuinely botched (uneven, not what was agreed):** say so calmly *at the chair*; shops fix or comp real misses. Discovered at home: return within a day or two — "could we even out the left side?" is a normal request.
- **F4 They cut fast past your brief without checking:** intervene once, clearly; if the shop culture is cut-first-listen-never, pay, leave, and update your shop choice — the log (step 6) includes where *not* to return.

## Verification

The mirror check was a genuine approval, the result matches the brief (or the agreed mid-cut revision), payment and tip are settled, and the spec that worked is written down for next time.

## Variations

- `jp`: many barbers include a shave/shampoo ritual in the base service; tipping is not done.
- `us`: 15–20% tip is customary; walk-in chains keep your cut history under your phone number — the log exists in their system too.
- Curly/textured hair: seek shops that cut it dry/curl-by-curl where possible — the brief includes your curl pattern's needs, and the wrong shop genuinely can't substitute.

## Safety & privacy

Low risk. The only real hazards are communication failures — mitigated by photos, early corrections, and the honest mirror check. Razor-line work around ears/neck: stay still when asked; that's what the request is for.
