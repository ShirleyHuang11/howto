## Goal

The toilet stops running between flushes: the tank fills once after a flush, the fill valve shuts off, and no water trickles into the bowl afterwards.

## Preconditions

- A toilet that runs continuously or refills by itself periodically ("ghost flushing").
- A towel to set the tank lid on, somewhere it cannot be knocked off.
- Location of the toilet's shutoff valve known (small oval handle on the wall or floor pipe behind the toilet).
- For part swaps: food coloring for the dye test, and a matching replacement flapper or fill valve from a hardware store.

## Steps

1. **Lift the lid and set it flat on a towel.** Tank lids are unglazed ceramic and crack or shatter if leaned against a wall and knocked over. → *Expect:* lid safe and flat; tank internals visible: fill valve on the left, overflow tube in the middle, flapper at the bottom, chain to the flush handle.
2. **Watch the water and diagnose.** [BRANCH: water pouring into the top of the overflow tube → float set too high or fill valve not shutting off, go to step 3 | water level below the tube top but a trickle runs into the bowl → leaking flapper, go to step 4 | tank silent now but refills every few minutes → slow flapper leak, confirm at step 4] → *Expect:* one of the three patterns clearly identified.
3. **Lower the float.** Turn the adjustment screw on the fill valve (or slide the clip on the float rod, or gently bend a metal float arm down) until the valve shuts off with the waterline about 2-3 cm below the top of the overflow tube. Many tanks have a molded fill line to match. → *Expect:* fill valve goes quiet with water below the tube top; running stops. If it does, skip to step 7.
4. **Confirm the flapper leak with the dye test.** Put 5-10 drops of food coloring in the tank water, do not flush, wait 15 minutes. → *Expect:* colored water appearing in the bowl proves the flapper is leaking; a clean bowl means recheck step 3.
5. **Check the chain and seat before buying anything.** A chain with no slack holds the flapper cracked open; a too-long chain can wedge its tail under the flapper. Set roughly 1 cm of slack and tuck the excess. Also rub a finger around the flapper's seat ring; mineral crust or grit there breaks the seal. → *Expect:* correct slack, clean smooth seat; if the running stops, skip to step 7.
6. **Replace the flapper.** Close the shutoff valve, flush and hold the handle to empty the tank, unhook the old flapper from the overflow tube's side ears and the chain, and take it to the store to match size (typically 2 or 3 inch). Install the new one, reattach the chain with 1 cm slack, and reopen the shutoff valve. → *Expect:* tank refills once, fill valve shuts off, bowl stays still.
7. **Verify a full flush cycle.** Flush normally and watch the whole refill. → *Expect:* strong flush, flapper drops and seals on its own, tank refills to the fill line, valve stops, then silence.
8. **Replace the lid.** Two hands, lower it flat. → *Expect:* lid seated, no grinding.

## Decision points

- Fill valve never shuts off even with the float set low, or shrieks and hisses constantly → the fill valve itself is worn; replace the whole fill valve (10-20 minutes, same shutoff-and-empty prep as step 6, one nut under the tank).
- Shutoff valve is seized or absent → use the home's main shutoff for the swap, and note the seized valve for replacement.
- Tank uses a flush tower or canister seal instead of a flapper (many modern one-piece toilets) → same diagnosis logic, but buy the model-specific seal ring instead of a flapper.

## Failure modes & recovery

- **F1 New flapper still leaks:** dye test positive after replacement → wrong size or a pitted seat; re-match the size, and dress a rough seat with an emery cloth or fit a seat-repair ring.
- **F2 Chain tail jams under the flapper:** intermittent running right after some flushes → shorten the chain and clip off or tuck the dangling excess.
- **F3 Handle sticks down:** flapper held open until someone jiggles the handle → loosen the handle mounting nut slightly (it is often reverse-threaded) and lubricate the pivot.
- **F4 Water on the floor during the swap:** shutoff valve weeping from its stem → snug the stem packing nut a quarter turn; catch drips with the towel, and finish the job before addressing the valve.
- **F5 Weak flush after the fix:** water level set too low in step 3 → raise the float until the line sits at the tank's marked fill line, still below the overflow tube.

## Verification

Thirty minutes after the last flush the tank is silent, the dye test leaves the bowl clear, the waterline sits at the fill line below the overflow tube top, and the toilet has not refilled by itself.

## Variations

- Dual-flush button tanks: internals are a canister with a rubber seal ring; diagnosis is identical, parts are model-specific.
- Well water or hard-water regions: flappers and seats scale up fast; expect flapper swaps every few years and prefer rigid-frame flappers over soft ones.

## Safety & privacy

Low risk. Tank water is clean supply water, not bowl water. The two real ways to cause damage are dropping the ceramic lid and over-torquing plastic fill-valve nuts under the tank (hand-tight plus a quarter turn is enough; cracking the tank bottom means replacing the toilet). A running toilet can silently waste hundreds of liters a day, so this fix usually pays for itself on the next water bill.
