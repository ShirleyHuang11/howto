## Goal

A picture hangs level, at the right height, on a fixing that matches the wall type and the frame's weight, without a hidden pipe or cable being the surprise.

## Preconditions

- The picture with a hanging point on its back (wire, D-rings, or a sawtooth), a pencil, a tape measure, and a level (a phone level app works).
- A fixing matched to the wall: nails or picture hooks for light frames on solid walls; wall plugs (anchors) and screws for masonry; drywall anchors for hollow walls and anything heavier than a poster frame. Renters: check what the lease allows first (`housing/report-a-repair-to-your-landlord`'s deposit logic applies to holes too).
- For drilling: a drill with the right bit, and a cable/pipe detector or the knowledge of where lines run.

## Steps

1. **Choose the spot at the right height.** Gallery convention: the picture's center at about 145 to 152 cm from the floor (eye level); above furniture: 15 to 25 cm above its top edge. Hold the picture up, or cut a paper template, and step back to judge. → *Expect:* a pencil tick marking the picture's intended top-center.
2. **Find the actual fixing point from the hanging hardware.** Pull the wire taut upward as it will hang, or measure D-ring positions; the fixing point sits *below* the frame's top edge by that offset. Transfer the measurement down from your top-center tick. → *Expect:* a pencil cross where the hook or screw actually goes, which is not where the frame's top will be.
3. **Check what is inside the wall before making any hole.** Detector pass, or the heuristics: avoid the vertical and horizontal lines running from sockets and switches, and the zones directly above and beside sinks and radiators. ⚠️ *Irreversible:* drilling into a cable or pipe converts a picture into an electrician or a flood; thirty seconds of checking is the whole insurance. → *Expect:* a clear spot, or a relocated pencil cross.
4. **Install the fixing for the wall type.** Solid masonry: drill the plug's stated diameter, insert plug flush, drive the screw leaving a few millimeters proud. Hollow drywall: appropriate anchor per its instructions. Light frame on wood or plaster: a picture hook nailed at its angle. Two-ring heavy frames: two fixings, leveled against each other with the tape and level. → *Expect:* a fixing that does not wiggle, sized to the frame's weight class.
5. **Hang and level.** Wire or rings onto the fixing, then the level on the frame's top edge, nudging corners until the bubble centers. → *Expect:* level frame; wire-hung frames stay adjustable, ring-hung ones are as level as your two fixings.
6. **Clean up and pressure-test gently.** Pencil marks erased or hidden, dust swept, and a light downward hand-press on the frame. → *Expect:* no shift, no plaster crumbs trickling, wall around the fixing intact.

## Decision points

- No-drill options (adhesive strips): honest for light frames on smooth painted walls, applied per the packet's weight rating and an hour of pre-press patience; they are also the renter's peace treaty. Overloading them is how frames wake households at 3 a.m.
- Grouped gallery walls: cut paper templates for every frame, tape the arrangement up, and drill through the paper; the composition gets judged before the first hole exists.
- Very heavy items (mirrors, shelves): stud-mounted or masonry-anchored, never drywall-anchor optimism; above beds especially, the fixing class goes up one notch from what seems enough.

## Failure modes & recovery

- **F1 Hole in the wrong place:** filler, a fingertip of paint, and the new hole a hand-width away; walls forgive more than pride does.
- **F2 Drill hit something hard or sparked resistance suspiciously:** stop instantly, do not probe the hole; re-check with the detector and relocate generously. If a cable was possibly nicked, that circuit stays off until an electrician looks.
- **F3 Anchor spinning loose in crumbly wall:** the next anchor size up, or a toggle-type anchor, or relocate to solid backing; a spinning anchor never tightens itself into virtue.
- **F4 Picture keeps tilting:** wire slides on a single point; add a second hook a hand-width apart, or a museum-putty dot at each lower corner ends the drift.

## Verification

The picture hangs level at the planned height, the fixing matches wall and weight, a gentle press moves nothing, no marks remain, and nothing inside the wall objected.

## Variations

- Picture rail systems (period homes) and tension-cable systems (galleries, some rentals) hang from above and need no wall holes at all.
- Concrete and tile walls escalate the drilling to hammer-drill and tile-bit territory; tile especially rewards tape-over-the-mark and slow starts to stop the bit wandering.

## Safety & privacy

Medium risk is entirely step 3: cables and pipes. After that it is dust, a level bubble, and the modest carpentry pride of a straight frame.
