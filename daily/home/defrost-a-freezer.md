## Goal

The freezer is ice-free, dry, running again at temperature, and restocked, with no food thawed past safety and no water on the floor or damage to the cooling lining.

## Preconditions

- A cool box (or a neighbor's freezer) with ice packs, sized for the freezer's contents; in cold winter climates, a shaded outdoor spot below 4 °C works.
- A stack of old towels, a baking tray or shallow pan, and a bowl for hot water.
- 2-4 hours at home; frost thickness over ~1 cm on the walls is the trigger for doing this at all.

## Steps

1. **Run stocks down first, then stage the cooler.** Plan the defrost for when the freezer is emptiest; load the cool box with ice packs 30 minutes ahead. → *Expect:* cool box cold inside before any food moves.
2. **Move all food to the cooler, triaging as you go.** Pack tightly (a full cooler holds cold far longer), most perishable items deepest against the ice packs. Toss anything freezer-burned beyond use or of unknown age. → *Expect:* freezer empty; cooler closed and kept shut.
3. **Switch off and unplug the freezer, door propped open.** Remove shelves and drawers to wash separately in the sink. ⚠️ *Irreversible:* food safety clock starts now; a packed cooler buys roughly 4-6 hours, so the defrost must finish inside that window. → *Expect:* freezer off, interior starting to warm, shelves soaking.
4. **Lay the towel and tray defenses.** Towels across the floor in front of and under the freezer edge; the baking tray under the door lip or beneath the drain outlet if the model has one at the back/bottom. Locate the internal drain channel and check it is not iced shut. → *Expect:* every path meltwater can take ends in a towel or tray.
5. **Speed the melt, gently.** [BRANCH: bowls of steaming (not boiling) water set inside on trivets, door shut, swapped every 15 minutes | a fan blowing room air into the open freezer, surprisingly effective and hands-off | patience only, 2-4 hours] Never chip at ice with a knife, screwdriver, or any sharp tool; one puncture of the wall lining hits a refrigerant line and writes off the whole appliance. A hairdryer only on low, kept moving, hands and cord clear of standing water. → *Expect:* sheets of ice loosening at the edges within 20-30 minutes.
6. **Remove ice as slabs, not shards.** As sheets loosen, ease them off whole with fingers or a plastic scraper and drop them in the sink; wring towels as they saturate and re-lay dry ones. → *Expect:* walls progressively bare; meltwater volume dropping.
7. **Drain and dry completely.** Sponge out the remaining water, clear the drain channel and drain hole (a straw of paper towel wicks the last of it), wipe the interior with a mild bicarbonate solution, then dry every surface. A wet interior refrosts instantly and puddles ice on the floor of the compartment. → *Expect:* interior bone dry to the touch, drain clear, no smell.
8. **Restart and wait for temperature before restocking.** Plug in, thermostat to normal, door shut. Wait until the compartment is properly cold: 30 minutes minimum, ideally until an interior thermometer reads near -18 °C. → *Expect:* compressor running; interior frosty-cold to the hand.
9. **Restock from the cooler, checking each item.** Anything fully thawed and warm does not go back in; fully-thawed-but-cold raw meat gets cooked today, not refrozen raw. Log the restock as the date of this defrost. → *Expect:* food back in, still hard-frozen or refrigerator-cold at worst; cooler empty.

## Decision points

- Frost is thin (<5 mm) and the freezer works fine → skip the full defrost; a quick scrape with the plastic scraper during normal operation is enough.
- Cooler time running out (food softening) before the ice is gone → stop accelerants, remove what ice will come off, restart the freezer, and finish the defrost another day; food safety outranks a perfect defrost.
- Freezer refrosts heavily within weeks → the door gasket is leaking (test: a paper strip closed in the door should resist a pull at every point) or the door is being left ajar; replace the gasket rather than defrosting monthly.
- Frost-free (no-frost) model showing ice → that is a fault (defrost heater or drain), not a maintenance task; manual defrost is a stopgap and the appliance needs service.

## Failure modes & recovery

- **F1 Water spreading past the towels:** melt is outrunning the defenses → close the door to slow melting, wring and re-lay towels, add the tray; check the drain hole is not blocked with ice.
- **F2 Ice slab stuck fast to the evaporator plate:** do not lever against the plate → hot-water bowl directly below it and wait; the slab releases on its own.
- **F3 Hissing sound after scraping:** the lining is punctured and refrigerant is escaping → stop, ventilate the room, keep flames away, and call for repair; do not run the appliance.
- **F4 Freezer won't restart after defrost:** some compressors need a few minutes after power cycling → wait 10 minutes and listen; still dead, check the outlet with another device before calling service.

## Verification

Interior walls are bare metal/plastic with no ice, the drain is clear, the floor around the appliance is dry, an interior thermometer reads at or near -18 °C, and all restocked food was still frozen or cold when it went back in.

## Variations

- Chest freezers: meltwater pools in the base; most have a bottom drain plug, so position the tray under it and pull the plug early.
- Winter method (cold climates): food sits outside in a shaded box below freezing, removing the time pressure entirely.
- Combined fridge-freezers: the fridge section is off too during the defrost; keep its door shut and treat its perishables with the same cooler logic.

## Safety & privacy

Medium risk from three directions: food poisoning if thawed food is refrozen raw (the cook-or-toss rule), electric shock from mixing mains appliances with standing water (unplug first, hairdryer at a respectful distance), and appliance write-off from sharp tools puncturing the refrigerant lining (plastic scrapers only, and patience is free). No personal data involved.
