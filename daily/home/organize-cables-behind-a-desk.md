## Goal

Desk cables are identified, grouped, routed, secured, and reconnected with enough slack for normal movement and no strain on ports.

## Preconditions

- Supplies: labels or masking tape, marker, hook-and-loop ties, cable sleeve, adhesive mounts or clips, power strip with surge protection.
- You can safely shut down and unplug desk devices.
- The desk surface and floor behind it are reachable.

## Steps

1. **Photograph the current wiring.** Take a clear picture of ports and power connections before unplugging. → *Expect:* a fallback reference exists.
2. **Power down and unplug.** Shut down computers, switch off the strip, and unplug from the wall before handling the bundle. → *Expect:* no powered cables are being pulled or tied.
3. **Label both ends.** Mark monitor, dock, charger, lamp, speakers, and any similar-looking cables. → *Expect:* each loose cable can be identified from either side.
4. **Remove unused cables.** Pull out old chargers, duplicates, and mystery cords that are not connected to a device. → *Expect:* the bundle is smaller before organizing.
5. **Group by destination.** [BRANCH: power | data | movable device] keep power together, data together, and keyboard or laptop cables separately flexible. → *Expect:* cables follow logical routes instead of one tangled rope.
6. **Mount the power strip.** Attach it under or behind the desk where plugs are reachable and the switch is not kicked. → *Expect:* heavy power cords are off the floor.
7. **Sleeve and tie with slack.** Wrap long runs in a sleeve and use hook-and-loop ties every 20-30 cm, leaving service loops near monitors and the laptop. → *Expect:* cables stay together but can move without port strain.
8. **Reconnect from the photo.** Plug in data first, then power, then turn the strip on and test each device. → *Expect:* everything powers up and the desk can move slightly without tugging.

## Decision points

- Standing desk → leave a full travel loop and test highest and lowest positions.
- Power brick is heavy → support it on a tray or mount, not by its cable.
- Cable gets hot or damaged → replace it instead of hiding it in a sleeve.
- Shared desk → label owner or device names, not private account details.

## Failure modes & recovery

- **F1 Device will not reconnect:** compare against the photo → trace both labeled ends before swapping ports.
- **F2 Desk movement pulls cables:** add slack at the moving leg or monitor arm → retest full movement.
- **F3 Adhesive mounts fall:** clean the surface with alcohol and use screw mounts if allowed → avoid hanging heavy bricks from adhesive.
- **F4 Hum or flicker appears:** separate power bricks from audio or display cables → reroute crossing cables at right angles.

## Verification

All desk devices work, cables are labeled, the floor is clear, and moving the chair, monitor, or desk does not tug a connector.

## Variations

- Rental desk or wall: use removable clips and under-desk trays instead of screws.
- Gaming setup: separate display, USB, audio, and power paths for easier troubleshooting.
- Minimal laptop desk: one dock cable should remain easy to reach.

## Safety & privacy

Low risk. Do not overload a power strip, do not coil damaged cords, and avoid labels that expose passwords, employer names, or private device contents.
