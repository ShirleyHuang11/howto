## Goal

Kitchen scraps are composted with the right balance of greens, browns, air, and moisture, while meat, dairy, and pest-attracting materials are kept out.

## Preconditions

- Supplies: countertop scrap container, outdoor bin or approved collection service, dry browns such as leaves, shredded plain paper, or cardboard.
- You know whether you are using backyard compost, municipal pickup, or a worm bin.
- The compost area is reachable without spilling scraps indoors.

## Steps

1. **Separate compostable scraps.** Collect fruit and vegetable peels, coffee grounds, tea leaves, eggshells, and small plant trimmings. → *Expect:* acceptable greens are separated from trash.
2. **Skip problem foods.** Keep meat, fish, dairy, grease, oily food, pet waste, and glossy packaging out. → *Expect:* the bucket contains no strong pest attractors.
3. **Chop large pieces.** Cut corn cobs, melon rinds, and stems smaller before adding. → *Expect:* scraps have more surface area and break down faster.
4. **Add browns with every deposit.** Cover kitchen greens with dry leaves, shredded paper, or cardboard. → *Expect:* scraps are hidden and the pile does not smell like garbage.
5. **Balance moisture.** [BRANCH: too dry | too wet] add water if dusty; add browns if slimy. → *Expect:* compost feels like a wrung-out sponge.
6. **Turn for air.** Mix the pile weekly or when it smells sour. → *Expect:* fresh air reaches the center and heat or decomposition resumes.
7. **Keep the lid and site managed.** Close the bin, bury fresh scraps, and wipe the countertop container. → *Expect:* fewer flies, rodents, and kitchen odors.
8. **Harvest when ready.** Use compost when it is dark, crumbly, earthy, and original scraps are no longer recognizable. → *Expect:* finished compost smells like soil, not food.

## Decision points

- Municipal compost accepts meat or compostable bags → follow the local list exactly.
- Backyard bin attracts rodents → stop adding rich foods, bury scraps deeper, and secure the bin.
- Worm bin → avoid citrus overload, onion, garlic, and heat extremes.
- Apartment without outdoor space → use collection, freezer storage, or a sealed bokashi system.

## Failure modes & recovery

- **F1 Pile smells rotten:** too wet or too many greens → turn and add dry browns until odor drops.
- **F2 Fruit flies gather:** exposed scraps are available → freeze scraps, empty container often, and cover with browns.
- **F3 Pile does not break down:** too dry, cold, or chunky → moisten, chop, and turn.
- **F4 Animals disturb the bin:** food is accessible → remove meat-like items and use a locking rodent-resistant bin.

## Verification

New kitchen scraps are covered with browns, the pile is moist but not slimy, excluded foods are absent, and finished material is dark, crumbly, and earthy.

## Variations

- Cold climate: compost slows in winter but resumes when warm.
- Bokashi: fermented scraps still need burial or municipal processing afterward.
- Yard-heavy compost: mix grass clippings with leaves so they do not mat.

## Safety & privacy

Low risk. Wash hands after handling scraps, avoid moldy dust inhalation when turning, and shred mail only if it contains no plastic windows or sensitive information.
