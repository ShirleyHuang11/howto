## Goal

Wash a dog safely with dog shampoo, rinse completely, and dry the coat without causing fear, skin irritation, or ear and eye problems.

## Preconditions

- Dog shampoo is available; human shampoo, dish soap, and harsh cleaners are not used unless a vet specifically directs it.
- Towels, a brush, non-slip mat, cup or sprayer, and treats are ready before the dog enters the bath area.
- The room is warm, the water can be made lukewarm, and the dog has had a chance to toilet first.
- Skin wounds, severe matting, ear infections, or medical shampoo instructions have been checked with a vet.

## Steps

1. **Brush first.** Remove loose fur, burrs, and small tangles before water tightens them. → *Expect:* the coat parts more easily and mats are visible before washing.
2. **Prepare the calm setup.** Put a mat in the tub or shower, lay towels within reach, and run water before bringing in a nervous dog if the sound worries them. → *Expect:* the space is ready and you do not need to leave the wet dog unattended.
3. **Check the water.** Test lukewarm water on your inner wrist and keep pressure gentle. → *Expect:* the water feels warm but not hot, with no startling blast.
4. **Wet from neck down.** Soak the coat slowly, avoiding eyes, ear canals, and the inside of the nose. → *Expect:* the body coat is wet while the face remains controlled and dry enough to manage.
5. **Apply dog shampoo.** Massage shampoo into the coat from shoulders to tail and down the legs, using the label amount and skipping the eyes and ears. → *Expect:* dirt lifts into light suds without stinging or strong perfume exposure.
6. **Clean the face carefully.** Use a damp cloth around muzzle wrinkles, chin, and forehead rather than spraying the face. → *Expect:* the face is cleaner without water running into eyes or ears.
7. **Rinse fully.** Rinse until water runs clear and the coat no longer feels slippery, checking belly, armpits, tail, and between toes. → *Expect:* no shampoo residue remains when you part the fur.
8. **Dry in stages.** Squeeze water from the coat, towel dry, and use a cool or low dryer only if the dog tolerates the sound and heat. → *Expect:* the dog is damp or dry enough to stay warm and not drip across the floor.
9. **Reward and reset.** Offer praise, treats, and a quiet place, then clean the tub and wash your hands. → *Expect:* the dog recovers calmly and the bathing area is safe again.

## Decision points

- If the dog has fleas, medicated shampoo, or skin allergies → follow the vet label for contact time and frequency.
- If mats are tight against the skin → do not cut blindly with scissors; book a groomer or vet.
- If bathing creates panic, slipping, or defensive biting → stop and use a groomer, trainer, or gradual desensitization plan.

## Failure modes & recovery

- **F1 Shampoo left behind:** coat feels slick or flakes later → rinse again with lukewarm water and use less shampoo next time.
- **F2 Water in ears:** head shaking or ear scratching begins → dry the outer ear gently and call a vet if odor, pain, or discharge appears.
- **F3 Dog slips:** paws slide or the dog scrambles → pause, add a mat or towel footing, and continue only when stable.
- **F4 Skin reacts:** redness, hives, or intense itching appears → rinse thoroughly and contact a vet.

## Verification

The dog's coat is clean, fully rinsed, warm enough, and free of obvious shampoo residue, with eyes and ears not irritated.

## Variations

- `small-dog`: a sink can work if the dog cannot fall and the drain is protected.
- `double-coat`: drying may take much longer, so part the coat and check the undercoat for trapped dampness.
- `mud-only`: plain lukewarm water may be enough if no odor or grease remains.

## Safety & privacy

Low risk centers on slips, hot water, skin irritation, and ear or eye exposure. Never leave a dog unattended in a tub, and ask a vet about wounds, rashes, parasites, or medicated products.
