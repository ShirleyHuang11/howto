## Goal

A loose screw is tightened with the right driver, or its stripped hole is repaired so the screw holds again.

## Preconditions

- Correct screwdriver or bit type and size.
- Replacement screw, toothpicks and wood glue, or wall anchor depending on the material.
- Awareness of what the screw supports.

## Steps

1. **Identify the screw head.** Match Phillips, flat, square, Torx, or hex with a driver that fills the recess. → *Expect:* driver sits deep without wobbling.
2. **Press straight and turn.** Apply inward pressure and tighten clockwise slowly. → *Expect:* screw head moves inward and resistance increases.
3. **Stop at snug.** Quit when the fastened part no longer moves. → *Expect:* surface is secure without dimpling or cracking.
4. **Check for a stripped hole.** If the screw spins without tightening, remove it and inspect the hole. → *Expect:* wood fibers, drywall dust, or enlarged plastic show why it will not grip.
5. **Fix wood holes.** Pack the hole with glue-coated toothpicks, trim flush, then reinstall the screw. → *Expect:* threads bite into the packed wood.
6. **Fix drywall or masonry holes.** Use the correct wall anchor or a larger anchor rated for the load. ⚠️ *Irreversible:* overtightening can strip the new anchor or crush the surface, so stop when the fixture is held firm. → *Expect:* anchor stays still while the screw tightens.
7. **Load-test gently.** Wiggle the attached item or apply light downward pressure. → *Expect:* screw and item do not shift.

## Decision points

- Screw head is damaged → replace it before it becomes impossible to remove.
- Screw is too short → use a longer screw only if it will not hit wiring, plumbing, or glass.
- Fastener holds weight overhead → use rated hardware, not a toothpick repair.
- Material is metal → use threadlocker or a machine screw only when appropriate.

## Failure modes & recovery

- **F1 Cam-out:** detect driver slipping and chewing the head; recover by pressing straight or changing bit size.
- **F2 Split wood:** detect cracking as screw tightens; recover by backing out and drilling a pilot hole.
- **F3 Anchor spins:** detect the wall anchor turning; recover by removing it and installing a larger correct anchor.
- **F4 Overcompressed surface:** detect crushed drywall or bent bracket; recover by loosening slightly and adding a washer if useful.

## Verification

The screw sits snugly, the attached part does not wobble under light use, and the head is not stripped or overdriven.

## Variations

- Loose hinge screw: toothpick and glue repair works well in solid wood cabinet frames.
- Loose outlet cover screw: tighten lightly only, since plastic plates crack easily.
- Outdoor screw: use corrosion-resistant replacement hardware.

## Safety & privacy

Low risk from sharp tools and hidden utilities. Before using longer screws in walls, consider what may be behind the surface.
