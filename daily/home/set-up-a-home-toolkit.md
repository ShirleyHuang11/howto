## Goal

A single, findable toolkit exists in the home containing the ten tools that cover roughly 90% of household fixes, plus basic consumables, stored so any tool can be found and returned in under a minute.

## Preconditions

- A budget of roughly 60-120 USD equivalent for a solid starter kit (pre-bundled "50-piece" kits are mostly filler; buying the ten individually beats them).
- A fixed storage spot chosen in advance: a closet shelf, entry cabinet, or under-sink space that every household member can reach.
- Access to a hardware store or an online seller with per-item reviews.

## Steps

1. **Buy the container first.** A 40-50 cm toolbox with a lid tray, or a soft tool bag for apartments. The container defines the kit's home; tools without a home scatter. → *Expect:* an empty box or bag sized to hold everything below with some spare room.
2. **Buy the ten essentials.** (1) 16 oz claw hammer, (2) multi-bit ratcheting screwdriver with at least PH1, PH2, and two flat bits, (3) 7.5 m / 25 ft tape measure, (4) adjustable wrench (200 mm), (5) slip-joint pliers, (6) needle-nose pliers with wire cutter, (7) utility knife with snap-off or replaceable blades, (8) torpedo level, (9) LED headlamp or flashlight, (10) hex key set (metric, plus imperial where flat-pack furniture demands it). → *Expect:* all ten in hand; nothing from the "novelty multitool" aisle.
3. **Spend quality money only where it matters.** Cheap is fine for the hammer, level, and pliers. Do not cheap out on: the tape measure (bad locks and floppy blades cause mismeasures), screwdriver bits (soft bits cam out and strip screw heads), and utility blades (dull blades cause the slips that cut people). Mid-tier brand-name versions of those three cost little more. → *Expect:* tape locks firmly and stands out 1 m unsupported; bits are labeled hardened S2 steel or a known brand.
4. **Add the consumables tray.** Assorted wood screws and drywall anchors, picture-hanging hooks, electrical tape, duct tape, super glue, wood glue, a small can of penetrating oil, spare utility blades, spare batteries for the light. → *Expect:* consumables in the lid tray or a zip pouch, not loose among tools.
5. **Stage the kit at its fixed spot and tell the household.** Label the box if others live with you. → *Expect:* everyone can answer "where are the tools" identically.
6. **Shake down the kit on a real task.** Hang a picture or tighten every loose screw and hinge in one room, using only the kit. → *Expect:* the task finishes without leaving the room for a missing tool; gaps found get added to a shopping note, not guessed at in advance.
7. **Adopt the return rule.** A tool leaves the box for a task and returns the same day; the box never migrates to "wherever the last project was". → *Expect:* box back at its spot after the shakedown task.

## Decision points

- You own or plan furniture assembly, shelving, or curtain projects → add a drill/driver as tool eleven; a 12V-class cordless drill covers household needs and holds the same hex bits. See `daily/home/hang-curtains`.
- Renting with a no-drill lease → skip the drill, add removable adhesive hooks and a stud finder anyway (you will want it eventually).
- Household includes small children → the utility knife and blades move to a latched or high spot; see `daily/home/childproof-a-room`.

## Failure modes & recovery

- **F1 Bundled 100-piece kit temptation:** detect by "amazing value" framing and dozens of identical hex keys → buy the ten individually; the bundle's tape measure and bits are exactly the parts that must not be cheap.
- **F2 Tools drift and vanish:** a needed tool is missing at task time → sweep the house once, return strays, and re-announce the same-day return rule; recurring loss means the storage spot is inconvenient, so move the spot rather than blaming people.
- **F3 Stripped screw heads on first uses:** bits slip out of screws under torque → wrong bit size or soft bits; match PH2 to most household screws, press hard along the screw axis, and replace no-name bits.
- **F4 Rust on tools:** stored damp under a sink → wipe with penetrating oil, relocate the kit to a dry spot, add a silica gel pack to the box.

## Verification

All ten tools plus consumables fit in one container at its declared spot; a household member other than the buyer can fetch the screwdriver in under a minute; one real task has been completed start-to-finish from the kit alone.

## Variations

- Apartment minimal: tools 1-9 in a drawer-sized pouch; the hex keys that came with your furniture live in the same pouch.
- Homeowner extension: add a drill/driver, stud finder, wire stripper, and a second larger box for garden and plumbing tools; keep the core ten separate so daily fixes stay fast.
- Metric vs imperial locales: buy the hex keys and tape measure to match local fasteners and building conventions; dual-scale tapes exist and are worth it near borders and for online-bought furniture.

## Safety & privacy

Low risk overall. The utility knife is the kit's main injury source: always cut away from the body, retract the blade before it goes back in the box, and snap off dull segments (dull blades slip). Store the kit out of small children's reach and keep blades and super glue in the latched tray.
