## Goal

A smoke alarm is confirmed to sound, have power, be within its service life, and be placed where it can warn people effectively.

## Preconditions

- Access to each smoke alarm, a stable step stool if needed, and hearing protection for sensitive ears.
- Household members warned before the test.
- Replacement batteries available if the unit uses replaceable batteries.

## Steps

1. **Notify people nearby.** Tell occupants you are testing alarms, not reporting a fire. → *Expect:* no one mistakes the test for an emergency.
2. **Locate every alarm.** Check bedrooms, hallways outside sleeping areas, each level, basement, and near living spaces. → *Expect:* all installed units are identified.
3. **Check placement.** Confirm alarms are on the ceiling or high on the wall and not blocked by furniture, vents, or heavy dust. → *Expect:* smoke can reach the sensor.
4. **Read the manufacture date.** Remove or rotate the alarm if needed to see the back label. → *Expect:* the unit is less than 10 years old or marked for replacement.
5. **Press the test button.** Hold the button until the alarm sounds. → *Expect:* a loud pattern starts within a few seconds.
6. **Confirm audibility.** Listen from sleeping areas or ask another person to confirm. → *Expect:* the alarm is clearly audible where people sleep.
7. **Interpret chirps.** Note any single periodic chirp after testing. → *Expect:* silence means normal; repeated chirp indicates low battery or end-of-life.
8. **Replace battery if needed.** Install the correct battery type and close the compartment fully. → *Expect:* the alarm powers up and the chirp stops.
9. **Clean lightly.** Vacuum or wipe dust from vents without spraying cleaner into the alarm. → *Expect:* vents are clear and the test button remains accessible.
10. **Record the monthly test.** Mark the date and replacement due date. → *Expect:* next test and expiration are known.

## Decision points

- Alarm is older than 10 years → replace the whole unit, even if the button still sounds.
- Hardwired alarm does not test → check breaker only if safe, then replace or call qualified help.
- Missing alarm near bedrooms or on a level → install one according to local code and manufacturer instructions.

## Failure modes & recovery

- **F1 No sound:** detect silence when holding the button; replace battery, reseat the unit, and replace the alarm if still silent.
- **F2 Persistent chirp:** detect a chirp every 30 to 60 seconds; replace battery or the unit if the label indicates end-of-life.
- **F3 Nuisance alarms:** detect frequent cooking or steam triggers; relocate away from the nuisance source while preserving required coverage.
- **F4 Blocked alarm:** detect paint, tape, dust, or furniture obstruction; clean or replace the alarm and keep the path clear.

## Verification

Each smoke alarm sounds from its test button, is audible from sleeping areas, is not expired, and is not obstructed.

## Variations

- Sealed 10-year battery: replace the entire alarm when it chirps end-of-life.
- Interconnected alarms: one test may trigger all units, but still inspect each device.
- Smart alarms: also confirm app status, but do not substitute app status for the physical button test.

## Safety & privacy

Low risk. Use a stable stool, protect sensitive hearing, and never disable an alarm permanently because of nuisance triggers.
