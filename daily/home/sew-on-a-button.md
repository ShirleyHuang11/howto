## Goal

A missing button is sewn back on securely, with the small detail (the thread shank) that makes it last years instead of weeks.

## Preconditions

- The button (the original, the spare sewn inside the garment's hem, or a close match), a needle, and thread near the garment's color, about an arm's length of it.
- Scissors, and ideally a spare toothpick or matchstick for the shank trick.
- The attachment point findable: old thread holes or the ghost mark where the button lived.

## Steps

1. **Thread the needle and anchor the thread.** Pass the thread through the eye, double it, and knot the two ends together; doubled thread halves the passes needed. → *Expect:* a doubled strand about forearm length with a solid knot at the end.
2. **Anchor in the fabric at the button's spot.** Two tiny stitches on top of each other on the right side, where the button will hide them. → *Expect:* the thread firmly rooted, knot side hidden under where the button sits.
3. **Sew the button on over a spacer.** Bring the needle up through one buttonhole, lay the toothpick (or a second needle) across the button's top, and stitch over it: down through the opposite hole, up again, six to eight passes total. Four-hole buttons: work two holes at a time in your choice of parallel bars or a cross. → *Expect:* firm, even stitches with the spacer trapped under them, button held but not yet crushed tight to the fabric.
4. **Make the shank.** Remove the spacer, push the needle up through the fabric but *not* through the button, and wind the thread around the stitches between button and fabric five or six times. This thread pillar is what gives the buttoned layer room to sit flat, and its absence is why replacement buttons pop off again. → *Expect:* a short wrapped stem under the button; the button stands slightly proud and wiggles just a little.
5. **Finish underneath.** Needle to the back side, three small stitches over each other into the stitch base, then pass the needle through the loop of the last one and pull snug for a knot. Trim close. → *Expect:* a tidy knot on the underside, no dangling tails.
6. **Test.** Button it. → *Expect:* the button passes through its hole with normal effort, sits flat when closed, and holds under a firm tug.

## Decision points

- Shirt buttons vs coat buttons: coats need the shank doubly (thicker layers pass through) and stronger thread doubled twice; shirts can go slightly flatter.
- Shanked buttons (metal loop on the back, no holes): skip the spacer entirely and stitch through the loop the same six-plus passes.
- No matching thread: slightly darker blends better than lighter; on a four-hole button, matching the *other buttons' stitch pattern* matters more than exact color.

## Failure modes & recovery

- **F1 Button sewn tight to the fabric with no shank, puckering when closed:** it will strangle its threads and pop again; do it over with the spacer. Ten minutes now beats resewing quarterly.
- **F2 Thread tangling and knotting mid-sew:** strand too long or twisting; work with forearm lengths, let the needle dangle occasionally to unspin.
- **F3 Fabric torn at the spot (button ripped out taking cloth):** put a small folded patch or interfacing scrap behind the tear and sew button and patch as a sandwich; for visible damage, that is a tailor's ten-minute job.
- **F4 The spare button is a near-miss in size:** slightly smaller works; larger risks not passing the buttonhole. Test through the hole *before* sewing (the two-minute check that saves fifteen).

## Verification

The button holds a firm tug, closes and opens with normal effort, sits flat when buttoned thanks to the shank, the underside shows a tidy knot, and the thread color disappears at conversational distance.

## Variations

- The same anchor-pass-knot skeleton repairs hooks, snaps, and small tears; buttons are the gateway repair.
- Sewing kits from hotels handle exactly this recipe; keeping one in the travel bag (`travel/pack-a-suitcase`) upgrades a trip wardrobe malfunction from crisis to anecdote.

## Safety & privacy

Low risk: a needle's worth. Park the needle in a pincushion or the thread spool, never the sofa arm, and count needles at the end if children or feet share your floors.
