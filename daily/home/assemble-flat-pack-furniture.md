## Goal

The furniture item is fully assembled, square, and stable in its final position, with every fastener accounted for and no panel installed backwards.

## Preconditions

- Enough clear floor space to lay out every panel flat, plus the flattened box or a blanket to protect both floor and panel finishes.
- The tools the manual lists, typically a Phillips screwdriver and the included hex key. A screwdriver with a hex bit beats the folded-metal key for anything with many bolts.
- 1-2 hours of uninterrupted time; rushing flat-pack is how panels get ruined.

## Steps

1. **Unbox onto the flattened cardboard and inventory everything.** Lay panels out, tip hardware into a bowl, and count every fastener type against the manual's parts list before starting. → *Expect:* every listed part present in the listed quantity; missing parts discovered now, not at step 9 of the build.
2. **Read the entire instruction booklet before touching a screw.** Note which panels look alike but differ (hole patterns, edge finishing) and where the build direction matters. → *Expect:* you can say in one sentence what order the major panels join and which face of each panel points out.
3. **Sort hardware by step.** Group fasteners as the manual numbers them; cam locks with cam locks, dowels with dowels. → *Expect:* each build step's hardware reachable without searching.
4. **Orient the first panels by their holes, not by guesswork.** The rule that saves rebuilds: pre-drilled holes face inward or toward mating panels; the unbroken finished face is the visible side. → *Expect:* panel orientation matches the manual's little arrows and hole diagrams exactly.
5. **Insert dowels and cam bolts before mating panels.** Cam-lock logic: the metal cam bolt screws into one panel, the round cam nut drops into the large hole of the other, arrow on the cam facing the incoming bolt. → *Expect:* dowels seated halfway, cam bolts standing perpendicular, cam nuts loose in their sockets with arrows toward the bolt holes.
6. **Mate panels and turn each cam nut about a half turn clockwise to lock.** Stop at firm resistance. Cam nuts strip their particleboard sockets if forced further, and a stripped socket is close to unrepairable. → *Expect:* panels pulled tight together; cam slot roughly perpendicular to the bolt.
7. **Assemble the full frame with screws only hand-tight.** Leave every screwed joint a quarter turn loose until all frame parts are connected. → *Expect:* a complete but slightly floppy frame that can still be racked by hand.
8. **Square the frame, then do the final tightening pass.** Push the frame square (equal diagonals, or against a known-square wall corner), then tighten every fastener fully in the same order they were installed. → *Expect:* diagonal measurements equal within a few millimeters; frame rigid.
9. **Fit the back panel and nail or screw it in square.** The thin back panel is what actually holds most flat-pack cabinets square; align its edges flush to the frame before fixing. ⚠️ *Irreversible:* nails through the back panel into the wrong line miss the shelf edge and are visible from inside; mark the nail line first. → *Expect:* back panel flush on all four edges, nails biting panel edges, unit no longer rackable.
10. **Add doors, drawers, shelves, and level in final position.** Adjust hinge screws so door gaps are even; anchor tall units to the wall with the included bracket. → *Expect:* doors close flush, drawers run smoothly, unit does not rock.

## Decision points

- Part missing or panel damaged at inventory → stop before building; retailers replace parts free but a half-built unit complicates return.
- Two near-identical panels and the manual is ambiguous → dry-fit both orientations loose before committing any cam locks.
- Building alone vs. with help → wardrobes and tall bookcases need a second person for the tip-upright moment; plan it before the frame is done.

## Failure modes & recovery

- **F1 Cam nut spins without tightening:** stripped socket or bolt not fully screwed in → back the cam off, re-seat the bolt to full depth; if the socket is stripped, epoxy the cam in place or move to a spare hole.
- **F2 Panel installed backwards, discovered late:** holes visible on the finished face → disassemble back to that panel; particleboard survives one careful disassembly, rarely two, so work slowly.
- **F3 Screw won't bite (hole stripped):** insert a wooden matchstick or toothpick with glue into the hole, snap flush, re-drive the screw.
- **F4 Frame tightened out of square, doors won't align:** loosen the full frame a quarter turn, re-square with diagonals, re-tighten; hinge adjustment cannot fix a racked carcass.

## Verification

Diagonals of the main frame are equal, no leftover fasteners remain beyond the manual's declared spares, all doors and drawers open and close without rubbing, and the unit stands without rocking (anchored to the wall if tall).

## Variations

- Solid-wood flat-pack (some higher-end brands): screws bite better and survive re-assembly, but pre-drill any hole the manual didn't.
- Power driver use: only with clutch set low; a drill at full torque strips particleboard instantly. Never power-drive cam locks.
- Moving house later: cam-lock furniture tolerates one disassembly at best; transport large pieces assembled when possible.

## Safety & privacy

Low risk overall. The real hazards are tip-over of tall unanchored units (always use the wall bracket, especially with children in the home) and back strain when tipping a heavy carcass upright; get help for that lift. No data or money exposure.
