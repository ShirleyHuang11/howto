## Goal

Trim a dog's nails in small, controlled cuts while avoiding the quick, keeping the dog calm, and knowing when to stop or get professional help.

## Preconditions

- A dog who can be touched on the paws without immediate panic or aggression.
- Dog nail clippers or a grinder made for pets, plus styptic powder, cornstarch, or a styptic pencil nearby.
- Treats, good lighting, and a stable non-slip surface.
- You can identify the quick, or you are prepared to trim only tiny tips until a groomer or vet shows you.

## Steps

1. **Set up a calm station.** Place the dog on a non-slip floor or low table, keep treats within reach, and let the dog sniff the clippers before you begin. → *Expect:* the dog notices the tools but can still take a treat and remain near you.
2. **Hold one paw lightly.** Lift a paw only as high as the dog's body comfortably allows, supporting the toe rather than squeezing the whole foot. → *Expect:* the paw is steady enough to see the nail, and the dog is not twisting hard away.
3. **Find the safe cutting zone.** On light nails, look for the pink quick and plan to cut below it; on dark nails, trim only the very tip and watch for a pale chalky center. → *Expect:* you have chosen a shallow cut that removes nail without approaching live tissue.
4. **Make the first small cut.** Clip straight across the tip with one decisive squeeze, not a crushing half-cut. ⚠️ *Irreversible:* cutting into the quick causes pain and bleeding, so confirm the cut is tiny before closing the clippers. → *Expect:* a small nail tip comes off and the dog stays recoverable with praise or a treat.
5. **Check the nail face.** Stop cutting that nail if you see a darker dot, a moist center, or the dog suddenly pulls from pain. → *Expect:* each nail ends with a dry cut surface and no blood.
6. **Reward between nails.** Give a treat, release the paw for a moment, and reset the light grip before moving to the next toe. → *Expect:* the dog learns that each nail is brief and followed by something good.
7. **Handle dewclaws separately.** Look for the thumb-like nail higher on the leg and trim it only if it is long enough to curl or snag. → *Expect:* no hidden nail is left growing into the skin.
8. **Stop early if needed.** [BRANCH: finish all nails | do one paw today] choose the shorter session if the dog stiffens, pants hard, growls, or stops taking treats. → *Expect:* the session ends before fear escalates.
9. **Smooth and clean up.** Use a grinder or nail file only if the dog tolerates it, then collect nail pieces and put clippers away. → *Expect:* the dog can walk normally and the trimmed nails do not scrape sharply.

## Decision points

- If nails are black and overgrown → trim only tiny tips weekly, or book a groomer or vet to map the safe length.
- If the dog guards paws, growls, snaps, or has a painful limp → stop and use a groomer, vet, or behavior-aware handling plan.
- If nails are curling into pads, split, infected, or bleeding before trimming → treat it as a veterinary issue, not a home grooming session.

## Failure modes & recovery

- **F1 Quick nicked:** blood appears or the dog yelps → press styptic powder or cornstarch to the nail for several minutes, stop trimming, and call a vet if bleeding continues.
- **F2 Dog panics:** panting, trembling, growling, or repeated pulling → release the paw, offer space, and restart another day with paw-touch practice only.
- **F3 Nail splinters:** a cracked edge remains → file lightly if tolerated, or leave it and ask a groomer if it snags.
- **F4 Cut too little:** nails still click loudly on the floor → schedule short repeat trims rather than chasing the quick in one session.

## Verification

All trimmed nails are shorter without bleeding, the dog is walking normally, and no nail has a sharp snag or curl toward the pad.

## Variations

- `grinder`: touch the spinning grinder for one-second passes, pause often, and avoid heating the nail.
- `puppy`: practice paw handling and clip one or two tiny tips per session before the nails truly need a full trim.
- `senior-dog`: use extra support, since sore joints can make paw lifting harder than the clipping itself.

## Safety & privacy

Medium risk comes from cutting the quick, stressing a fearful dog, or missing painful nail disease. Keep styptic ready, never punish a dog for resisting, and use a groomer or vet when visibility, behavior, or nail health makes the job unsafe.
