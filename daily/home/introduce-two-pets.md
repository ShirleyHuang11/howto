## Goal

Introduce two pets gradually so they can share a home with lower stress, fewer fights, and clear escape options.

## Preconditions

- Each pet has a separate safe area with food, water, bedding, litter or toilet access, and a door or barrier.
- Vaccines, parasite control, and health concerns have been addressed with a vet when relevant.
- Leashes, baby gates, crates, carriers, or visual barriers are ready for controlled meetings.
- You have enough time to supervise and are willing to slow down if either pet struggles.

## Steps

1. **Separate first.** Put the new or visiting pet in a private room before any face-to-face greeting. → *Expect:* both animals know another pet is present but cannot reach each other.
2. **Swap scent.** Trade bedding, toys, or cloths between areas, then reward calm sniffing. → *Expect:* each pet investigates the scent without prolonged hiding, barking, hissing, or lunging.
3. **Feed near the barrier.** Place meals or treats on opposite sides of a closed door or gate at a distance where both can eat. → *Expect:* the other pet's presence predicts good things without blocking appetite.
4. **Allow brief visual contact.** Crack the door, use a gate, or leash both animals with space between them. → *Expect:* they can see each other for seconds without charging or freezing.
5. **Use neutral space when possible.** For dogs, choose a neutral outdoor area; for cats or small pets, use a controlled room neither animal owns strongly. → *Expect:* territory pressure is reduced and movement stays loose.
6. **Run short supervised meetings.** Keep sessions to a few minutes, reward calm glances away, and end before tension builds. → *Expect:* the meeting finishes while both pets are still manageable.
7. **Watch body language.** Look for soft bodies, loose tails, sniffing breaks, and the ability to disengage; treat hard staring, stalking, raised hackles, pinned ears, growling, hissing, or lunging as stop signs. → *Expect:* you respond to early signals rather than waiting for contact.
8. **Separate and slow down.** [BRANCH: continue gradually | return to scent work] move forward only after several calm sessions; otherwise go back to barriers and scent swaps. → *Expect:* progress is based on behavior, not the calendar.
9. **Supervise shared time.** Keep food bowls, high-value toys, litter boxes, and sleeping spots separate until trust is established. → *Expect:* no pet needs to defend a resource during early access.

## Decision points

- If one animal is a prey species or much smaller → use physical barriers as the default and ask a vet or behavior professional before direct contact.
- If either pet has a bite history, severe fear, or intense predatory focus → get professional help before face-to-face meetings.
- If introductions improve for several days → increase duration slowly, then add ordinary household activity one layer at a time.

## Failure modes & recovery

- **F1 Fight or chase:** contact, screaming, biting, or pursuit occurs → separate with barriers, not bare hands between mouths, and restart at scent-only work after everyone is calm.
- **F2 One pet stops eating:** meals near the barrier are refused → increase distance and reduce exposure until eating returns.
- **F3 Resource guarding:** stiff guarding over food, toys, people, or beds appears → remove shared resources and reintroduce them only under supervision.
- **F4 Hidden stress:** litter accidents, overgrooming, pacing, or stomach upset appears → slow the plan and consult a vet if it persists.

## Verification

Both pets can spend a supervised session in the same area with relaxed body language, no chasing or threats, and easy access to separate exits.

## Variations

- `cat-to-cat`: expect the process to take days to weeks, with scent and feeding steps doing most of the work.
- `dog-to-dog`: parallel walks often work better than nose-to-nose indoor greetings.
- `dog-to-cat`: leash the dog, give the cat height and escape routes, and reward the dog for looking away.

## Safety & privacy

Medium risk includes bites, scratches, and chronic stress. Do not force animals together, do not punish warning signals, and involve a vet or credentialed behavior professional if aggression, fear, or prey behavior is strong.
