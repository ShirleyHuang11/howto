## Goal

A fitted sheet folded into a flat, stackable rectangle instead of the usual resentful ball, via the corner-nesting technique that everyone claims is impossible and is actually four moves.

## Preconditions

- A clean, dry fitted sheet (`embodied/household/hang-laundry-to-dry` delivers it; damp sheets fold into mildew).
- Arm room: a clear table or bed helps enormously; experts do it standing, mortals use a surface.
- Acceptance that attempts one and two will be mediocre. This is a motor skill, not a fact.

## Steps

1. **Find two adjacent corners along one short edge and put a hand inside each, seams facing you.** The sheet hangs from your hands like a limp puppet, inside-out pockets on your fists. → *Expect:* two cornered fists, elastic edge running between them, the rest of the sheet dangling.
2. **Nest corner one over corner two.** Bring your hands together and flip the corner on your right hand over the one on your left, so one pocket swallows the other; the seam edges align. → *Expect:* one hand now wearing both corners as a single tidy pocket; the visible fold edge is straight.
3. **Repeat for the other short edge, then nest the pairs.** Run your free hand down the elastic to the third corner, scoop it, nest the fourth over it as before; then flip that pair over the pair on your other hand. → *Expect:* all four corners stacked on one hand; the sheet now hangs as a rough half-square with one clean folded edge and one elastic-gathered edge.
4. **Lay it down and square the shape.** On the table, arrange it into an approximate rectangle: the clean folded edge straight, the elastic curve folded inward so the gathered mess hides inside an L-shaped border. → *Expect:* a wobbly but recognizable rectangle with all chaos tucked into one interior curve.
5. **Fold into thirds, then thirds again.** Fold the long sides inward over the elastic first (locking the mess inside), then fold the strip into thirds the other way. Smooth with flat palms between folds (`embodied/household/fold-a-tshirt`'s smoothing habit). → *Expect:* a flat, calm packet that looks indistinguishable from a folded flat sheet.
6. **Store it inside its own pillowcase with the flat sheet.** The bedding-set-in-a-pillowcase trick: the whole set stays together, shelves stay tidy, and future-you grabs one bundle. → *Expect:* one pillowcase parcel per bedding set on the shelf.

## Decision points

- Deep-pocket or king sheets: the same moves with more fabric; do these on a bed, not in the air, and nest corners one edge at a time.
- Two people: each takes a short edge, nest toward each other, and steps 1 to 3 take ten seconds; folding sheets is the classic two-person kitchen dance for a reason.
- Genuinely out of patience: the honorable fallback is the "flat-enough" fold: corners roughly matched, rectangle imposed by force, stored inside the pillowcase where nobody can judge it. Function over form is a real option; this recipe just makes form cheap.

## Failure modes & recovery

- **F1 Lost track of which corner is which mid-nest:** the elastic edge is your map; both hands should always hold corners from the same seam line. Shake it out fully and restart from step 1; untangling midway costs more than restarting.
- **F2 The rectangle keeps bulging at the elastic curve:** the gathered edge was left on the outside; redo step 4 folding the curve *inward* first, then the thirds lock it.
- **F3 Folded but it springs open on the shelf:** fold once more or store under the flat sheet's weight; the pillowcase parcel (step 6) ends this problem permanently.
- **F4 It still looks like a croissant after five tries:** watch your own hands in a mirror or film one attempt; the error is almost always flipping the second corner *under* instead of *over*. One observed rep fixes what ten blind reps cannot.

## Verification

The sheet sits flat and rectangular with no elastic visible, stacks stably, fits inside its pillowcase with its set, and the fold took under two minutes without any negotiation with the fabric.

## Variations

- Standing method (no table): identical nesting, then the squaring happens against your torso; impressive at parties, unnecessary at home.
- Crib and bassinet sheets: same technique in miniature, and the best size to practice on.

## Safety & privacy

None to speak of. This is the corpus's monument to the fact that "everyone knows how" and "no one wrote it down" are both true; the four moves above are the entire secret.
