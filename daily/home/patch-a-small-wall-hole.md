## Goal

A nail, screw, or anchor hole up to about 1.5 cm across is filled, sanded flush, and painted so the repair is invisible from one meter away in normal light.

## Preconditions

- The hole is small: through-holes bigger than about 1.5 cm need a mesh patch or backing, not just filler (see Decision points).
- Lightweight spackle or multi-purpose filler, a flexible putty knife slightly wider than the hole, 220-grit sandpaper, a damp cloth.
- Primer or a stain-blocking spray, plus matching wall paint and a small brush or mini roller for the spot coat.
- Active time is about an hour; drying waits (filler and paint) add several hours of elapsed time.

## Steps

1. **Prepare the hole.** Pull out any remaining anchor or screw, brush loose debris out of the hole, and press raised paper or plaster edges flat by dimpling them inward with the round end of a screwdriver handle. → *Expect:* nothing proud of the wall surface around the hole; ragged edges pushed slightly below flush.
2. **Wipe the area.** Damp cloth over the hole and 5 cm around it, then let it dry. Filler grips clean matte surfaces, not dust. → *Expect:* clean, dry, dull patch of wall.
3. **Press filler into the hole.** Load the knife, push filler in across the hole from two or three directions so it packs the cavity, not just caps it. → *Expect:* filler visible at full depth in the hole, no air gap at the back.
4. **Overfill slightly and strike off.** Leave the patch a hair proud of the wall (a fraction of a millimeter), then one smooth diagonal pass with the knife nearly flat. Filler shrinks as it dries; a flush-struck patch dries into a dimple. → *Expect:* a thin, slightly raised smear feathering to nothing at the edges.
5. **Let it dry completely.** Follow the tub's time (30 min to a few hours by depth); many spackles change color when dry. Deep holes: fill in two lifts rather than one thick one. → *Expect:* uniform dry color, hard to a fingernail press.
6. **Sand flush.** 220-grit wrapped around a flat block, light circular strokes, feathering outward. Stop as soon as your flat hand cannot find the patch by feel. → *Expect:* patch undetectable by touch with eyes closed; surrounding paint only lightly scuffed.
7. **Dust off and prime the spot.** Wipe with the barely damp cloth, then dab or spray primer just over the patch. Unprimed filler drinks paint and "flashes" as a dull spot through the topcoat. → *Expect:* thin even primer film over the patch only, dry to touch before painting.
8. **Spot paint with feathered edges.** Small brush or mini roller, patch plus 2-3 cm around it, rolling or stippling the edge out to nothing. Two thin coats beat one thick one. → *Expect:* wet paint blends past the patch boundary; after drying, color matches.
9. **Check in raking light.** Hold a flashlight flat against the wall across the repair. → *Expect:* no shadowed dimple, ridge, or sheen halo; if there is, see F1 or F4.

## Decision points

- Hole is a popped drywall screw over a stud (a shallow crater with the screw head visible) → drive the screw slightly below flush and add a new screw 3-4 cm above or below it into the stud before filling, or the pop returns.
- Hole is larger than about 1.5 cm or has crushed edges → use a self-adhesive mesh patch and joint compound in two to three widening coats; plain filler will sink or crack.
- No leftover matching paint → take a chip or the paint code to be color-matched, and paint the repair corner to corner if the wall color has faded, since even a perfect match shows against sun-faded paint.

## Failure modes & recovery

- **F1 Dimple after drying:** filler shrank below flush → apply a second thin skim coat over the dried first one, dry, and re-sand; never try to fix it with thicker paint.
- **F2 Filler cracks or falls out of a deep hole:** filled too deep in one lift → rake out the loose material and refill in two lifts, letting the first set fully.
- **F3 Sanding gouged the patch or fuzzed the drywall paper:** too coarse a grit or too much pressure → skim a whisper of filler over the damage, dry, and finish with light 220-grit passes only.
- **F4 Sheen halo around the repair:** the spot coat reads shinier or duller than the wall at an angle → the fix is a full coat over a larger area, ideally the whole wall panel, breaking at corners where sheen differences vanish.
- **F5 Patch shows as a smooth island on a textured wall:** match the texture before priming (dab with a damp sponge, or use spray texture for orange peel), then prime and paint.

## Verification

From one meter away under both overhead light and raking flashlight, the repaired spot cannot be located: no dimple, ridge, color difference, or sheen difference, and a hand run over the area feels only flat wall.

## Variations

- Plaster (not drywall) walls: same procedure, but chase any hairline cracks radiating from the hole and fill them in the same pass.
- Rental turnover with many nail holes: fill and strike all holes first, sand them all in one pass, then spot-prime and roll each wall once rather than spot-painting dozens of dots.
- Quick-dry setting compounds (powder mix): sandable in under an hour and shrink less, but they cure hard, so strike them off cleanly rather than planning to sand heavily.

## Safety & privacy

Low risk. Wear a dust mask when sanding and keep sanding dust off electronics and food surfaces. In buildings painted before 1978 (US; similar cutoffs elsewhere), do not dry-sand old paint layers broadly since they may contain lead; a small patch with light hand sanding is the sensible limit before lead-safe methods apply.
