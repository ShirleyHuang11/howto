## Goal

A heavy furniture item is relocated to its destination with no injury to the movers and no damage to the item, the floors, or the doorframes.

## Preconditions

- A tape measure, furniture sliders (felt for hard floors, hard plastic for carpet), and moving blankets or thick towels.
- For genuinely heavy items (sofa, wardrobe, dresser): a second person. One person plus sliders handles more than you expect on flat ground, but stairs and lifting are two-person work.
- Closed-toe shoes on everyone involved.

## Steps

1. **Measure before you move anything.** Measure the item (all three dimensions) and every doorway, hallway pinch point, and stair turn on the route. Compare the smallest opening against the item's smallest two dimensions, since items can travel tilted. → *Expect:* a route where the item fits at every point, or a decision to disassemble first.
2. **Strip the item.** Empty drawers and shelves, remove loose shelves and drawers entirely, take off legs and doors where fittings allow, tape or tie doors that stay on. → *Expect:* item at minimum weight; nothing shifts when you rock it.
3. **Clear the entire path.** Rugs up, cables away, door mats gone, doors propped fully open, children and pets in another room. → *Expect:* an unobstructed corridor from start to destination, walked once to confirm.
4. **Fit sliders under the load points.** Tilt one side just enough for a helper to slip sliders under the feet or corners, then the other side. Felt side down on hard floors; hard smooth side down on carpet. → *Expect:* item slides with a firm push instead of scraping.
5. **Slide, don't carry, wherever the floor allows.** Push low on the item, from a lunge stance, moving it in short controlled shoves; pull only when pushing is impossible. → *Expect:* steady low-effort movement, floor unmarked behind it.
6. **Where lifting is unavoidable, lift with the legs.** Feet shoulder-width, squat with a straight back, grip low on the frame (not handles or trim, which rip off), stand up by straightening the legs, keep the load against your body. Never twist while loaded; turn with your feet. → *Expect:* the load rises with the legs doing the work; no back rounding.
7. **Agree the calls before a two-person lift, then use them.** Decide who walks backwards (the stronger navigator faces forward and steers verbally), then use fixed phrases: "lift on three: one, two, three", "set down on three", "stop" means freeze instantly. The person moving backwards gets continuous guidance: "two steps to the door, curb behind you". → *Expect:* both people lift and set down in the same instant, every time.
8. **Take stairs high-low.** The stronger person takes the low end (it bears most of the weight), item tilted to match the stair angle, one slow step at a time, resting on landings. ⚠️ *Irreversible:* a dropped load on stairs injures whoever is below it; never position anyone under a load they cannot hold alone for a few seconds. → *Expect:* controlled descent/ascent with the load never supported by one person alone.
9. **Place, then reassemble.** Set down on the agreed call, remove sliders (tilt method again), refit legs, doors, drawers, shelves, contents. → *Expect:* item in final position, level and stable, route furniture and rugs restored.

## Decision points

- Item does not fit the route even tilted → disassemble further (sofa feet and back cushions, bed frames fully) or find the alternate route (window/balcony jobs are professional territory, stop there).
- Anyone feels a back twinge mid-move → set down immediately on the call, rest, and re-plan with sliders or more people; finishing through pain is how a strain becomes a tear.
- Appliance rather than furniture (fridge, washer) → these are top-heavy and water/gas-connected; use an appliance dolly with a strap and disconnect services first.
- Solo and it weighs more than about half your body weight → don't lift it; slide it, walk it corner-by-corner, or wait for help.

## Failure modes & recovery

- **F1 Item stuck mid-doorway:** stop forcing → back it out, re-measure, try the other diagonal (tilt and rotate around the top corner of the frame, the "sofa pivot"); remove the door from its hinges for 2-3 cm of extra width.
- **F2 Floor scratch appearing behind the slide:** grit under a slider or a slider slipped off → stop, lift the corner, clear debris, re-seat the slider before continuing.
- **F3 Grip failing mid-carry:** call "set down" early rather than adjusting grip in the air → set down fully, re-grip low on the frame, resume.
- **F4 Wall or frame gouged on a turn:** padding was skipped → wrap remaining tight corners with the moving blanket taped around the item's leading edges before continuing.

## Verification

The item stands stable and undamaged at its destination, doorframes, walls, and floors along the route show no new marks, all removed parts and contents are back in place, and nobody is nursing a strain.

## Variations

- Carpet throughout: hard-shell sliders or a "walk it" technique (pivot on alternating corners); felt sliders dig in and stall.
- Stairs with a mid-flight turn: measure the turn's diagonal ceiling height, the usual failure point for sofas; sometimes the item must go up vertically ("on end").
- Renting a shoulder-dolly / lifting-strap pair: excellent for boxy items with two trained users, wrong for wardrobes that must stay upright.

## Safety & privacy

Medium risk: back injury from bad lifting technique and crush injuries to feet and fingers are the classic outcomes. The hard rules: legs lift and feet turn (never twist a loaded spine), agreed verbal calls before any two-person lift, and nobody stands downhill of a load they cannot hold. No personal data or money involved.
