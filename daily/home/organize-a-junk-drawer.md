## Goal

The drawer holds only items that earn their place, sorted into categories, each category in its own container, with a rule in place that keeps it from refilling with clutter.

## Preconditions

- One target drawer (resist doing the whole kitchen; this recipe is one drawer, one hour).
- A clear table or counter nearby, covered with a towel for the dump.
- A trash bag, a recycling bag, and an empty box labeled "relocate".
- A tape measure and a handful of small boxes or trays to test as dividers (shoebox lids, jar lids, and phone-packaging trays work; buy organizers only after measuring).

## Steps

1. **Stage the sorting surface.** Spread the towel, line up trash bag, recycling bag, and relocate box within arm's reach. → *Expect:* an empty surface bigger than the drawer's contents, three exits ready.
2. **Empty the drawer completely.** Pull it out if the slides allow and dump everything onto the towel. Partial emptying is how junk drawers stay junk drawers. → *Expect:* the drawer holds nothing, not even the crumbs.
3. **Wipe the drawer clean.** Vacuum or brush out debris, wipe with a damp cloth, let it dry. → *Expect:* a clean, dry, empty drawer.
4. **Triage every item with a fast three-way sort.** Keep (used in the last year and belongs at this station), relocate (useful but lives elsewhere), or toss. Duplicates: keep the best one. Dead batteries and expired coupons go straight out. Mystery keys and orphan cables go to a quarantine bag, not back in. → *Expect:* the towel shows only keepers; the other piles are in their bags.
5. **Discard the toss pile now.** Batteries and electronics to their recycling stream, blades wrapped in tape before binning. ⚠️ *Irreversible:* photograph mystery keys and one-of-a-kind small parts before tossing, in case something turns out to have a lock. → *Expect:* trash and recycling bags are out of the room.
6. **Cluster the keepers into categories.** Typical sets: writing tools, tape and adhesives, batteries, small tools, keys and fobs, chargers, coupons and stamps. Five to eight categories is right; twenty means you are keeping too much. → *Expect:* distinct piles with nothing left in a "misc" heap.
7. **Fit containers to the drawer.** Measure the drawer's interior length, width, and depth, then dry-fit trays so every category has a walled home and the drawer still closes. → *Expect:* trays cover most of the floor with no tray taller than the drawer's interior depth.
8. **Load with frequency-based placement.** Most-used categories at the front, rare ones at the back. Anchor sliding trays with rubber shelf liner or museum putty under each. → *Expect:* the drawer opens and closes without trays shifting or contents jamming the slide.
9. **Deliver the relocate box and declare the one-drawer rule.** Walk each relocate item to its real home in one pass. The rule going forward: nothing enters the drawer loose; it goes into a category tray or it does not go in. → *Expect:* relocate box empty; household members told the rule.

## Decision points

- Item is a "might need it someday" with no use in a year → quarantine bag, dated, kept in a closet; anything unclaimed after 30 days is tossed without re-sorting.
- Contents genuinely exceed one drawer even after triage → the drawer keeps the daily-use categories; tools and hardware move to a labeled bin elsewhere, not a second junk drawer.
- No trays on hand → cut-down cereal boxes work for weeks; buy fitted organizers only once the category sizes have proven stable.

## Failure modes & recovery

- **F1 Sort stalls on sentimental or mystery items:** you have handled the same item three times → stop deciding, quarantine bag with a date, move on.
- **F2 Trays slide and mix on every open:** detect by rattle and drift → line the drawer floor with grippy shelf liner or putty-dot each tray corner.
- **F3 Drawer refills within a month:** loose items appear on top of trays → the one-drawer rule lapsed; do a five-minute reset and re-announce that loose items get relocated daily.
- **F4 Project sprawls to other drawers mid-task:** you find yourself emptying a second drawer → put its contents back untouched, finish the target drawer, schedule the next one separately.

## Verification

The drawer closes freely; every item inside sits in a category container; the trash, recycling, and relocate piles are gone from the room; opening the drawer and naming where any given item goes takes under three seconds.

## Variations

- Deep drawers: stack two shallow tray layers with the bottom layer for rarely-used categories, or use vertical file-style dividers.
- Shared households: label each tray; unlabeled trays revert to junk fastest.
- Rental with warped drawers: stick-on liner and lightweight trays only; skip drilled-in dividers.

## Safety & privacy

Low risk. Wrap loose blades and razor cartridges before disposal, tape the terminals of loose 9V and lithium batteries (fire risk in trash), and check old mail and receipts for personal data before recycling; shred anything with account numbers.
