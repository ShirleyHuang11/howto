## Goal

One room is made safe for an unsupervised-for-seconds crawler or toddler: nothing tippable, strangling, shocking, sharp at head height, or swallowable is reachable, verified from the child's own eye level.

## Preconditions

- Screwdriver and drill, stud finder, furniture anti-tip straps, outlet covers (sliding-plate type), corner guards, and cord shorteners or cleats purchased.
- Know the child's current and next mobility stage (a room safe for a crawler fails for a climber within months; proof for the next stage, not the current one).
- An empty toilet-paper tube as a choke-size gauge.

## Steps

1. **Survey at crawl level.** Get on hands and knees and cross the whole room slowly. Note everything graspable, every cord, every gap, every glinting small object under furniture. This one step finds hazards no standing walkthrough does. → *Expect:* a written list of 10+ items; fewer usually means the survey was done standing.
2. **Clear the floor and low zone of swallowables.** Anything that fits through the toilet-paper tube is a choking hazard: coins, button batteries, magnets, pen caps, beads. Button batteries and high-powered magnets are medical emergencies if swallowed, so hunt for them specifically (remotes, tea lights, greeting cards). → *Expect:* nothing tube-sized reachable below 1 m; remote battery doors taped or screwed shut.
3. **Anchor every tippable furniture piece.** Dressers, bookcases, and shelving over 60 cm tall get anti-tip straps into wall studs (find studs with the stud finder; drywall anchors alone will not hold a climbing child). TVs strap to the wall or their stand. Test by pulling hard on an open top drawer. ⚠️ *Irreversible:* tip-over injuries are a leading cause of child furniture deaths; do not defer this step to "when they start climbing", they start without notice. → *Expect:* no piece moves when an adult pulls on it; straps taut, screwed into studs.
4. **Cover outlets and manage cords.** Replace reachable outlet plates with sliding self-closing covers (loose plastic plug caps are themselves a choking hazard once pried out). Shorten or hide appliance cords; a dangling kettle or iron cord pulls the appliance down. → *Expect:* no open outlet slots; no cord hangs within reach or crosses the floor as a pull-line.
5. **Eliminate blind and curtain cord loops.** Looped window-covering cords are a strangulation hazard. Fit cordless blinds, or cut loops and fit cleats above 1.5 m and wind cords fully after every use. → *Expect:* no loop of any cord hangs below 1.5 m anywhere in the room.
6. **Guard corners and edges.** Fit corner guards on hard furniture at toddler head height (roughly 40-90 cm): coffee tables, hearths, low sills. Glass-topped tables either get guards and a stability check or leave the room. → *Expect:* running a hand along low furniture finds no bare hard corner.
7. **Lock away chemicals, medicines, and sharp items.** Anything in this room that cleans, medicates, or cuts goes above 1.5 m or behind a latched door. Handbags and visitor coats (which carry pills and coins) get a designated high hook. → *Expect:* latches click shut on their own; the visitor-bag hook is in use.
8. **Secure doors, windows, and heavy swinging things.** Window stops limit opening to under 10 cm on above-ground floors; door pinch guards on hinge sides where fingers get crushed; doorstops without removable rubber caps. → *Expect:* windows open only to the stop; doors cannot slam a finger flat at the hinge.
9. **Re-survey at crawl level.** Repeat step 1 against the original list. → *Expect:* every listed item resolved; nothing new found.

## Decision points

- Room contains stairs or opens onto them → hardware-mounted gate at the top (pressure gates are for bottoms of stairs and doorways only; they fail under leaning weight at a top).
- Freestanding heater, fireplace, or radiator in the room → fixed fireguard anchored to the wall, not a movable screen.
- Rental where drilling is restricted → anti-tip straps are the one item worth negotiating or eating the deposit cost for; there is no adequate no-drill substitute for anchoring.

## Failure modes & recovery

- **F1 Child defeats a latch or cover:** you observe them opening it once → treat as fully defeated everywhere, upgrade that device class immediately; children rehearse.
- **F2 Straps installed into drywall only:** strap pulls out under a firm adult tug → relocate into studs or use heavy-duty toggle anchors rated for the load, and re-test.
- **F3 Safety devices drift out of use:** adults leave the gate open or outlets unplugged "for a minute" → the device is too inconvenient; replace with a lower-friction version rather than relying on vigilance.
- **F4 New hazards migrate in:** toys with button batteries, gifted furniture, holiday decorations appear → put a recurring monthly crawl-survey on the calendar; childproofing decays without re-checks.

## Verification

An adult at crawl level can cross the room and find no reachable swallowable, no unanchored tippable furniture (pull-test passes), no open outlet, no cord loop below 1.5 m, no bare hard corner at head height, and all latched storage self-closes.

## Variations

- Kitchen: add stove-knob covers, a locked knife drawer, and keep pot handles turned inward; the highest-hazard room deserves its own full pass.
- Bathroom: toilet lock, water heater set at or below 49 C / 120 F, medicines in a latched cabinet regardless of "childproof" caps.
- Grandparent or visit-only homes: a portable kit (outlet covers, a pressure gate, cord winders) covers a weekend; still do the crawl survey on arrival.

## Safety & privacy

Medium risk: the stakes are child injury, and the big three killers in rooms are furniture tip-overs, cord-loop strangulation, and button-battery ingestion; those steps are non-negotiable even in a quick pass. Childproofing supplements supervision, never replaces it. Post the local poison-control number where adults will find it.
