## Goal

A dripping compression tap is opened, its worn washer or O-ring is replaced, and the tap is returned to service without leaks.

## Preconditions

- You have the correct washer or O-ring size, an adjustable spanner, screwdriver, cloth, and penetrating oil if the tap is old.
- You know where the fixture isolation valve or main water shutoff is.
- The sink drain is plugged or covered so small screws cannot disappear.
- This procedure is for compression taps, not sealed ceramic cartridges unless the cartridge is being replaced whole.

## Steps

1. **Turn the water off first.** Close the isolation valve under the sink or the main shutoff, then open the tap fully. → *Expect:* water flow stops after the pipe drains.
2. **Protect the finish and drain.** Lay a towel in the basin, cover the drain, and wrap chrome parts with cloth before gripping. → *Expect:* the work area catches parts and tools do not bite into visible metal.
3. **Remove the handle cap and screw.** Pry the decorative cap gently, back out the screw, and lift the handle straight up. → *Expect:* the valve stem or bonnet is visible.
4. **Loosen the bonnet or packing nut.** Hold the tap body steady with one hand and turn the nut counterclockwise with the spanner. → *Expect:* the stem assembly begins to unscrew without twisting the whole tap.
5. **Withdraw the stem assembly.** Turn it out slowly and set parts down in removal order. → *Expect:* the washer at the bottom or O-ring on the stem is exposed.
6. **Swap the sealing part.** Remove the retaining screw if present, fit the same-size washer or O-ring, and seat it flat. [BRANCH: flat washer | O-ring] → *Expect:* the new part sits square with no bulge or twist.
7. **Reassemble without over-tightening.** Thread the stem in by hand first, snug the bonnet, reinstall the handle, and leave the tap closed. → *Expect:* the handle turns normally and the bonnet is seated.
8. **Restore water and test.** Open the shutoff slowly, then open and close the tap several times while watching the base and spout. → *Expect:* flow returns, the tap closes cleanly, and no new seep appears.

## Decision points

- If the tap has quarter-turn movement only, it likely uses a ceramic cartridge; replace the cartridge rather than forcing a washer repair.
- If the retaining screw is corroded solid, stop before stripping it and soak with penetrating oil.
- If hot water is involved, let the line cool before disassembly.
- If the tap body moves in the sink while loosening the bonnet, support it from below or call a plumber before a supply line is stressed.

## Failure modes & recovery

- **F1 Water keeps running:** detect flow after the shutoff is closed, recover by finding the correct isolation valve or closing the main.
- **F2 Bonnet stuck:** detect rounded nut corners or no movement, recover with penetrating oil, correct-size spanner, and steady counterpressure.
- **F3 Wrong washer size:** detect washer rocking, folding, or not fitting the cup, recover by matching the old washer at a hardware store.
- **F4 Leak at handle:** detect water around the stem after reassembly, recover by snugging the packing nut or replacing the stem O-ring.
- **F5 Drip continues:** detect a slow spout drip with the tap closed, recover by inspecting the valve seat for pitting or replacing the cartridge.

## Verification

With water restored, the tap runs normally when opened, stops completely when closed for 2 minutes, and the handle base and under-sink valves remain dry.

## Variations

- Two-handle mixer taps: repair one side at a time so hot and cold parts do not get mixed.
- Older taps: photograph each layer during disassembly because trim rings and packing parts vary.
- Rental housing: document the leak before and after, and follow lease rules for plumbing repairs.

## Safety & privacy

Medium risk comes from water damage and hot water. Confirm the water is off before opening the tap body, keep small parts out of the drain, and never force a seized valve hard enough to twist supply pipes.
