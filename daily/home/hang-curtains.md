## Goal

A curtain rod is mounted level and secure, sized and positioned by the standard proportion rules, with panels that hang to the intended length and draw fully clear of the glass.

## Preconditions

- Rod, brackets, and panels purchased after step 1's measurements (buying first is the most common mistake in this task).
- Drill with driver bits, pencil, tape measure, spirit level, stud finder, and drywall anchors rated for the combined rod-plus-panel weight (blackout panels are heavy).
- A stable step stool or ladder on firm flooring; a second person is worth recruiting for rods over 1.5 m.

## Steps

1. **Measure the window and apply the placement rules.** Width: rod extends 15-25 cm past the frame on each side so open panels stack on wall, not glass. Height: brackets 10-15 cm above the frame top, or two-thirds of the way from frame to ceiling for a taller look. Record rod length, bracket height, and floor-to-bracket drop. → *Expect:* three written numbers; rod length equals frame width plus both side extensions.
2. **Buy to the measurements.** Rod rated for the span (spans over 120 cm need a center support bracket). Panel math: total panel width 1.5-2x the rod span for proper fullness; skimpier looks flat when closed. Length: floor-length panels should kiss the floor or float at most 1 cm above; "high-water" panels ending mid-wall are the classic regret. When between stock lengths, buy long; hemming down is easy, adding length is not. → *Expect:* rod, matching brackets (three for wide spans), and panels totaling 1.5-2x span width.
3. **Mark the first bracket.** Measure up from the floor (not down from the ceiling) to the bracket height at one side of the window, at the planned side-extension distance, and mark. → *Expect:* one pencil mark at the recorded height and offset.
4. **Mark the second bracket level with the first.** Measure the same floor-to-mark height on the other side, then verify by holding the rod with the level across both marks. If the ceiling line is visibly out of level, split the difference between true level and ceiling-parallel; a rod that fights a crooked ceiling looks worse than one that is slightly off-level. → *Expect:* bubble centered (or a deliberate compromise line); marks at equal floor heights.
5. **Check what is behind each mark.** Run the stud finder; also check for hidden pipes and wires near the frame edges (headers above windows often contain a lintel that takes screws well). [BRANCH: mark lands on stud or solid lintel: screws go straight in | hollow drywall: use rated anchors, self-drilling or toggle type, not the flimsy ribbed plugs bundled with cheap rods] → *Expect:* fastening plan per hole; no drilling planned over a suspected cable drop.
6. **Drill pilot holes and mount the brackets.** Hold each bracket to its mark, transfer screw-hole positions, drill pilots (or set anchors), and screw brackets snug. ⚠️ *Irreversible:* holes in the wall; re-measure both marks and re-check the level line once more before drilling. → *Expect:* brackets tight to the wall with no wiggle under a firm tug.
7. **Dress the rod and seat it.** Thread panels (and center bracket ring allocation: split rings evenly each side of a center support) onto the rod on the floor, then seat the rod in the brackets and tighten set screws. → *Expect:* rod seated, finials on, panels sliding freely past everything except the center support.
8. **Hem check after hanging.** Let panels hang 2-3 days so folds and length settle, then pin the hem at kiss-the-floor or 1 cm float and hem (sew, iron-on tape, or a tailor). Steam out packing creases. → *Expect:* even hem line along the full width; no puddling unless deliberately styled.

## Decision points

- Concrete or brick walls → masonry bit and plug anchors; abandon the stud plan entirely and drill slow with steady pressure.
- Rental with no-drill terms → tension rod inside the frame, or adhesive-mount brackets rated for the weight; accept lighter panels.
- Radiator or deep sill under the window → sill-length panels (ending 1 cm above the sill) instead of floor-length; floor-length over a radiator blocks heat.

## Failure modes & recovery

- **F1 Anchor spins or pulls out:** hole is overbored or anchor underrated → move up to a toggle-style anchor in a fresh hole 3 cm away, or shift the bracket to reach the stud; fill the failed hole.
- **F2 Rod sags in the middle:** visible bow with panels mounted → add the center support bracket; long spans need it regardless of what the box claims.
- **F3 Panels hang high-water:** hem floats well above floor → lower is impossible without new holes, so first try letting down the existing hem, adding clip rings (which drop panels 2-4 cm), or buying the next length up.
- **F4 Rod visibly off-level:** doorframe-eye check fails after mounting → loosen one bracket, elongate the pilot slightly, re-level, and use the next-size screw for grip.
- **F5 Drill hits something hard or sparks:** stop immediately → suspect pipe or cable, relocate the hole, and get the wall checked before drilling that line again.

## Verification

The rod passes a level check and a firm downward tug on each bracket; panels drawn open clear the glass entirely; drawn closed they meet with fullness and hem 0-1 cm off the floor evenly across the width.

## Variations

- Ceiling-mount track: same measuring discipline, but fasteners must find joists or use toggle anchors; gives a floor-to-ceiling look in low rooms.
- Double rod: sheer layer behind blackout layer; weight doubles, so stud or toggle mounting stops being optional.
- Homes with young children: choose cordless panel styles; looped draw-cords are a strangulation hazard, see `daily/home/childproof-a-room`.

## Safety & privacy

Medium risk: ladder falls and drilling into concealed electrical cables are the two real hazards. Keep the ladder on firm ground with three points of contact, never drill directly above or below an outlet or switch without checking, and wear eye protection when drilling overhead into masonry.
