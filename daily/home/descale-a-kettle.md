## Goal

The kettle's limescale is dissolved with a mild acid, rinsed away completely, and the appliance boils clean again, faster and without white flakes in the tea.

## Preconditions

- A kettle showing the signs: white or brown crust on the element or floor, flakes floating in boiled water, longer boil times, or a crackly boil sound.
- One descaling acid: white vinegar (cheapest), citric acid powder (works best, smells least), or a commercial descaler. Never mix them with each other or anything else.
- Half an hour in which nobody will absent-mindedly make tea from the acid bath. A visible note on the kettle covers this better than optimism.

## Steps

1. **Prepare the solution in the kettle.** Vinegar: half water, half white vinegar to cover the scale. Citric acid: one to two tablespoons in half a kettle of water. Commercial: its packet's word is law. → *Expect:* solution covering all visible scale; the warning note on the kettle.
2. **Heat, but do not necessarily boil.** Bring it near-boiling (vinegar especially turns the kitchen sharp when boiled), then switch off. → *Expect:* hot solution; audible fizzing from the scale is the reaction working.
3. **Let it sit 20 to 30 minutes.** Heavy crusts can take an hour or a second round; patience does what scrubbing cannot reach. → *Expect:* scale visibly thinning, dissolving, or detaching in flakes.
4. **Empty, and rub off any loosened remnants.** Pour the solution away (it is drain-safe and even mildly helpful to drains), then a soft cloth or sponge on any softened patches. No metal scouring on the element or coating. → *Expect:* interior surfaces smooth to the touch.
5. **Rinse, then boil-and-dump twice with fresh water.** ⚠️ This is the step that keeps the next cup from tasting of vinegar and, more importantly, ensures nobody drinks descaler: two full boil-discard cycles minimum, three for commercial products. → *Expect:* boiled water that smells and tastes of nothing.
6. **Remove the note, and clean the outside while you are there.** A damp cloth for the body, and the limescale ring on the spout mesh (many kettles have a removable spout filter that rinses under the tap). → *Expect:* kettle back in service, filter re-seated, no note left to cry wolf.

## Decision points

- Frequency by water hardness: hard-water regions monthly to quarterly; soft-water regions may never need it. The boil sound and the flakes are the honest sensors.
- The same chemistry serves the coffee machine, the iron (`daily/home/iron-a-shirt` F3), and the showerhead, each per its manual; the kettle is just the gateway descale.
- Prevention: emptying the kettle after use rather than storing water in it slows scale meaningfully, and a kettle-filter cartridge system is worth it only in genuinely hard water.

## Failure modes & recovery

- **F1 Someone made tea mid-soak:** vinegar and citric acid in those dilutions are unpleasant, not dangerous; commercial descaler warrants a check of its packet's advice. The note habit exists because this failure is common and entirely preventable.
- **F2 Scale survives round one:** repeat with a fresh, slightly stronger soak and more time; years of crust dissolves in layers, not in one heroic bath.
- **F3 Vinegar taste lingering after two rinses:** one more boil-dump with a spoon of baking soda in it neutralizes the last of it, then a plain rinse.
- **F4 Black flecks after descaling:** the acid exposed a damaged element coating or seal debris; if flecks persist after rinses, the kettle is telling you about its retirement rather than its hygiene.

## Verification

The element and floor show bare metal or clean coating, the boil is quiet and quick again, water boiled afterward tastes of nothing, the spout filter is clean and seated, and the warning-note discipline held for the whole soak.

## Variations

- Stovetop kettles: identical chemistry, with the whistle cap off during soaking; the spout gets a bottle-brush.
- Espresso machines and pod machines: same acids, their own manuals, and a much stricter rinse count because the liquid path is longer.

## Safety & privacy

Medium risk is a single scenario: descaler tea. The visible note and the mandatory boil-dump cycles are the two locks. Beyond that, it is warm acid, an old cloth, and a kettle that sounds young again.
