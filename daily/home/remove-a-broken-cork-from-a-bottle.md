## Goal

Remove or manage a broken cork so the bottle can be poured without cork bits in the drink.

## Preconditions

- The bottle is upright on a stable surface.
- A corkscrew, clean screw with pliers, fine mesh strainer, coffee filter, funnel, or decanter is available.
- The bottle neck is intact with no cracked glass.

## Steps

1. **Inspect the break.** Look at how much cork remains above or inside the neck. → *Expect:* you can tell whether the cork can be gripped or must be pushed in.
2. **Choose the path.** [BRANCH: extract with screw | push cork in] Extract if a firm cork section remains; push in if the cork is crumbly, too short, or already falling. → *Expect:* the chosen method matches the cork condition.
3. **Extract with a screw.** Twist the corkscrew or clean screw into the cork center, then pull slowly with steady upward pressure. → *Expect:* the cork rises without shredding further.
4. **Push in if extraction fails.** Use the blunt handle of a clean wooden spoon or similar tool to push the cork into the bottle. → *Expect:* the cork drops below the liquid surface.
5. **Strain the pour.** Place a fine strainer, coffee filter, or clean cloth in a funnel over a decanter or clean pitcher. → *Expect:* the receiving container is ready before pouring.
6. **Pour slowly.** Tilt the bottle and pour through the filter, stopping before sediment or large cork pieces rush the neck. → *Expect:* liquid passes through while cork bits stay behind.
7. **Decant for service.** Move the strained drink to a decanter, pitcher, or clean bottle if it will be served at the table. → *Expect:* the serving container has clear liquid with no visible cork pieces.
8. **Discard the damaged cork.** Do not try to reinsert a broken, wet, or crumbly cork. → *Expect:* the bottle is closed with a clean stopper if anything remains.
9. **Prevent the next break.** Store corked bottles on their side when appropriate, use a sharp corkscrew, and pull straight rather than levering sideways. → *Expect:* the next cork has less chance of tearing.

## Decision points

- Cork is dry and crumbly → push it in and strain instead of drilling repeatedly.
- Glass neck is cracked → stop and discard the bottle contents safely.
- Drink is sparkling → chill first, work slowly, and expect pressure when the cork drops.

## Failure modes & recovery

- **F1 Cork crumbles:** detect pieces breaking away during pulling → stop extracting, push remaining cork in, and strain.
- **F2 Cork blocks the neck:** detect cork floating into the pour path → pause, rotate the bottle, and pour more slowly.
- **F3 Filter clogs:** detect slow or stopped flow → replace the filter and continue with a clean funnel.
- **F4 Glass damage:** detect chips or cracks near the lip → do not serve from that bottle and discard contents.

## Verification

The drink has been poured or decanted through a filter, and no visible cork bits or glass damage remain in the serving container.

## Variations

- No corkscrew: use a clean screw and pliers, pulling straight up.
- No decanter: strain into a clean measuring cup or pitcher, then serve.
- Older wine: pour slowly to avoid mixing sediment with cork fragments.

## Safety & privacy

Low risk. Broken glass is the main hazard; stop immediately if the bottle lip cracks or chips.
