## Goal

The joint between bathtub and wall is sealed with a clean mildew-resistant bead that cures before water reaches it.

## Preconditions

- Silicone bathroom caulk, caulk gun, utility knife or scraper, painter's tape, rags, and rubbing alcohol.
- Gloves, ventilation, and a trash bag for old caulk.
- The tub area is dry and available to stay dry for the full cure time.
- Optional caulk removal gel for stubborn old silicone.
- Manufacturer label visible for tooling and cure instructions.

## Steps

1. **Confirm the joint is dry.** Stop using the tub and wipe the seam before starting. → *Expect:* no water beads appear when pressed with a dry towel.
2. **Remove old caulk.** Cut along both edges, peel strips away, and scrape residue without gouging tile or tub. → *Expect:* the joint is open with loose caulk removed.
3. **Clean the seam.** Vacuum debris, wipe with rubbing alcohol, and let it evaporate. → *Expect:* surfaces look dull-clean and dry.
4. **Mask straight edges.** Apply painter's tape parallel to the joint, leaving the desired bead width exposed. → *Expect:* two clean tape lines frame the gap.
5. **Cut the nozzle small.** Slice the caulk tip at a 45-degree angle slightly smaller than the joint. → *Expect:* test squeeze makes a controlled bead.
6. **Apply a continuous bead.** Push or pull the gun steadily along the seam, filling the gap without stopping mid-run. → *Expect:* wet caulk bridges both surfaces with no hollow breaks.
7. **Tool the bead promptly.** Smooth once with a damp finger or caulk tool, pressing caulk into the joint. → *Expect:* bead is concave or gently rounded and fully adhered.
8. **Remove tape before skin forms.** Pull tape back over itself at a low angle. ⚠️ *Irreversible:* cured silicone is difficult to correct cleanly; inspect the line before it sets. → *Expect:* edges are sharp and excess caulk leaves with the tape.
9. **Clean small smears now.** Wipe stray wet caulk with the solvent recommended on the tube. → *Expect:* surrounding tile and tub are free of haze or blobs.
10. **Let it cure before water.** Keep the seam dry for the label cure time, often 24 hours. → *Expect:* the tub remains unused until the caulk is firm and non-tacky.

## Decision points

- Gap is wider than about 1/4 inch → install backer rod before caulking.
- Mold is under old caulk → remove all affected caulk and dry thoroughly before sealing.
- Surface is acrylic or stone → confirm caulk and solvent compatibility first.
- Tube says paintable acrylic latex → use only where water exposure is low; tub seams usually need silicone.
- Bead skips or sputters → stop, clear the nozzle, and restart before tooling.

## Failure modes & recovery

- **F1 New caulk peels:** old residue or moisture blocked adhesion; remove failed bead, clean deeper, and recaulk dry.
- **F2 Ragged edge:** tape was pulled late or bead overtooled; trim after cure only if necessary, then patch carefully.
- **F3 Mildew returns quickly:** moisture is trapped behind the joint; improve drying and investigate leaks.
- **F4 Bead too large:** wipe off immediately while wet and reapply smaller; do not smear it wider.
- **F5 Water hit before cure:** dry it, inspect for soft spots, and replace any section that washed out.

## Verification

The cured bead is continuous, adhered to both sides of the joint, non-tacky, and has had no water exposure before the stated cure time.

## Variations

- Clear silicone: hides uneven tile color but shows trapped dirt more easily.
- White silicone: matches many tubs and makes gaps visually cleaner.
- Caulk strip products: faster but less durable around irregular corners.

## Safety & privacy

Medium risk from blades, solvents, and slippery tubs. Ventilate, cut away from hands, and do not seal over active leaks or moldy wet material.
