## Goal

A squeaky or sticky keyed lock turns smoothly after dry lubricant is applied without attracting grit into the cylinder.

## Preconditions

- You have the correct key and the lock is not broken, frozen, or visibly damaged.
- You have powdered graphite or dry PTFE lock lubricant, paper towel, and optional gloves.
- Do not use household oil in the keyway, because oil attracts dust and can make the lock worse.

## Steps

1. **Confirm the symptom.** Insert the key and turn gently to feel squeak, drag, or sticking. → *Expect:* the key still operates the lock with effort.
2. **Clean the key.** Wipe the key with a dry paper towel to remove pocket lint and grime. → *Expect:* the key surface is visibly clean.
3. **Choose dry lubricant.** [BRANCH: graphite | dry PTFE] use a small puff of graphite powder or a short burst of dry PTFE labeled for locks. → *Expect:* no wet oil film is introduced.
4. **Apply sparingly.** Aim into the keyway and add a very small amount. → *Expect:* lubricant enters the cylinder without clumps or runoff.
5. **Work the key.** Insert the key fully, remove it, then insert and turn it several times. → *Expect:* movement becomes smoother with each cycle.
6. **Wipe residue.** Clean the key and lock face with a paper towel. → *Expect:* no black powder or spray residue transfers to fingers.
7. **Test from both sides.** If it is a door lock with interior and exterior cylinders, test each side separately. → *Expect:* both sides turn without squeak or drag.
8. **Check the latch alignment.** Open the door and turn the key or thumb turn, then close the door and try again. → *Expect:* the lock is smooth when open and closed.

## Decision points

- Lock is smooth when door is open but binds when closed → adjust strike plate or hinge alignment, not lubricant.
- Key is bent or badly worn → use a fresh copy made from an original or call a locksmith.
- Lock is frozen from cold → use lock de-icer, not graphite alone.
- Smart lock or electronic deadbolt sticks → check manufacturer guidance before adding any lubricant.

## Failure modes & recovery

- **F1 Too much graphite:** powder spills or key gets black, wipe repeatedly and cycle the key to distribute it.
- **F2 Oil was used before:** keyway feels gummy, use dry lubricant lightly and consider locksmith cleaning if sticking remains.
- **F3 Key will not turn:** key stops hard, do not force it and check that it is the correct key.
- **F4 Key breaks or cracks:** key weakens during testing, stop and use a spare or call a locksmith.

## Verification

The correct key inserts fully, turns the lock three times in a row without squeak or sticking, and comes out clean enough not to mark a paper towel heavily.

## Variations

- Padlocks: apply dry lubricant to the keyway and a small amount to the shackle holes if the maker allows.
- Car locks: use automotive lock lubricant and avoid overspray on paint.
- High-security cylinders: follow manufacturer instructions, because some cylinders restrict graphite use.

## Safety & privacy

Low risk: the main hazard is damaging the lock or staining surfaces. Use dry lock lubricant sparingly, avoid oil, and call a locksmith for seized cylinders, broken keys, or security-critical locks.
