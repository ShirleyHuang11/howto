## Goal

A running or weak-flushing toilet gets a matching new flapper so the tank seals, refills, and flushes correctly.

## Preconditions

- You can reach the toilet's wall shutoff valve.
- You have a replacement flapper matched by size and style, rubber gloves, towel, and possibly scissors for the chain.
- Turn the water off first; if the shutoff valve leaks or the fill valve breaks, stop and call a plumber.

## Steps

1. **Shut off the toilet water.** Turn the wall valve clockwise until it stops. → *Expect:* the fill valve does not refill after flushing.
2. **Drain the tank.** Flush once and hold the handle down until most water leaves the tank. → *Expect:* only a shallow puddle remains.
3. **Remove the lid safely.** Lift the tank lid with two hands and set it flat on a towel. → *Expect:* the lid cannot slide or crack.
4. **Inspect the old setup.** Note where the flapper ears attach and how much chain slack reaches the handle arm. → *Expect:* you have a clear reference for reassembly.
5. **Unclip the old flapper.** Detach the chain, then pull the rubber ears off the overflow tube posts. → *Expect:* the old flapper is free without bending the flush valve.
6. **Clean the seat.** Wipe the flush valve seat with a cloth to remove slime or mineral grit. → *Expect:* the sealing surface feels smooth.
7. **Match and install the new flapper.** [BRANCH: two-post ears | ring adapter] use the ears on side posts, or slide the ring over the overflow tube only if that is the toilet's style. → *Expect:* the flapper pivots freely and sits centered.
8. **Set chain length.** Clip the chain to the handle arm with about 1/4 to 1/2 inch of slack when closed. → *Expect:* the flapper rests flat and lifts promptly when the handle moves.
9. **Restore water.** Turn the wall valve counterclockwise slowly and let the tank fill. → *Expect:* water reaches the fill line and stops.
10. **Test flushes.** Flush three times while watching the flapper close. → *Expect:* each flush releases fully, then seals without tank refilling.

## Decision points

- Flapper is 2 inch or 3 inch → match the old part or measure the flush valve opening.
- Chain is too tight → flapper leaks because it cannot sit flat.
- Chain is too loose → handle moves but does not lift the flapper enough.
- Water runs even with a good flapper → check the fill valve height and overflow tube level.

## Failure modes & recovery

- **F1 Toilet still runs:** tank refills between flushes, reseat the flapper and adjust chain slack.
- **F2 Wrong flapper size:** flapper overlaps poorly or falls into the opening, buy the correct diameter and style.
- **F3 Weak flush:** bowl clears poorly, shorten the chain one link so the flapper opens farther.
- **F4 Shutoff valve drips:** water beads around the valve stem, close gently and call a plumber if tightening the packing nut does not stop it.

## Verification

After three flushes, the tank reaches its fill line, the fill valve shuts off, and no refill sound occurs for ten minutes.

## Variations

- Dual-flush toilets: use the manufacturer's seal or canister gasket rather than a universal flapper.
- Hard-water tanks: clean mineral grit from the seat before judging the new flapper.
- Rental housing: keep the old part until the repair is accepted if required by the lease.

## Safety & privacy

Medium risk: water damage is possible if a valve leaks or the tank cracks. Shut off water before working, never lean on the tank, and call a plumber for leaking shutoffs, cracked porcelain, or repairs inside wall-mounted toilet tanks.
