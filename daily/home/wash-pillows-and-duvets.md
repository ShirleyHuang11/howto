## Goal

Pillows or a duvet come out clean, fully dry to the core, and with the filling redistributed and lofted rather than clumped, ready to sleep on without any mildew risk.

## Preconditions

- The item's care label is present and legible (the label overrules everything in this recipe).
- A washing machine large enough: a double/queen duvet needs roughly a 7-8 kg drum minimum, a king duvet 9 kg or more. If the item has to be crammed in, the machine is too small.
- A tumble dryer plus 3-4 clean tennis balls (or wool dryer balls), or a full sunny day and a place to line-dry flat.
- Time: drying dominates and takes hours; start in the morning.

## Steps

1. **Read the care label first.** [BRANCH: machine washable → continue | dry-clean only or "do not wash" (common for wool and some down) → stop, use a professional cleaner | memory foam or latex pillow → never machine wash; see Variations] → *Expect:* a labeled max temperature and drying instruction you will follow.
2. **Inspect seams and fabric before washing.** Check for tears, thin fabric, and open seams; a small hole becomes a machine full of feathers. Spot-repair or hand-stitch any gap first. → *Expect:* no openings anywhere on the shell.
3. **Load the machine with balance in mind.** Pillows: always wash two at a time (or one plus towels) so the drum spins balanced. Duvet: alone, loosely folded, never packed tight. → *Expect:* the item fits with room to shift; the drum turns it, not just vibrates it.
4. **Wash on a gentle cycle, warm (usually 40 °C / warm setting), with about half the normal detergent.** Excess detergent is the main cause of clumped, soap-stiff filling because it never rinses out of down or fiberfill. → *Expect:* cycle running; no over-sudsing visible through the door.
5. **Run an extra rinse and an extra spin.** Filling holds soap and water tenaciously; one added rinse cycle plus a second high spin removes most of both and cuts hours off drying. → *Expect:* item comes out damp, not dripping, with no soap smell.
6. **Dry low and long with tennis balls.** Into the dryer on low heat with 3-4 tennis balls or dryer balls; the balls beat the filling apart as it dries, which is the difference between a lofted pillow and a lumpy one. → *Expect:* rhythmic thumping; item visibly re-lofting over the first hour.
7. **Break up clumps by hand every 30-45 minutes.** Pull the item out, massage apart any wet lumps of filling (they hide in corners and baffles), shake, return. → *Expect:* clumps getting smaller and fewer each check.
8. **Apply the full-dry rule.** Keep drying until the filling is bone dry at the core, not just the shell. Test: squeeze the thickest part hard for ten seconds; any coolness or dampness means keep going. A damp core mildews inside a week and the smell never leaves. Expect 2-3 hours or more for down. → *Expect:* every part of the item warm and weightless, no cool damp spots, no musty smell.
9. **Air, loft, and re-dress.** Let it cool and air for 30 minutes, fluff thoroughly, then back into a clean pillowcase or duvet cover. → *Expect:* item at full loft, smelling of nothing.

## Decision points

- Duvet too big for the home machine → a laundromat's large-capacity machine is the standard answer and costs far less than a ruined duvet or a burned-out home motor.
- Down/feather vs. synthetic → both machine-wash, but down demands the low-heat, multi-hour dry with balls; synthetic tolerates medium heat and dries in roughly half the time.
- Yellowed but not dirty pillow → sun exposure for an afternoon plus a wash; persistent deep yellowing means sweat saturation, and pillows more than 2-3 years old are often better replaced.
- No dryer available → line-dry flat across two lines in full sun, re-fluffing hourly; only attempt on a hot dry day, and allow a second day if the core is not fully dry.

## Failure modes & recovery

- **F1 Filling clumped solid after drying:** dried too fast or with soap residue → re-wet is not needed; run another low-heat cycle with balls and massage clumps apart by hand between cycles.
- **F2 Musty smell days later:** the core was not fully dry → re-dry immediately on low for 1-2 hours; if the smell persists, rewash and re-dry, since mildew has started.
- **F3 Feathers leaking in the machine or dryer:** a seam opened → stop, stitch the gap, run the machine empty to clear feathers from the drum and filter, then continue inside a duvet cover as a protective bag.
- **F4 Machine bangs violently on spin:** unbalanced load → pause, redistribute the item or add towels, restart the spin.

## Verification

Squeezed hard at its thickest point for ten seconds, the item feels warm and dry with no cool spots; the filling moves freely with no fist-sized clumps; it smells of nothing; and it has returned to something near its original loft.

## Variations

- Memory foam and latex pillows: never machine wash or tumble dry (the foam tears and traps water); vacuum the surface, spot-clean, and wash the removable cover only.
- Wool-filled duvets: most are not machine washable at all; air outdoors regularly and dry-clean rarely.
- Duvet cover discipline: washing covers fortnightly means the duvet itself only needs washing once or twice a year.

## Safety & privacy

Low risk. The one meaningful hazard is fire from an overloaded dryer running hot for hours: use low heat, keep the lint filter clean, and stay home while it runs. Overloading a small machine can also burn out its motor or bearings.
