## Goal

A wrinkled shirt is ironed crisp in a fixed order that avoids re-wrinkling what you already did, without scorch marks on the shirt or the board.

## Preconditions

- An iron with water for steam, an ironing board (or a towel-padded table as the honest substitute), and a hanger waiting for the result.
- The shirt's care label read once: the iron-symbol dots set the temperature (one dot low/synthetics, two dots medium/wool-blends, three dots high/cotton and linen). Wrong temperature is where shirts die.
- The shirt slightly damp (best) or a spray bottle at hand; bone-dry wrinkles resist.

## Steps

1. **Heat the iron to the label's setting with water in the tank.** Test on an inside seam or hem first. → *Expect:* the test patch smooths without any shine, stick, or smell.
2. **Iron the collar first, wrong side then right side, from the points inward.** → *Expect:* flat collar; ironing from points toward center pushes any crease into the invisible middle.
3. **Do the cuffs and sleeves next.** Cuffs open and flat, inside then outside; sleeves laid flat by the seam, ironed from shoulder to cuff. A crisp sleeve crease is a choice: crease along the fold line for formal, or roll the sleeve slightly and iron flat-ish to avoid a crease. → *Expect:* cuffs crisp, sleeves smooth with your chosen crease policy.
4. **Iron the yoke and shoulders over the board's narrow end.** Slide the shoulder over the board's nose. → *Expect:* the upper back panel smooth; this is the section everyone skips and everyone sees.
5. **Do the front panels, button side and buttonhole side.** Point the iron's nose *between* buttons, never over them (buttons crack and their imprint shines through). Work top to bottom alongside the placket. → *Expect:* flat fronts; placket crisp; all buttons intact.
6. **Finish with the back in two overlapping halves.** Lay half the back flat, iron top to bottom, rotate, do the other half over the pleat if there is one. → *Expect:* a smooth back with the box pleat (if any) pressed as a pleat, not flattened randomly.
7. **Hang it immediately, top button closed.** ⚠️ Then the iron: upright on its heel, cord safe, switched off, and cooling somewhere no hand or child will find it; irons stay burn-hot for many minutes after power-off. → *Expect:* shirt cooling into shape on the hanger; iron parked safe and off.

## Decision points

- Steam vs dry: steam plus cotton is the default; delicate synthetics prefer lower heat, no steam, and a pressing cloth (a thin cotton cloth between iron and garment) as cheap insurance.
- Many shirts: batch them damp from the dryer, iron in one session, and the order collar-cuffs-sleeves-yoke-fronts-back becomes an assembly line.
- Genuinely no time: hang the shirt in the bathroom during a hot shower (`daily/self-care/take-a-shower`'s steam as a free tool) and accept eighty-percent results, or use the "iron only what the jacket shows" triage: collar, cuffs, front placket.

## Failure modes & recovery

- **F1 Shine marks on dark fabric:** heat too high or ironing the right side of delicate cloth directly. Use the pressing cloth and lower heat; existing shine sometimes lifts with steam hovering (iron held just above, steam only).
- **F2 Scorch (yellow-brown):** the iron parked flat on fabric or way too hot. Light scorch on cotton may fade with immediate cold-water rinse and a re-wash; real scorch is permanent, and the lesson is the heel-rest habit.
- **F3 Water spitting and staining:** the iron is not hot enough for steam yet, or the tank has mineral crud. Wait for full temperature, and descale the iron per its manual (the `daily/home/descale-a-kettle` chemistry in a different appliance).
- **F4 Ironed wrinkles *into* the shirt:** the fabric shifted mid-stroke; smooth each section flat by hand before the iron touches it, and keep finished sections hanging off the board, not bunched under your forearm.

## Verification

Collar, cuffs, plackets, and yoke are crisp, panels are smooth with no shine or scorch, the shirt is on a hanger while still warm, and the iron is off, upright, and cooling out of reach.

## Variations

- Trousers, skirts, and pleats each have their own order but reuse the rules: label temperature, pressing cloth for delicates, sections held flat, garment hung hot.
- Steamers replace this recipe for knits, suits, and hurry: slower per-wrinkle but zero scorch risk.

## Safety & privacy

Medium risk is entirely the hot iron: heel-rest between strokes, off at the end, cooled out of reach, and never left face-down or unattended while hot. The shirt is the hostage, your hand is the stake.
