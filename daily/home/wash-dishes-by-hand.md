## Goal

A sinkful of dishes washed in the efficient order, actually clean rather than rearranged grease, dried and put away, with the sink itself finishing clean.

## Preconditions

- Dish soap, a sponge or brush (with a life expectancy; a smelly sponge is a bacteria applicator), a drying rack or towel-spread counter, and hot water.
- Scraps and grease policy set: solids scraped to the bin first, and cooking grease *never* down the drain; it goes into a jar or the bin (`daily/home/unclog-a-drain` is the recipe this rule prevents).
- Sharp knives announced: they never soak invisibly in cloudy water (`embodied/household/tidy-a-room` F2's blind-grab rule, at the sink).

## Steps

1. **Stage: scrape everything, stack by category, start the worst soaking.** Plates scraped to the bin, pots with stuck food filled with hot water and a drop of soap (`daily/food/clean-as-you-cook` step 5 ideally already did this), glasses and cutlery grouped. → *Expect:* no solids heading for the drain; burnt pots soaking; a wash order visible on the counter.
2. **Set up the wash: hot soapy water in the basin, rinse side ready.** Two-basin sinks: wash in one, rinse in the other. Single basin: a washing-up bowl for suds and a thin stream or a filled kettle-pot for rinsing. Water as hot as gloved or bare hands comfortably bear; heat cuts grease. → *Expect:* a suds basin and a rinse plan.
3. **Wash in the cleanest-to-dirtiest order: glasses, cutlery, plates and bowls, serving pieces, then pots and pans last.** The water stays usable longest this way; greasy pans first is the classic self-sabotage. → *Expect:* each category washed inside and out (glass rims, fork tines, the *backs* of plates), then to the rinse.
4. **Rinse off all suds with clean hot water.** Soap film left on dishes is both a taste and a stomach complaint. → *Expect:* squeaky surfaces, no bubbles sliding off into the rack.
5. **Handle knives and the soaked pots deliberately.** Knives washed one at a time, blade away, straight to the rack's safe slot or the towel, never into a crowded caddy edge-up. Pots: their soak has loosened the stuck layer; a brush finishes it, and the baking-soda boil trick (`daily/food/clean-as-you-cook` F3) handles the burnt outliers. → *Expect:* fingers accounted for; pots actually clean, not "dark-bottomed but probably fine".
6. **Dry and put away, then close the sink down.** Air-drying in the rack beats a damp towel for hygiene, but a *clean* towel is fine and faster; either way, put things away when dry (a permanently full rack is storage, not workflow). Then: drain the basin, rinse the sink walls, wring the sponge dry, and hang the cloth to dry. → *Expect:* empty sink, cleared or clearing rack, sponge and cloth drying rather than souring.

## Decision points

- Water strategy honestly: a filled basin plus efficient rinsing uses far less hot water than a running tap throughout; the running tap is the habit to break.
- Wooden boards and handles, cast iron, and good knives: washed promptly, never soaked (wood swells, cast iron rusts, edges corrode), dried immediately.
- Gloves: they let you use hotter water and save your skin on big sessions; personal call for small ones.

## Failure modes & recovery

- **F1 Water gone gray and cold halfway:** the order slipped or the load outmatched the basin; drain, refresh, continue. Washing in spent water is theater.
- **F2 Dried dishes with visible film or grease spots:** the rinse (step 4) or the water heat failed; those pieces go back through, and the rack neighbors get spot-checked.
- **F3 Sponge smells:** it is broadcasting bacteria; microwave it damp for a minute, run it through the dishwasher, or retire it. Rotation beats loyalty.
- **F4 Chipped a glass or found one in the water:** stop, drain carefully, remove glass with a cloth, and check the basin floor before hands return; a chipped rim glass retires to recycling wrapped (`daily/home/sort-recycling`'s glass rule).

## Verification

Everything washed in order and soap-rinsed, knives and pots handled by their rules, the rack draining or emptied, grease in the jar rather than the pipes, and the sink, sponge, and cloth left clean and drying.

## Variations

- With a dishwasher present, hand-washing shrinks to the machine's refusals: wood, cast iron, good knives, crystal, and the pot that will not fit (`embodied/kitchen/load-a-dishwasher` preconditions name the same list).
- Camping and tiny-sink versions run the same order with two bowls and a kettle, plus strict gray-water disposal rules outdoors.

## Safety & privacy

Medium risk is concentrated in two objects: invisible blades in cloudy water (never soak them) and the grease that wants to meet your plumbing (the jar). Hot water and a chipped glass supply the minor hazards; the order and the rinse supply the actual cleanliness.
