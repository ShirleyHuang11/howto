## Goal

The chair sits solid on a flat floor with no rock and no frame flex, either by correcting a short leg or by re-securing loose joints, and glides quietly on felt pads.

## Preconditions

- A verified-flat test surface (kitchen tile or a large cutting board on a table; many "wobbly chairs" are actually wobbly floors).
- Screwdrivers and hex keys for mechanical fasteners; for glued wooden joints: wood glue (PVA), a rubber mallet, a band clamp or ratchet strap with corner padding, rags, and 24 hours of patience.
- Felt pads sized to the leg feet.

## Steps

1. **Diagnose: leg or joint.** Set the chair on the flat test surface and rock it corner to corner, then grip the backrest and seat and twist gently. [BRANCH: chair rocks but frame feels rigid: a short/long leg, go to step 2 | frame flexes, creaks, or a rail shifts in its socket: loose joint, go to step 3 | both: fix joints first, since re-gluing changes the leg geometry] → *Expect:* a clear verdict; mark the offending leg or joint with tape.
2. **Correct a leg-length mismatch.** Identify the gap under one leg on the flat surface and measure it with feeler-style shims (folded paper). Fix by sticking a felt pad or a trimmed hard shim under the short leg. Sanding the three long legs to match is a last resort. ⚠️ *Irreversible:* wood removed cannot go back; sand in tiny passes with the chair re-tested on the flat surface after every pass, and expect to chase it around all four legs if you overdo one. → *Expect:* no rock in any direction on the test surface; skip to step 7.
3. **Tighten mechanical fasteners first.** Flip the chair on a padded table. Snug every visible bolt, screw, and corner-block fastener. Snug means firm, not gorilla-tight; overtorquing strips wood threads. → *Expect:* wobble gone (done, go to step 7) or fasteners tight but a glued joint still moves (continue).
4. **Open the loose glued joint.** Work the loose rail or spindle free with hand pressure and mallet taps on a padded block, opening it just enough to expose the mating surfaces. Only open joints that are actually loose; sound joints stay untouched. → *Expect:* tenon or dowel end visible, joint apart or hinged open.
5. **Remove all old glue.** Scrape and lightly sand dried glue off the tenon and out of the socket down to bare wood. This is the step that decides the repair: new PVA glue does not bond to old cured glue, and gluing over it fails within weeks. → *Expect:* mating surfaces look and feel like bare wood, dry-fit is snug.
6. **Glue, clamp, and square.** Coat both surfaces thinly, reassemble, wipe squeeze-out with a damp rag, and pull the frame tight with the band clamp around all four legs. Before walking away, set the chair upright on the flat surface and check it sits flat and the seat frame diagonals measure equal, then let it cure 24 hours undisturbed. → *Expect:* joints closed with a fine glue line, no rock while clamped, equal diagonals.
7. **Fit felt pads and final-test.** Clean the leg feet, apply felt pads, set the chair on its real floor, rock-test and sit-test with full adult weight and a deliberate lean. → *Expect:* silent, solid seat; no movement under a twist.

## Decision points

- Joint is loose but will not come apart by hand and mallet → do not force it past cracking; inject glue instead (drill a discreet 2 mm hole into the socket, syringe in glue, clamp). Second-best to a clean re-glue, but far better than gluing the outside seam.
- Antique or pre-1950s chair with crystallized brown glue → likely hide glue, which softens with heat and moisture and rebonds to itself; either re-glue with hide glue or hand a valuable piece to a restorer, since PVA over hide glue fails.
- Stripped screw hole in wood → pack the hole with wood-glue-coated toothpicks or a hardwood matchstick, let dry, trim flush, re-drive the screw into the new bite.
- Cracked or snapped rail (not just loose) → this is a break repair, not a wobble fix; glue both faces of a clean fresh break immediately and clamp, or replace the part.

## Failure modes & recovery

- **F1 Wobble returns within weeks of gluing:** joint moves again → old glue was left in the socket; reopen, clean to bare wood properly, re-glue.
- **F2 Chair cured out of square:** solid but rocks after unclamping → the clamp pulled it crooked; reheat/soften is impractical with PVA, so treat the high leg with the step-2 shim method rather than re-breaking joints.
- **F3 Glue starves or joint stays slightly open:** visible gap at the shoulder after clamping → clamp pressure was late or low; if within 15 minutes, re-open, add glue, re-clamp harder; after cure, inject at the gap.
- **F4 Overtightened fastener spins freely:** screw turns without gripping → stripped threads; use the toothpick fix from decision points, or the next-gauge screw.
- **F5 Loose joint too sloppy to grip glue:** tenon rattles in its socket even dry → bulk it up: glue a shaving or cotton thread wrap around the tenon, let dry, then glue the joint normally.

## Verification

On a verified-flat surface the chair shows no rock in any direction, no frame movement under a firm twist at the backrest, and it holds a full-weight seated adult leaning side to side silently; felt pads are on all four feet.

## Variations

- Metal-framed chairs: wobble is nearly always fastener or floor; add thread-locker fluid to bolts that repeatedly loosen.
- Flat-pack chairs with cam locks: retighten cams and dowels; wobble that survives means missing dowel glue, one drop per dowel cures it permanently (and ends future disassembly).
- Bar stools and swivel chairs: check the base plate and gas-lift collar bolts before blaming legs.

## Safety & privacy

Low risk. Wear eye protection when tapping joints apart, keep wood glue off skin and floors (wipes off wet, chisels off dry), and do not return a structurally cracked chair to service on the strength of a surface glue line; a chair that fails under a sitter causes real falls.
