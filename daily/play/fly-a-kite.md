## Goal

A kite launched without a running start, flown stable at altitude, and landed gently, in a spot chosen so the only thing it can hit is grass.

## Preconditions

- A kite with its line wound on a spool or handle; a simple delta or diamond for beginners.
- Wind between a light and a fresh breeze: leaves and small branches moving, roughly 10 to 25 km/h. Dead calm and storm gusts both ground the day.
- An open site with the hard rule attached: **far from power lines, roads, airports, and trees.** Kite string plus power lines is the recipe's one genuinely dangerous combination; wet string doubly so.

## Steps

1. **Read the wind and take your position.** Feel the direction (grass throws, flag checks), then stand with your *back* to the wind; the kite launches downwind of you. → *Expect:* wind on the back of your neck, open space downwind for twice your intended line length.
2. **Assemble and preflight the kite.** Spars seated, bridle untangled, line tied to the bridle point (not a spar), tail attached if the kite has one. → *Expect:* a taut sail, symmetric frame, line running clean off the spool.
3. **Launch from the hand, no running.** Hold the kite up by its bottom edge at arm's length (or have a helper hold it 10 to 20 meters downwind), wait for a gust, release as you pull the line firmly. Running launches teach nothing except falling. → *Expect:* the kite climbing away on the gust as you pull; pumping the line (pull-release-pull) gains altitude in light wind.
4. **Feed line to climb, tension to steer.** Let line out smoothly while the kite pulls; stop feeding when it sits at a stable angle. Pull in to make it climb and gain authority; ease out to descend. → *Expect:* the kite parked in steady wind above the turbulent ground layer, tugging gently, needing only occasional corrections.
5. **Handle trouble by line, not by hope.** Dives and loops: give slack the moment it noses over (slack kills its speed), then re-tension as it settles. Persistent circling: add or lengthen the tail (stability) or the wind is too strong for this kite. → *Expect:* recoveries becoming reflexive; the dive-slack response is the whole pilot's license.
6. **Land deliberately.** Walk toward the kite while winding, or hand-over-hand the line down; in stronger wind, walk the line down with a helper collecting the kite before it can drag. Wind the line properly on the spool as you go — future-you inherits either a spool or a nest. → *Expect:* kite down flat and undamaged, line fully wound, site clear.

## Decision points

- Wind too strong (kite pulling hard, line singing): land now; broken lines and dragged kids come from stubbornness in freshening wind, and the string can cut hands — gloves or a wrap around the spool, never around fingers.
- Kids on the line: clip or hand them the spool with the line already low-tension, stay hands-adjacent, and keep sessions shorter than their arms think.
- No wind after all: the kite becomes a picnic decoration with dignity; running to simulate wind entertains only the spectators.

## Failure modes & recovery

- **F1 Kite refuses to climb, keeps sagging:** wind too light or too turbulent near obstacles; move to more open ground and use the pump technique, or accept the day's verdict.
- **F2 Line tangle mid-flight:** do not fight knots under tension; land first, untangle on the grass (`daily/play`'s cousin skill: patience).
- **F3 Tree capture:** gentle line pulls only; a snagged kite yanked hard becomes a permanent installation. Slack, angle changes, and walking around the tree free most; the rest cost either a climb-safe adult decision or a philosophical shrug.
- **F4 Kite into a power line:** let go of the line immediately and walk away; nothing about that kite is retrievable by you — utility numbers exist for lines with debris. This is the one failure with real stakes, which is why the site rule leads the recipe.

## Verification

Launched from the hand, flew stable above the ground layer with dive-recovery demonstrated at least once, landed on grass with the line wound on the spool, and the whole session stayed the mandated distance from wires, roads, and trees.

## Variations

- Two-line stunt kites turn steering into the whole sport; learn the single-line stability instincts first.
- Beach flying adds the best wind and the softest landings; watch for other flyers' lines crossing yours (crossed lines saw each other).

## Safety & privacy

Medium risk with one sharp point: power lines, plus string cuts in strong wind and sunburnt necks from looking up all afternoon (`daily/self-care/apply-sunscreen` applies vertically). Everything else is physics on a string being exactly as delightful as advertised.
