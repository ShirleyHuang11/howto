## Goal

A child has planted a seed in a pot with their own hands, the pot sits on a suitable windowsill with a care routine the child owns, and a sprout appears within the seed's normal germination window.

## Preconditions

- Fast, reliable seeds. This matters more than anything else: pick beans, peas, sunflowers, radishes, or cress. These germinate in 3 to 10 days (cress in 2 to 4) and are big enough for small fingers. Avoid slow or fussy seeds (peppers, lavender); a kid's patience budget is about a week.
- A pot with drainage holes and a saucer, roughly 10 cm or larger. A yogurt cup with holes poked in the bottom works fine and the kid can decorate it.
- Potting mix (not garden soil or "moisture control" mixes). A small bag from any garden store.
- A sunny windowsill (south-facing in the northern hemisphere), a spray bottle or small cup for watering, and newspaper or a tray to contain the mess.
- Realistic adult expectations: the mess is the activity. Budget for it.

## Steps

1. **Set up a mess zone and let the kid do the touching.** Newspaper down, sleeves up. Your hands stay off the materials except to demonstrate; narrate instead of doing. → *Expect:* the kid is handling soil and seeds; you are talking, not planting.
2. **Fill the pot with potting mix to 2 cm below the rim.** Kid scoops, you level. Tap the pot gently to settle; do not pack it hard. → *Expect:* loose, fluffy soil near the rim with room for watering.
3. **Pre-moisten the soil.** Water a little at a time and let the kid stir with a finger until the mix feels like a wrung-out sponge, damp but not dripping. Dry potting mix repels water at first; this step prevents the seed washing out later. → *Expect:* uniformly dark, damp soil; no puddle.
4. **Plant the seed at the right depth.** Rule of thumb the kid can learn: a seed goes as deep as two or three times its own size. Big bean: knuckle-deep hole, seed in, cover loosely. Tiny cress: sprinkle on the surface, dust of soil over. Plant 2 or 3 seeds per pot as insurance. → *Expect:* seeds covered at roughly the right depth; the kid can say the depth rule back to you.
5. **Water gently and place on the windowsill.** Spray bottle or a slow dribble at the edge, not a pour on top of the seed. Saucer under the pot. → *Expect:* surface moist, seed still where it was planted, pot in bright light.
6. **Make the waiting visible.** [BRANCH: plant one extra bean in a wet paper towel inside a clear jar or zip bag taped to the window (germination is visible in 2 to 3 days, which carries the kid through the invisible soil wait) | draw a calendar and cross off days, with the expected sprout window marked] → *Expect:* the kid has something to check daily that changes before the soil does.
7. **Hand over a bounded care routine.** The kid's job: touch the soil every morning; if the top feels dry, water a little. Not more. Overwatering by an enthusiastic child is the number-one killer here. → *Expect:* the kid states the rule ("finger test first, then water") unprompted.
8. **Greet the sprout and thin later.** When sprouts are up and 3 to 4 cm tall, keep the strongest one per pot and snip (not pull) the others at soil level, pulling disturbs the winner's roots. Let the kid choose the keeper. → *Expect:* one healthy seedling per pot, leaning toward the window.
9. **Rotate the pot a quarter turn every couple of days.** Kid-friendly job that prevents a permanently bent stem. → *Expect:* seedling grows roughly straight over the following week.

## Decision points

- Kid loses interest by day 3 → point at the paper-towel jar (step 6), where the root is visibly longer than yesterday; interest usually reboots on visible change.
- Nothing has sprouted at the outer edge of the germination window plus 4 days → excavate one seed gently with the kid and inspect: swollen with a root nub means wait; mushy or unchanged means replant fresh seeds a bit shallower (framed as "attempt two", not failure).
- No sunny windowsill available → choose cress or pea shoots, which tolerate lower light and are edible in 2 weeks, giving a payoff without strong sun.

## Failure modes & recovery

- **F1 Soil constantly soggy, possible fuzzy mold:** overwatering → move the pot somewhere brighter and airier, stop watering until the top 2 cm dry out, and re-teach the finger test; if the seed rotted, replant.
- **F2 Seedling tall, pale, and floppy:** not enough light (it is stretching) → move to the brightest window immediately and rotate daily; badly stretched seedlings of beans can be replanted deeper up to the first leaves.
- **F3 Seed sits on the surface after watering:** washed out by a hard pour → push it back to depth, switch to spray-bottle watering.
- **F4 Kid pulls the seed up daily "to check":** normal curiosity, fatal to the seed → give the checking impulse a legal target: the clear-jar bean is the one you may look at; the pot is the one we trust.

## Verification

A live seedling stands in the pot on the windowsill within the seed packet's germination window (plus a few days); the child can state the finger-test watering rule and has performed it at least three times without prompting; the pot drains into its saucer, not the sill.

## Variations

- Edible fast track: cress on damp cotton wool in an eggshell face grows "hair" the kid can trim onto a sandwich within 2 weeks.
- Outdoor transplant: once a bean or sunflower has two sets of true leaves and outdoor nights are frost-free, harden it off (a few hours outside daily for 3 to 5 days) before planting out with the kid.
- Classroom scale: paper-towel bags for every kid, one shared pot; germination race with daily measurements.

## Safety & privacy

Low risk. Seeds are a choking hazard for children under 3; buy untreated seeds (some commercial seed is fungicide-coated, often dyed pink or blue, and should not be handled by kids who mouth their fingers). Wash hands after handling potting mix, and keep it away from faces; adults with immune concerns should moisten mix before use to avoid dust.
