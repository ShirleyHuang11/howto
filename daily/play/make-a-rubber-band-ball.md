## Goal

You can build a round rubber band ball from a small core by wrapping bands in changing directions with even tension.

## Preconditions

- A pile of rubber bands in mixed sizes.
- A small core such as folded paper, a crumpled band bundle, or an eraser.
- Clear table space away from small children and pets.

## Steps

1. **Make the core.** Fold a small piece of paper tightly or wad several rubber bands into a compact lump. → *Expect:* a firm center about marble size.
2. **Anchor the first bands.** Wrap two or three bands around the core in different directions so they cross and hold the shape. → *Expect:* the core no longer unfolds.
3. **Change direction every wrap.** Add each new band around a different axis: north-south, east-west, then diagonal. → *Expect:* the ball grows evenly instead of becoming a flat bundle.
4. **Use steady tension.** Stretch bands enough to grip, but not so much that they are near snapping. [BRANCH: loose band | tight band] Twist loose bands once; discard bands that feel brittle or overstretched. → *Expect:* each band lies snugly without biting into one groove.
5. **Correct the shape early.** If one side bulges, wrap the next bands around the smaller side to build it out. → *Expect:* the ball becomes rounder every few wraps.
6. **Size up gradually.** Use small bands near the core and larger bands as the ball grows. → *Expect:* bands fit without excessive twisting.
7. **Finish with clean wraps.** Add the last few bands evenly spaced so the surface looks tidy and holds together. → *Expect:* the ball can be tossed hand to hand without shedding bands.

## Decision points

- If bands snap, they are old, sun-damaged, or stretched too far.
- If the ball becomes egg-shaped, wrap across the narrow dimension until it rounds out.
- If the core slips out, add two crossing anchor bands and squeeze the center smaller.

## Failure modes & recovery

- **F1 Loose nest:** bands slide off the core → start smaller and use tighter crossing wraps.
- **F2 Flat puck:** every band follows the same path → rotate the ball a quarter-turn between wraps.
- **F3 Snapping bands:** tension is excessive or bands are brittle → discard suspect bands and protect eyes.
- **F4 Lumpy surface:** one area receives too many wraps → build the opposite side with several bands.

## Verification

The rubber band ball is roughly round, holds together when bounced lightly in your hand, and has bands crossing in several directions.

## Variations

- A marble or eraser core makes the early stage easier, but creates a harder ball.
- Color bands can be layered into stripes if each color is wrapped around the same axis for a few turns.
- Very large balls become heavy and need thicker bands.

## Safety & privacy

Low risk. Rubber bands can snap toward eyes, so stretch them away from faces, discard brittle bands, and keep the finished ball away from small children who might mouth loose bands.
