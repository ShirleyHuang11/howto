## Goal

A scavenger hunt is planned and staged: clues hidden in a defined area, a difficulty ladder that keeps every player engaged, and a prize at the end that someone actually finds.

## Preconditions

- A bounded play area you control or have permission to use (house, yard, park section).
- Paper and pen for clues, tape or containers for hiding, a prize (small treats work better than one big item when players are kids).
- Knowledge of the players: ages, reading level, group size. This drives every design choice below.

## Steps

1. **Define the boundary explicitly.** Walk the area and pick landmarks players can name ("the fence, the driveway, nothing upstairs"). Write the boundary on the first clue sheet. → *Expect:* a boundary you can state in one sentence, with no ambiguous zones (garages, neighbors' property, water) inside it.
2. **Choose hunt structure.** [BRANCH: chain hunt (each clue points to the next, ends at the prize; best for one team) | checklist hunt (find N listed items in any order; best for competing teams, no shared clue to camp on)] → *Expect:* structure matches group: chain for cooperation, checklist for racing.
3. **Write clues on a difficulty ladder.** First clue trivially easy (early success hooks players), middle clues moderate, and only the last one or two genuinely hard. For mixed ages, write each clue with a picture hint on the back so non-readers stay in. → *Expect:* 6-10 clues; you can solve each yourself in under 2 minutes; the first is solvable by the youngest player.
4. **Number every clue on its back.** This is the non-obvious step that saves the hunt: when clue 4 goes missing, you know instantly which one to re-seed. → *Expect:* every clue carries its sequence number and the hunt is reconstructable from your master list.
5. **Hide in reverse order, prize first.** Place the prize, then walk the chain backwards so each clue you hide points to a spot you have already confirmed. Waist height or below for young kids; never inside anything that latches shut. → *Expect:* every clue physically present, secure against wind, at the reachable height of the shortest player.
6. **Do a full dry run yourself.** Follow the chain from clue 1 reading only what is written, not what you meant. → *Expect:* you reach the prize without using memory; any clue that made you pause gets rewritten now.
7. **Brief the players and launch.** State the boundary, the "ask for a hint after 5 stuck minutes" rule, and (checklist hunts) the return time. Keep the master list in your pocket. → *Expect:* players moving toward clue 1; you positioned where you can see most of the area.
8. **Shadow the hunt and drip hints.** Hint ladder: hot/cold first, then "look lower", then point at the zone. Never hand over the answer; the find must feel earned. → *Expect:* stuck time never exceeds ~5 minutes; energy stays up through the hard clues.

## Decision points

- Group larger than ~6 → split into teams with separate clue chains (different colored paper) so nobody trails uselessly behind the fast kids.
- Outdoor hunt and rain likely → clues into zip bags or move the whole hunt indoors; soggy paper ends hunts abruptly.
- Adult players → invert the ladder: open hard, and use wordplay or coordinates instead of location riddles.

## Failure modes & recovery

- **F1 Clue missing (wind, dog, another kid found it):** players stuck at a dead spot longer than the hint ladder fixes → check your master list, verbally give the missing clue's content, and re-seed from your list. This is why step 4 exists.
- **F2 One player finds everything:** the fast kid solo-runs the chain → impose "a different person opens each clue" mid-hunt; it is an accepted convention, not a rule change.
- **F3 Prize found early by accident:** someone stumbles onto the endpoint → laugh it off, relocate the prize while they finish the chain, and quietly reward the accidental finder too.
- **F4 Boundary breach:** players drift out of the area → recall everyone, restate the boundary, and add a visible marker (chalk line, cone) at the breach point before resuming.

## Verification

The prize has been found by following the clues (not by accident), every clue on the master list is accounted for and collected, all players stayed inside the boundary, and nobody disengaged for more than a few minutes.

## Variations

- Photo hunt: checklist of things to photograph instead of collect; works in public spaces where you cannot hide objects.
- Night hunt (older kids/adults): flashlights required, reflective tape on clues, boundary tightened to half the daytime area.
- Long-distance version: clues sent by text with photo confirmation of each find; the "prize" is a mailed package.

## Safety & privacy

Low risk. Keep hiding spots away from roads, water, tools, and anything that latches shut. In public parks, brief kids on the stay-in-sight rule and never write a child's name or address on clues that strangers might find.
