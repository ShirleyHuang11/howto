## Goal

Better-than-chance results at rock paper scissors against ordinary human opponents, using the documented behavioral biases of the species — plus the meta-skill of running the match itself cleanly so the result stands.

## Preconditions

- An opponent playing casually (the edges below exploit human habits; against a randomizing nerd or a coin, the game is fair and this recipe gracefully retires).
- Agreement on the protocol *before* throwing: best-of-how-many, throw on "three" or on "shoot," and what counts as a late throw. Half of all RPS disputes are protocol disputes.

## Steps

1. **Fix the protocol out loud.** "Best of three, throw on three: one, two, THROW." Demonstrate the cadence once. → *Expect:* both parties throwing simultaneously in the practice beat; the dispute surface pre-drained.
2. **Round one against a new opponent: throw paper.** The most reliable bias in the literature: beginners, and especially males in casual play, open with rock more often than chance. Paper harvests this quietly. → *Expect:* a statistically tilted first round; treat a loss as information, not refutation.
3. **After they lose a round: expect their upgrade.** Losers tend to switch to the throw that would have *beaten* what just beat them (lost to your paper → they play scissors next). So after winning with paper, play rock. → *Expect:* the "losers step forward" cycle paying off across rounds.
4. **After they win a round: expect the repeat.** Winners tend to stay with their winning throw. Beat the throw they just won with (they won with rock → you play paper). → *Expect:* the "winners stand still" bias captured.
5. **Break your own patterns.** The biases run both ways; a sharp opponent reads you. Between rounds, genuinely randomize your fallback (seconds on a watch, mental die) whenever no read is live. → *Expect:* your own throws unpredictable except when deliberately exploiting steps 2 to 4.
6. **Close the match with grace.** Announce the score as it stands, offer the rematch ritual once, and never explain the method mid-series — the edge evaporates on contact with air, and the mystique is half the fun. → *Expect:* a decided match, a baffled opponent, and the recipe's secrecy intact until the car ride home.

## Decision points

- High-stakes single throws (who does the dishes): the paper opener is your best single-shot edge; announce the cadence firmly so the throw is truly simultaneous.
- Against children: they telegraph (watch the hand start to flatten or fork mid-count) — and the ethical setting is to lose theatrically about half the time, because the recipe's real purpose there is delight.
- Tournament rules (yes, these exist): straight-hand "paper" flat, scissors as a proper V, no vertical rock-hammer ambiguity; adopt them whenever money or chores ride on it.

## Failure modes & recovery

- **F1 Simultaneity dispute ("you threw late!"):** replay the round without argument and re-fix the cadence; the protocol from step 1 exists so this happens at most once.
- **F2 Your reads keep losing:** the opponent knows the same biases and is counter-exploiting; switch to pure randomization (step 5) and the game returns to fair, which against a counter-reader is your best case.
- **F3 Tilted after losses, throwing angry rock:** you have become the exploitable one; breathe, randomize, and remember the sample size is small and the dishes are just dishes.
- **F4 Accused of dark arts after winning too often:** correct response is a single raised eyebrow; alternate response is teaching them this recipe, which converts an opponent into a rival and improves all future matches.

## Verification

Protocol was fixed before the first throw, your openers and adaptations followed the bias table rather than habit, your fallback throws were genuinely randomized, the score was cleanly agreed — and over enough casual matches, you win noticeably more than a third of decided rounds.

## Variations

- Rock-paper-scissors-lizard-Spock expands the graph and dilutes the biases; the winners-stay/losers-upgrade dynamics still apply.
- Cultural cadences differ (`jp` janken's "saisho wa guu" opening ritual standardizes the first beat brilliantly); adopt the local liturgy and the disputes vanish.

## Safety & privacy

None, unless you count the metaphysical risk of taking a children's game this seriously — which the corpus officially endorses. The real lesson smuggled inside: humans are predictable under pressure, protocols prevent disputes, and randomness is a skill.
