## Goal

Recognizable animal shadows appear crisp on a wall, you can hold and animate three or four classic shapes, and a short shadow story has been performed for an audience.

## Preconditions

- A single bright point-ish light source: phone flashlight, desk lamp with the shade off, or a bare flashlight. Diffuse ceiling lights will not work.
- A pale, flat wall or a hung white sheet, with 2-3 meters of clear space in front of it.
- A room you can make dark, or at least dim.

## Steps

1. **Set up the light.** Place the light 2-3 m from the wall, at roughly the height your hands will be, resting on a table or shelf so it cannot move. Kill the other lights. → *Expect:* an even bright patch on the wall; the darker the room, the higher the contrast.
2. **Find the sharp zone.** Put your hand in the beam and slide it between light and wall. Close to the wall: small, sharp shadow. Close to the light: huge, blurry shadow. → *Expect:* you have located the distance (usually 30-60 cm from the wall) where the shadow is both large and crisp; this is your performance zone.
3. **Learn the dog.** Palm facing sideways to the beam, fingers together and extended for the snout, thumb up for the ear, pinky dropped slightly to open the mouth. → *Expect:* a dog head profile on the wall; flapping the pinky makes it "bark".
4. **Learn the bird.** Hook both thumbs together, palms facing the wall, fingers spread as wings; flap by bending the fingers, not the wrists. → *Expect:* a bird whose wings beat smoothly; wrist-flapping instead makes it lurch, which is the usual beginner error.
5. **Learn the rabbit.** One fist knuckles-out for the head, index and middle finger up in a V for ears; the other hand cupped under it as the body. Wiggle the two ear fingers independently. → *Expect:* a rabbit with twitching ears; small finger motions read as large on the wall.
6. **Add one hard shape.** [BRANCH: crocodile (two hands stacked palm-to-palm, hinging open as jaws) | wolf howl (dog shape tilted 45° upward with the mouth open)] → *Expect:* the shape reads instantly to someone who was not told what it is; if they have to guess, simplify the silhouette.
7. **Compose a 2-minute story.** Three beats is enough: characters meet, a chase or problem, a resolution. Move puppets by walking your hands across the beam; grow a character by moving toward the light, shrink it by retreating to the wall. → *Expect:* a story arc where size changes and entrances/exits are deliberate, not accidental.
8. **Perform.** Audience sits facing the wall; you crouch below or beside the beam so your head and body stay out of the light. Narrate in character voices. → *Expect:* the audience sees only puppets, never your silhouette blocking half the screen.

## Decision points

- Only diffuse lamps available → phone flashlight face-down over the edge of a table beats any shaded lamp; a fuzzy double shadow means the source is too big or too far.
- Multiple shadows on the wall → more than one light is on somewhere; hunt down and kill secondary sources including TVs and hallway spill.
- Audience of young kids → keep villains mild and add sound effects; the dark room itself can be scary, so leave a dim night light behind the audience.

## Failure modes & recovery

- **F1 Blurry shadow everywhere:** edges fuzzy at every distance → the source is too large or diffuse; switch to a smaller, brighter point source or move the light further back.
- **F2 Hands cramp mid-show:** shapes collapsing after a few minutes → shake out between scenes and alternate which hand carries the complex shape; nobody holds a crocodile for ten minutes.
- **F3 Shape unrecognizable:** audience guesses wrong → rotate your hand so the wall sees the profile, not the palm; shadows are silhouettes and only the outline exists.
- **F4 Performer silhouette invades the screen:** your head shadow appears → drop lower or shift sideways out of the beam cone; check by glancing at the wall, not the light.

## Verification

A person who was not told the shapes can name the dog, bird, and rabbit from their shadows alone, the story ran start to finish with at least one size change, and the light source never got knocked over.

## Variations

- Cardboard cutout puppets on sticks: trace, cut, tape to skewers; frees your hands for scenery and survives long shows.
- Sunlight version: low afternoon sun through a window makes a natural beam; no darkness needed but no size control either.
- Screen show: hang a sheet and put the light behind it with performers between light and sheet; the audience on the far side sees a classic shadow theater.

## Safety & privacy

Low risk. Incandescent and halogen bulbs get hot: never touch the bulb and keep paper puppets away from it (LED sources are cool and safer). Keep the floor path clear since performers move in the dark.
