## Goal

You can settle into a calm back float in shallow supervised water, breathe normally, and recover to standing without panic.

## Preconditions

- A lifeguarded pool or a competent adult supervising within arm's reach.
- Shallow water where you can stand with your head fully above the surface.
- No alcohol, sedating medication, rough water, current, or solo practice.
- Goggles are optional; comfort matters more than equipment.

## Steps

1. **Choose the safe practice spot.** Stand in chest-deep or shallower pool water near the wall, with your supervisor beside you. → *Expect:* you can stand instantly and hold the wall without swimming.
2. **Fill the lungs gently.** Take a comfortable breath in, keep the chest broad, and avoid forcing a giant inhale. Full lungs add buoyancy. → *Expect:* your upper body feels lifted before you lean back.
3. **Let the head go back.** Bend knees, lean back, put ears in the water, and look at the ceiling or sky. The face can stay above water while the ears submerge. → *Expect:* the water supports the back of your head instead of your neck straining.
4. **Open the body shape.** Let hips rise, spread arms slightly, and let legs drift long. [BRANCH: legs sink | body rises] If legs sink, do not kick hard; press the chest slightly upward and relax the knees. → *Expect:* your torso floats first and your legs become lighter.
5. **Scull with quiet hands.** Move hands in small figure-eights beside your hips, palms angled like tiny wings. → *Expect:* gentle pressure on the palms keeps you level without splashing.
6. **Breathe on purpose.** Inhale calmly, exhale slowly, and notice that the body sinks a little on exhale and rises on inhale. → *Expect:* floating feels adjustable rather than fragile.
7. **Practice panic recovery.** Tuck the chin, bring knees toward the chest, sweep hands downward, and put both feet on the bottom. → *Expect:* you can return to standing in one controlled motion.
8. **Repeat shorter floats.** Float for 3 seconds, stand, then 5 seconds, then 10 seconds, always ending before panic takes over. → *Expect:* each repetition ends by choice, not surprise.

## Decision points

- If water enters your nose, lower the float duration and use a nose clip until confidence improves.
- If your legs sink every time, accept a slightly diagonal float; many bodies do not lie flat without motion.
- If you cannot stand easily, the water is too deep for this recipe.

## Failure modes & recovery

- **F1 Head lifted:** hips sink and the float collapses → put ears back in the water and look straight up.
- **F2 Breath held too hard:** the body becomes rigid → take a normal inhale and let small bubbles out through the nose.
- **F3 Panic flail:** splashing pushes the face lower → use the step 7 tuck, stand, hold the wall, and rest.
- **F4 Solo practice:** nobody can intervene if panic or cramps happen → stop and resume only with supervision.

## Verification

In shallow supervised water, you float on your back for 10 seconds, breathe during it, and recover to standing calmly.

## Variations

- A helper may support one hand under the shoulder blades, then remove support for one second at a time.
- Salt water and wetsuits increase buoyancy, but the recovery skill still belongs in shallow water first.
- A pool noodle under the knees can teach relaxation before unsupported attempts.

## Safety & privacy

High risk because water can become dangerous quickly. Practice only in shallow supervised water, stay within arm's reach of help, do not practice in open water for this first skill, and stop immediately if chilled, dizzy, cramped, or frightened.
