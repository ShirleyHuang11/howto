## Goal

Make a simple knitted cord using fingers instead of needles, then bind it off so it does not unravel.

## Preconditions

- A ball of yarn, preferably medium or bulky weight.
- Clean hands with rings removed if they snag yarn.
- A seated position where the yarn can unwind without tangling.

## Steps

1. **Anchor the tail.** Hold the yarn tail between thumb and palm, leaving about 15 cm free. → *Expect:* the tail stays trapped while the working yarn can move.
2. **Cast on across the fingers.** Weave the working yarn over and under four fingers from index to pinky, then back again. → *Expect:* each finger has one loop sitting near the base.
3. **Make a second row of loops.** Weave across the fingers again above the first row. → *Expect:* each finger has two loops, a lower loop and an upper loop.
4. **Pull over the index loop.** Lift the lower index-finger loop over the upper loop and off the fingertip. → *Expect:* one loop remains on the index finger.
5. **Pull over the remaining fingers.** Repeat the lower-over-upper move on middle, ring, and pinky fingers. → *Expect:* one loop remains on each finger and a cord starts forming behind the hand.
6. **Push loops down.** Slide the remaining loops back toward the base of the fingers without tightening hard. → *Expect:* there is room to weave the next row.
7. **Repeat rows.** Weave a new upper row, then pull each lower loop over again. → *Expect:* the cord grows from the back of the hand.
8. **Keep tension relaxed.** Tug the finished cord gently after every few rows, not the live loops. → *Expect:* stitches look even and fingers do not feel squeezed.
9. **Bind off.** Move the pinky loop to the ring finger, pull the lower ring loop over, then continue moving loops toward the index finger. → *Expect:* one final loop remains.
10. **Secure the final loop.** Cut the yarn with a tail, pass the tail through the final loop, and snug it. ⚠️ *Irreversible:* cutting yarn fixes the finished length, so confirm the cord is long enough first. → *Expect:* the cord stays intact when lightly stretched.

## Decision points

- Yarn is very thin → double it or use fewer fingers for a less floppy cord.
- Loops tighten painfully → loosen the next weave and push loops down only gently.
- You want a wider piece → use four-finger knitting for a cord, not a flat scarf; switch to arm knitting or needles for width.
- A child is learning → use bulky yarn and two fingers first.

## Failure modes & recovery

- **F1 Dropped loop:** detect a loose loop falling off a finger, recover by placing it back before the next row.
- **F2 Yarn tangle:** detect resistance from the ball, recover by pausing and freeing yarn before pulling stitches tight.
- **F3 Uneven cord:** detect tight sections and loose sections, recover by tugging the finished cord gently and using steadier tension.
- **F4 Unraveling end:** detect the last loop opening, recover by threading the tail through again and tying a small knot if needed.

## Verification

The finished cord has a secured beginning and end, and it does not unravel when lightly pulled.

## Variations

- Two-finger cord: faster for bracelets and drawstrings.
- Four-finger cord: thicker and more flexible for garlands.
- Color change: tie on a new yarn end between rows and weave loose tails into the cord.

## Safety & privacy

Low risk. Keep yarn away from very young children and pets, and do not wrap loops tightly enough to restrict circulation.
