## Goal

Tie a neat square knot that joins two ends of the same kind of cord for light duty bundling, then check that it is not the weaker granny knot.

## Preconditions

- Two free rope or cord ends of similar diameter and texture.
- Good light so the crossings can be inspected.
- A non-critical use: parcels, a bandage wrap, a bundle, or practice cord.
- Do not use this knot for climbing, towing, hammocks, rescue, or any load where failure can injure someone.

## Steps

1. **Hold one end in each hand.** Keep the standing parts hanging down and the working ends pointing up so the first crossing is easy to see. → *Expect:* two ends are separated, untwisted, and long enough to work with.
2. **Cross right over left.** Put the right working end over the left working end, then tuck it under and pull both ends until the first half-knot sits flat. → *Expect:* one simple overhand crossing holds the cords together.
3. **Cross left over right.** Take the end that is now on your left, put it over the end on your right, tuck it under, and pull gently. → *Expect:* the knot begins to look symmetrical, with matching loops on both sides.
4. **Dress the knot.** Slide the crossings together, remove twists, and align each working end beside its own standing part. → *Expect:* the two short tails leave the knot on the same side as their matching long parts.
5. **Set the knot.** Pull all four strands in pairs, first the standing parts, then the tails, using steady pressure. → *Expect:* the knot tightens into a compact, flat block.
6. **Check for the granny failure.** [BRANCH: square | granny] If both tails lie parallel to their own standing parts, continue; if one tail crosses away diagonally, untie and repeat right-over-left then left-over-right. → *Expect:* the finished knot is flat, balanced, and not twisted.
7. **Use only for light duty.** Place it on the bundle or wrap and leave tails at least several cord diameters long. → *Expect:* the knot holds light tension without capsizing when tugged by hand.

## Decision points

- Different cord sizes: choose another bend, since a square knot can spill when one rope is much thicker.
- Slippery synthetic cord: add backup overhand knots in the tails or use a better knot.
- A person, pet, vehicle, or heavy object depends on it: stop and choose a load-rated knot and gear.
- The knot must be untied later: leave generous tails and do not over-tighten it.

## Failure modes & recovery

- **F1 Granny knot:** tails exit diagonally instead of paired, untie fully and repeat right-over-left then left-over-right.
- **F2 Knot capsizes:** the bundle pulls the knot into a lopsided shape, remove load and use a more secure bend.
- **F3 Tails too short:** less than several cord diameters remain, retie with more tail before trusting it.
- **F4 Mixed materials slip:** one cord creeps through the knot, replace with matching cord or use a different knot.

## Verification

The square knot is verified when it lies flat, both tails pair with their matching standing parts, and a firm hand tug does not make it roll or spill.

## Variations

- `bandage`: keep pressure gentle and never tie so tight that skin beyond the wrap changes color.
- `package`: press the knot flat so it does not snag during handling.
- `practice`: tie it ten times without looking, then inspect every result before counting it.

## Safety & privacy

Risk is low only when the knot is used for light, non-life-supporting work. The real safety rule is to refuse square knots for climbing, towing, hanging loads, animal restraints, or anything over people.
