## Goal

The dog chases a thrown toy, brings it back, and releases it to your hand on cue, for a session that ends with the dog pleasantly tired rather than obsessive or lame.

## Preconditions

- A safe fetch toy sized for the dog: a ball big enough that it cannot slip past the molars into the throat (tennis-ball size is dangerous for large breeds; use an oversized rubber ball). Never sticks, which splinter mouths and can impale a dog that catches one end-first in the ground.
- Two identical toys (the core of teaching retrieve) and a few treats.
- An enclosed area, or a long line if recall is unproven; grass or dirt footing, not hot asphalt or slick tile.
- A dog warmed up with a couple minutes of walking, not straight off the couch.

## Steps

1. **Build toy value.** Wiggle the toy on the ground, let the dog grab it, play a few seconds of gentle tug, trade for a treat. A dog that does not care about the toy will not fetch it. → *Expect:* the dog orients to the toy the moment it appears.
2. **Throw short and low.** Say your release word ("get it") and throw the toy 3 to 5 meters, low and away from the dog, never at it or straight up over its head (vertical leaps and hard stops are how fetch injures joints). → *Expect:* the dog chases and picks the toy up.
3. **Trigger the return.** The moment the dog has the toy, crouch, open your arms, and move backward a few steps while calling its name brightly. Moving away pulls a dog toward you; walking at it starts a keep-away game. → *Expect:* the dog turns and comes at least most of the way back.
4. **Cue the drop with a trade.** Say "drop it" once, then present the second identical toy (or a treat) at its nose. When the jaw opens, mark ("yes"), let it take the trade, and pick up the dropped toy. Never chase or pry the mouth. → *Expect:* the toy hits the ground within a couple seconds of the cue.
5. **Restart instantly.** Throw the toy you now hold; the fast restart itself becomes the reward for the drop and the trade toy loses its value. → *Expect:* after several cycles, the dog starts dropping the toy at your feet unprompted to make the next throw happen.
6. **Add structure as skill grows.** Ask for a sit before some throws; lengthen throws gradually; occasionally reward the return with a treat instead of a throw to keep the recall strong. → *Expect:* sit-throw-return-drop cycles running smoothly without chasing or shouting.
7. **Stop before the dog does.** End after 10 to 20 throws, or at the first sign of heavy panting, wide flat tongue, lagging returns, or any limp. Fetch-obsessed dogs will run themselves into heatstroke; the human ends the game, always with a final treat and a calm minute of leash walking. → *Expect:* the dog is tired but recovers normal breathing within a few minutes.

## Decision points

- Hot day (above roughly 25 °C, or humid) → shorten drastically, play in shade with water available, or swap fetch for indoor nose games; flat-faced breeds overheat far sooner.
- Puppy under about 12 to 18 months → few, short, low throws only on soft ground; repetitive sprint-and-brake loads growing joints.
- Dog guards the toy (stiffens, growls when you approach it) → stop reaching for toys entirely; trade at a distance by tossing the treat away from the toy, and get a trainer's help for resource guarding.
- Dog loves chasing but never picks the toy up → play with a tug-friendly toy instead and build the pickup indoors first.

## Failure modes & recovery

- **F1 Dog plays keep-away:** you have chased it before → never chase; turn and run away, sit on the ground, or start playing with the second toy loudly by yourself until the dog trades in.
- **F2 Dog will not release the toy:** trade value too low → upgrade to a better treat or a more exciting second toy; do not pry the mouth open.
- **F3 Dog loses interest after two throws:** toy too boring or dog tired → shorten sessions, use the toy only for fetch (put it away after), and re-run step 1.
- **F4 Dog limps during or after play:** stop immediately, leash up, check paw pads for cuts and thorns → rest; a limp that persists past a day is a vet visit.
- **F5 Gagging or the ball disappears into the mouth:** ball too small → this is an emergency airway risk; if breathing is blocked, push firmly upward on the throat from outside below the ball and get to a vet. Prevent it by sizing up the ball.

## Verification

Across the last five throws of a session the dog returned to within arm's reach and released the toy on one "drop it" cue, the session ended on your initiative with the dog breathing normally within minutes, and the toy is put away until next time.

## Variations

- Water fetch: floating toy only, calm shallow water, and watch for water swallowing during grabs; rinse coat after.
- Ball launcher: extends throws but multiplies joint load and overheating risk; halve the throw count.
- Indoor hallway fetch: soft toy, carpet or rug runner, no furniture corners in the run path; good for rainy days.

## Safety & privacy

Low risk with the right toy; most fetch harm comes from three things: undersized balls (choking), sticks (mouth impalement), and overdoing it (heatstroke, soft-tissue injury). In public parks keep the dog leashed until recall is proven, give other dogs and people wide berth, and stop the game whenever another dog approaches so the toy does not spark a fight.
