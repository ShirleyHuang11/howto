## Goal

You can run a three-ball cascade for at least 10 consecutive catches, built from a stable one-ball arc and a clean two-ball exchange.

## Preconditions

- Three balls of equal size and weight, ideally beanbags (they do not roll away, which halves your practice overhead). Tennis balls work; filled balloons or fruit do not.
- Standing room with nothing fragile in a 2 m radius and a ceiling above head-plus-arm height.
- Expect this to take multiple sessions across days. Drills below are ordered; do not skip ahead when frustrated, skip back.

## Steps

1. **Set your stance and frame.** Feet shoulder-width, elbows at your sides bent 90°, hands at waist height, palms up. Throws come from waist height; catches return there. Eyes fixed at the peak height, not on your hands. → *Expect:* hands stay in a "tray" plane at your waist; you can see both hands with peripheral vision while looking at eye level.
2. **Drill the one-ball arc (10-15 min).** Throw from one hand to the other in an arc peaking at eye level, crossing your midline. Same height every time, no hand movement to rescue bad throws: let bad ones drop. → *Expect:* 20 consecutive throws landing in the waiting palm without the catching hand chasing; equal quality both directions.
3. **Drill the two-ball exchange (this is the real gatekeeper, often 1-2 sessions).** One ball in each hand. Throw ball A; when A peaks, throw B from the other hand under it; catch A, catch B. Say "throw, throw, catch, catch" out loud. → *Expect:* two distinct throws and two distinct catches, both balls tracing arcs. The classic failure is handing B across instead of throwing it: if B never rises to eye level, you are passing, not juggling.
4. **Mirror the exchange.** Repeat step 3 starting from the other hand until both sides are equal. Almost everyone has a reluctant hand; it gets extra reps. → *Expect:* 10 clean exchanges starting left and 10 starting right.
5. **Add the third ball: the flash.** Two balls in your dominant hand, one in the other. Throw 1 (front ball of the pair), then 2 when 1 peaks, then 3 when 2 peaks; catch all three and stop deliberately. → *Expect:* three throws, three catches, then stillness. Stopping on purpose is the drill; do not chase a fourth throw yet.
6. **Extend the cascade.** Same pattern, keep throwing: every ball is thrown when the incoming ball peaks, always thrown under the incoming arc, always to the same eye-level height. Count catches out loud. → *Expect:* runs lengthening over sessions: 4, then 7, then 10+ catches. Rhythm should sound even, like a slow clock.
7. **Fix forward drift.** [BRANCH: pattern creeps forward and you chase it (throws are slightly outward: practice facing a wall an arm's length away, or juggle over a bed) | pattern stays in plane (continue)] → *Expect:* your feet do not move during a 10-catch run.

## Decision points

- Shoulders burning or throws decaying → stop the session; juggling is motor learning and consolidates between sessions. Three 15-minute sessions beat one 45-minute grind.
- Balls colliding mid-air → throws are too close to the midline and same-height; make each arc peak slightly toward the catching side, alternating.
- Progress stalled for a full session at step 6 → drop back one step for 5 minutes; the earlier drill has decayed, not you.

## Failure modes & recovery

- **F1 Passing instead of throwing (step 3):** the second ball travels flat across, never peaking → slow down, exaggerate both arcs to eye level, and say the throw-throw-catch-catch chant; speed is never the fix.
- **F2 Forward drift:** you walk forward chasing the pattern → wall drill or over-a-bed drill from step 7; the cause is throwing "out" instead of "up".
- **F3 One hand always fails:** the non-dominant side drops its throws → return to one-ball arcs, non-dominant throws only, 5 minutes before every session.
- **F4 Panic acceleration:** each throw faster than the last until collapse → freeze after every third throw (repeated flashes) until the tempo internalizes; the pattern needs less speed than your panic thinks.

## Verification

Standing on a mark, you complete a three-ball cascade of at least 10 catches without stepping off the mark, and you can start the pattern from either hand.

## Variations

- Over-the-bed practice: cuts pickup time dramatically during the drop-heavy stages; also removes drop noise for apartment practice.
- Scarf juggling: featherweight chiffon scarves slow the whole pattern down; a good pre-step for kids or anyone finding beanbag speed overwhelming.
- Next steps after 30+ catches: reverse cascade (throw over the incoming ball) or the shower pattern (circular, one hand high-throws, the other feeds).

## Safety & privacy

Low risk. Clear lamps, screens, and drinks from the practice radius, and mind ceiling fans and light fixtures. Never learn with hard or heavy objects; face and finger hits during learning are guaranteed, so make them beanbag hits.
