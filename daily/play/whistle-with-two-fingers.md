## Goal

A loud two-finger whistle is produced by shaping the fingers, lips, tongue, and airflow into a small controlled opening.

## Preconditions

- Clean hands and short enough fingernails to avoid scratching the mouth.
- Private or outdoor practice space where loud sounds are acceptable.
- Hydrated mouth, but not full of food or gum.
- Patience for many silent attempts.
- No mouth injury or dental work that makes pressure unsafe.

## Steps

1. **Wash hands.** Clean fingers thoroughly before putting them in the mouth. → *Expect:* hands are visibly clean and rinsed.
2. **Choose finger shape.** [BRANCH: two index fingers | two middle fingers | thumb and index finger in an O] → *Expect:* chosen fingers can form a narrow V or ring.
3. **Wet the lips lightly.** Moisten lips so they seal around fingers. → *Expect:* lips feel flexible, not dry or cracked.
4. **Curl lips over teeth.** Tuck upper and lower lips inward as if hiding the teeth. → *Expect:* teeth are covered and lips create a soft seal.
5. **Place fingers.** Insert fingertips just inside the mouth, angled toward the tongue, with nails facing inward or downward. → *Expect:* fingers hold the lips tight without pain.
6. **Push the tongue back.** Use fingers to press the tongue tip back and slightly down. → *Expect:* a small air channel remains above the tongue.
7. **Seal around the fingers.** Tighten lips so air can escape only through the central gap. → *Expect:* cheeks do not leak air at the sides.
8. **Blow with focused air.** Start with steady medium pressure, then adjust angle and gap by millimeters. → *Expect:* breath noise sharpens toward a hiss or whistle.
9. **Tune one variable at a time.** Change tongue distance, finger angle, or lip tightness separately. → *Expect:* some attempts become louder or clearer.
10. **Rest and retry cleanly.** Pause if lightheaded or irritated, then reset the same shape. → *Expect:* practice remains controlled and comfortable.

## Decision points

- Only airy sound → lips are leaking or the central gap is too wide.
- Spitting occurs → reduce airflow and swallow before resetting.
- Sound is faint → increase lip tension and focus the air stream.
- Mouth hurts → stop and reduce finger pressure next time.
- Need quiet practice → learn the shape silently, then test outdoors.

## Failure modes & recovery

- **F1 No sound at all:** gap or tongue position is wrong; move fingers slightly deeper and push tongue back more.
- **F2 Air leaks from cheeks:** lip seal is loose; tuck lips farther over teeth.
- **F3 Gag reflex triggers:** fingers are too deep; use shallower placement and a smaller V.
- **F4 Lightheadedness:** too many forceful blows; sit down and rest before continuing.
- **F5 Inconsistent whistle:** changing many variables hides the working setup; repeat the last successful position.

## Verification

A clear whistle tone is produced at least once with clean fingers, covered teeth, tongue held back, sealed lips, and controlled airflow.

## Variations

- One-hand O: thumb and index finger make a ring, useful when the other hand is occupied.
- Four-finger version: both index and middle fingers create a wider V for some mouths.
- Non-finger whistle: uses only lips and tongue but usually takes different practice.

## Safety & privacy

Low risk. Wash hands, avoid practicing near unwilling listeners, stop if dizzy, and do not press on braces, fresh dental work, or mouth injuries.
