## Goal

A closed loop of daisies linked stem-through-stem, sized as a crown, bracelet, or necklace, that holds together when worn.

## Preconditions

- A patch of daisies (or clover, dandelions, buttercups) you may pick from, with stems at least 8 cm long. Thick, juicy stems slit cleanly; dry thin stems split.
- A thumbnail of usable length, or a small blunt point (a hairpin, a twig). Scissors are optional and mostly unnecessary.
- Somewhere to sit; this is a sitting-in-the-grass task.

## Steps

1. **Pick a working pile.** Pinch each stem off low, near the base, keeping the stem as long as possible; a crown takes roughly 15 to 25 daisies, a bracelet 8 to 10. Pick a third more than you think you need, because some stems will split. → *Expect:* a pile of long-stemmed daisies with firm, green, unkinked stems.
2. **Slit the first stem.** Hold the stem between thumb and forefinger about halfway down, and press your thumbnail through it lengthwise to open a slit about 1 to 1.5 cm long. Keep the slit at least 2 cm above the stem's cut end, since a slit near the end tears out under any tension. → *Expect:* a clean eye-shaped slot through the stem, both edges intact.
3. **Thread the next daisy.** Push the second daisy stem-end-first through the slit and pull it gently all the way through until its flower head rests against the slit. Pull along the stem's direction, not sideways. → *Expect:* two daisies locked head-to-stem, hanging as a short chain.
4. **Repeat down the chain.** Slit the newly threaded daisy's stem and thread the next one, keeping slit lengths consistent so the flowers space evenly. Every few links, hold the chain up and check length against the wearer (around the head for a crown, wrist for a bracelet). → *Expect:* a growing chain with flowers evenly spaced and no torn links.
5. **Close the loop.** When the chain reaches the target length, make the final slit longer, about 2 to 3 cm, in the last stem. Then thread the entire first flower, head and all, carefully through this big slit; the head acts as the knot. Ease the petals through with fingertips rather than yanking. → *Expect:* a closed ring; the first flower's head sits snug against the final slit and cannot slip back through.
6. **Fit and wear.** Settle the crown or bracelet in place, adjusting spacing by sliding flowers gently along their slits. → *Expect:* the loop stays on during normal movement without any link tearing.

## Decision points

- Stems shorter than about 6 cm → make a bracelet instead of a crown, or switch to a longer-stemmed flower like dandelions.
- Chain must last past an hour or two (photos later, a gift) → keep it in a shallow dish of water or the fridge until needed; a worn daisy chain wilts visibly within a couple of hours in the sun.
- Small children who cannot manage the thumbnail slit → an adult makes all the slits, children do all the threading; that split of labor is the classic way to teach it.

## Failure modes & recovery

- **F1 Stem splits fully open while slitting:** nail pressed too hard or stem too dry → discard that stem (the flower can be re-picked with a fresh stem from the pile) and slit the next one with lighter, slower pressure.
- **F2 Link tears out when the chain is pulled:** slit was too long or too close to the stem end → re-thread the surviving flower through a fresh slit made higher up on the previous stem.
- **F3 Flower head jams while closing the loop:** final slit too short → lengthen the slit a few millimeters at a time and compress the petals with fingers while easing the head through; forcing it tears the stem.
- **F4 Chain looks gappy and loose:** slits spaced inconsistently → slide flowers along their slits to even out spacing; add one more daisy if a gap will not close.
- **F5 Everything wilts mid-build:** hot sun and picked-too-early pile → work in shade, pick in smaller batches as you go, and drop the pile in water if a cup is handy.

## Verification

The chain forms a closed loop with every flower held by a stem slit, the first flower's head anchors the final link, and the loop survives being picked up, worn, and taken off again without a link tearing.

## Variations

- Clover chain: same slit-and-thread method; clover stems are tougher and forgive beginners.
- Knot method (no slits): tie each stem in a loose overhand knot around the next flower's neck; faster, chunkier, works with fragile stems.
- Double chain: thread two daisies through each slit for a fuller crown; needs long slits and patience.

## Safety & privacy

Low risk. Pick only where allowed and leave plenty of flowers behind. Buttercup sap can irritate skin and daisies bother some latex-allergic people, so wash hands afterward, and keep stems away from mouths of toddlers who chew everything.
