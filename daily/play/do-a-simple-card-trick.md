## Goal

You can reliably perform a key-card trick: a spectator freely picks and returns a card, and you reveal it with confident patter, without exposing the method.

## Preconditions

- A complete, reasonably clean 52-card deck (sticky or bent cards betray sleights and clump during shuffles).
- 20-30 minutes of solo practice before any audience: the method is easy, the naturalness is not.
- One willing spectator, ideally not another card enthusiast for your first performances.

## Steps

1. **Learn the key-card principle (solo).** Shuffle, then secretly look at the bottom card of the deck. That is your key card. Everything in this trick reduces to placing the key card directly on top of the spectator's card. → *Expect:* you can name the bottom card at any moment without visibly checking.
2. **Practice the secret glimpse.** Natural cover actions: glance at the bottom card while squaring the deck after a shuffle, or while gesturing with the deck as you talk. The glimpse takes half a second and happens while you speak. → *Expect:* in a mirror, the glimpse is invisible; your eyes are on the "audience" when it happens.
3. **Rehearse the pick and return.** Spread the deck face-down, let the spectator take any card and memorize it. Split the deck: spectator's card goes on top of the lower half, then drop the upper half (with your key card on its bottom) on top. → *Expect:* the key card now sits directly on the spectator's card; the cut looked like ordinary handling.
4. **Rehearse the find.** Spread through the deck face-up "looking for a feeling". Find your key card; the spectator's card is the one immediately after it in the spread direction. Do not stop on it yet: note it and keep spreading a few more cards. → *Expect:* you can identify the selection every time; hesitation at the key card is the tell you are training out.
5. **Build the reveal.** [BRANCH: dramatic pull (cut the selection to the top, snap your fingers, flip it) | mind-reading reveal (name it slowly: color, then suit, then rank, reading their face) | spelling reveal (advanced: spell the card name dealing one card per letter)] → *Expect:* one reveal chosen and drilled until smooth; the mind-reading reveal gets the strongest reactions for zero extra skill.
6. **Script your patter.** Patter is not decoration, it is misdirection: while you talk ("squeeze the card, picture it, I want you to project it to me"), nobody watches your hands. Write three sentences and say the same ones every time. → *Expect:* you can run the whole trick while talking continuously, no silent concentrating pauses.
7. **Perform it once.** One spectator, one performance. Take the applause, and put the deck away or move on. ⚠️ *Irreversible:* repeating a trick for the same audience is the one way to burn it; second viewings are watched for method, not magic, and a discovered method cannot be un-discovered. → *Expect:* a reaction, a smile, and a deck back in your pocket while they are still asking "wait, how".

## Decision points

- Spectator wants to shuffle after returning their card → allow ONE genuine cut (cuts do not separate the key pair) but never a real shuffle; deflect with "the energy is set now" patter.
- Spectator asks "do it again" → never the same trick; offer a different one or the honest exit line "a magician never repeats, that's the first rule they make us swear".
- You lose track of the key card mid-trick → abort gracefully into a joke ("the spirits are off tonight") rather than fishing; a failed guess exposes more than a cancelled trick.

## Failure modes & recovery

- **F1 Spectator's card and key card separated (a real shuffle happened):** you spread and the sequence makes no sense → abort with humor, re-set, and next time control the deck handling yourself after the return.
- **F2 Key card forgotten:** mid-trick blankness → cover patter while you think ("focus harder on your card..."); if truly gone, abort per the decision point, never guess.
- **F3 Glimpse spotted:** spectator says "you looked at the bottom" → deny nothing, smile, and switch to a different trick later; arguing confirms the method.
- **F4 Two candidate cards (spectator's card happened to land next to a duplicate-looking spread):** unsure which side of the key card → the selection is always on the side the top half was dropped from; re-derive from your handling, and slow the spread.

## Verification

The spectator confirms the revealed card is theirs, they cannot explain how, and the trick was performed exactly once for that audience.

## Variations

- The 21-card trick: fully self-working (deal three columns, ask which column, restack with the chosen column in the middle, repeat twice); a good fallback that survives spectator shuffling between rounds.
- A simple force as an upgrade: control a known card to the top, then use a riffle-and-stop or cross-cut so the "free" choice is your card; opens up predictions written before the trick starts.
- Kids' audience: use the 21-card trick and let them deal; key-card handling frustrates small hands.

## Safety & privacy

Low risk. The only asset at stake is the secret: never explain the method to the audience that just saw it, and never perform the same trick twice for the same people. Avoid betting money on trick outcomes; it changes the social contract and the mood when it fails.
