## Goal

You can start, balance, pedal, steer, and stop a bicycle on flat ground without a helper holding the bike.

## Preconditions

- A bicycle that fits: with the seat lowered, both feet can touch the ground flat or nearly flat.
- A helmet that sits level and buckles snugly, plus closed-toe shoes.
- A flat, open, car-free practice area such as an empty court, quiet paved path, or short grass field.
- A helper is useful for traffic spotting and encouragement, but should not hold the saddle once gliding begins.

## Steps

1. **Set the bike for learning.** Lower the seat so your feet can dab the ground, check tire pressure, test both brakes, and remove the pedals if you can do so safely. [BRANCH: pedals come off | pedals stay on] Use a pedal wrench for removal, or keep them on and place your feet wide enough to avoid scraping. → *Expect:* the bike rolls freely, stops on command, and feels low enough to step off.
2. **Practice brake stops before speed.** Walk the bike, squeeze the brake levers gently, then harder, feeling which lever controls which wheel. Avoid grabbing only the front brake. → *Expect:* you can stop the bike while walking without skidding or pitching forward.
3. **Balance-glide with both feet.** Sit on the seat, push with both feet, lift them for one second, then put them down again. Look 10 to 20 m ahead, not at the front wheel. → *Expect:* short straight glides that end calmly with feet down.
4. **Stretch the glide.** Push, lift both feet, and steer tiny corrections by leaning and turning the bars slightly. Repeat until you can glide for 5 to 10 m. → *Expect:* the bike tracks forward while your shoulders stay loose.
5. **Add pedals when balance is real.** Reinstall pedals if removed, set one pedal high at about 2 o'clock, press it down to launch, then place the other foot on its pedal. → *Expect:* the bike moves from a stopped start without a running shove.
6. **Pedal while looking ahead.** Keep light pressure on the bars, pedal smoothly, and aim for a distant point. Speed helps balance, but sprinting does not. → *Expect:* several pedal strokes in a line without staring down.
7. **Turn wide and slow.** Make large circles in both directions, keeping your inside shoulder relaxed and your eyes toward the exit of the turn. → *Expect:* turns widen or tighten on purpose, not by panic.
8. **Stop under control.** Coast, squeeze both brakes progressively, place one foot down as the bike slows, then step off. → *Expect:* a complete stop without jumping clear.

## Decision points

- If fear spikes, return to balance-glides for five minutes; pedaling should feel like an addition, not a rescue.
- If the bike wobbles at every start, raise the first pedal higher and push it down firmly before searching for the second pedal.
- If the practice area has traffic, steep slopes, loose gravel, or crowds, leave and find a safer area.

## Failure modes & recovery

- **F1 Staring at the wheel:** the bike wanders wherever your eyes panic → name a fixed object ahead and ride toward it.
- **F2 Helper over-holding:** the rider never learns balance → the helper walks nearby and spots hazards, not the saddle.
- **F3 Hard front-brake grab:** the rider jolts forward → practice two-finger braking while walking, then at glide speed.
- **F4 Pedal strike on shins:** pedals scrape during gliding → remove pedals or keep feet wider until the pedaling stage.

## Verification

On flat, car-free ground, you start from stopped, ride 20 m, make a wide turn, brake smoothly, and put one foot down without falling.

## Variations

- Grass practice is slower and softer, but pedaling takes more effort.
- Adult learners often progress faster with a smaller borrowed bike for the first session, then return to their normal bike.
- Coaster-brake bikes need extra brake practice because stopping happens by backpedaling.

## Safety & privacy

Medium risk: falls are expected during learning. Wear a helmet every session, use pads if desired, avoid traffic and hills, and stop when tired because balance errors rise sharply with fatigue.
