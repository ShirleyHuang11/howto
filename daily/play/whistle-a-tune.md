## Goal

You can make a clear whistle, adjust pitch with tongue position and air control, and play a short tune.

## Preconditions

- A quiet place where repeated whistles will not bother people.
- Moist lips and a normal comfortable breath.
- No mouth pain, dental numbness, or breathing difficulty.

## Steps

1. **Shape the lips.** Purse the lips into a small relaxed opening, like saying "oo" without pushing hard. → *Expect:* air focuses through a narrow gap.
2. **Place the tongue.** Rest the tongue lightly behind the lower teeth or slightly raised in the middle, leaving a channel for air. → *Expect:* the mouth feels like a small adjustable chamber.
3. **Find one note.** Blow steadily and adjust lips by tiny amounts until a tone appears. [BRANCH: tone appears | only air] If only air, make the opening smaller and relax the jaw. → *Expect:* a clear pitch, even if brief.
4. **Stabilize the air.** Use a gentle continuous stream instead of a burst. → *Expect:* the whistle lasts two seconds without cracking.
5. **Move the pitch.** Raise the middle of the tongue slightly for higher notes and lower it for lower notes, keeping lips steady. → *Expect:* pitch changes without losing the whistle.
6. **Find a three-note scale.** Pick low, middle, and high notes you can repeat, then move between them slowly. → *Expect:* the notes are distinct enough to hear as steps.
7. **Whistle a tiny tune.** Choose a simple melody fragment and map it onto your three notes before adding more range. → *Expect:* someone can recognize the contour, even if the exact pitch is imperfect.
8. **Rest the mouth.** Pause when lips or cheeks feel tired. → *Expect:* later attempts sound cleaner than forced ones.

## Decision points

- If the tone vanishes when changing notes, return to one stable note and move the tongue less.
- If cheeks puff, release tension and let air flow from the lungs through the lips.
- If practicing around others, switch to silent mouth-shape drills or leave the area.

## Failure modes & recovery

- **F1 Air hiss only:** the lip opening is too large or uneven → make a smaller round opening and blow more gently.
- **F2 Squeak:** pressure is too high → reduce airflow and relax the jaw.
- **F3 Pitch jumps:** tongue movement is too big → practice tiny tongue lifts while holding the same lip shape.
- **F4 Dry lips:** tone cracks quickly → sip water and rest for a minute.

## Verification

You produce a clear whistle for two seconds and then whistle a recognizable short tune using at least three different pitches.

## Variations

- Inward whistling uses inhaled air and may be easier for some people.
- Finger whistling is louder and more advanced, so learn quiet lip whistling first.
- Humming the tune before whistling helps separate melody memory from mouth technique.

## Safety & privacy

Low risk. Avoid loud whistling near ears, workplaces, classrooms, or shared quiet spaces, and stop if your mouth, jaw, or breathing feels strained.
