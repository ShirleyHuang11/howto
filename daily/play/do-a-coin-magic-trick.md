## Goal

You can perform the French Drop (a coin vanish) smoothly enough that a casual spectator believes the coin left one hand when it secretly stayed in the other, followed by a "reappearance" reveal.

## Preconditions

- A large-ish coin with some weight (half-dollar, 2-euro, old 50p; small light coins are harder to handle quietly).
- A mirror at roughly spectator height, or a phone propped up recording video.
- 30 to 60 minutes of private practice time before any performance. This trick fails in public exactly as many times as it was skipped in the mirror.

## Steps

1. **Learn the honest action first.** Hold the coin horizontally by its edge in the left hand, between thumb (below) and first two fingertips (above), palm loosely up. With the right hand, reach over, thumb going under the coin and fingers over it, and genuinely take it. Do this 10 times watching what it really looks like. → *Expect:* you can describe the honest take: the right hand closes around the coin, turns, and moves away; the left hand relaxes and drops naturally.
2. **Learn the secret action.** Repeat the same reach, but as the right fingers screen the coin from the spectator's view, relax the left thumb so the coin drops silently into the left fingers' loose curl (finger curl, not a tight fist). The right hand closes on nothing and moves away exactly as before. → *Expect:* coin rests hidden in the loosely curled left hand; the right fist looks like it did in step 1.
3. **Fix the two tells.** First: the left hand must fall naturally to your side or to a rest position, dead relaxed, as if empty, because a hand guarding a coin stiffens. Second: no sound; the drop is a release into flesh, not a toss. → *Expect:* in the mirror, the honest take and the fake take are indistinguishable, and the drop is silent.
4. **Add the misdirection layer: eyes and attention.** Your eyes follow the (empty) right fist; your body turns slightly toward it; your intention says "the coin is here now". Spectators watch where you watch. The left hand becomes uninteresting and is forgotten. → *Expect:* on video, your gaze locks onto the right fist the moment it closes, never flicking to the left hand.
5. **Rehearse the vanish moment.** Hold the right fist up, squeeze slowly, open finger by finger to show it empty. Pause. Let the vanish land before doing anything else. → *Expect:* a clean beat: take, gaze, squeeze, open, empty.
6. **Choose and rehearse a reveal.** [BRANCH: reach up and "pull the coin from" a spectator's ear or collar with the left hand (classic, kid-friendly) | reach into your own pocket with the left hand, ditch or "find" the coin there | simply re-grip and open the left hand after a magical gesture] → *Expect:* the coin's reappearance reads as magic, and the left hand's approach looks motivated (an adjustment, a point, a reach), never like delivering a payload.
7. **Video-test the full routine before performing.** Record from spectator distance, watch for the three killers: left-hand stiffness, gaze flick, audible drop. → *Expect:* three consecutive recorded runs you cannot catch yourself in.
8. **Perform once, small.** One trick, one spectator, no repeat. → *Expect:* a genuine reaction; requests for "do it again" politely declined (see decision points).

## Decision points

- Spectator says "do it again" → never repeat a vanish for the same audience in the same sitting; repetition tells them where to look. Offer a different trick or a shrug and a smile.
- Spectator grabs your left hand → laugh it off, and next time keep more distance and stronger misdirection; hecklers target the hand you protect.
- Small coins only available → switch to a bottle cap or a folded bill ball; handling beats coin authenticity.

## Failure modes & recovery

- **F1 Coin talks (clinks or thuds) during the drop:** grip is too open or drop too far → the coin should fall 1 cm into curled fingers; slow-practice the release in silence until it is soundless ten times running.
- **F2 Spectator's eyes stay on the left hand:** your misdirection failed before the trick started → your own eyes went there; retrain step 4, and add a verbal hook ("watch the coin") aimed at the right fist.
- **F3 Coin visibly flashes from the left hand:** finger curl too loose or hand turned palm-out → keep the left hand's back to the audience and let it hang naturally; check angles in the mirror at spectator height, not your own viewpoint.
- **F4 Freeze at the reveal:** no rehearsed ending → the vanish alone is only half a trick; script the reveal in step 6 and practice the whole arc, not just the sleight.

## Verification

On recorded video from spectator distance, three consecutive full performances show no gaze flick, no left-hand stiffness or flash, and no drop sound; a live spectator reacted to the vanish without pointing at the correct hand.

## Variations

- Coin behind the ear for kids: same French Drop, reveal at the child's ear; kids track eyes even harder than adults, so the gaze discipline matters more, not less.
- Table version (advanced): vanish the coin, then reproduce it from under a cup you placed earlier; requires planning the load before the trick starts.
- No-coin start: the same drop mechanics work with any small object (die, ring, grape); rings are quieter, dice are harder.

## Safety & privacy

Low risk. Coins are a choking hazard around toddlers; do not perform put-in-mouth gags. Social note: never use borrowed coins you might drop down a drain during practice, and never film spectators without asking.
