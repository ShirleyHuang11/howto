## Goal

A classic dart paper airplane, folded with sharp creases and symmetric wings, that flies straight across a room instead of nose-diving at your feet.

## Preconditions

- One sheet of standard printer paper (A4 or US Letter). Fresh, uncrumpled paper flies measurably better than a rescued draft.
- A hard flat surface for creasing; a fingernail or ruler edge to sharpen folds.

## Steps

1. **Fold the sheet in half lengthwise, crease hard, and open it flat again.** This center crease is the spine and the symmetry reference for everything after. → *Expect:* a sharp valley line dividing the sheet into equal halves.
2. **Fold the two top corners to the center line.** Each corner's edge lands exactly on the spine, making a point at the top. Crease with the fingernail. → *Expect:* a symmetric point; both diagonal edges kiss the center line with no overlap or gap.
3. **Fold the angled edges to the center line again.** Take each new diagonal edge and fold it to the spine, sharpening the nose into a long point. → *Expect:* a narrow dart shape; the nose is a tight point and the symmetry still holds. Asymmetry here is the number-one cause of curving flights; refold rather than accept a 2 mm error.
4. **Fold the plane in half along the original spine, folds inside.** → *Expect:* a slim triangle silhouette with the layered folds hidden inside the keel.
5. **Fold the wings down, one per side.** From the nose, fold each side down so the wing's leading edge lies parallel to the keel's bottom, about 2 cm of keel remaining below. Match the second wing to the first by holding them together against the light. → *Expect:* two identical wings; viewed head-on the plane is a Y, wings level or in a slight upward V (dihedral), which is what makes it self-stabilize.
6. **Trim and launch.** Grip the keel at the balance point (roughly a third back from the nose), throw straight ahead at a level or slightly upward angle with a smooth medium motion. Hard throws are for footballs; darts want speed the paper can hold. → *Expect:* a straight glide of several meters. Adjust from what you see: nose-dive → bend the wings' trailing edges up slightly; climbing-then-stalling → less up-bend or a touch more nose weight (a paperclip on the keel's front).

## Decision points

- Distance vs. tricks: the dart above is the distance-and-accuracy classic; wide-winged gliders (fold the top down before step 2) float slower and longer; a slight rudder bend makes deliberate curves for doorways and games.
- Kids: pre-crease step 1 for small hands, and let them own the trim adjustments in step 6; the cause-and-effect of bending flaps is the actual physics lesson smuggled inside.
- Indoor rules: eye-level launches at people are the one banned throw; the pointed nose has earned this rule at every office and classroom in history.

## Failure modes & recovery

- **F1 Veers consistently to one side:** asymmetry in steps 2, 3, or 5, or a wing bent in a crash landing; hold it head-on to the light, match the wings, straighten the keel.
- **F2 Immediate nose-dive:** trailing-edge up-bend (small; 5 mm of flap at 20 degrees is plenty) or the throw was a downward hurl; level launches, medium speed.
- **F3 Flutters and stalls:** wings folded too wide for a dart or thrown too softly; sharpen the creases, narrow the wings, add a touch of speed.
- **F4 Crumpled nose after wall impact:** re-point what you can, then accept that airframes have a service life; fold a fresh one, faster this time — the second plane always takes half the time.

## Verification

The plane holds a straight-line glide across a room, both wings match against the light, the dihedral is visible head-on, and your last adjustment produced the change you predicted — which is the moment paper folding quietly became aeronautics.

## Variations

- A5/half-sheets make quick pocket darts; heavier paper needs proportionally harder throws and sharper creases.
- The world-record-style designs (nose-heavy, tiny wings) trade all charm for distance; look one up after this recipe stops being consulted.

## Safety & privacy

Low risk beyond the no-faces rule and paper-cut respect at fresh edges. Fly responsibly near coffee cups.
