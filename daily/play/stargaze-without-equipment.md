## Goal

You have spent an hour under a night sky with fully dark-adapted eyes, identified at least two or three constellations or landmarks (and the Milky Way if the site allows), and returned safely.

## Preconditions

- A clear or mostly clear night forecast; check cloud cover, not just "no rain".
- Moon phase known: within a few days of new moon is best. A full moon washes out all but the brightest stars.
- A site darker than a city center: a large park, a field edge, or anywhere you can put buildings between yourself and the brightest lights. Even a rooftop away from streetlights beats a lit sidewalk.
- Warm layers (you will be motionless; it feels 5-10 degrees colder than walking), something to lie on, and a way home in the dark.
- Optional: a star-map app installed and set to night/red mode before leaving, or a printed planisphere.

## Steps

1. **Pick the night and site in advance.** Aim for new moon, clear sky, and the darkest reachable location; a light-pollution map (web search "light pollution map") ranks candidate sites. → *Expect:* a chosen spot, a time at least 90 minutes after sunset, and a plan for getting back.
2. **Arrive and settle before switching off lights.** Lay out the blanket or recline the chair while you can still see; note trip hazards and the way out. → *Expect:* a stable, dry spot with an open view of most of the sky.
3. **Kill every white light and start the dark-adaptation clock.** Phone on red/night mode face-down or pocketed. Full adaptation takes 20 to 30 minutes and a single glance at a white screen resets it. → *Expect:* over 20 minutes, faint stars keep appearing; the sky visibly "fills in".
4. **Anchor yourself with one bright landmark.** [BRANCH: northern hemisphere (find the Big Dipper, follow the two pointer stars of its bowl to Polaris, which fixes north) | southern hemisphere (find the Southern Cross and its two pointer stars; extend the cross's long axis about 4.5 lengths toward the south celestial pole)] → *Expect:* you can point to a cardinal direction using stars alone.
5. **Hop from the anchor to neighbors.** From the Dipper: arc along its handle to Arcturus ("arc to Arcturus"). Near Orion (winter/summer depending on hemisphere): his belt of three stars points down-left to Sirius, the brightest star in the sky. → *Expect:* two or three named patterns you can find again on demand.
6. **Use averted vision on faint targets.** Look slightly to the side of a dim object (the Milky Way band, a faint cluster); the eye's periphery is far more light-sensitive than its center. → *Expect:* faint objects pop into view when you look just off them and fade when you stare directly.
7. **Confirm doubtful identifications with the app, sparingly.** Hold the phone (red mode, minimum brightness) up to the sky only when stuck; planets give themselves away by not twinkling. → *Expect:* the app's overlay matches what you see; adaptation loss is minimal.
8. **Pack out by red light or phone-at-minimum.** Sweep the spot for dropped items before leaving. → *Expect:* nothing left behind; safe route home.

## Decision points

- Sky hazy or moon bright on the planned night → reschedule rather than fight it; one dark clear night is worth five mediocre ones.
- Satellites vs. planes vs. meteors: steady moving dot with no blinking = satellite; blinking = aircraft; fast streak that lasts under a second = meteor.
- You can only stargaze from a city → limit goals to the Moon, planets, and the top ~20 brightest stars; constellations like Orion still work, the Milky Way will not.

## Failure modes & recovery

- **F1 Nothing but a dozen stars visible after 30 minutes:** site too bright or sky hazy → shield your eyes from any direct light source with a hand or hood; if no improvement, note the site as bad and pick a darker one next time.
- **F2 Dark adaptation keeps resetting:** notifications lighting the screen → airplane mode, brightness to minimum, screen face-down between uses.
- **F3 Cold ends the session early:** you underdressed for stillness → next time add a hat, an insulating pad under you (ground steals heat), and a hot drink.
- **F4 Lost confidence in what you found:** patterns don't match the chart → check the chart's time and orientation settings; a planisphere held upside-down or an app set to the wrong hemisphere mislabels everything.

## Verification

You can, without the app, point to a cardinal direction using the stars and re-locate at least two constellations or named stars you identified; your eyes reached full adaptation (faint stars visible that were invisible on arrival); you are home with all your gear.

## Variations

- Meteor-shower night (Perseids mid-August, Geminids mid-December): same procedure, but lie flat, watch the whole sky rather than one point, and expect the best rates after local midnight.
- Binoculars upgrade: any 7x to 10x binoculars turn the Moon, the Pleiades, and the Milky Way into showpieces; brace elbows on the ground or a rolled jacket.

## Safety & privacy

Low risk. Main hazards are terrain in the dark (scout while light remains), cold exposure from lying still, and site choice: prefer legal, publicly accessible spots and tell someone where you are going if the site is remote. Location-sharing apps and astrophoto metadata can reveal a favorite dark site; strip GPS tags before posting.
