## Goal

The dog sits promptly on a single verbal cue or hand signal, without a treat visible, in everyday locations. Each training session is 3 to 5 minutes; most dogs get there in days of short sessions.

## Preconditions

- A dog that is comfortable with you, not hungry to the point of frantic, and not exhausted.
- Pea-sized high-value treats (cheese, chicken, soft training treats), 20 to 30 per session, in a pocket or pouch, not rustling in a bag.
- A quiet, boring room for the first sessions; distractions come later.
- Optional: a clicker, or pick one short marker word ("yes") and keep it.

## Steps

1. **Charge the marker.** Say "yes" (or click) and immediately give a treat; repeat 5 to 10 times doing nothing else. → *Expect:* the dog's head snaps toward you on the marker sound alone.
2. **Lure the sit.** Hold a treat at the dog's nose, then move it slowly up and slightly back over the head between the ears. As the nose follows, the rear drops. Never push the rear down with your hand; dogs resist pressure and learn nothing from it. → *Expect:* rear touches the floor within a second or two of the lure.
3. **Mark the instant the rear touches.** Timing is the whole game: mark at rear-contact, not a second later when the dog is already bouncing up. Deliver the treat to the dog's mouth while it is still sitting. → *Expect:* over 5 to 10 reps, the dog folds into the sit faster and stays seated for the treat.
4. **Fade the lure to a hand signal.** Repeat the same rising hand motion with an empty hand; mark and treat from the other hand or pocket when the rear touches. → *Expect:* the dog sits for the empty-hand motion at least 4 times out of 5.
5. **Add the verbal cue.** Say "sit" once, pause half a second, then give the hand signal; mark and treat. After many reps, start waiting a beat longer after the word. Never repeat the cue ("sit, sit, SIT" teaches that the cue is "sit sit sit"). → *Expect:* the dog begins sitting on the word before the hand moves.
6. **Keep sessions short and end on a win.** Stop at 3 to 5 minutes or after a particularly good rep, whichever comes first; a bored or frustrated dog unlearns. → *Expect:* the dog is still eager when the session ends.
7. **Generalize.** Over following days, repeat step 5 in new places in rising difficulty: other rooms, the yard, on walks, near mild distractions. Dogs do not transfer context automatically; "sit" in the kitchen is a different skill than "sit" at the park. → *Expect:* first-cue sits in at least three different locations.
8. **Thin the rewards.** Once reliable, reward the best sits (fastest, most distracted setting) and pay others with praise or life rewards (leash on, door opens, dinner bowl down). Never drop food rewards to zero abruptly. → *Expect:* the dog still sits on the first cue when no treat is visible anywhere.

## Decision points

- Dog too excited to think (jumping, mouthing) → burn off energy with a short play session first, then train.
- Dog is a young puppy → keep sessions to 1 or 2 minutes and expect wobbly, brief sits; that is fine.
- Household has multiple people → agree on one cue word and one hand signal, or the dog gets four dialects and learns none well.
- Dog shows pain sitting (slow, crooked, avoids it) → stop training and see a vet; sitting is physically uncomfortable for dogs with hip or knee issues.

## Failure modes & recovery

- **F1 Dog jumps at the treat:** lure held too high → keep the treat brushing the nose as it moves; restart the rep calmly if the dog jumps.
- **F2 Dog backs up instead of sitting:** the lure travels backward too fast → slow the lure, or practice with the dog's rear a step from a wall or couch.
- **F3 Dog sits then instantly pops up:** treat delivered too slowly or too high → mark at rear-contact and feed low, at chest height, while the rear is down; occasionally feed several treats in a row in position.
- **F4 Dog only sits when it sees food:** lure never faded → go back to step 4 and be strict that the signaling hand is empty, food arriving only after the sit from elsewhere.
- **F5 Dog "goes deaf" in a new place:** distraction level jumped too far → move farther from the distraction and pay better; close distance only as performance recovers.

## Verification

Say "sit" once, with empty visible hands, in a location other than the training room: the dog's rear is on the floor within 2 seconds, and it repeats this at least 4 times out of 5.

## Variations

- Capturing method: skip the lure entirely, mark and treat whenever the dog sits on its own through the day; slower to start but produces a very durable sit.
- Clicker instead of marker word: identical mechanics, crisper timing; keep the clicker in the training pocket so sessions stay deliberate.

## Safety & privacy

Low risk. Keep training treats within the dog's daily calories (shrink meals accordingly). Do not train "sit" on slick floors with senior or dysplastic dogs. Never use leash jerks or pushing the rear down; beyond being unkind, both slow the learning this recipe depends on.
