## Goal

Tell a short calming bedtime story that gives the child a satisfying role and helps the room settle toward sleep.

## Preconditions

- The child is already in the bedtime routine or in bed.
- Lights, noise, and screens are reduced enough for listening.
- You can spend several uninterrupted minutes speaking calmly.

## Steps

1. **Set the length.** Say whether this is a three-minute, five-minute, or one-chapter story. → *Expect:* the child knows the story has an endpoint.
2. **Choose the hero.** Make the child, a favorite stuffed toy, or a recurring character the main character. → *Expect:* the listener recognizes who the story follows.
3. **Pick a gentle want.** Give the hero a simple goal, such as finding a blanket, helping a moon-baker, or delivering a sleepy letter. → *Expect:* the story has direction without high stakes.
4. **Add one small problem.** Use a quiet obstacle like a lost path, a shy cloud, or a riddle at a gate. → *Expect:* there is enough tension to listen, not enough to energize.
5. **Let the child help.** Ask one controlled question, such as choosing a color, tool, or friend. → *Expect:* the child participates without taking over the pace.
6. **Resolve with kindness.** The hero solves the problem by noticing, sharing, asking, resting, or being brave in a small way. → *Expect:* the story moves toward safety and completion.
7. **Slow the language.** Lower your voice, lengthen pauses, and use repeated phrases near the end. → *Expect:* the room feels quieter than at the start.
8. **Close the arc.** Bring the hero home, tuck in the recurring character, and state that the adventure is done for tonight. → *Expect:* the story feels finished.
9. **Repeat a familiar ending.** Use the same closing sentence each night if it helps, then stop adding plot. → *Expect:* the child hears a cue that sleep follows.

## Decision points

- The child gets excited → shrink the plot and move directly to the calming ending.
- The child asks for scary elements → make them silly, tiny, or already solved before bedtime.
- The child interrupts constantly → offer one choice now and one choice for tomorrow's story.
- You are tired → reuse a recurring character and a familiar three-part arc.
- The child fears separation → keep the hero close to home and end with a reliable caregiver nearby.

## Failure modes & recovery

- **F1 Endless story:** detect repeated requests for "one more thing", recover by naming the final scene and ending there.
- **F2 Overexciting plot:** detect louder voice or bouncing, recover by slowing down and moving to a safe resting place.
- **F3 Too many choices:** detect the child directing every detail, recover by offering two options only.
- **F4 Scary image:** detect worry or silence after a detail, recover by making the danger harmless and resolved.
- **F5 Lost thread:** detect you forgot the plot, recover by returning the hero home with the original goal completed.

## Verification

The story has a beginning, small problem, calm resolution, and clear ending, and the child remains in the bedtime routine.

## Variations

- Recurring characters: keep a small cast so the child can settle into familiar patterns.
- Very young children: use repetition, soft sounds, and concrete objects more than plot.
- Older children: let them choose tomorrow's setting before lights out.
- Travel nights: set the story in the temporary room so unfamiliar sounds feel included.

## Safety & privacy

Low risk. Avoid threats, shame, or scary consequences as sleep tools, and do not include private family conflicts in stories a child may repeat elsewhere.
