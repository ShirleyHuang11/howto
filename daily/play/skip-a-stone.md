## Goal

A stone skipped across calm water for three or more bounces, via the three variables that actually matter: stone selection, spin, and a low flat launch angle.

## Preconditions

- Calm, open water: a lake, pond, or slow river reach with no swimmers, boats, waders, or wildlife rafts in your arc. The downrange check is the recipe's one hard rule.
- A shoreline with stones. If it has skipping stones, it has a supply; selection is half the sport.

## Steps

1. **Select the stone like it matters, because it is most of the result.** Flat, smooth, palm-sized, roughly round or triangular, about the weight of a large cookie; a slight thickness (not wafer-thin) carries momentum. Collect three or four candidates before throwing anything. → *Expect:* a small arsenal of flat stones; the perfect one gets saved for when your form warms up.
2. **Grip for spin.** Index finger curled along the stone's edge, thumb on top, the flat faces parallel to the ground; the finger along the rim is the spin engine. → *Expect:* a grip where a wrist flick would clearly rotate the stone like a frisbee.
3. **Set the stance low and sideways.** Feet sideways to the water like a discus thrower, knees bent, throwing shoulder dropped; the whole art is getting the release close to the water. → *Expect:* your throwing hand at knee height or lower at the release point.
4. **Throw flat and fast with a hard wrist snap.** Sidearm swing, release when the stone points at the water 5 to 10 meters out, snapping the wrist so the stone leaves spinning hard. The launch angle wants to be shallow: stone nearly parallel to the surface, striking at roughly 20 degrees. → *Expect:* a spinning, flat, fast stone; the first contact throws a small skip, not a plunge.
5. **Read the failure and adjust one variable.** Single plunge: angle too steep, get lower and flatter. Skips but dies fast: more wrist snap (spin is what stabilizes it against tipping). Veers and rolls into the water: the stone wobbled at release, grip flatter and snap cleaner. → *Expect:* each throw diagnosing itself; bounce counts climbing across the arsenal from step 1.
6. **Count generously and pass the skill on.** House rules everywhere: the pitty-pat finale counts. Teaching a kid the grip in step 2 is the traditional payment for the shoreline you used. → *Expect:* three-plus bounces on your better throws, and the small ceremony of a personal record acknowledged aloud.

## Decision points

- Waves or current: skipping fights chop badly; find the lee of a point, a sheltered cove, or come back at evening calm when lakes go glass.
- Left-handers: mirror everything; the spin direction differs and matters not at all.
- No flat stones: skimming culture accepts nothing lens-shaped will ever fly; enjoy plunking contests instead (biggest splash, a different and equally honorable discipline).

## Failure modes & recovery

- **F1 Every throw plunges:** you are releasing at waist height like a baseball; the fix is embarrassment-proof: kneel on one knee for ten throws to feel the low release, then stand back up with the memory.
- **F2 Shoulder or elbow complaint:** the sidearm snap is a repetitive strain when cold; a dozen easy lobs first (`daily/self-care/warm-up-before-exercise` applies to shorelines too), and stop at fatigue rather than through it.
- **F3 Stone toward something living:** the downrange check lapsed; hold every throw until the arc is verifiably clear — ducks arrive silently and own the lake.
- **F4 The perfect stone, wasted on a bad throw:** grief is appropriate; the shoreline provides again. This is also the recipe's life lesson.

## Verification

Your better throws bounce three or more times, you can name which variable (stone, spin, angle) each failed throw missed, the arc stayed clear of every living thing, and at least one bystander asked how you did it.

## Variations

- Competition skimming (yes, world championships exist) standardizes stones and measures distance, not bounces; the technique above is the same.
- Frozen lakes in winter offer stone-sliding, a sibling sport with its own satisfying sounds and the same downrange ethics.

## Safety & privacy

Low risk: the downrange rule, wet-rock footing at the shoreline, and never skipping toward swimmers or wildlife. Everything else is physics, patience, and the ancient satisfaction of a good stone doing what it was born for.
