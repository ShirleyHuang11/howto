## Goal

Flowers flattened and dried to paper thinness with their color largely intact, ready to mount on cards, in frames, or in a journal. Active work is under an hour; the press itself takes two to four weeks.

## Preconditions

- Fresh flowers you are allowed to pick: naturally flat faces press best (pansies, violets, daisies, cosmos, single-petal roses). Thick fleshy flowers need splitting first.
- Absorbent, smooth, uncoated paper: printer paper, parchment, or blotting paper. Not paper towels (their texture embosses petals) and not glossy magazine pages (ink transfers, no absorption).
- A heavy book you will not need for a month, plus extra weight (more books, a brick).
- Tweezers and white PVA glue for mounting, and a shelf in a dry room.

## Steps

1. **Pick in the late morning of a dry day.** Wait until dew has evaporated; surface moisture is the main cause of browning and mold. Choose blooms just fully opened, not fading. Cut with a bit of stem. → *Expect:* petals dry to the touch, no browning edges, no insects hiding in the center.
2. **Prepare each flower flat.** [BRANCH: flat-faced flower → press whole, face down | thick flower (rose, carnation) → split it in half through the stem with scissors, or press individual petals] Snip off bulky green backing parts that would tent the petals. → *Expect:* each specimen lies nearly flat on the table on its own.
3. **Arrange between paper.** Fold a sheet of paper in half, lay flowers inside with no two touching, and shape petals with the tweezers exactly as you want them forever. ⚠️ *Irreversible:* petals dry in whatever position they are pressed; overlapped or folded petals cannot be unfolded later. → *Expect:* every petal splayed, nothing overlapping, layout final.
4. **Load the book.** Place the paper folder deep inside the heavy book with at least a centimeter of pages on each side to buffer moisture, then close it and stack the extra weight on top. Label a sticky note with the date and page. → *Expect:* the loaded book sits flat under weight on a dry shelf; date noted.
5. **Change the paper after 3 to 4 days.** Open carefully, lift each flower with tweezers (they are limp and tear-prone now), and transfer to fresh dry paper in the same book. This single swap prevents most mold and browning. → *Expect:* the old paper shows faint damp shadows; flowers still colorful, not slimy or spotted.
6. **Press undisturbed for 2 to 4 weeks total.** Thin flowers finish in 2 weeks; split thick ones need 4. Resist peeking, since every opening risks shifting petals. → *Expect:* on opening, flowers are paper-thin, dry, and stiff, and lift off cleanly.
7. **Mount.** Handle only with tweezers. Apply glue in pin-head dots to the flower's back (stem line and center, not the petal tips), place on the card or backing, cover with clean paper, and weight for an hour. → *Expect:* the flower is bonded flat with no glue showing at the edges.

## Decision points

- Flower needed for a specific date less than a week away → use the iron method (Variations); the book cannot be rushed.
- Specimen shows any mold at the paper change → discard it and its neighbors on the same sheet; mold spreads through the batch.
- Mounting in a frame → use a frame that clamps glass directly against the backing, or the flower will curl over years.

## Failure modes & recovery

- **F1 Flowers turn brown:** picked damp, or paper never changed → not recoverable for that batch; re-pick on a dry day and do the day-3 paper swap.
- **F2 Mold spots on paper or petals:** too much moisture, humid room → discard affected specimens, move the book to a drier room, change paper every 2 days for the next batch.
- **F3 Petals stick to the paper:** paper too textured or flowers too fleshy → slide a thin knife or floss under the petal at a shallow angle; next time use smoother paper and split fleshy flowers.
- **F4 Petal tears during transfer:** normal for limp mid-press flowers → mount torn pieces composed together; the seam disappears under glass.
- **F5 Book pages wrinkled or stained:** flowers placed without enough paper buffer → accept the loss (choose a sacrificial book) and use thicker paper folders next time.

## Verification

Each flower is dry, stiff, and translucent-thin, holds its color, shows no brown or fuzzy patches, and either lies loose ready for storage or is glued flat to its backing with no visible adhesive.

## Variations

- Iron method (same-day): flower between two sheets of paper, press with a dry iron on low for 15 seconds, lift the iron and let the paper cool, repeat 3 to 4 times; color is slightly duller than book pressing.
- Wooden flower press with wing nuts: same layers, tighten nuts a quarter turn every few days; dries faster and flatter than a book.
- Store unmounted flowers between paper in a rigid box; they keep for years but shatter if flexed.

## Safety & privacy

Low risk. Only pick where permitted; protected wildflowers are illegal to take in many regions, so when in doubt pick from your own garden or buy blooms. Some sap (euphorbia, daffodil) irritates skin, so wash hands after handling cut stems.
