## Goal

A structurally sound blanket fort with standing headroom for its seated occupants, a defensible entrance, and interior lighting, that survives the whole movie without a mid-film roof collapse.

## Preconditions

- Frame material: a sofa, 3 to 6 chairs, or a table (the load-bearing aristocracy of fort construction).
- Roof material: 2 to 4 large sheets or light blankets (heavy duvets are roof-collapse in waiting; they are flooring).
- Ballast: books, cushions, or clips to anchor roof edges; interior kit: cushions, duvet flooring, a lamp or string lights (never hot bulbs against fabric; see Safety).

## Steps

1. **Site survey and frame layout.** Pick a corner or sofa-adjacent zone away from doors and radiators; arrange chairs back-outward in a rough rectangle, or claim the dining table whole. Corners and walls are free structure; use them. → *Expect:* a stable perimeter frame with a planned entrance gap, footprint sized to occupants-plus-snacks.
2. **Engineer the ridge line.** The single biggest upgrade over amateur forts: a spine for the roof. A broom handle bridging two chair backs, a sofa-to-bookshelf cord (taut, tied to something that cannot tip), or the table's underside as a ready-made ceiling. → *Expect:* a high line the roof can drape over, creating pitch instead of sag.
3. **Drape the roof sheets over the ridge and frame.** Lightest sheets on top spans; overlap edges by a hand-width where two sheets meet. → *Expect:* full coverage with pitched sides; the middle clears a seated head.
4. **Anchor every edge with ballast.** Books or cushion weights on the chair seats and floor edges, clips or rubber bands pinching sheet-to-chair joints; the sofa's own cushions clamp that flank. → *Expect:* a tug test on each sheet meets resistance; no edge relies on hope.
5. **Fit out the interior.** Duvet flooring, cushion seating against the hard chair legs, snack corner away from the entrance's traffic, and lighting: LED string lights or a cool lamp *inside a jar or well clear of fabric*. → *Expect:* a habitable interior that a seated adult enters without shoulder-checking a load-bearing chair.
6. **Commission the fort.** Entrance flap test (openable one-handed from inside), full-occupancy wiggle test, and the solemn naming ceremony, which is not optional. → *Expect:* everyone in, roof standing, name declared, movie starting.

## Decision points

- Under-table forts trade headroom for invincibility: zero collapse risk, ideal for reading nooks and small architects.
- Multi-room ambitions: separate ridge lines per room and a doorway sheet between; complexity rises fast, and the second room is where most dynasties overreach.
- Sleepover-grade forts: double the ballast, ban roof-touching games in advance, and route any light cable along a wall where no sleeper rolls onto it.

## Failure modes & recovery

- **F1 Roof sag onto heads:** the ridge is too low or the sheet too heavy; raise the spine (taller chair, added broom) or swap in a lighter sheet; interior tent-poles (a mop, gently) prop emergency spans.
- **F2 Chair tipped inward:** back-outward orientation was skipped or ballast sat on the wrong edge; re-orient chairs so pulls tighten rather than topple, weight the seats.
- **F3 Total collapse mid-occupancy:** evacuate laughing, audit which anchor failed, rebuild that flank with double ballast; forts teach failure analysis better than most engineering courses.
- **F4 Territorial disputes among occupants:** partition with a cushion border or invoke rotating sovereignty; the naming ceremony's authority helps here.

## Verification

The roof clears seated heads with visible pitch, every edge passes the tug test, the entrance works one-handed, lighting is cool-source and fabric-clear, and the structure outlives the movie including one accidental kick.

## Variations

- Clothesline-and-sheet garden forts move the recipe outdoors (`daily/play` meets fresh air); pegs replace books.
- Cardboard-box annexes make superb turrets and tunnels for the under-eight demographic.

## Safety & privacy

Low risk with two real rules: no heat near fabric (incandescent bulbs and radiators are fire logic, LED only), and no unattended candles ever; plus the structural one — nothing heavy balanced overhead, because ballast belongs at the edges, not the sky.
