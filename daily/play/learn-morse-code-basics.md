## Goal

You can recognize and send a few basic Morse code letters, including SOS, using clear dot, dash, letter, and word timing.

## Preconditions

- A tapping surface, flashlight, buzzer, or simply your voice saying "dit" and "dah."
- A printed or memorized Morse chart for reference.
- A quiet place to practice short patterns without annoying nearby people.

## Steps

1. **Learn the timing unit.** Treat a dot as one beat, a dash as three beats, the gap inside a letter as one beat, between letters as three beats, and between words as seven beats. → *Expect:* the rhythm feels measured instead of random.
2. **Send E and T.** Practice E as one dot and T as one dash, tapping or flashing each ten times. → *Expect:* you can make the shortest contrast cleanly.
3. **Add common letters.** Learn A dot-dash, N dash-dot, I dot-dot, M dash-dash, S dot-dot-dot, and O dash-dash-dash. → *Expect:* short words become possible.
4. **Practice SOS.** Send S O S as three dots, three dashes, three dots, with letter gaps between them. → *Expect:* the pattern is recognizable and evenly spaced.
5. **Tap tiny words.** Try "me," "to," "tea," "no," and "sit" using the chart only when stuck. → *Expect:* you hear letters as whole shapes, not counted parts.
6. **Receive before speed.** Have a partner tap one learned letter at a time, or record yourself and play it back. [BRANCH: you recognize it | you count every mark] If counting dominates, slow down and repeat fewer letters. → *Expect:* at least E, T, A, N, S, and O are recognized by sound or flash.
7. **Build practice words.** Mix learned letters into five short words per session, sending slowly with clean spacing. → *Expect:* timing improves more than raw speed.

## Decision points

- If dots and dashes blur, increase the dash length and the spaces.
- If you forget letters, group opposites: E/T, I/M, A/N, S/O.
- If using light signals outdoors, avoid aiming bright lights at drivers or homes.

## Failure modes & recovery

- **F1 Rushed spacing:** letters merge into noise → count beats aloud until gaps are steady.
- **F2 Visual chart dependence:** you cannot receive by ear → practice listening to only three letters at a time.
- **F3 Uneven dashes:** dashes become just long dots → make each dash last three dot beats.
- **F4 Nuisance signaling:** repeated beeps or flashes bother others → switch to finger taps on a soft surface.

## Verification

You correctly send SOS and recognize at least six learned letters when they are tapped, flashed, or spoken slowly.

## Variations

- Voice practice with "dit" and "dah" is useful anywhere quiet.
- Flashlight practice makes timing visible, but short sessions protect eyes and neighbors.
- Amateur radio Morse uses the same alphabet but requires licensing to transmit on radio bands.

## Safety & privacy

Low risk. Do not present SOS as a real distress call unless there is an emergency, avoid distracting signals in public, and keep practice messages non-sensitive if others can hear or see them.
