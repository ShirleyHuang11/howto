## Goal

A campfire built legally and safely, lit with one or two matches, enjoyed, and then extinguished so thoroughly that the ground is cold to a bare hand, which is the step that defines whether you get to be proud of the rest.

## Preconditions

- **Legality and conditions first, absolutely:** fire rules for this exact place and day (fire bans move with drought and wind), an established fire ring or a legal spot, and wind calm enough that sparks stay home. High wind or a ban means the recipe ends here; a stove or lantern substitutes.
- Water for extinguishing (a full bucket or several liters) staged *before* the first match, plus a shovel or stick for stirring ash.
- Fuel in three sizes gathered before lighting: tinder (dry pencil-shaving-fine material: birch bark, dry grass, cotton ball), kindling (pencil-to-finger-thick dry sticks, a big double armful), and fuel wood (wrist-to-arm thick). Dead, down, and snappable-dry; green or damp wood smokes and sulks.

## Steps

1. **Prepare the site.** Use the existing ring; clear a meter-plus circle down to mineral soil of leaves and debris; overhanging branches disqualify a spot. → *Expect:* a bare-soil circle, water bucket standing by, fuel sorted into its three piles within reach.
2. **Build small and hollow.** A fist-sized tinder nest in the center; kindling around it in a small teepee or lean-to against a thicker stick, with finger-gaps for air. Fire eats oxygen before it eats wood; the classic beginner error is building a woodpile, not an air structure. → *Expect:* a structure you can see through, tinder reachable at its base.
3. **Light the tinder at its base, upwind side.** One match, cupped from the breeze; let flame climb into the kindling. → *Expect:* tinder catching, kindling crackling within a minute; resist rearranging while it establishes.
4. **Feed by size, patiently.** Add kindling until a solid crackle, then fuel wood one piece at a time, laid to preserve airflow. Blowing gently at the base's embers revives hesitation; dumping the big log on early smothers everything. → *Expect:* a self-sustaining fire stepping up through the sizes; flames sized to the ring, never taller than needed.
5. **Run the fire like the custodian you are.** Someone attentive within reach at all times (an unattended campfire is not a thing that exists, only abandonment); shoes on around it; marshmallow radius enforced for kids; new fuel added with placed, not tossed, motions. → *Expect:* a stable, sociable fire with its keeper present and the water still staged.
6. **Extinguish with the full protocol, starting 30+ minutes before leaving.** Let it burn down; spread the coals; water poured on in stages, *stirring* ash and embers with the stick between pours; repeat until no hiss, no steam, no glow. Then the hand test: hover, then touch the ash with a bare hand. ⚠️ *Irreversible:* leaving warm coals is how forests burn down days later; cold-to-the-touch is the only pass grade. → *Expect:* wet, cold, stirred ash; the site cool everywhere a hand checks.

## Decision points

- Wet weather: dry tinder lives inside standing dead wood (split to the dry core), under overhangs, or in your pocket (bring firelighters or petroleum-jelly cotton balls without shame; the skill is the structure, not the martyrdom).
- Cooking fires want coals, not flames: burn down to glowing embers, cook over those (`daily/food` recipes adapt), flames are for warmth and morale.
- Leave-no-trace beyond the ring: where no ring exists and rules allow, a mound fire or fire pan protects the soil; scatter cold ash broadly and re-naturalize the site.

## Failure modes & recovery

- **F1 Fire dies repeatedly at the kindling stage:** fuel is damp or the structure is too tight; rebuild hollower with the driest material split thin, and double the tinder.
- **F2 All smoke, no flame:** green or wet wood entered the diet; pull it, return to known-dry sizes, and let the bed re-establish.
- **F3 Wind gusts throwing sparks:** shrink the fire immediately or extinguish; a spark landing outside the circle is the emergency, and the staged water answers it now, not after discussion.
- **F4 Clothing or hair too close (melted synthetics, singed sleeve):** synthetic layers melt onto skin — wool and cotton near flames; if clothing catches: stop-drop-roll is still the protocol, then cool the burn with water (`daily/food/boil-water` F4's burn care generalizes).

## Verification

The fire was legal, ringed, sized modestly, never unattended, and the site you leave passes the bare-hand test on cold wet ash with the surroundings clear of stray embers — the campfire's real final exam.

## Variations

- Fire types by purpose: teepee for quick flame, log cabin for stable cooking coals, lean-to for wind; all obey the same air-first physics.
- Backyard fire pits and bowls follow the identical protocol with the legality question answered by local ordinance and the neighbors' windows.

## Safety & privacy

High risk, honestly rated: this recipe's stakes are measured in landscapes. The load-bearing lines: legality and wind first, water staged before the match, never unattended, and cold-to-the-touch before leaving. Everything between those is the fun part, and the fun part is real.
