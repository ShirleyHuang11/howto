## Goal

A classic folded paper boat (the "newspaper hat" style with a raised sail) sits upright and floats on water for at least a minute or two before soaking through.

## Preconditions

- One rectangular sheet of paper. Standard printer paper (A4 or letter) works; newspaper works for large boats. Square paper does not work for this fold without trimming.
- A flat, hard folding surface. Crisp folds are what make the final unfolding steps possible.
- Water for the float test: sink, basin, bathtub, or a calm puddle or stream.

## Steps

1. **Fold the sheet in half top-to-bottom.** Short edges together, crease sharply with a fingernail. Keep the folded edge at the top for all following steps. → *Expect:* a half-size rectangle with the crisp fold along the top.
2. **Mark the center line.** Fold in half left-to-right, crease lightly, unfold. → *Expect:* a visible vertical center crease.
3. **Fold both top corners down to the center line.** The two corners of the folded (top) edge meet at the crease, forming a point at the top, like starting a paper airplane. → *Expect:* a triangle sitting on a rectangular strip of two loose flaps at the bottom.
4. **Fold the bottom flaps up, one per side.** Fold the front strip up over the triangle's base; flip the model and fold the back strip up the same way. Fold the little protruding corners of each strip around the triangle's edges so nothing sticks out. → *Expect:* a clean paper hat shape (it literally fits on a head if made from newspaper).
5. **Open the hat and flatten into a diamond.** Put thumbs inside the bottom opening, pull the front and back apart, and bring the left and right points together, re-flattening the model 90 degrees from before. Tuck one bottom flap corner over the other so they interlock. → *Expect:* a flat diamond (square standing on a corner).
6. **Fold the bottom point of the diamond up to the top point; flip and repeat.** One loose bottom layer folds up on each side. → *Expect:* a smaller triangle again.
7. **Open and flatten into a diamond a second time.** Same move as step 5: open the bottom, collapse the other way. → *Expect:* a smaller flat diamond with two loose top-corner layers.
8. **Pull the boat open.** Grip the two loose top corners of the diamond (one per hand) and pull gently outward and apart. The model unfolds itself: hull opens, center sail rises. Ease it, don't yank; this is where tearing happens. → *Expect:* a boat with a flat-ish hull, two pointed ends, and an upright triangular sail.
9. **Trim the hull and float it.** Pinch along the bottom to flatten the keel so it sits level, then set it gently onto calm water, lowering it flat rather than dropping it. → *Expect:* the boat floats upright, sail up, for at least a minute.

## Decision points

- Paper tears at step 8 → the earlier tuck (step 5) wasn't interlocked or folds weren't sharp; start over with a fresh sheet, creasing harder.
- Boat for actual outdoor sailing (stream, race with kids) → rub a candle or crayon wax over the hull's underside before floating, or fold from waxed/glossy flyer paper; unwaxed printer paper swamps in roughly 2 to 5 minutes.
- Very thick paper (card stock) refuses the double collapse → drop to normal-weight paper; thickness fights steps 5 and 7.

## Failure modes & recovery

- **F1 Boat lists or capsizes immediately:** hull creased asymmetrically or sail off-center → re-pinch the bottom flat, spread the hull walls evenly, and relaunch; a small pebble or coin dropped in the center as ballast also stabilizes it.
- **F2 Ends unfold on the water:** the corner tucks worked loose → refold the tucks tightly; on a rebuilt boat, tuck first, then open.
- **F3 Soaks and sinks fast:** normal for plain paper → accept the ~2 minute service life, wax the hull (see decision points), or race before saturation.
- **F4 Lost the fold midway:** the two flatten-and-collapse steps (5 and 7) confuse everyone the first time → do the whole sequence once slowly with a large newspaper sheet where the geometry is easier to see, then return to small paper.

## Verification

The finished boat has the classic silhouette (two pointed ends, central sail), was produced without tears, and passed the float test: upright and dry-topped on calm water for at least 60 seconds.

## Variations

- Newspaper giant: a full broadsheet makes a boat big enough for a doll crew; same folds, and the intermediate step 4 hat is wearable.
- Race version: waxed hull, coin ballast, and a straw to blow it across a bathtub; first sail to the far edge wins.
- Foil upgrade: aluminum foil folded over the finished hull makes it effectively unsinkable for a kid's bath.

## Safety & privacy

Low risk. Paper cuts are the only real hazard of the folding; for the float test with children, standard water-supervision rules apply (never leave a small child alone at a filled tub), and boats sailed on streams are litter once lost, so retrieve or use plain unwaxed paper that breaks down.
