## Goal

A stable three-ball snowman, decorated, standing where it can be seen without blocking anyone, built without wrecked backs or lost gloves.

## Preconditions

- Packing snow: temperature near 0 °C, snow that squeezes into a ball that holds its shape. Powder at low temperatures will not bind.
- At least 10 cm of snow over an open area of several meters, since rolling strips a wide path.
- Waterproof gloves (wool soaks through in minutes) and decorating items: sticks for arms, a carrot, stones or buttons, an old scarf or hat.
- A flat spot, ideally shaded, away from paths, driveways, and roads.

## Steps

1. **Test the snow.** Squeeze a handful hard. [BRANCH: it compacts into a solid ball → build now | it crumbles → wait for warmer hours or the afternoon sun; a light water mist on the snow surface can rescue marginal snow] → *Expect:* a fist-sized snowball that survives a gentle toss.
2. **Pack a dense starter ball.** Compress a snowball between both hands until it is hard, about melon-core density. A loose starter makes every later layer flaky. → *Expect:* a solid ball roughly 15 cm across that does not crumble under thumb pressure.
3. **Roll the base.** Roll the starter through fresh snow, changing direction every few pushes so it grows round instead of into a cylinder, and pat it firmly every few rolls to bind the new layer. Stop around knee height. → *Expect:* a roughly spherical base you can no longer roll easily; a stripped spiral path behind it.
4. **Seat the base and flatten its top.** Push it to the display spot, then press down and pack a flat platform on top, about two hands wide. → *Expect:* the base does not rock; the top platform is level.
5. **Roll and mount the middle ball.** Roll a second ball to about two-thirds the base diameter. Lift it with your legs, not your back, keeping it close to your body; get a second person for anything heavier than comfortable. Set it on the platform and pack a collar of loose snow into the seam all the way around, pressing hard. → *Expect:* the middle ball sits centered and cannot be slid off by a firm push.
6. **Make and mount the head.** Pack the head by hand at about half the middle ball's size; oversized heads are the number one cause of decapitation. Flatten the middle ball's top, seat the head, and weld the seam with packed snow. For icy or windy days, drive a straight stick down through head and middle as a spine first. → *Expect:* the head stays put when you press its top lightly.
7. **Decorate.** Push stick arms into the middle ball at a slight upward angle, the carrot nose into the head's center, and press stones in firmly for eyes, mouth, and buttons. Add scarf and hat last. → *Expect:* a face readable from 10 m; nothing falls off when the wind gusts.
8. **Clear the site.** Collect tools and any dropped gloves, and note every non-snow item on the snowman for later retrieval. → *Expect:* nothing left on the ground that the melt will not absorb.

## Decision points

- Snow is deep but powdery all day → build a smaller snowman from hand-packed snow near a wall where sun-warmed snow is damper, or skip to snow-fort building.
- Ground shows through while rolling → move to fresh snow; grass and gravel embedded in the balls weaken them and look bad.
- Building with small children → they roll the head and decorate; an adult lifts anything above the base.

## Failure modes & recovery

- **F1 Ball falls apart mid-roll:** layers not bonding → stop rolling, pat the whole surface firmly for a minute to compact it, then continue with shorter pushes.
- **F2 Middle ball too heavy to lift:** do not deadlift it → roll it up a small snow ramp packed against the base, or shave it down with gloved hands until manageable.
- **F3 Head rolls off:** head too big or seam unpacked → rebuild the head smaller, flatten both mating surfaces, and use the stick spine.
- **F4 Snowman leans after a sunny day:** melt on the sun side → prop the lean side with packed snow buttresses in the evening when it refreezes; a shaded site prevents this.
- **F5 Fingers numb or white:** gloves soaked → go inside and warm up before continuing; cold injury outlasts any snowman.

## Verification

Three balls in decreasing size stand plumb and survive a firm push on the middle ball; face and arms are attached; the site is free of tools and litter; hands are dry and warm again indoors.

## Variations

- Two-ball snowman: base plus head only, faster and stabler for shallow snow or young kids.
- Column method for wet heavy snow: pile and carve a single tapered column instead of lifting balls.

## Safety & privacy

Low risk overall. The two real hazards are back strain from lifting the middle ball (lift with legs, or ramp it) and wet-glove cold exposure in children who will not self-report numb fingers. Melt etiquette: retrieve scarf, hat, stones, and carrot before or right after the melt; organic items can stay, plastics and fabric become litter. Do not build where the snowman or its melt puddle blocks a sidewalk or driveway sightline.
