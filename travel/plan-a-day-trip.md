## Goal

You produce a realistic one-day itinerary built around a single anchor sight, with the transit timing worked out both ways, meals placed sensibly, and a weather-driven decision made, so the day flows instead of collapsing into rushing and hunger.

## Preconditions

- A destination and a fixed date, plus a rough sense of your budget and energy level.
- Access to a maps and transit app and the ability to check opening hours and the weather forecast for the date.
- Knowledge of any bookings that sell out or require timed entry for your candidate sights.

## Steps

1. **Pick one anchor sight and build the day around it.** Choose the single must-do, check its opening days and hours, and note whether it needs a timed or advance ticket. A day with one solid anchor beats a day chasing five. → *Expect:* one anchor chosen, confirmed open on your date, with any ticket requirement identified.
2. **Book the anchor if it needs a timed entry.** Popular sights sell out days ahead. ⚠️ *Irreversible in effect:* timed tickets are often non-refundable and date-locked, so confirm your date before paying. → *Expect:* a booked slot (or a confirmed no-booking-needed) anchoring a specific time in your day.
3. **Do the transit math both directions.** Look up realistic travel time from your base to the anchor and back, including the first sensible departure and the last return service. Add buffer for missed connections. → *Expect:* a departure time, an arrival time, and a hard last-return time written down, all achievable.
4. **Slot two or three lighter stops around the anchor, geographically.** Add nearby sights that sit on the route to or from the anchor, so you are not backtracking. Keep it loose. → *Expect:* a rough sequence where each stop is near the next, with slack between them.
5. **Place meals deliberately.** Fix a lunch spot and time near the anchor (research one option so you are not hunting while hungry in a tourist trap), and decide whether dinner is at the destination or back home. Carry water and a snack. → *Expect:* a lunch plan tied to a place and time, and a dinner decision, with a snack packed.
6. **Make the weather call the day before and morning-of.** Check the forecast; if it turns, swap an outdoor anchor for an indoor one or move the outdoor stop to the drier part of the day. Pack layers, rain gear, or sun protection accordingly. → *Expect:* an itinerary matched to the forecast and a bag packed for the actual conditions.
7. **Write the day as a simple timeline and keep it flexible.** List departure, anchor slot, meals, and last return as fixed points, with everything else optional. → *Expect:* a one-screen timeline with a few fixed anchors and room to drop stops without breaking the day.

## Decision points

- Anchor is outdoors and the forecast is poor: switch to an indoor anchor (museum, market, aquarium) or shift the whole trip to a better day if the sight is the whole point.
- Transit is sparse (rural, limited service, last bus early): let the last return time drive the plan and cut stops rather than risk being stranded.
- Traveling with children or older companions: fewer stops, longer buffers, and a guaranteed rest and meal built in beat an ambitious list.

## Failure modes & recovery

- **F1 Anchor closed or sold out on arrival:** the advance open-hours and ticket check in step 1 prevents this; if it still happens, pivot to the nearest backup sight you noted.
- **F2 Running behind schedule:** protect the anchor and the last return; drop optional stops rather than compress the whole day into a rush.
- **F3 Missed the last return service:** know the number before you leave; recover with a later intercity option, a rideshare, or, worst case, an unplanned overnight. Building buffer into step 3 avoids it.
- **F4 Weather turns mid-day:** carry a compact rain layer, retreat to an indoor stop, and reorder rather than abandon the day.

## Verification

You hold a one-day timeline with a confirmed anchor, an achievable outbound and last-return time, a placed lunch and dinner decision, and a plan that already accounts for the forecast, with backup stops noted in case something is closed.

## Variations

- `city` day trips lean on frequent transit and can absorb a dropped stop easily; `rural` and island trips hinge on sparse schedules, making the last-return time the hardest constraint in the plan.
- Car-based day trips swap transit math for drive time, parking research, and fuel, but keep the same anchor-and-buffer logic; see `travel/rent-a-car`.

## Safety & privacy

Low risk. The real failure is logistical, not physical: an over-packed plan that leaves you rushed, hungry, or stranded. Keep the anchor and last-return as the two hard constraints, carry water and a charged phone, and tell someone your rough plan if you are heading somewhere remote.
