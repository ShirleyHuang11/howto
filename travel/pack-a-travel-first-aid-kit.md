## Goal

Pack a compact travel first-aid kit with essentials, labeled medicines, legal quantities, and safe storage for the trip.

## Preconditions

- You know the destination, trip length, climate, and access to pharmacies or medical care.
- You know travelers' allergies, prescriptions, and routine medical needs.
- You have a small pouch and original medicine packaging or labels.

## Steps

1. **List likely problems.** Consider cuts, blisters, pain, fever, stomach upset, allergies, motion sickness, and sun exposure. → *Expect:* the kit matches the trip rather than a random drawer.
2. **Pack wound basics.** Add adhesive bandages, gauze pads, medical tape, antiseptic wipes, blister pads, and small scissors if allowed. → *Expect:* minor cuts and blisters can be cleaned and covered.
3. **Add symptom medicines.** Include pain reliever, fever reducer, antihistamine, anti-diarrheal, motion-sickness remedy, and rehydration salts as appropriate. → *Expect:* each common symptom has one suitable option.
4. **Keep prescriptions labeled.** Carry medicines in original packaging or with pharmacy labels showing name, dose, and prescriber. → *Expect:* inspectors and clinicians can identify each medication.
5. **Calculate quantities.** Pack enough for the trip plus a few delay days, without carrying excessive amounts. → *Expect:* supply covers normal use and travel disruption.
6. **Check customs and transport rules.** [BRANCH: domestic | international] confirm restrictions for controlled medicines, liquids, needles, and scissors. → *Expect:* nothing in the kit is likely to be confiscated or illegal.
7. **Separate critical medicines.** Keep essential prescriptions, inhalers, epinephrine, or glucose supplies in carry-on or day bag. → *Expect:* critical items are available if checked luggage is lost.
8. **Store safely.** Use a labeled pouch, keep medicines dry and cool, and separate sharp items if required. → *Expect:* the kit is protected and easy to find.
9. **Record key details.** Include allergy list, emergency contacts, insurance number, and generic medicine names. → *Expect:* another adult can use the information in an emergency.

## Decision points

- Destination has limited pharmacies → pack more self-care supplies and a clinician-approved prescription reserve.
- Traveling with children → use age-appropriate formulations and dosing tools.
- Controlled medication is needed → carry a doctor's letter and verify destination rules before travel.
- Hot climate → choose storage that protects heat-sensitive medicines.

## Failure modes & recovery

- **F1 Medicine unlabeled:** detect loose pills or mixed containers, recover by returning them to labeled packaging.
- **F2 Expired supplies:** detect old dates or damaged packaging, recover by replacing before departure.
- **F3 Customs restriction:** detect banned or controlled item, recover by getting documentation or using a permitted alternative.
- **F4 Checked-bag loss:** detect essential medicine in checked luggage, recover by moving it to carry-on before bag drop.
- **F5 Wrong dose risk:** detect missing measuring tool or unclear label, recover by adding dosing syringe and written instructions.

## Verification

The kit contains wound supplies, appropriate symptom medicines, labeled prescriptions, legal travel quantities, and emergency details in an accessible pouch.

## Variations

- Hiking trip: add elastic wrap, tick tool, water purification, and larger blister supplies.
- Cruise: include motion-sickness options and enough prescriptions for missed-port delays.
- Business travel: keep the kit smaller but never remove essential prescriptions.
- Cold destination: protect liquids and devices from freezing.

## Safety & privacy

Medium risk from medication errors and legal restrictions. Keep medicines away from children, do not share prescriptions, and protect medical information from unnecessary disclosure.
