## Goal

Through the airport security checkpoint in one pass: pockets and bags pre-staged, the liquids and electronics rules already satisfied by your packing, and nothing of yours left in a gray tray.

## Preconditions

- Boarding pass accessible and the liquids/battery rules already honored at packing time (`travel/pack-a-suitcase` step 4: ≤100 ml containers in one clear bag for carry-on, power banks in the cabin bag).
- Timing per your airport's live guidance; the classic defaults (2 h domestic, 3 h international) exist because security queues are the least predictable leg of any journey.

## Steps

1. **Pre-stage while you queue.** The queue is prep time, not phone time: pockets emptied into the bag's top pocket (keys, coins, wallet, phone at the end), liquids bag and large electronics moved to the top of the carry-on, belt-and-boots question answered by looking at what this checkpoint is making people do. → *Expect:* by the trays, you are a ten-second unpacker while others excavate.
2. **Present the pass/ID at the entry check.** Airline pass (and ID where required) scanned or eyeballed. → *Expect:* waved into the lanes; family/assistance lanes taken when they apply to you.
3. **Load trays by the local rules, decisively.** Watch the signage and the people ahead: laptops/tablets out (unless this airport's scanners say leave them in — obey the local sign over habit), liquids bag out if required, jacket always, belt/shoes if directed, bag flat. Trays spaced, not stacked. → *Expect:* your items in 2 to 3 trays, nothing forgotten in a pocket — the pat-down you avoid is the one caused by one forgotten coin.
4. **Walk the scanner when beckoned.** Arch or body scanner per direction, pockets empty, arms as instructed. Alarms mean a calm secondary check: cooperate, it is routine and faster than it feels. → *Expect:* through in seconds, or a brief professional pat-down/swab resolved politely.
5. **Reclaim *everything*, then clear the area to repack.** Count your trays' contents back: laptop, liquids, jacket, belt, phone, watch, passport — the checkpoint's true toll is collected in forgotten items. Repack at the benches *behind* the belt, not at the rollers. → *Expect:* item count matches step 1's mental list; the belt area left free for the next traveler.
6. **Re-stage your documents for the gate.** Pass and passport back to their fixed pocket (the same pocket every time is the entire system). → *Expect:* documents at their home, hands free, gate-ward (`travel/check-in-for-a-flight` step 7 takes over).

## Decision points

- Trusted-traveler/fast-track lanes (PreCheck, registered schemes): different divestment rules — obey that lane's signs, not the main lane's ritual.
- Medical liquids, baby food, and mobility equipment: exempt from standard limits but declare them proactively at the belt; the announced exception clears fast, the discovered one slowly.
- Wearing the metal (piercings, medical implants): expect the alarm calmly and say the sentence early ("I have a hip implant"); screeners hear it hourly.

## Failure modes & recovery

- **F1 Oversized liquid discovered at the belt:** surrender it without litigation (the bin is full of better arguments than yours), or step out to check/chug/gift it if time and rules allow; repack lessons feed back to `travel/pack-a-suitcase`.
- **F2 Left an item in a tray (realized later):** immediately contact that terminal's security lost-and-found — trays are the top source of airport lost property and recovery rates are decent same-day.
- **F3 Selected for extra screening:** it is a sample, not a verdict; cooperate, watch your other belongings' trays from where you stand (ask a screener to hold them together), and budget the ten minutes.
- **F4 Queue catastrophically long, flight at risk:** tell a staff member your departure time — most airports quietly expedite tight connections; begging the queue works worse than asking the uniform.

## Verification

Through in one pass with no forgotten-pocket alarm, every tray item recovered against the mental count, liquids and electronics never argued with the rules, documents re-homed, and the belt area left clear behind you.

## Variations

- `us` TSA vs `eu` vs `asia` checkpoints differ mainly in shoes, liquids-scanner generations, and laptop rules; the read-the-local-signs habit is the universal adapter.
- Traveling with kids: one adult loads trays, one shepherds; strollers fold at the belt and everything takes one announcement longer — the family lane exists, use it.

## Safety & privacy

Medium risk to schedule and property, not person. The recipe's spine: comply with the local signage, pre-stage in the queue, count your items back out. Security screeners' day improves measurably per prepared traveler, and the queue behind you inherits the same gift.
