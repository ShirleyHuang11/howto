## Goal

Install a stair gate that fits snugly, latches reliably, and uses hardware mounting at the top of stairs.

## Preconditions

- You have a gate rated for the child's age and the stair location.
- You have the manufacturer's template, screws, brackets, drill, level, and measuring tape.
- Walls, banisters, or posts can accept hardware or approved mounting kits.
- You have permission to screw into surfaces if renting.

## Steps

1. **Choose top or bottom treatment.** ⚠️ *Irreversible:* use a hardware-mounted gate at the top of stairs; pressure-mounted gates can dislodge and should not be relied on there. → *Expect:* the top-of-stairs plan uses screwed brackets.
2. **Measure the opening.** Measure width at top, middle, and bottom, plus baseboard or banister shape. → *Expect:* the gate fits the narrowest and widest points within its rated range.
3. **Check swing direction.** Configure the gate so it does not swing out over the stairs if the model allows blocking that direction. → *Expect:* an opened gate does not create a fall path.
4. **Mark bracket height.** Use the template and level on both sides, keeping the bottom gap within the manufacturer's limit. → *Expect:* bracket marks align and leave no child-sized gap.
5. **Drill pilot holes.** Drill into studs, posts, banister kits, or solid mounting boards as specified. → *Expect:* screws will hold without splitting or stripping.
6. **Fasten brackets firmly.** Drive screws fully and check each bracket for movement by hand. → *Expect:* brackets do not flex or pull away.
7. **Hang and adjust the gate.** Set width, hinge tension, and latch alignment. [BRANCH: wall-to-wall | banister-to-wall] use the correct adapters. → *Expect:* the gate closes squarely.
8. **Test latch and pressure.** Pull, push, lift, and shake at child force and adult force without climbing on it. → *Expect:* the latch stays closed and the gate stays mounted.
9. **Teach household use.** Close it every time, avoid stepping over it, and remove it when the child outgrows the need. → *Expect:* adults use the gate consistently without creating trip hazards.

## Decision points

- Top of stairs → hardware-mounted gate only.
- Bottom of stairs → pressure-mounted may be acceptable if rated and checked often.
- Banister has no flat surface → use approved banister adapters, not improvised straps.
- Opening is too wide or angled → buy an extra-wide or angled-installation gate.

## Failure modes & recovery

- **F1 Gate loosens:** detect bracket movement or widening gaps → stop using it and refasten into solid structure.
- **F2 Latch does not catch:** detect bounce-open or partial closure → realign latch and hinges.
- **F3 Child can climb it:** detect footholds or climbing attempts → replace with a taller, vertical-bar design and increase supervision.
- **F4 Gap is too large:** detect head, torso, or limb-sized space → adjust height or choose a different gate.
- **F5 Adult trips over threshold:** detect stumbles or step-over behavior → use a no-threshold top-of-stairs model where possible.

## Verification

The installed gate is hardware-mounted at the top of stairs, fits within its rated width, has no unsafe gaps, and remains latched and fixed when pushed, pulled, and lifted.

## Variations

- `top-of-stairs`: hardware-mounted, no pressure-only gate, and avoid swing-over-stairs.
- `bottom-of-stairs`: pressure mount may be acceptable if the manufacturer permits it.
- `banister`: use a banister adapter kit to avoid weak or damaging attachment.
- `pet-use`: pet gates are not automatically child-safe; use a child-rated gate for children.

## Safety & privacy

High risk comes from falls. Follow the manufacturer's limits, mount into solid structure, and inspect regularly. Do not step over gates while carrying a child. A gate is a backup barrier, not a substitute for supervision.
