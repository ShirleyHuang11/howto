## Goal

Install a door chain or door guard that limits door opening while acknowledging that it is not a substitute for a proper lock.

## Preconditions

- You are allowed to drill or screw into the door and frame.
- The door, frame, and trim are solid enough to hold screws.
- You have the chain or guard kit, pencil, drill, pilot bit, screwdriver, and suitable screws.
- You know whether the door opens inward or outward.

## Steps

1. **Choose the correct side and height.** Position the guard where an adult can operate it and children cannot easily reach it. → *Expect:* the hardware is usable without bending or stretching.
2. **Check for solid frame material.** Press, tap, or inspect trim to avoid thin decorative casing only. → *Expect:* screws will bite into solid frame or blocking.
3. **Mark the frame piece.** Hold the fixed plate level on the frame and mark screw holes. → *Expect:* the plate sits flat and does not block weatherstripping.
4. **Mark the door piece.** Align the chain receiver or guard arm with enough slack for limited opening. → *Expect:* the door can open slightly but not widely when engaged.
5. **Drill pilot holes.** Use a bit smaller than the screws and stop before drilling through the door. → *Expect:* screws start straight and wood does not split.
6. **Fasten into solid material.** Drive screws firmly into the frame and door, using longer screws where safe and appropriate. → *Expect:* plates do not wiggle under hand pressure.
7. **Test the guard.** Engage it, open the door gently, then pull from inside with moderate force. → *Expect:* it holds normal limited opening without bending.
8. **Test normal door use.** Disengage it and open, close, latch, and lock the door normally. [BRANCH: rubs | clears] adjust placement if it catches. → *Expect:* the main lock and latch still work.
9. **Set household rules.** Use the guard only as a conversation barrier, not as primary security. → *Expect:* everyone understands its limits.

## Decision points

- Door opens outward → many chains are unsuitable; choose a security bar or outward-door guard rated for that use.
- Frame is hollow or weak → install blocking or choose a different product.
- Fire exit route is involved → confirm the guard can be released quickly from inside.
- Small children in home → place it high enough to prevent unsupervised use.

## Failure modes & recovery

- **F1 Screws strip out:** detect spinning screws or loose plate → use longer screws into solid framing or repair holes.
- **F2 Door will not close:** detect rubbing hardware → reposition the plate slightly.
- **F3 Chain gives a false sense of security:** detect reliance on it instead of lock → use deadbolt first and chain only for controlled opening.
- **F4 Frame trim splits:** detect cracks while fastening → stop, drill proper pilot holes, and move to stronger material.
- **F5 Guard traps someone:** detect hard-to-release operation → replace with a model that opens quickly from inside.

## Verification

The chain or guard is firmly screwed into solid door and frame material, limits opening when engaged, and does not interfere with the primary lock or emergency exit.

## Variations

- `chain`: simple and cheap, but weaker and easier to force than a solid swing guard.
- `swing-bar-guard`: often stronger if mounted into solid material.
- `rental`: ask permission and keep installation neat to avoid repair deductions.
- `metal-door-frame`: use manufacturer-approved screws or anchors.

## Safety & privacy

A chain slows casual entry but will not stop a determined forced entry. Do not open the door to threats just because the chain is engaged. Medium risk comes from drilling, weak mounting, and blocked emergency exit if poorly placed.
