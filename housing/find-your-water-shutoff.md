## Goal

You locate, test, and label the main water shutoff and useful fixture shutoffs before an emergency.

## Preconditions

- You have access to the home, basement, utility area, crawlspace, garage, or meter area.
- You have a flashlight, tag or tape, marker, and phone camera.
- You can run a faucet to test whether water stops.
- You know whether you rent, because some meter valves may be utility or landlord property.

## Steps

1. **Start where water enters.** Look along the wall facing the street, basement front wall, garage, utility closet, or near the water heater. → *Expect:* you find a pipe entering the building or a likely valve.
2. **Identify the main valve.** Look for a wheel handle, lever handle, or stopcock on the cold water line before branches split. → *Expect:* one valve appears to control the whole home.
3. **Check individual fixture valves.** Look under sinks, behind toilets, at washing machine hoses, and near the water heater. → *Expect:* you find smaller local shutoffs for common leak points.
4. **Test the main gently.** Turn a wheel clockwise or move a lever perpendicular to the pipe, using moderate hand pressure only. → *Expect:* the valve moves without bending pipe.
5. **Open a cold faucet.** Run a sink or tub after closing the suspected main valve. → *Expect:* flow slows and stops after pressure drains.
6. **Reopen the valve fully.** Return the wheel or lever to open position, then run water until air clears. → *Expect:* normal flow returns.
7. **Tag the valve.** Label "main water shutoff" with direction to close and date tested. → *Expect:* another person can identify it quickly.
8. **Photograph and share location.** Save a photo and tell household members or landlord where it is. → *Expect:* the shutoff can be found during a leak.

## Decision points

- If the valve is at the meter outside, confirm whether residents are allowed to operate it.
- If a valve will not turn by hand, do not force it; schedule replacement before an emergency.
- If the home has a well, the shutoff may be near the pressure tank rather than a street-side meter.
- If only hot water stops, you found a heater or branch valve, not the main.

## Failure modes & recovery

- **F1 Wrong valve:** detect hot or cold water still running normally, recover by tracing pipes closer to the entry point.
- **F2 Stuck valve:** detect no movement with moderate hand force, recover by labeling it untested and arranging repair.
- **F3 Valve leaks after testing:** detect drips around the stem, recover by reopening slightly or calling for packing or replacement.
- **F4 Cannot access meter:** detect locked pit, cover, or utility room, recover by contacting landlord, building manager, or utility.
- **F5 Household does not know:** detect others cannot find it from memory, recover by adding a visible tag and shared photo.

## Verification

After the main valve is closed, a cold faucet slows to a stop; after reopening, normal flow returns and the valve is labeled.

## Variations

- Apartments: each unit may have local riser valves, while the building main may be staff-only.
- Cold climates: shutoffs may be indoors to prevent freezing near the street wall.
- Older homes: gate valves may fail internally, so test before relying on them.

## Safety & privacy

Medium risk because a failed shutoff can worsen water damage. Test gently, avoid utility-owned valves unless allowed, and keep location details within the household or property team.
