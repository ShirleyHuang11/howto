## Goal

You keep indoor humidity and surface condensation low enough that mold cannot establish, and you clean up any small patches early so they do not spread or damage health.

## Preconditions

- The ability to open windows or run extractor fans in kitchen and bathroom.
- Optionally a hygrometer to read indoor humidity, and a fan heater or dehumidifier for damp rooms.
- Basic cleaning supplies and, for cleanup, gloves and a mask.

## Steps

1. **Find where moisture is produced and vent it at the source.** Cooking, showering, and drying clothes indoors are the big three. Run the extractor and open a window during and 15 minutes after. → *Expect:* steam clears quickly; mirror/tiles not left streaming.
2. **Air the whole home briefly each day.** Open windows wide for 5 to 10 minutes (cross-ventilation) even in winter; short and full beats permanently ajar. → *Expect:* a noticeable freshening of stale, damp air.
3. **Keep indoor humidity in range.** Aim for roughly 40 to 60 percent relative humidity; a hygrometer confirms it. If it sits high, run a dehumidifier or ventilate more. → *Expect:* readings settling into the 40 to 60 band.
4. **Leave an air gap behind furniture on cold external walls.** Pull wardrobes and beds a few centimetres off north-facing/outside walls so air can circulate and the wall stays warmer than its dew point. → *Expect:* no trapped, cold, still air pocket behind large furniture.
5. **Wipe condensation before it feeds mold.** Squeegee shower walls and wipe window sills each morning in cold weather; do not let pooled water sit. → *Expect:* dry sills and glass, not daily puddles.
6. **Keep rooms gently and evenly warm.** Cold surfaces are where water condenses; a little constant background heat beats brief blasts in an otherwise cold flat. → *Expect:* no single room left much colder than the rest.
7. **Deal with any patch early.** [BRANCH: small surface patch (< ~1 m², non-porous) → clean it yourself | large area, or on plaster/behind walls, or you have respiratory issues → get a professional and find the source] → *Expect:* a clear self-treat versus escalate decision.
8. **Clean a small patch safely.** Wear gloves and a mask, ventilate, wipe with a mold cleaner or diluted solution, and dry fully. ⚠️ *Irreversible:* do not dry-brush or vacuum dry mold (it aerosolises spores through the room); wipe damp and bin the cloth. → *Expect:* the patch removed and the surface left dry.

## Decision points

- Mold keeps returning in the same spot → it is a symptom, not the cause; find the leak, cold bridge, or ventilation failure behind it.
- Mold on a bedroom wall where someone sleeps, or an occupant with asthma/allergy → prioritise fixing it and consider relocating the sleeper meanwhile.
- Damp rising from the floor or spreading tide-marks → likely a building fault (rising/penetrating damp), not condensation; landlord or specialist territory.

## Failure modes & recovery

- **F1 Drying laundry indoors with windows shut:** one load dumps litres of water into the air → dry outside, in a vented room, or with a dehumidifier running.
- **F2 Blocked or disabled extractor fan:** steam lingers after showers → clean/repair the fan or open a window every time; do not just live with it.
- **F3 Furniture-mold on a cold wall:** black patch found when moving a wardrobe → clean it, then keep the gap and improve airflow so it does not recur.
- **F4 Bleaching over the surface only:** patch reappears in weeks → the moisture source remains; treat ventilation and the cold surface, not just the visible stain.

## Verification

Indoor humidity reads in the 40 to 60 percent range, kitchen and bathroom are vented at the source, furniture on cold walls has an air gap, condensation is wiped rather than pooling, and any small patch has been wiped away damp (never dry-brushed) leaving a dry surface, with larger or recurring growth escalated.

## Variations

- Humid climates: a dehumidifier or air conditioning becomes the primary tool rather than window ventilation.
- Rented homes: report persistent or structural damp to the landlord in writing; ongoing condensation is shared responsibility but rising/penetrating damp is theirs.

## Safety & privacy

Medium risk: the hazard is respiratory. Disturbing large areas of mold dry releases spores, so always wipe damp, ventilate, and wear a mask; anyone with asthma or a weakened immune system should not do the cleanup themselves and should escalate larger growth to a professional.
