## Goal

A defect in your rental (leak, broken heating, faulty appliance, mold) is reported to the landlord/manager in a way that gets it fixed — documented well enough to protect your deposit and your rights if it doesn't.

## Preconditions

- A genuine repair issue in a rented home, and clarity on whether it's landlord-side (structure, plumbing, heating, supplied appliances) or tenant-side (your lightbulbs `daily/home/change-a-lightbulb`, your drain habits `daily/home/unclog-a-drain` — many leases split these; read yours once).
- The reporting channel from your lease/move-in pack: portal, email, or phone (emergencies).
- **Emergency carve-out:** gas smell (`housing/set-up-utilities` step 5's hard-stop), major water gushing, no heat in freezing weather, electrical burning smells — these are *call now* items on the emergency line, not tickets.

## Steps

1. **Document before you report.** Photos/video of the defect with something for scale, timestamps; note when it started and what it affects ("bedroom window won't close → room at 12 °C at night"). → *Expect:* evidence that shows the problem to someone who isn't standing in your flat.
2. **Mitigate reasonable damage meanwhile.** Water: containment (bucket, towels, stop-tap if it's live flow — know where yours is *today*); electrical: stop using the circuit. Tenants have a duty to limit avoidable worsening. → *Expect:* the problem contained to its footprint; your mitigation noted (it's part of the story).
3. **Report through the official channel, in writing.** Even after a phone call, follow up in writing (email/portal — the paper trail *is* the protection): what, where, since when, photos attached, impact, and access availability ("home after 17:00, or contractor may enter with notice"). → *Expect:* a dated written report with evidence attached; ticket/reference number captured.
4. **Ask for the timeline and log the response.** Reasonable timeframes scale with severity (heating in winter: days; a dripping tap: weeks). Note their committed date. → *Expect:* a stated plan, or a follow-up date you set yourself ("I'll check back Friday if I haven't heard").
5. **Enable the fix.** Confirm access windows, secure pets, clear the work area (`embodied/household/tidy-a-room` around the defect); be present or explicitly authorize entry per your comfort and local law. → *Expect:* contractor access happens on the first attempt — failed-access loops are the most common self-inflicted delay.
6. **Verify the repair before closing the loop.** Test the actual function (window latches, heater cycles, leak dry after a week — some fixes fail slow); photograph the after-state. Reply to the thread: fixed and confirmed, thanks — or *not* fixed, with the new evidence. → *Expect:* the written record ends in either verified-fixed or an escalated reopened ticket.

## Decision points

- No response by the committed/reasonable date → escalate in writing up the chain: manager → landlord → (jurisdiction-dependent) the local tenancy authority, repeating the dated history. The escalation letter is trivially easy *because* steps 1–4 built the record.
- Tempted to withhold rent → in most jurisdictions this backfires without following an exact legal procedure — get advice from a tenants' association *before* touching rent; the documented-report path is the safe leverage.
- Repair is tenant-caused (your picture-hanging hole, your cracked hob) → different conversation: report honestly and expect to pay — hiding it until move-out costs more in deposit and trust.
- Recurring defect (third mold regrowth) → shift the framing in writing from "fix the symptom" to "investigate the cause" — recurrence documentation is what forces the real fix.

## Failure modes & recovery

- **F1 Reported by phone only, months pass, no record:** reconstruct now — write the summary email "as discussed on [date]…" and restart the clock with today's photos; the lesson is step 3's in-writing rule.
- **F2 Contractor no-shows:** log each failed window in the thread (dates matter for escalation); propose concrete alternatives; three strikes justifies the escalation path.
- **F3 "Fixed" but isn't:** your step-6 verification catches it while the ticket is warm — reopen with the after-photos; a closed ticket reopened beats a new ticket in every queue.
- **F4 Landlord blames tenant use:** your move-in inventory/photos (this is why they exist) and the mitigation record are the counter-evidence; tenants' associations adjudicate these patterns daily — join one before you need it.

## Verification

The defect is fixed and function-tested, the written thread runs report → response → repair → your confirmation with photos at both ends, and your deposit-relevant record is stronger, not weaker, than before the defect.

## Variations

- `de`: Mängelanzeige has formal standing and rent-reduction (Mietminderung) is codified but procedural — association advice first; `uk`: repair obligations are statutory (Landlord and Tenant Act-class), councils enforce; `us`: state-by-state habitability law, some states allow repair-and-deduct with strict steps.
- Managed buildings with portals: the portal *is* the paper trail — resist the temptation of the hallway verbal report to the friendly super.

## Safety & privacy

Medium risk split between the emergencies (which skip this recipe for the phone) and the money (deposit/rights, protected by the written record). Photos of your home go only into the repair thread — crop what the defect doesn't need.
