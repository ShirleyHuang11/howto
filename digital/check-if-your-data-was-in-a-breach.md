## Goal

You determine whether an email address or phone number appears in known data breaches, then take password and two-factor actions for affected accounts.

## Preconditions

- You know the email addresses and phone numbers you use for accounts.
- You have access to your password manager or a place to update passwords safely.
- You are prepared to treat breach results as a prompt for action, not as proof of immediate account takeover.

## Steps

1. **Use a reputable breach-check service.** Open a known service such as Have I Been Pwned or a password manager's built-in breach monitor by typing the address directly. → *Expect:* the site explains what it checks and does not ask for your password.
2. **Check one identifier at a time.** Enter an email address or phone number, then submit. → *Expect:* the result lists no breaches or names specific breach incidents.
3. **Record affected services.** Note breached sites, data types exposed, and breach dates. → *Expect:* you have a short action list by account.
4. **Prioritize passwords reused anywhere.** Search your password manager for the affected email and reused or weak passwords. → *Expect:* reused passwords are identified before lower-risk cleanup.
5. **Change exposed or reused passwords.** Log in through each service's official site and set a unique password generated by the password manager. → *Expect:* the password manager stores a new unique password for each affected account.
6. **Turn on two-factor authentication.** Prefer authenticator app, passkey, or security key over SMS when available. → *Expect:* the account shows 2FA enabled and recovery codes saved privately.
7. **Review account activity.** Check recent logins, devices, recovery email, phone number, forwarding rules, and payment details on affected accounts. → *Expect:* no unknown sessions or settings remain.
8. **Monitor for follow-up scams.** Treat breach-themed calls, texts, and emails as suspicious, especially if they cite old personal details. → *Expect:* you verify through official channels before acting.

## Decision points

- [BRANCH: password reused | password unique] Reused passwords require immediate changes everywhere they appear; unique passwords mainly require monitoring and 2FA.
- Breach exposed financial or identity data → consider card replacement, credit freeze, or identity monitoring based on what was exposed.
- Breach result is for an old account → close the account or change its password if it still exists.
- Service asks for your current password outside its login page → stop, because breach checkers do not need it.

## Failure modes & recovery

- **F1 Fake breach checker:** detect requests for passwords, payment, or alarming popups; recover by closing it and using a known service typed directly.
- **F2 Cannot access affected account:** detect failed login or unknown recovery details; recover through official account recovery and secure email first.
- **F3 Password manager shows many reused passwords:** detect duplicates; recover by changing the highest-value accounts first: email, bank, work, cloud, social.
- **F4 Unknown login found:** detect unfamiliar device, IP, or session; recover by signing out all sessions, changing password, and checking recovery settings.

## Verification

Every affected account with reused, weak, or exposed credentials now has a unique password and 2FA enabled where supported, and account activity shows no unknown active sessions.

## Variations

- `mobile-app`: password manager apps often include Watchtower, Security Dashboard, or Password Checkup views.
- Family accounts: check shared email addresses and old phone numbers used for school, utilities, or subscriptions.
- Work accounts: follow company security reporting rules if a work email appears in a breach.

## Safety & privacy

Medium risk because breach data enables credential stuffing and targeted scams. Never enter passwords into breach lookup sites, and expect criminals to reuse old personal details to sound convincing.
