## Goal

An elderly relative has practical scam protections, a calm family response plan, and a no-shame route for reporting suspicious calls, texts, emails, or payments.

## Preconditions

- The relative agrees to discuss safety and retains control of their choices unless a legal arrangement says otherwise.
- You know which phone, email, bank, social, and shopping accounts they use.
- You can offer help without demanding passwords or taking over unnecessarily.

## Steps

1. **Start with a calm conversation.** Frame the topic as criminals getting better, not the relative being careless. → *Expect:* the relative is willing to talk without feeling accused.
2. **Name the common scams.** Cover grandchild emergency, romance, tech support, bank fraud, government threat, lottery/prize, delivery, investment, and gift-card scams. → *Expect:* the relative recognizes at least a few scripts.
3. **Create a verification rule.** Agree that urgent money or secrecy requests require calling a known family number or official number first. → *Expect:* the rule is simple enough to remember under stress.
4. **Harden phone and messages.** Enable spam filtering, block unknown repeat callers, silence unknown callers if acceptable, and pin trusted contacts. → *Expect:* fewer suspicious calls and easier access to real contacts.
5. **Protect financial accounts.** Turn on transaction alerts, set lower transfer limits where practical, and discuss trusted-contact or view-only monitoring options. → *Expect:* unusual transactions generate alerts before losses grow.
6. **Secure key accounts.** Use unique passwords, a password manager if acceptable, and 2FA for email, bank, and phone carrier accounts. → *Expect:* account takeover becomes harder.
7. **Limit exposed payment paths.** Discuss avoiding gift cards for emergencies, not storing cards on unfamiliar sites, and using a credit card rather than debit for online purchases when possible. → *Expect:* risky payment methods are less available in panic moments.
8. **Make an after-a-hit plan.** Write down who to call first: bank/card, trusted family contact, local police/nonemergency if needed, and account providers. → *Expect:* the relative has a visible action list.
9. **Schedule gentle check-ins.** Review recent calls, texts, charges, and mail periodically with permission. → *Expect:* problems surface early without interrogation.

## Decision points

- [BRANCH: independent relative | needs formal support] Independent relatives need consent-based help; impaired decision-making may require durable power of attorney or adult protective advice.
- Relative resists password manager → prioritize unique written passwords stored securely over reused passwords.
- Bank offers trusted contact or view-only access → use it if the relative agrees and understands what it permits.
- A scam already happened → focus on stopping loss and reporting, not blame.

## Failure modes & recovery

- **F1 Conversation turns defensive:** detect anger, embarrassment, or withdrawal; recover by pausing and returning later with one practical protection.
- **F2 Scammer instructed secrecy:** detect hidden calls, gift cards, or fear; recover by validating the fear, then verifying with known contacts.
- **F3 Money already sent:** detect charge, transfer, or gift card purchase; recover by contacting the payment provider immediately and preserving receipts/messages.
- **F4 Helper overreaches:** detect loss of trust or password sharing discomfort; recover by using consent, view-only tools, and documented boundaries.

## Verification

The relative can state the verification rule, has spam and transaction alerts enabled where acceptable, and has a written after-a-hit contact plan.

## Variations

- `phone-call`: a printed script near the phone can say, "I do not handle money on surprise calls. I will call back."
- Long-distance family: use shared alerts, scheduled calls, and trusted local contacts.
- Dementia or cognitive decline: consult legal and care professionals about authority, safeguards, and mandatory reporting rules.

## Safety & privacy

High risk because scams can drain savings and cause shame that delays help. Respect autonomy, minimize password sharing, and keep records of any financial-monitoring permissions.
